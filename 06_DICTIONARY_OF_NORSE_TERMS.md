# Dictionary of Norse Terms — Iron Ledger

**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr

**Purpose:** A comprehensive glossary of terminology, language,
idiom, and usage specific to this setting. Covers Old Norse
roots, in-world coinages, mercenary slang, trade language,
supernatural vocabulary, material culture, and the everyday
speech of the Rimevegr. Each entry is a tool for prose -- use
it to give characters the right word for the right thing. A man
who calls his axe a "brand" knows a different world from a man
who calls it a "chopper."

**Usage rules:**

- Characters use these terms naturally. Never define a term in
  narration. Meaning arrives through context.
- The glossary includes real Old Norse words (adapted for
  readability), Rimevegr coinages, and mercenary slang that
  developed in the three centuries since the Cracking.
- Pronunciation does not matter. The reader will sound them in
  their head however they see fit. Spelling is for the eye.
- Terms marked **[Historical]** derive from attested Old Norse
  or early medieval Scandinavian usage. Terms marked **[Coined]**
  are invented for this setting. Terms marked **[Slang]** are
  mercenary or common speech, often crude.

> **Cross-reference:** `01_RIMEVEGR_SETTING_BIBLE.md` for world
> context, `pantheon.md` for divine terminology, `character-bible.md`
> for individual speech patterns, `dialogue-patterns.md` for
> voice rules.

## A

**Aesrgrim** — [Coined] "The Grim Gods." Collective name for
the High Court of the pantheon: Odynn, Thurr, Fraeja, Lopt, and
Hael. The gods who dealt with war, death, and binding. Used in
formal oaths and camp curses. "By the Aesrgrim" is serious. "By
every grim god" is not -- it is frustration.

**Aett** — [Historical] A family group or clan. Eight families
made an aett. In the Rimevegr the word survives as a unit of
social belonging -- "he has no aett" means a man has no kin
network. The loneliest thing you can say about someone.

**Ale-mist** — [Slang] Cheapest grade of ale brewed from
whatever grain is on hand. Barely alcoholic. Tastes of sour
water and old bread. The standard mercenary drink when anything
better is unavailable. "Ale-mist and hard bread" is shorthand
for subsistence-level marching.

**Ale-price** — [Coined] What a settlement charges for
hospitality. The literal cost of food and drink for quartering
a band. Negotiated before weapons are set down. "What is the
ale-price?" is the first question Gest asks at any gate.

**Allting** — [Historical] A general assembly. Larger than a
local thing. Multiple settlements send representatives. In the
Rimevegr, alltings are rare and dangerous -- assembling leaders
in one place invites ambush.

**Amadou** — [Historical] Fire-starting material made from
bracket fungus (Fomes fomentarius). Scraped to a felt-like
texture, it catches and holds an ember better than any other
tinder. Carried in the fire-pouch. Worth its weight in copper
to a man on the march.

**Amber-path** — [Coined] The old southern trade route that
carried amber north and silver south before the Cracking. Now
a broken series of mud tracks and frozen ruts. Used
metaphorically: "He walks the amber-path" means a man is trying
to do business the old way, when the old way no longer works.

**Anchor-stone** — [Coined] A heavy flat stone used as a base
for a temporary forge. Also: the central stone of a fire-ring
in camp. Metaphorically, the person or thing the band cannot do
without. "Dalla is the anchor-stone" means the cook holds
everything together.

**Anvil-song** — [Slang] The sound of a blacksmith working.
Heard at a distance, it means a settlement is alive and
producing. A hammer striking nothing (a cold anvil) means the
smith is dead or fled.

**Arm-ring** — [Historical] A coiled band of silver or bronze
worn on the upper arm. Not decoration -- portable wealth. Arm-
rings can be hacked into pieces (hack-silver) for small
transactions. A man's arm-rings are his treasury. "He wears
three rings" means he has three contracts' worth of savings.

**Ash** — [Historical/Coined] 1. The tree (Fraxinus). Preferred
wood for spear shafts and tool handles -- strong, flexible,
straight-grained. 2. Call-name for the band's galdr-worker. 3. What remains after a fire. In the Rimevegr, where fire is
life, "ash" carries triple weight.

**Ash-born** — [Coined] Call-name for Thorne. Refers to the
hall-fire that scarred him. In broader usage: anyone born out of
a catastrophe. Applied to settlements rebuilt after burning. "An
ash-born village" means it has been destroyed and remade.

**Ashfall** — [Coined] A named barrow near Frostfjord Hollow.
Cleared by the band. Also used as a common descriptor for the
grey particulate that falls with heavy rime-fog -- not true ash,
but it looks and tastes like it.

**Axe-debt** — [Slang] What a man owes when someone else's axe
saved his life. Informal, unrecorded, but remembered. An axe-
debt between Named Men is serious. Between commons, it is a bond
that elevates both.

**Axe-hand** — [Historical] The dominant hand. Also: a general
term for a competent fighter. "He has an axe-hand" means the
man can swing and hit what he aims at. The minimum qualification
for band membership.

**Axe-haft** — [Historical] The wooden handle of an axe.
Checked daily for looseness. A loose haft kills the wielder. In
prose, Voss's hand resting on his axe-haft is his centering
gesture -- the way other men press palms together.

**Ale-sick** — [Slang] Hungover. Useless until midday. "He is
ale-sick" is said without sympathy. The band has no patience
for men who drank what the band could not afford.

**Ale-wife** — [Historical] A woman who brews and sells ale.
In many settlements, the only woman conducting independent
trade. Ale-wives know the price of grain before anyone else.

**Alm** — [Historical] Elm. The wood for bows and shield-
planks. Alm-wood is resilient -- it bends before it breaks.
"He has alm in him" means a man absorbs punishment.

**Altar-stone** — [Historical] A flat rock, usually glacial,
used for blóts and oath-swearing. Stained dark with old blood.
Permanent fixtures at older settlements.

**Amber** — [Historical] Fossilized tree resin. Trade good.
Rare in the Rimevegr since the Cracking. A piece of amber is
worth more than its weight in silver, primarily to southern
traders who no longer come.

**Andvari's curse** — [Slang] Gold or silver that brings bad
luck. Named for the old saga. "That loot has Andvari's curse"
means spending it will cost more than it yields.

**Andaþrumur** — [Coined] Breath-thunder. The roaring inward
noise heard when the Veil presses too close to the living body:
pulse, blood, breath, and bone all sounding too loud inside the
skull. Common among outlaws and overworked seiðr-practitioners.

**Andavörðr** — [Coined] Spirit-ward. An object saturated by
long use, oath, grief, or repeated touch until it behaves as if
it remembers its owners. Not fully alive, not safely inert.

**Aptrgangr skugga** — [Coined/Historical] Returning-shadow. The
weakest kind of dead presence: not a full revenant, but a
repeating impression of motion, fear, or habit left in a place.

**Angle-iron** — [Coined] An L-shaped iron bracket. Used for
structural joints in wooden construction. A settlement with
angle-iron available has a good smith.

**Anvil** — [Historical] The smith's primary tool. A heavy iron
block with a flat face, a horn, and a heel. The sound of hammer
on anvil carries across any settlement. Silence from the smithy
means trouble.

**Arm-brace** — [Historical] A leather guard worn on the bow-
arm to prevent string-slap. Simple and necessary. Bruised
forearms mark a man who forgot his arm-brace.

**Arrow-bag** — [Coined] A quiver. Leather tube with a flap.
Carried across the back or at the hip. A full arrow-bag holds
twenty shafts. After a fight, arrows are recovered and returned
to the bag -- shafts are too costly to leave in the ground.

**Ash-pit** — [Historical] The depression beneath a hearth where
ash accumulates. Cleaned weekly in a settlement, never on the
march. Ash-pit ash is used for making lye soap.

**Ash-tree** — [Historical] Yggdrasil, the World-Tree, was an
ash. In the Rimevegr, ash trees are practical: they provide the
best spear-shafts and axe-handles. The mythological connection
makes ash-wood feel more important than it has a right to feel.

**Ashen Reach** — [Coined] A village of 142 souls. Ruled by the
Pale Widow. Grain economy. Significant political player.

**Auger** — [Historical] A T-handled boring tool. Drills round
holes in wood. Used for barrel-making, boat repair, and
construction. Every settlement smithy makes them.

**Awl** — [Historical] A pointed tool for punching holes in
leather. Essential for field repairs to belts, boots, and
harness. Carried by Gest alongside the ledger kit.

**Auðsýn** — [Coined] First-sight. The initial völva break in
ordinary seeing, when patterns begin to glimmer at the edge of
the visible world. Feared because it usually arrives unasked.

**Axle** — [Historical] The shaft connecting two wheels. In the
Rimevegr, axles are rare because carts are rare. The word
appears in settlement contexts and as machinery metaphor: "the
axle of the operation" means the part that keeps things turning.

## B

**Back-trail** — [Slang] The path the band has already walked.
Checking the back-trail means looking for followers. "Our back-
trail is clean" means no one is tracking us.

**Bait-fire** — [Slang] A fire left burning at an abandoned
campsite to delay pursuit. The enemy investigates; the band
gains distance. Also: a fire set as a trap -- enemies approach
the light and find arrows instead of warmth.

**Bale-fire** — [Historical] A large outdoor fire, typically
for burning the dead or their possessions. A bale-fire for a
Named Man uses good wood. For a common, whatever is at hand. A
bale-fire seen at distance tells you someone died and was
important enough to burn.

**Band-break** — [Coined] The moment a mercenary band
disintegrates. Not a battle -- a collapse. Pay is too late, the
captain is too weak, the grievances pile too high. Men walk
away in the night. "We are three bad days from a band-break"
is the arithmetic of failure.

**Beinasöngr** — [Coined] Bone-song. The low male mourning
chant sung at the barrow while the dead are settled into earth
or stone. Rhythmic, whispered, and more felt in the ribs than
heard in the ear.

**Blóðminni** — [Coined] Blood-memory. The residue left in a
weapon, garment, or place by intense pain, oath, or killing.
Some seiðr-workers and völvas can read it like heat in metal.

**Bank-fire** — [Historical] A fire covered in its own ash to
preservecoals overnight. The morning ritual: scrape ash, expose
coals, blow gently, add tinder. Flame in two minutes. Letting
the bank-fire die is a punishable failure on watch.

**Barley-water** — [Historical] Grain soaked for hours and the
runoff drunk. Mildly nutritious. The drink of sick men and those
whose teeth have given out. Not complimentary.

**Barn-post** — [Historical] The structural upright of a byre.
Carved with runes in older settlements -- livestock is watched
over, even if no one admits by whom.

**Barrow** — [Historical] A burial mound. In the Rimevegr,
barrows are dangerous -- the dead inside may not be quiet. A
sealed barrow is a problem for later. An open barrow is a
problem for now.

**Barrow-breath** — [Coined] The cold, stale air that pours out
of an opened barrow. Smells of old earth, old bone, and
something sweet-rotten that has no name. Men who have cleared
barrows can identify the smell from twenty paces.

**Barrow-black** — [Coined] Total darkness inside an unopened
barrow. Torchlight reaches only a few paces. Beyond is nothing.
The darkness has weight.

**Barrow-price** — [Coined] The fee for clearing a barrow.
Higher than any other contract because the work is terrible and
men do not always come back. Paid in advance -- half before entry,
half on completion.

**Barrowrise** — [Coined] A named month in the Rimevegr
calendar. The Veil is thinnest, the dead most restless. Travel
is avoided. Settlements bolt their doors. Mercenary bands demand
double rates.

**Barrow-wight** — [Historical] An older term for a restless
dead within a barrow. Essentially synonymous with draugr but
more literary. Skalds use "barrow-wight"; fighting men say
"the dead thing."

**Bark-bread** — [Historical] Ground inner bark (pine, birch)
mixed with whatever flour is available and baked into flat
loaves. Famine food. The taste is resinous and bitter. Keeps
a man alive when grain has failed.

**Barter-weight** — [Coined] The agreed exchange value of goods
in a non-coin transaction. "What is the barter-weight of a
good axe?" Answer: two months' grain for one family. Gest
maintains barter-weights in the ledger.

**Bast** — [Historical] Inner bark of lime or linden trees.
Twisted into rope, woven into rough cloth, or used raw as
bandage material. The cheapest cordage available. "Bast-rope"
means improvised and temporary.

**Baton** — [Historical] A thick stick used to drive a blade
through wood when splitting kindling. The knife is placed on the
wood; the baton strikes the knife spine. Every man in the band
knows this technique.

**Bed-roll** — [Historical] A sleeping arrangement. Wool blanket
or hide, ground cloth, rolled tight for march. The man who loses
his bed-roll sleeps in his cloak on bare ground. "He has no
bed-roll" is shorthand for destitute.

**Bed-rock** — [Slang] A heated stone wrapped in cloth, placed
in the sleeping area for warmth. Heated by the fire, carried to
the bed-roll. Holds warmth for two to three hours. The
difference between sleeping and shivering.

**Berserker** — [Historical] A warrior who enters killing fury.
In the Rimevegr, not supernatural -- probably. The eyes change,
the pain stops, the reason leaves. What remains is a weapon
that does not know when to stop swinging. Feared by both sides.
"He went berserker" is not a compliment.

**Biface** — [Historical] A stone tool knapped on both sides.
Paleolithic technology, but in settlements where iron is scarce,
flint bifaces still serve for scraping hides and cutting leather.

**Bilge** — [Historical] The lowest interior of a ship's hull,
where foul water collects. By metaphor: the worst position in
any group. "He is in the bilge" means he is at the bottom.

**Bile-water** — [Slang] Brackish or contaminated water.
Standing pools, runoff near latrines, anything that smells
wrong. Drinking bile-water brings gut-sickness within hours.
Dalla's first rule: clear water or boiled water, never
bile-water.

**Biting-wind** — [Coined] Wind cold enough to cause frostbite
on exposed skin within minutes. The first stage of a rime storm.
When the biting-wind starts, shelter becomes urgent.

**Black-iron** — [Coined] Unfinished or poorly forged iron.
Brittle, pitted, prone to cracking. Common in weapons looted
from dead men or bought cheap in failing settlements. "He
carries black-iron" means a man's equipment will fail him when
he needs it most.

**Black Pine** — [Coined] The dominant tree of the Outvegr --
tall, close-growing, dark-needled conifers. Their resin is
useful. Their shade kills undergrowth. Their density muffles
sound. Walking through the Black Pine Wilds is walking through
an absence.

**Black-tongue** — [Slang] 1. A disease: ulcers on the gums and
tongue from vitamin deficiency. Common in late Long Dark when
foraging yields nothing green. 2. A gossip or slanderer.

**Blade-edge** — [Historical] The sharpened cutting face of a
weapon. Maintaining a blade-edge in the field is daily work.
"He has lost his blade-edge" describes a man past his prime.

**Blade-light** — [Slang] The faint gleam of a well-maintained
weapon in firelight. A fighting man's blade should show blade-
light -- it means the steel is clean, oiled, and sharp. "No
blade-light" is an insult: the man does not care for his tools.

**Blót** — [Historical] A sacrifice. Animal blood poured on an
altar stone or the roots of a sacred tree. In the Rimevegr,
blóts continue even though the gods do not answer. The ritual
is the point. The silence afterward is everyone's private
problem.

**Blood-eagle** — [Historical] A ritual execution of disputed
historicity. In the Rimevegr, referenced as a threat but never
performed on-page. The name alone carries enough weight. "He
deserves the blood-eagle" is the worst thing one man can say
about another.

**Blood-galdr** — [Coined] Galdr performed using another
person's blood as material component. Universally forbidden.
Automatic outlawry if discovered. The blood must be unwilling --
a galdr-worker who uses his own blood is merely paying the
standard cost. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.3
(blood-galdr exception).

**Blood-mark** — [Coined] The stain left on the ground where
a man died. In frozen ground, blood-marks persist for weeks --
dark patches in the frost. The band walks past blood-marks from
previous fights. Sometimes their own.

**Blood-oath** — [Historical] An oath sealed by exchanging
blood on iron. Binds individuals, not institutions. Stronger
than a verbal oath, weaker than an Odynn-sworn contract.
Breaking a blood-oath does not end a career, but it ends a
reputation. Men who break blood-oaths find fewer men willing
to bleed with them.

**Blood-price** — [Historical] See _weregild_. The silver value
of a human life. "What is the blood-price for a farmer?" is a
question that gets asked at contract negotiations.

**Blood Sun** — [Coined] An extremely rare weather event. The
sun appears blood-red and radiates no warmth. Animals freeze in
place. Wise folk stay indoors. No one has a satisfactory
explanation.

**Bloom** — [Historical] A mass of spongy iron produced in a
bloomery furnace. Must be hammered repeatedly to drive out slag
before it becomes usable iron. The starting point for all
Rimevegr iron-work.

**Bloomery** — [Historical] A small furnace for smelting iron
ore. Clay-walled, charcoal-fired, bellows-driven. Produces a
bloom of crude iron. A working bloomery is a settlement's most
strategically important installation after the grain store.

**Blowpipe** — [Historical] A hollow tube for directing breath
onto an ember or coal. Precise and effective. Faster than
bellows for starting fires. Part of the fire-pouch kit for
some men.

**Boar's-bristle** — [Historical] Stiff pig hair. Used for
brush-making, thread-threading (the stiff point acts as a
needle), and wound probes.

**Board-game** — [Historical] Settlement entertainment. Mainly
hnefatafl and variants. Carved from whatever wood is on hand.
The pieces are sometimes bone, sometimes pebbles.

**Bodkin** — [Historical] A thick, blunt needle for threading
cord through eyelets. Field repair tool. Also: a type of narrow
arrowhead designed to punch through mail.

**Bog-cotton** — [Historical] Eriophorum. White-tufted marsh
grass. The tufts are collected for lamp-wicks, wound padding,
and emergency tinder. One of the few useful plants found in
moorland bogs.

**Bog-iron** — [Historical] Iron extracted from peat bogs. Low
quality but available where ore is not. Smelted in a clay
furnace. The resulting metal is spongy and must be heavily
worked. Bog-iron weapons are better than no weapons.

**Bone-dice** — [Historical] Gambling dice carved from knuckle-
bones (astragali). Four faces: broad flat, narrow flat, convex,
concave. The primary camp game. The game matters less than the
social dynamics around it.

**Bone-meal** — [Historical] Ground bones added to stew for
calcium and thickening. Extracted from marrow-cracked long
bones after the marrow has been eaten. Nothing is wasted.

**Bone-needle** — [Historical] A sewing needle carved from
animal bone. Less effective than iron, but iron needles are
expensive and bone is free. Most clothing repairs in the field
are done with bone-needles.

**Bone-Walker** — [Coined] Epithet for Skathi, goddess of
winter and the hunt. Also applied to anyone who walks the High
Rime-Moors alone, because only the mad or the divine would.

**Bondi** — [Historical] A free farmer. The backbone of
settlement economy. A bondi owns land, pays no lord's rent
(there are no lords, only jarls with swords), and votes at the
thing. The bondi's opinion matters because the bondi grows the
grain.

**Brand** — [Historical] A sword. More literary than common
speech -- skalds use "brand" in stories. Working men say
"sword" or "blade." When a character says "brand," he is
quoting a saga or trying to sound important.

**Brazier** — [Historical] A metal container for holding
burning coals. Portable heat. Rare and expensive in the
Rimevegr. Jarls have them. The band does not.

**Breach** — [Historical] 1. An opening in a wall or
palisade. 2. A violation of oath. Both meanings are equally
dangerous. "He made a breach" requires context to know which.

**Break-ice** — [Coined] The man sent first onto frozen water.
Lightest in the band. He walks, stops, stomps. If the ice holds,
the column follows. Break-ice duty is assigned by weight, not
by cowardice.

**Breast-plate** — [Historical] A shaped piece of iron covering
the chest. Rarer than brynja in the Rimevegr. A breast-plate
protects less but weighs less. Infantry prefer brynja; scouts
prefer leather.

**Brine** — [Historical] Salt water. Used for preserving meat
and fish. A brine barrel is part of the band's essential
supply train. Brine also cleans wounds (painfully).

**Brine-barrel** — [Coined] A barrel of salt water used for
preserving fish or meat on the march. Heavy, awkward, vital.
Gest's accounting treats the brine-barrel as a strategic asset:
its weight is a cost; its contents are survival.

**Brynja** — [Historical] A mail shirt. Chainmail. The Norse
term. Weight: 20-30 pounds. Sound: constant jingling. A man
wearing a brynja cannot sneak, but he can survive a sword
stroke that would open an unarmored man from shoulder to hip.
In the Rimevegr, a brynja is wealth you wear.

**Brúðarboð** — [Coined] The bride-offer. A song performed by the
groom's party at the threshold of the bride's hall, listing his
lineage, property, and deeds. A legal document set to melody.
The door stays closed until the song is finished.

**Brúðargrátr** — [Coined] The bride-grief. The women's mourning
song for the bride who is leaving her family. Minor key,
descending melody. Acknowledges the loss the wedding represents.
The bride herself is silent.

**Brúðarlög** — [Coined] Wedding-songs. The full body of
marriage verse: threshold songs, bride-grief, ribald feast
verses, and wedding-night songs that turn household transfer
into communal memory.

**Burn rate** — [Coined] The daily cost of keeping the band
alive. Approximately one silver per day for food, replacement
gear, and minor operating costs. Gest tracks this daily. The
burn rate is the band's heartbeat. When it exceeds income, the
band is dying.

**Byre** — [Historical] An animal shelter or barn. In
settlements, the byre adjoins the longhouse. The animals' body
heat warms the building through the shared wall. Being
quartered in the byre means the settlement tolerates the band
but does not trust it.

**Byrnie** — [Historical] A short mail shirt. Lighter than a
full brynja. Covers the torso, not the thighs. A compromise
between protection and mobility.

## C

**Cairn** — [Historical] A pile of stones marking a trail, a
grave, or a boundary. In the Rimevegr, older cairns were built
by people no one remembers, for purposes no one can explain.
Newer cairns mark paths through the Outvegr.

**Calf-skin** — [Historical] The softest available leather.
Used for writing surfaces (vellum), glove linings, and fine
bags. Expensive because every calf-skin means one less calf
grown to a cow.

**Call-name** — [Coined] An earned name that replaces the birth
name in daily use. Never self-chosen. Given by the band when a
man has done something memorable or displays an inescapable
trait. "Cold-Eye" (Voss), "Hook" (Kell), "Ledger" (Gest).
Receiving a call-name is informal promotion -- the band has
noticed you.

**Camp-follower** — [Historical] Anyone who travels with a
military force without being a fighter. Cooks, healers,
servants, partners. In the Rimevegr, Dalla is technically a
camp-follower. She would disagree with the term.

**Candle-mark** — [Historical] A unit of time. One candle-mark
is approximately one hour -- measured by how far a candle burns
between scored lines. A settlement technique. On the march, time
is sun position and gut feeling.

**Cadence** — [Coined] The span a chronicle post covers. Daily
cadence means one day only. Weekly cadence means every day in the
week-long span. Monthly and seasonal cadence work the same way for
their longer spans. Used by the time manager when a simulation
result should appear across more than one day.

**Daily post** — [Coined] A chronicle entry tied to one day only.
Used for weather, single events, and point-in-time state.

**Weekly post** — [Coined] A chronicle entry that applies across
every day of the week it covers. Used when a full weekly
simulation should read as present throughout that week.

**Monthly post** — [Coined] A chronicle entry that applies across
every day of the month it covers. Used for broader settlement or
economy results.

**Seasonal post** — [Coined] A chronicle entry that applies across
every day of the season it covers. Used for slow, large-scale
changes that are meant to feel present throughout the whole
season.

**Cauldron** — [Historical] A large iron or bronze cooking pot.
The communal cauldron is the band's second most important
possession after the strongbox. Dalla's instruments. It hangs
from a tripod over the fire.

**Cave-camp** — [Coined] A natural cave used for shelter. Better
than open ground but dangerous -- caves collect smoke, harbor
animals, and sometimes connect to barrows. Enter with torch and
weapon.

**Caulk** — [Historical] Material used to seal gaps -- in ships,
in buildings, in barrels. Pine pitch, moss, animal hair mixed
with tar. The smell of caulk is the smell of construction.

**Cape-weather** — [Slang] Wind and rain bad enough to pull the
cloak over the head and hunch forward. Not shelter-worthy, just
miserable. "It is cape-weather" means keep marching, but it
will cost morale.

**Captain's share** — [Coined] The portion of loot or payment
that goes to the band's captain. In a standard svarthird: 40%.
In a tyrant hird: 60%. The percentage is set when the band
forms. Changing it mid-contract is a band-break trigger.

**Char-cloth** — [Historical] Linen scorched in a sealed tin
until carbon-black. The best fire-starting tinder. Catches a
spark instantly. Carried in the fire-pouch. Making char-cloth
is a camp task -- everyone contributes old cloth scraps.

**Charcoal** — [Historical] Wood burned in a sealed kiln until
only carbon remains. Burns hotter than wood. Essential for
smithing. Charcoal kilns are strategic assets -- a settlement
without charcoal cannot forge.

**Charcoal-ledger** — [Coined] The band's financial record.
Kept by the sergeant (Gest) in charcoal notation on scraped
hide or rough parchment. Numbers, names, dates, debts. The
ledger is the band's institutional memory. It outlasts men.

**Chest-beat** — [Historical] The physical invocation of Thurr.
A closed fist struck against the sternum, hard enough to
bruise. Done before a fight. Not prayer -- more like priming a
weapon.

**Chin-rime** — [Coined] Frost that forms on beards and
eyebrows in deep cold. Every exhaled breath freezes on facial
hair. After an hour of marching in deep Long Dark, every man's
face is white-crusted. The chin-rime is shaken loose with a
hand-swipe that becomes unconscious habit.

**Cloak-weight** — [Slang] How heavy your cloak is from
absorbed moisture. A dry wool cloak weighs five pounds. A
soaked one weighs fifteen. "Full cloak-weight" means the
weather has been bad for days and nothing has dried.

**Close-quarter** — [Coined] Fighting in confined space --
inside a longhouse, a barrow corridor, between trees. Shields
are useless. Seax and short-axe prevail. The tall man has no
advantage. The vicious man does.

**Coal-bed** — [Historical] The bed of glowing coals beneath a
mature fire. The coal-bed, not the flame, provides heat. A
good fire-keeper maintains a deep coal-bed and keeps the flames
low. Flame is waste; coal is warmth.

**Cod-stack** — [Coined] Dried cod stacked on outdoor racks in
the wind. The primary preserved food of coastal settlements.
Hard as wood, keeps for months. Must be soaked before cooking.
The smell of a cod-stack carries on the wind -- the first sign
of a coastal village.

**Cold-camp** — [Slang] A camp without fire. Made when
concealment outweighs warmth -- enemy nearby, or fuel
unavailable. Cold camp in deep winter is survival risk. The
men eat cold food, sleep in piles for body heat, and do not
speak above a whisper.

**Cold-Eye** — [Coined] Voss's call-name. The clouded left eye
from an old axe wound, and the temperament behind it.

**Cold-iron** — [Historical] Iron without carbon content --
wrought iron, not steel. Softer, bends rather than breaks.
Used for nails, hinges, chains. In superstition, cold-iron
repels the hidden folk. Whether this is true in the Rimevegr
is deliberately ambiguous.

**Column** — [Coined] The marching order of the band. The
column has a head (lead scout), a body (main group with mule),
and a tail (rear guard). Column discipline means maintaining
spacing and silence. Column breaks happen at fords, rough
terrain, and ambush sites.

**Comb** — [Historical] Bone or antler, double-sided.
Surprisingly common personal item. Not vanity -- lice
management. A man who does not comb attracts vermin. Vermin
spread disease. Disease kills bands.

**Commons** — [Coined] The lowest rank in a mercenary band. New
or unproven fighters. They carry the most weight, do the worst
jobs, eat last, and die first. Advancement from common to
veteran requires surviving long enough to be worth trusting.

**Contract-stone** — [Coined] A flat stone used as a surface
for signing contracts. The rope-and-eye mark (Odynn's sign) is
scratched into the stone beside the terms. After the contract
is fulfilled, the stone is scored through -- marking the debt
paid. Some settlements have permanent contract-stones near the
gate.

**Copper-count** — [Slang] A small amount. "That is worth a
copper-count" means it is nearly worthless but not quite
nothing. Also: the exact tally of copper coins in the band's
treasury, which Gest knows to the piece.

**Cotter** — [Historical] A tenant farmer who works another
man's land for a share of the harvest. Not a thrall -- free to
leave, but leaving means starvation. The functional difference
between a cotter and a thrall is that the cotter can choose to
starve.

**Cow-hide** — [Historical] Untanned leather. Stiff, thick.
Used for shield-facing, crude bags, and temporary repairs. Must
be worked (softened, oiled, scraped) before fine use.

**Crack-Handed** — [Coined] Epithet for Thurr, god of thunder.
His hands are split knuckles and broken fingers healed wrong.

**Cracking, the** — [Coined] The event -- or process -- that
damaged the Veil between the living world and whatever lies
beyond. Whether it was a single catastrophe or a slow
degradation is disputed. What matters: the Veil is thinner now,
the dead walk, and no one knows if it will get worse. See
`08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §1.

**Cradle** — [Historical] 1. An infant bed. 2. The framework
that holds a boat during construction or repair. 3. In mining:
a simple rocking device for washing alluvial gold from river
gravel. Meaning depends on context.

**Craw** — [Historical] The crop of a bird. By extension: the
throat or gullet. "That sticks in my craw" means it is
unacceptable. A man with something stuck in his craw will not
let it go.

**Cresset** — [Historical] An iron basket on a pole, filled
with burning pitch or fat, used as a beacon or warning signal.
Settlement defenses light cressets along the palisade when
attack is expected.

**Croft** — [Historical] A small farm. Smaller than a steading.
One man's work. A croft produces enough for one family with
nothing left over. The smallest economic unit that can sustain
itself.

**Cross-grain** — [Historical] Wood cut perpendicular to the
grain. Weak, splits easily. A cross-grain axe-haft will snap
at the first hard swing. By extension: anything poorly planned.
"That plan is cross-grain" means it will fail under stress.

**Cruck** — [Historical] A curved timber pair forming the main
structural arch of a building. Crucks are set in the ground and
joined at the top. Simple, strong, achievable with primitive
tools.

**Cud** — [Historical] Rechewed food. Also: something chewed
over mentally. "He is chewing his cud" means the man is
thinking hard and not sharing his conclusions.

**Curfew** — [Historical] The practice of banking or covering
the fire at night. In settlements with wooden buildings, an
open fire at night is a risk to the entire village. The curfew
bell signals fire-banking.

**Curry** — [Historical] To groom an animal with a curry-comb:
a toothed metal scraper. Currying removes mud, loose hair, and
parasites. A curried horse performs better than a neglected one.

## D

**Dagger** — [Historical] A short stabbing weapon distinct from
the seax (which is a utility knife that fights). A dagger is a
weapon that happens to cut rope sometimes.

**Dagmál** — [Coined] Day-speech. The public or daylight face of
a rune: the meaning used in lawful inscription, healing, oath,
and ordinary warding.

**Djúpsýn** — [Coined] Deep-sight. The second and more
controlled level of völva seeing, where patterns no longer
flash -- they hold.

**Dýrríða** — [Coined] The Riding. The second soulbane, in
which something from beyond seems to sit inside a living body
and use it as borrowed meat.

**Dagfinn** — [Coined] A common man in the band who signed his
contract with an X. This is not unusual. Most commons are
illiterate.

**Dall** — [Historical] A shallow dish or bowl, usually carved
from wood or shaped from clay. The standard eating vessel. Men
eat from dalls around the fire. A man with his own dall has a
small claim to civilization.

**Danegeld** — [Historical] A tribute paid to avoid violence.
In the Rimevegr, the word is not used (no Danes) but the
practice is identical. Settlements pay svarthird bands to leave,
and both sides pretend it is a contract for services.

**Dark-count** — [Coined] The number of Long Darks a man has
survived on the march. "He has six dark-counts" means six
winters in the field. More than ten and the man is either very
capable or very lucky. Very few reach fifteen.

**Deadfall** — [Historical] 1. Fallen timber. The primary fuel
source on the march. Standing dead wood is preferred (drier
than ground-contact wood). 2. A trap: a heavy log propped on
a trigger stick, designed to crush whatever disturbs it.

**Dead-man's boots** — [Slang] Equipment inherited from a
killed comrade. "He is wearing dead-man's boots" is literal.
Boot inheritance is one of the band's recurring material
realities. Haldr's boots on Lump.

**Dead-name** — [Coined] The name of a man who has died. Not
spoken for a time after death -- then someone tests it at the
fire. Speaking a dead-name too soon is felt as wrong but not
articulated as a rule.

**Dead-reckoning** — [Historical] Navigation by estimated
distance and direction from a known point. The primary method
on the march when landmarks are hidden by rime-fog. Relies on
pace-count and compass sense. Accumulates error with every step.

**Death-seiðr** — [Coined] Seiðr that compels the dead rather
than asking them. Spirit-lore rank 4+ effects -- binding
spirits, commanding lesser dead. Classified as forbidden by all
jurisdictions in the Rimevegr. The mechanical line: rank 1-3 is
counsel, rank 4+ is coercion. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`
§11.8.4.

**Deepholm** — [Coined] Largest settlement in the region
(population 520). Jarl Sigrun's seat. Stone fortress, iron
gate, masterwork smithy, barrow archive, galdr workshop.

**Degradation** — [Coined] The cumulative physical decline of
magical practitioners from years of practice. Galdr-workers
lose fine motor control. Seiðr-speakers take longer to recover
from trance. Wyrd-readers lose signal clarity. Tracked
mechanically in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.9. Every
practitioner knows it is coming. None of them stop.

**Distaff** — [Historical] A wooden rod that holds raw fiber
for spinning. The distaff is women's technology, but in a
mercenary band anyone who can spin thread spins thread. Thread
is the medium of repair.

**Ditch** — [Historical] A defensive excavation. Even a shallow
ditch (knee-deep) slows a charge and breaks formation. The
simplest fortification. A camp with a ditch and a hurd-fence
sleeps better.

**Dog-watch** — [Slang] The worst watch shift -- late night,
before dawn. Cold, dark, lonely. The man on dog-watch fights
sleep. "He drew dog-watch" means bad luck or punishment.

**Door, the** — [Coined] Kell's earliest call-name, earned
for holding a barrow entrance single-handed.

**Draugr** — [Historical] The restless dead. In the Rimevegr,
not mindless -- they remember old grudges. Physical. Can be
fought, blocked, burned. Their nature is never confirmed as
supernatural. They might be the abandoned dead. They might be
Hael's subjects who refused to sit down.

**Draugr-stink** — [Slang] The smell of the restless dead. Not
rot exactly -- something drier, sweeter, wrong. Men who have
fought draugr can smell it on the wind. It follows you home in
your clothes.

**Draw-knife** — [Historical] A two-handled blade pulled toward
the user to shave wood. Used for making arrow shafts, peeling
bark, shaping hafts. A skilled man with a draw-knife and
suitable wood can produce serviceable tool handles in an
afternoon.

**Dread-mark** — [Coined] A carved or painted rune left as a
warning. Not magical in itself -- but superstitious travellers
avoid marked paths. Galdr-workers sometimes leave dread-marks
to claim territory or deter trespassers. The marks work because
people believe they work. Costs nothing and requires no skill.

**Drift-ice** — [Coined] Ice that has broken free from shore
and moves with current or wind. Navigating drift-ice on foot is
lethal -- the ice shifts underfoot with no warning. Crossing a
frozen fjord during thaw means reading drift-ice patterns.

**Drit** — [Historical] Excrement. The blunt Norse word. Used
as a general-purpose profanity: "Drit!" is the equivalent of a
short sharp obscenity. Also: "He is drit-lucky" means the man's
survival defies explanation.

**Drop-edge** — [Coined] The rim of a cliff, ravine, or steep
slope. The kill-zone in ambush terrain. A man pushed to the
drop-edge has no retreat.

**Dross** — [Historical] Waste material from metal-working.
Slag that separates during smelting. In slang: anything
worthless. "That offer is dross" means the deal is not worth
the breath it took to speak it.

**Dry-camp** — [Slang] A camp without water nearby. An
acceptable one-night risk in cool weather. In Long Summer heat,
a dry-camp becomes dangerous by midday. Water-skins must be
full before making dry-camp.

**Dub** — [Historical] A pool of standing water. Dark, still,
often peat-stained. The Outvegr is full of dubs. They look
shallow; some are not.

**Dust-iron** — [Coined] Iron filings. By-product of grinding
and filing. Saved by smiths for use in charcoal-ledger ink
(mixed with vinegar to make a dark permanent stain).

**Dwale** — [Historical] A sedative drink made from henbane,
lettuce, bryony, and opium poppy (when available). Used by
healers to dull pain during wound treatment. Too much kills.
The dosage is guesswork.

**Dyke** — [Historical] A drainage ditch or earthen wall.
Settlement technology for water management. A well-dyked field
survives spring melt without flooding.

## E

**Eagle** — [Historical] A large raptor. In Norse culture,
eagles circle battlefields alongside ravens. An eagle over a
camp means open ground and high thermals. An eagle over a
battle means carrion.

**Ear-mark** — [Historical] A notch cut into a livestock
animal's ear to mark ownership. Extended to mean any
identifying sign. "He has the ear-mark of a deserter" means
the signs are visible.

**Ear-rime** — [Coined] Ice that forms inside the ear canal
during extreme cold. Painful and disorienting. A cloth cap worn
under a helm prevents it.

**Eld** — [Historical] Fire. The Norse root. "Eld-light" is
firelight. "Eld-keeper" is whoever tends the flames. The word
carries more weight than the English "fire" -- eld is survival,
community, light against the dark, the center of everything that
is not wilderness.

**Eld-keeper** — [Coined] The designated fire-tender, especially
on watch. Letting the eld die in deep cold is a punishable
offense. The eld-keeper feeds the fire, banks it for the night,
and resurrects it at dawn.

**Elkhide** — [Historical] Tanned elk skin. Thicker than deer-
hide. Used for heavy-duty leather goods: belts, scabbards,
pack-straps. An elkhide shield-cover absorbs sword blows better
than cow leather.

**Elm-bow** — [Historical] A bow made of elm wood. Less powerful
than yew, but elm is available in the Rimevegr where yew is not.
Effective for hunting at moderate range. Not a battlefield weapon
-- in the close terrain of the Outvegr, a thrown axe reaches
farther than an arrow flies.

**Ember-pot** — [Coined] A clay or iron vessel for transporting
live coals during a march. Packed with ash and carried carefully.
A good ember-pot means not striking fire from cold at every camp.

**Entrails** — [Historical] The viscera of a slaughtered animal
or a dead man. Wyrd-readers study entrails for omens. Practical
men study them for parasites. Both find what they are looking
for.

**Escort-fee** — [Coined] Payment for accompanying a merchant
or traveler through dangerous territory. The band's most common
contract type. Escort-fees are calculated per day, per man, per
risk level. "Double escort" means the route is actively hostile.

**Ewe** — [Historical] A female sheep. Ewes produce wool, milk,
and lambs. A settlement's ewe-count is an economic indicator --
more ewes, more wool, more trade.

**Ergi** — [Historical] A term of deep insult implying
unmanliness or sexual passivity. In historical Norse culture,
the worst accusation. In the Rimevegr, the taboo survives in
weakened form, applied primarily to male seidr-practitioners.
Thorne avoids the word by calling his rune-work "reading" -- a
deliberate linguistic dodge.

**Eye-debt** — [Coined] What is owed when you have seen
something you should not have -- a theft, a secret meeting, a
moment of weakness in a Named Man. Eye-debts are never spoken.
They are banked. They surface when leverage is needed.

**Eye-guard** — [Coined] A strip of leather or bark with thin
slits cut for vision. An improvised defense against frost-
blindness. Ugly, effective, essential on bright snow.

**Eyelet** — [Historical] A small hole reinforced with stitching
or a metal ring, used for threading laces through leather or
cloth. Boot eyelets, tent-flap eyelets, armor lacing eyelets.
The work is tedious. The result is critical.

**Eyre** — [Historical] A circuit court. A traveling judge who
moves between settlements dispensing justice. In the Rimevegr,
no formal eyre exists -- the closest equivalent is a
well-respected jarl who arbitrates disputes between settlements.

## F

**Fallow** — [Historical] Agricultural land left unplanted for
a season to restore fertility. In the Rimevegr's short Long
Summer, fallowing means losing half a settlement's food
production. The decision to fallow is agonizing.

**Farrier** — [Historical] A specialist in horse and mule hoof
care. The band does not have one. Someone learned the minimum.
A farrier's rasp and hoof-knife are part of the mule's gear.

**Fat-lamp** — See _tallow-lamp_.

**Feather-bed** — [Historical] A luxury mattress stuffed with
goose or duck down. Exists in jarls' halls. The band has never
slept in one. The word is used sarcastically: "This rock is a
proper feather-bed" means the ground is terrible.

**Fehu** — [Historical] The rune ᚠ, meaning cattle/wealth.
Carved on strongboxes and purse-clasps as a ward against theft.
Whether the ward works is another of those questions the novel
does not answer.

**Fen** — [Historical] Low-lying marshy ground. Softer than a
bog, wetter than a moor. Walking through fen country exhausts
the legs and damages boots. The mule sinks to the fetlock.
Progress halves.

**Fen-fire** — [Historical] A ghostly light seen over marshes at
night. Methane ignition from decaying matter. Or the dead. In
the Rimevegr, the scientific explanation and the supernatural
one coexist, and neither is confirmed.

**Fenrir's teeth** — [Slang] A line of jagged rocks, ice
formations, or any natural barrier that looks like it was
designed to kill. "We have Fenrir's teeth ahead" means bad
terrain.

**Fire-circle** — [Coined] The nightly campfire arrangement
around which the band eats, talks, gambles, and establishes
hierarchy. Seating is social positioning. Changes in who sits
where communicate power shifts without words.

**Fire-pouch** — [Historical] A waterproof leather bag
containing fire-starting materials: fire-steel, flint, char-
cloth, amadou, birch bark. Sealed with beeswax or fat.
Losing the fire-pouch in bad weather is a survival emergency.

**Fire-ring** — [Coined] A circle of stones containing the
campfire. Defines the cooking area, the social center, the
warmth boundary. Beyond the fire-ring is darkness.

**Fire-steel** — [Historical] A C-shaped striker of high-carbon
steel. Struck against flint, it sheds tiny curls of hot metal
that ignite tinder. The steel sparks, not the flint -- this is
the physical fact that separates men who can light fires from
men who cannot.

**Fjord** — [Historical] A deep, narrow coastal inlet carved
by glacial action. The primary geography of the Inner Fjords
region. Where most people live. Fog clings to fjord water even
at midday.

**Fjord-born** — [Coined] Raised in the Inner Fjords. Implies
familiarity with boats, fish, salt wind, and the particular
claustrophobia of living between vertical rock walls and cold
water.

**Fjord-side** — [Coined] The strip of usable land between the
water and the cliff. Where fjord-born people build, plant, and
die. Never wide enough.

**Fjörbaugsgarðr** — [Legal] Lesser outlawry. Three-year exile from one jurisdiction with loss of property. The outlaw may be killed within the exile territory but is protected elsewhere. Weregild payment can end the exile early. See `data/culture/outlawry_system.yaml`.

**Flake** — [Historical] A wooden drying rack for fish. Cod
split and salted is draped over flakes in the wind. The smell
announces a fishing settlement before the rooftops appear.

**Flat-bread** — [Historical] Unleavened bread cooked on a hot
stone. Dense, gritty, filling. Goes stale in two days and hard
as wood in four. Hard flat-bread is soaked in stew or ale.
Teeth have been broken on four-day flat-bread.

**Fley** — [Historical] To frighten, to scare away. "The
barrow fleyed them" means the men were scared off. Not a mark
of cowardice in the Rimevegr -- some things should fley you.

**Flint-knapping** — [Historical] The art of shaping flint by
striking off flakes. Ancient technology that survives because
flint is freely available and iron is not. Arrowheads, scrapers,
and fire-making flints are all knapped.

**Flint** — [Historical] The striking surface for fire-making.
The sharp edge against which the fire-steel is scraped. Flint
does not spark -- the steel does. This distinction matters.

**Fold** — [Historical] An enclosure for livestock. A sheep-
fold, a goat-fold. By extension: any enclosed space. "Back to
the fold" means return to safety, but in mercenary usage it
carries irony -- the fold is wherever the band is camped, which
is rarely safe.

**Folk magic** — [Historical/Coined] The household layer of
protective practice -- herb-bundles above doorways, salt lines
on thresholds, carved threshold runes. Does not produce
mechanical magical effects but affects morale: men who see
familiar rituals feel safer. See `08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §3
and `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §6 (folk magic morale modifiers).

**Forager** — [Coined] A band member assigned to find food.
Foraging is survival, not supplement. Terrain determines what
is available. A forager returning empty-handed means the band
goes hungry.

**Fordable** — [Historical] A river crossing shallow enough to
walk. Whether a river is fordable changes with season, weather,
and recent rain. A ford that was knee-deep last week may be
neck-deep after three days of thaw.

**Forecastle** — [Historical] The forward platform of a ship.
By extension: the leading position of any group. "He stands at
the forecastle" means he takes point.

**Forge** — [Historical] A controlled fire hot enough to work
metal. The forge is the heart of a smithy. A portable forge is
an anvil and a bellows on the mule's back -- crude but
functional for emergency repairs.

**Fork-beard** — [Slang] A man who splits his beard into two
points. A common descriptive call-name for men who lack a more
interesting one.

**Foster** — [Historical] To raise another's child. Fostering
is a political act -- it creates bonds between families. A
foster-son owes loyalty to both birth and foster parents. The
resulting network of obligation is the closest thing the
Rimevegr has to diplomacy.

**Fraeja** — [Coined] The Grief-Bride. Goddess of the grieving,
barren fields, and women who outlive everyone. She taught women
seidr -- her cruelest gift. Her tears became the Veil-fog.
Invoked mostly by women, mostly in silence.

**Freedman** — [Historical] A former thrall who has earned or
purchased freedom. A freedman can become a full member of the
band, but the thrall-mark (see _thrall-mark_) on the story of
his past follows him.

**Frost-blind** — [Coined] Temporary blindness caused by light
reflecting off ice and snow. The Rimevegr's equivalent of snow
blindness. Treated by sitting in dark shelter for a day. On the
march, a frost-blind man is led by the arm and contributes
nothing.

**Frost-heave** — [Historical] Ground that has risen from ice
expansion beneath the surface. Creates uneven terrain that
breaks ankles and wheels. Frozen roads in the Rimevegr are not
flat -- they are frost-heaved ridges and hollows.

**Frostfjord Hollow** — [Coined] A village of 87 people. Jarl
Hrothgar's seat. Dried-cod economy. The band's first named
settlement interaction.

**Frost-nail** — See _ice-nail_.

**Frost-sore** — [Coined] A weeping skin wound caused by
repeated freezing and thawing. Common on hands, ears, and feet.
Painful, slow to heal, and prone to infection.

**Fuel-budget** — [Coined] How much wood or peat the band needs
per night versus what is available. Gest tracks fuel-budget
as ruthlessly as food. A forest camp has unlimited fuel. A
moorland camp may have none.

**Fulling** — [Historical] The process of cleaning and
thickening woven wool by pounding it in water mixed with urine
or clay. Fulled wool is denser, more water-resistant, and warmer.
The smell during fulling is terrible. The result is worth it.

**Fury-drunk** — [Slang] Berserker state. Also applied loosely
to any man who has lost control in a fight. "He went fury-
drunk" means the man stopped thinking and started destroying.
Usually the man does not remember clearly afterward.

**Furrow** — [Historical] A long, narrow trench cut by a plough.
The basic unit of agricultural work. In the Rimevegr's thin
topsoil and short growing season, furrows are shallow and
closely spaced.

**Futhark** — [Historical] The runic alphabet. Named for its
first six characters: F, U, Th, A, R, K. The writing system
of Norse culture. In the Rimevegr, futhark literacy is rare.
Most men cannot read. Rune-carvers guard the knowledge.

## G

**Gait** — [Coined] The pace the band sets on the march. Three
gaits: road-pace (standard), hard-march (forced), and crawl
(bad terrain or injury). The gait determines daily distance and
daily exhaustion.

**Galdr** — [Historical] Rune-scribing magic. The art of carving
runes and speaking words of power. Practical effects: wards,
blade-strengthening, trail-marking. Always costs blood and
willpower. Miscarved runes twist their intent. The most accepted
magical tradition -- even jarls keep a galdr-worker. But even
they are watched.

**Galdr-worker** — [Historical] A practitioner of galdr. Gender-
neutral in the Rimevegr (Ash is female). Negotiates services
like a mercenary: terms, conditions, price. Keeps separate fire.
Sets own terms.

**Galdramenn** — [Historical] Plural of galdr-worker. Those who
practice runic inscription. Feared and needed in equal measure.

**Gjafaþrál** — [Coined] Gift-thralldom. The binding created by
repeated gifts, shelter, or favor until refusal becomes
socially impossible. A soft chain no less real for being soft.

**Gambeson** — [Historical] A padded cloth jacket worn as armor
or under mail. Linen or wool stuffed with scraps and quilted.
Surprisingly effective -- a thick gambeson stops a glancing cut.
The most common body armor among commons. Cheap, bulky, warm.

**Garth** — [Historical] An enclosed yard or farmstead. "Behind
the garth-fence" means within the domestic compound.

**Gate-ask** — [Coined] The conversation that happens before a
mercenary band enters a settlement. Who are you. What do you
want. How long. At whose cost. The gate-ask determines whether
weapons are set down or drawn.

**Gate-Blind** — [Coined] Epithet for Heimr, god of thresholds.
He sees everything and recognizes nothing.

**Gilt** — [Historical] Gold-coated. By extension: anything
that looks better than it is. "Gilt promises" means the
contract sounds generous but the terms will not hold.

**Gimbal** — [Historical] A pivot-mounted holder for a lamp
or candle on a ship. The light stays level while the ship
moves. Uncommon inland but known in coastal settlements.

**Girdle** — [Historical] A belt. Not ornamental -- functional.
The girdle holds the seax, the purse, the fire-pouch, and any
small tools. A broad girdle distributes the weight of a brynja
at the hips instead of the shoulders.

**Glaive** — [Historical] A pole weapon with a single-edged
blade at the head. Uncommon in the Rimevegr -- the terrain is
too close for long weapons. Sometimes seen at settlement gates
because they look intimidating.

**Gnaw-bone** — [Slang] A bone given to chew for the last
traces of marrow and gristle. "He is on the gnaw-bone" means
supplies are critical.

**Goat-path** — [Coined] A trail too narrow and steep for
anything but goats and desperate men. The Outvegr has goat-
paths that connect valleys no road reaches.

**Going dark** — [Slang] Breaking camp and leaving before dawn
without fire or noise. Escape protocol. "We go dark at second
watch" means the band is in trouble and needs to disappear.

**Goose-grease** — [Historical] Rendered goose fat. Used for
waterproofing leather, treating chapped skin, lubricating
metal, and cooking. More available in settlements than on the
march. A small pot of goose-grease is a luxury item.

**Grátlög** — [Coined] Weeping-songs. The mourning keening
performed by women around the dead. A sustained rising wail
that layers as more women join, producing a chord that shifts
and never resolves. The children's grátlög uses the cradle-song
melody sung backward. Men have the _beinasöngr_ (bone-song) at
the barrow.

**Greyling** — [Slang] A man whose hair is going grey. Not a
term of respect or mockery -- just identification. "The
greyling with the limp" is how commons reference unnamed
veterans. In a band, grey hair means survival, which means
competence.

**Grief-gift** — [Coined] An object left with the dead. Personal
items placed on the bale-fire or in the burial. The grief-gift
is what the living can spare, which is usually nothing of value.

**Grief-Bride** — [Coined] Epithet for Fraeja. She outlived
every love. Her dowry was grief.

**Grievance** — [Coined] A formal resentment tracked in the
charcoal ledger. Late pay, dead Named Men, broken promises --
all recorded as grievances. When grievances accumulate past a
threshold, the band fractures.

**Grim-gods** — See _Aesrgrim_.

**Grimholt** — [Coined] A large village on the High Rime-Moors.
Population 118. Warchief Ordovast's stronghold. Iron mining.
Strong defenses.

**Grip-leather** — [Historical] Leather wrapped around a sword
hilt or axe haft for purchase. Applied wet (rawhide shrinks as
it dries, tightening the grip). Roughened with a knife-point or
rubbed with pine resin when it wears smooth.

**Grist** — [Historical] Grain destined for the quern. By
extension: raw material for any process. "That is grist for
the ledger" means the information goes into Gest's records.

**Ground-cloth** — [Coined] A piece of oiled leather or hide
laid beneath the sleeping roll to keep moisture from wicking
upward. Without a ground-cloth, a man wakes wet. Wet in the
Long Dark means cold. Cold means danger.

**Ground-frost** — [Historical] Ice that forms in the top layer
of soil. Ground-frost makes digging impossible. Latrine
trenches must be dug before the ground freezes. Missing that
window has consequences no one wants to describe.

**Gruel** — [Historical] Thin porridge. The least amount of
grain that can be suspended in the most water. What the band
eats when supplies are critically low. "We are on gruel" means
three days from starvation.

**Gut-rot** — [Slang] Dysentery or severe food poisoning.
The single most dangerous non-combat condition a band faces.
Weakens men, slows the march, spreads through the camp.
"Two men have gut-rot" means four will have it tomorrow.

**Guðníðing** — [Coined] God-scorned. One who has deliberately
placed himself outside sacred order through defilement,
blasphemous practice, or appetites that even outlaws fear.

**Guðslag** — [Coined] God-stroke. A catastrophic rupture in the
body -- seizure, burst vessel, or collapse -- brought on by
overreaching in galdr or forcing too much power through voice.

**Gutter-fire** — [Slang] A fire that has been badly made and
burns unevenly. Spits, smokes, gives no heat. Usually made by
a man who does not know to split his wet wood for dry interior.

**Gyrfalcon** — [Historical] A large arctic hunting bird. The
most valued bird for falconry. In the Rimevegr, wild gyrfalcons
are admired at distance and eaten when caught.

**Gyre** — [Historical] A circular or spiral motion. Water
gyres in a whirlpool. Wind gyres in mountain passes. A gyre of
ravens above a battlefield is the first sign visible at
distance.

**Gust-wall** — [Coined] A sudden blast of wind in mountain
passes that hits like a physical barrier. Gust-walls knock men
off their feet and scatter loose gear. The band learns to rope
together in exposed terrain after losing a supply pack to a
gust-wall.

## H

**Hack-silver** — [Historical] Silver cut into weighed pieces
for transactions. A arm-ring hacked into thirds is three
payments. The pieces are weighed on a balance scale, not
counted. Size is irrelevant; weight is everything.

**Hael** — [Coined] The Half-Rotted. Goddess of the dead, the
barrows, the space between dying and being forgotten. Not evil
-- indifferent. Draugr are Hael's subjects who refused to sit
down.

**Hael's chair** — [Slang] Death. "He took Hael's chair" means
he died.

**Hael's half** — [Slang] The share of supplies reserved for
wastage, spoilage, and loss. Gest builds Hael's half into every
calculation. If ten days of food remain, Gest counts eight.

**Haugvættr** — [Coined/Historical] Barrow-wight. A mound-bound
presence made from dead flesh, grave-goods, old oath, and place-
memory compacted together until the burial itself seems to wake.

**Holdboð** — [Coined] Flesh-command. A galdr effect where the
body obeys before the mind has agreed: knees buckle, hands open,
voice stops, or the gut turns.

**Haft** — [Historical] The handle of any striking tool -- axe,
hammer, mattock. Always wood. The haft transmits the shock of
impact to the hands. A bad haft breaks at the worst moment.

**Hair-rope** — [Coined] Rope twisted from horsehair or cattle-
tail hair. Rough, strong, resistant to moisture. Used for tying
loads and repairing tack. Not comfortable against skin.

**Half-Rotted** — [Coined] Epithet for Hael. Her left side is
living flesh; her right side is green-grey bone.

**Hall** — [Historical] A longhouse large enough for communal
gathering, feasting, and governance. A jarl's hall is his seat
of power. Being quartered in the hall means honored guest.
Being quartered outside the hall means tolerated.

**Hall-burned** — [Coined] Descriptor for someone who lost
their home to fire or attack. A hall-burned man has nothing.
Some become mercenaries. Some become thralls. Some become both.

**Halter** — [Historical] A rope headpiece for leading a horse
or mule. The mule wears a halter. The man who holds the halter
walks at mule-pace, which is the band's actual speed regardless
of what the captain orders.

**Hammer-scale** — [Historical] Tiny flakes of iron oxide that
fly from heated metal when struck. The black grit that covers
a smithy floor. Crunches underfoot. The sound and smell of a
working smithy.

**Hand-axe** — [Historical] A light single-handed axe. The
primary weapon and tool of commons. Cuts wood, splits skulls,
hammers tent pegs. The most versatile object a poor man owns.

**Hand-quern** — [Historical] A simple grain mill. Upper stone
rotated on lower stone, grain fed through a center hole. The
flour is coarse. The work is tedious. Grinding enough grain
for twelve mouths takes an hour. This is why grain is usually
traded as flour, not whole.

**Hard-bread** — [Historical] See _flat-bread_. Specifically
the stale version -- four days old, dense as wood, carried
as trail food. Requires soaking to be edible.

**Hard-march** — [Slang] An accelerated march without rest
stops. Covers distance at the cost of exhaustion. After three
days of hard-march, men check for desertion each morning.

**Hare-snare** — [Coined] A simple wire or cord loop set on a
game trail. The primary protein trap in the Outvegr. Setting
snares is evening work; checking them is dawn work. When the
snares are empty three days running, the terrain is hunted out.

**Harness** — [Historical] The complete set of straps, buckles,
and fittings for a pack animal. The mule's harness is checked
every morning. Chafing causes sores; sores cause infection;
infection loses the mule. Lose the mule, carry everything.

**Hauberk** — [Historical] A knee-length mail shirt. Rarer and
heavier than the short haubergeon. In the Rimevegr, owning a
full hauberk is wealth and status. Most men fight in leather
and hope.

**Haubergeon** — [Historical] A short-sleeve, thigh-length mail
shirt. The standard brynja. 20-30 pounds. Sound: constant
jingling.

**Headman** — [Historical] The leader of a village. Not elected,
not formally hereditary. The headman is whoever the village
trusts to deal with armed strangers. Usually the oldest
competent landowner.

**Hearth-stone** — [Historical] The flat stone at the center of
a longhouse fire pit. The symbolic center of domestic life. A
man who loses his hearth-stone has lost his home. Metaphorical
use: "He has no hearth-stone" means a man belongs nowhere.

**Hearth-right** — [Coined] The claim to shelter and fire that
a settled person has and a landless wanderer does not. A bondi
has hearth-right. A svarthird does not. This is the fundamental
social division.

**Hemp** — [Historical] The plant Cannabis sativa, grown for
fiber rope and coarse cloth. Not cultivated for intoxicants in
the Rimevegr -- labor is too scarce to grow plants that do
nothing structural. Hemp rope is the standard cordage.

**Hen-house** — [Historical] A small wooden structure for
chickens. One of the few sources of fresh protein (eggs)
available year-round. Raiding a settlement's hen-house is war.

**Hel-road** — [Historical] The path to the realm of the dead.
"Walking the Hel-road" means dying. In the Rimevegr, the Hel-
road is metaphorical for most people -- but the barrows are
literal, and what is inside them suggests the road is shorter
than anyone likes.

**Helm** — [Historical] An iron helmet. In the Rimevegr,
simple iron caps with nose-guards -- no horns, no wings, no
decoration. Practical headgear. A sharp axe blow that hits a
bare head splits the skull. The same blow on a helm dents the
metal but the man lives.

**Hervísur** — [Coined] Battle-songs. The vocal progression that
accompanies a shield-wall advance: chest-drone, rhythmic
syllables, name-chant, roar. Synchronizes breathing, drowns
fear, and terrifies the enemy. A band that has lost its
hervísur has lost its cohesion.

**Herd-law** — [Coined] The unwritten rules of livestock
management that every rural settlement follows. How many
animals the pasture can support. Whose goat eats what. When
culling happens. A herd without herd-law starves itself.

**Hide** — [Historical] 1. Animal skin, raw. 2. A unit of land
measurement (approximately what one family can work). Both
meanings are in common use. "He owns three hides" means land.
"He is scraping a hide" means leather-work.

**Highseat** — [Historical] The jarl's chair in the hall.
Flanked by carved posts. The physical manifestation of
authority. When a jarl sits in the highseat, he is ruling.
When the highseat is empty, the hall is just a room.

**Hilt** — [Historical] The handle assembly of a sword.
Crossguard, grip, pommel. The hilt balances the blade. A good
hilt is leather-wrapped iron. A poor hilt is wrapped in cord
that will slip with blood.

**Hird** — [Historical] A household guard or retinue. The
personal fighting force of a jarl or warchief. A hird eats
well, drills regularly, and dies for its employer. The
mercenary svarthird is a landless hird -- loyalty for silver
instead of hearth-right.

**Hnefatafl** — [Historical] A Norse board game. One side
defends a king piece (the hnefi) against an attacking force.
Strategic, asymmetric. Played by Named Men in settlements.
Too bulky and too delicate for the march.

**Hollow** — [Historical/Coined] A depression in terrain. Also:
the suffix of place-names (Frostfjord Hollow). A hollow is
sheltered from wind but collects cold air. Camp in a hollow
for wind-break; pay with ground-chill.

**Hook** — [Coined] Kell's earlier call-name. Origin unstated
in the text. A hook is a tool, not a weapon -- which says
something about what Kell was before the band.

**Hornblast** — [Historical] A signal blown on an animal horn.
One blast: attention. Two: alarm. Three: retreat. The sound
carries half a mile in clear air. In rime-fog, much less. The
horn is the band's only long-range communication tool.

**Horn-spoon** — [Historical] A spoon carved from cattle horn.
Personal eating utensil. Most men carry one. Losing a horn-
spoon means eating with fingers or whittling a replacement.

**Horse-price** — [Coined] An extravagant cost. "That is a
horse-price" means the amount demanded is 20-30 silver -- the
cost of a decent horse, which is to say, a luxury the band
cannot afford.

**Hrime** — [Historical] Rime. Frost crystals. The white crust
that covers every surface during the Long Dark. The Rimevegr is
named for it. Hrime thickens on surfaces overnight and must be
scraped from weapons, gear, and faces each morning.

**Huh** — [Slang] A universal grunt of acknowledgment. Not
agreement. Not refusal. Just: message received. "Huh" is the
most common sound in camp.

**Hull** — [Historical] The body of a ship. By extension: any
large container. A settlement's grain hull is its storage
building.

**Hurd-fence** — [Historical] A woven-stick fence. Quick to
build, light, temporary. Used for livestock enclosures, camp
perimeters, and wind-breaks. Two men can build twenty feet of
hurd-fence in an hour.

**Hush, the** — [Coined] A supernatural phenomenon. In the deep
woods, all sound dies completely for minutes at a time. Every
bird stops singing at once. Something is listening. The Hush is
more common during the Long Dark. Old woodsmen argue the name
covers more than one event: a place-fixed hush, a wandering hush,
and the hush that lingers after killing grounds go quiet. No one
has an explanation that satisfies.

**Husk** — [Historical] The outer shell of a grain kernel.
Removed during milling. Husk is fed to animals or, in
desperation, mixed into flour to extend supply. Husk-bread
is gritty and tastes of nothing.

**Huscarl** — [Historical] A professional warrior in a jarl's
household guard. The closest historical equivalent to a
permanent soldier. Huscarls drill, eat well, are paid
regularly, and owe loyalty to a specific lord. A band composed
of former huscarls is called "Old Huscarls" and holds itself
to professional standards.

**Hut-circle** — [Historical] The stone foundation of a
demolished or abandoned roundhouse. Common in the Outvegr.
Visible as a ring of moss-covered stones. Useful for shelter --
the wind-break still works even without walls.

**Hymn** — [Historical] A sacred song. In the Rimevegr, hymns
to the gods are rare -- the gods went silent. Old hymns survive
as work-songs with the religious words changed to profanity.

## I

**Ice-axe** — [Coined] A short-hafted axe used for cutting
steps in ice slopes and breaking trail through packed snow.
Doubles as a weapon but was not designed for it.

**Ice-bound** — [Coined] Frozen in place. A settlement that is
ice-bound cannot be reached by road until thaw. A river that
is ice-bound is crossable on foot. The condition is both trap
and opportunity.

**Ice-bridge** — [Coined] A frozen river or fjord crossing
usable in deep Long Dark. Tested by the lightest man, alone.
If he crosses, the column follows. Ice-bridges fail
unpredictably during thaw season. Losing a man through thin ice
is the fifth most common non-combat death.

**Ice-nail** — [Coined] Short iron spikes driven into boot soles
for grip on ice. Improvised from any available iron -- nails,
pins, broken fittings. Walking indoors with ice-nails destroys
flooring, which is why boots come off at settlement thresholds.

**Ice-quake** — [Coined] A sudden crack and boom as a frozen
lake or river adjusts under temperature change. Can be heard
for miles. Startling. Sometimes the prelude to an ice-break
that swallows anyone standing on the surface.

**Ice-Reach** — [Coined] The Northern Ice-Reach. The edge of
inhabitable land. The Veil is thickest. Almost no one goes
there. Some say the gods retreated there.

**Icicle-beard** — [Slang] See _chin-rime_. The more extreme
version: actual icicles hanging from a man's beard after a day
of hard marching in deep cold.

**Ingle** — [Historical] A fire or fireplace. More domestic
than eld. "Sit by the ingle" means take warmth and be welcome.
Settlement word. The band does not have an ingle -- they have a
fire-circle on bare ground.

**Ingot** — [Historical] A bar of metal, usually iron or
silver. Trade goods rather than currency. An iron ingot is raw
material for a smith. A silver ingot is wealth in an
untransacted form.

**Ink** — [Historical] A liquid for writing. Made from iron
gall (crushed oak-galls mixed with iron vitriol and gum arabic).
The Rimevegr has limited ink production -- most writing is
carved (runes) or scratched (charcoal). Ink is a settlement
luxury.

**Insole** — [Historical] A shaped piece of leather or wool
placed inside a boot for insulation and comfort. Insoles wear
out faster than the boot itself. Replacement insoles are a
field necessity.

**Iron-tongue** — [Slang] A smith. Someone who works iron.
"Find me the iron-tongue" means find the settlement smith.
Iron-tongues are the most protected craftsmen in any settlement.

**Ironmoon** — [Coined] A named month in the Rimevegr calendar.
Hard cold, deep Long Dark. The name describes what the month
does to people, not what people feel about it.

**Iron-rot** — [Coined] Rust so severe the metal is structurally
compromised. An iron-rot weapon looks functional but will snap
under impact. Gest checks the band's equipment for iron-rot
after every wet stretch.

**Iron-shod** — [Coined] Fitted with iron reinforcement. An
iron-shod boot has a metal plate on the toe. An iron-shod door
resists axes. The term indicates serious investment.

## J

**Jagged-ice** — [Coined] Broken, refrozen ice along river
banks and lake shores. Unstable, sharp, impossible to walk on
without ice-nails. The zone between solid ice and solid ground
where falls happen.

**Jarl** — [Historical] The highest secular authority in the
Rimevegr. Rules a town and the territory the town can project
force over. A jarl's domain extends exactly as far as his
warriors can march in a day and return. No feudal chain --
jarls deal with each other as independent powers.

**Jarl-seat** — [Coined] The settlement where a jarl holds
court. Also: the physical chair (see _highseat_).

**Jaw-bone** — [Slang] A man who talks too much. "Shut your
jaw-bone" is camp language for be quiet.

**Jerkin** — [Historical] A sleeveless leather or wool jacket.
Worn over a tunic for extra warmth or minimal protection. A
fur-lined jerkin is good camp clothing. A hardened leather
jerkin is poor-man's armor.

**Jotun** — [Historical] A giant of old saga. In the Rimevegr,
jotun have not been seen since the Cracking. Referenced in
place-names (Jotun-pass, Jotun's Step) and idiom. Whether they
ever existed is a question no one asks aloud.

**Jotun-cold** — [Slang] Cold so extreme it feels intentional.
Named for the giants of old saga. "Jotun-cold tonight" means
no one sleeps well and frostbite is a real risk.

**Juniper** — [Historical] An evergreen shrub whose berries are
used for flavoring mead and preserving meat. Juniper smoke
repels insects. Burning juniper branches inside a longhouse
clears the air -- or at least masks the smell. A bundle of
juniper tied to a pack keeps moths from eating wool.

**Jury-rig** — [Historical] A temporary repair made with
whatever is available. A jury-rigged axe-haft is a branch
lashed to a head with wet-hide strips. It will work for one
fight. The term has no connection to courts -- it is a nautical
word meaning "improvised mast."

## K

**Keel** — [Historical] The backbone timber of a ship. The
strongest single piece of wood in a vessel. By extension: the
most important structural element of anything. "Cut the keel"
means destroy the foundation. "Sound in the keel" means
structurally solid.

**Karl** — [Historical] A free man. The base social unit. A karl
has rights: he can own land, speak at the thing, bear weapons.
Below the karl is the thrall. Above him is the jarl. Most of
the band's commons are karls with nothing left to lose.

**Keelson** — [Historical] The internal spine of a ship's hull.
By extension: any structural backbone. "The keelson of the
band" is Gest -- take him out and the structure collapses.

**Keep** — [Historical] A fortified structure. In the Rimevegr,
stone keeps are rare -- most defenses are wooden palisades. A
settlement with a stone keep is wealthy and afraid.

**Kenning** — [Historical] A compressed metaphor in skaldic
poetry. "Whale-road" for sea. "Wound-dew" for blood. In the
Rimevegr, kennings survive in everyday speech as fossils --
people use them without thinking of them as literary devices.
"The wound-dew on his hands" is not poetry. It is how a man
says "blood" without saying "blood."

**Kern** — [Historical] A light-armed foot soldier. Irish term
but applicable. In the Rimevegr, the word is not used, but
the concept maps to the band's commons -- poorly armored men
with hand-axes and determination.

**Keel-haul** — [Historical] A naval punishment. Dragged under
the ship's hull. Referenced in coastal settlements. Applied
metaphorically: "He got keel-hauled" means punished severely
for incompetence.

**Kettle-hat** — [Historical] A wide-brimmed iron helmet shaped
like an upside-down cooking pot. Protects the head and
shoulders from overhead blows. Practical, ugly, effective.

**Kyrrseta** — [Coined] Sitting-still. The core völva discipline
of holding the body quiet long enough for deeper sight to rise
without panic or self-loss.

**Kvikindi-barn** — [Coined/Insult] Beast-child. A fear-term for
a child believed marked by animality, corruption, or a broken
line of human inheritance.

**Kiln** — [Historical] A furnace for firing pottery, burning
lime, or producing charcoal. Settlement infrastructure. Kiln-
smoke is a navigation landmark -- you can smell a settlement's
kiln before you see its palisade.

**Kin-price** — [Coined] The blood-money paid specifically to
a victim's family (kin) rather than to a court. Distinct from
weregild in that kin-price is personal and negotiated face-to-
face with grieving relatives.

**Kirtle** — [Historical] A long garment, essentially a tunic
reaching to the knees or ankles. Worn by both men and women.
A woman's kirtle is her primary garment. A man's working kirtle
is shorter, belted, and worn under an outer coat.

**Kill-money** — [Coined] The informal bonus for a confirmed
enemy kill during a contract engagement. Not formalized -- the
captain decides. Kill-money is how commons earn extra. It
encourages aggression, which is a double-edged policy.

**Kindling** — [Historical] Small, dry material used to build
a fire from tinder to fuel. Thin sticks, bark strips, dry
grass. The stage between spark and flame. A man who cannot
produce kindling in wet conditions will die.

**King-post** — [Historical] The central vertical timber in a
roof truss. The building's spine. "Taking away the king-post"
means removing the one thing holding the structure together.

**Kin-bond** — [Historical] Blood relation or shield-oath
equivalent. The strongest social bond in the Rimevegr. A
kin-bonded man will fight for his bonded kin when he would not
fight for silver.

**Kin-clan** — [Coined] A mercenary band composed of blood
relatives or shield-oath siblings. See "Blood-Shield" archetype.
Morale checks easier when defending kin.

**Kit** — [Slang] A man's total personal equipment: weapon,
armor, clothing, tools, fire-pouch, water-skin, bedroll. "His
kit is poor" means the man is inadequately equipped. "Good kit"
means he can march and fight without needing resupply. Kit
quality determines survival.

**Knee-timber** — [Historical] Naturally curved wood used for
structural joints in ship-building and construction. Cut from
branch junctions. Strong because the grain follows the curve.

**Knife-rest** — [Slang] The moment at a negotiation when both
sides stop reaching for weapons. "We are past the knife-rest"
means the danger has passed and talking can begin. Not always
true.

**Knorr** — [Historical] A broad, deep-hulled Norse cargo ship.
Designed for ocean crossings. In coastal Rimevegr settlements,
knorr-hulks sit beached and rotting -- relics of the trade
networks that died with the Cracking.

**Knot** — [Historical] A fastening made by intertwining rope.
A man who cannot tie three knots (bowline, clove-hitch,
sheet-bend) is useless on a march. Knots hold shelters together,
secure loads to the mule, and bind wounds.

**Krona** — [Coined] A silver coin. The standard currency in
larger settlements. Stamped with a jarl's mark. Smaller
transactions use hack-silver. A krona buys a day's room and
board in a settlement, or a week's bread on the road.

## L

**Lackey** — [Slang] A derogatory term for a man who follows
another without independent will. Not the same as a loyal
follower -- a lackey does as told because he has no capacity
for independent action.

**Ladle** — [Historical] A long-handled spoon for serving stew
from the communal pot. Dalla controls the ladle. The ladle is
power -- who gets served first, who gets the thick portion,
who gets broth.

**Landless** — [Coined] The Rimevegr's underclass. Free men
who own nothing -- no property, no kin network, no prospects.
They drift between settlements seeking seasonal work. In good
times, they labor. In bad times, they steal or join mercenary
bands. The band's commons are recruited from the landless.

**Landmark** — [Coined] A recognizable feature used for
navigation. A distinctive rock, a dead tree, a curve in the
river. The Rimevegr has no maps. Landmarks are traded between
bands like currency. "There is a split pine two days north"
is navigation in the Rimevegr.

**Lanolin** — [Historical] The natural oil in sheep's wool.
Makes wool partially water-resistant. Raw wool retains lanolin;
washed wool loses it. Do not over-wash wool garments. They
should smell somewhat of sheep. The lanolin is doing its job.

**Lífinnanlíf** — [Coined] Life-inside-life. The terrifying
experience of standing inside another person's memory so fully
that their fear, motion, and last decisions feel like your own.

**Ljóðveiðr** — [Coined] Verse-hunting. The galdr-worker's
practice of deliberately initiating a söngœði by planting a
seed-line, then recording the truths the collective singing
produces. The galdr-worker triggers the song but does not
control it -- he sets the trap and writes down the catch.

**Löglag** — [Coined] Law-songs. The Rimevegr's legal code
encoded in verse for memorization. Performed by the Lawspeaker
at the Thing in a chanting monotone. A forgotten verse is a
forgotten law. A sung law cannot be burned.

**Last-fire** — [Coined] The final fire of a camp before
moving. The last-fire is used to burn anything not worth
carrying and to signal departure to anyone watching. A camp
left without a last-fire looks abandoned in panic.

**Latch** — [Historical] A simple bar-and-slot door fastener.
The difference between a latched door and an unlatched door is
measured in seconds of warning when someone forces entry.

**Lath** — [Historical] A thin strip of wood. Used for
wall-construction, roof-support, and temporary splints.
Readily split from any straight-grained log.

**Lean-to** — [Historical] A simple shelter: poles leaned
against a tree or wall, covered with branches or hides. The
fastest camp shelter. One man can raise a lean-to in minutes.
It blocks wind from one direction only.

**Leather-jack** — [Historical] A leather drinking vessel,
often waxed inside to hold liquid. More durable than clay, less
precious than horn. A hardened leather-jack can stop a weak
knife thrust -- not by design, just by accident of thickness.

**Leather** — [Historical] Tanned animal hide. The universal
material. Boots, belts, scabbards, shield-covers, pouches,
armor, rope, water-skins. A man with leather-working skill
never starves -- the demand is constant.

**Ledger, the** — [Coined] The charcoal record kept by Gest.
Pay rituals, foraging quotas, supply counts, grievances, debts.
The ledger is the band's institutional memory. When it is full,
the information is condensed onto a new sheet and the old one
is burned. The burn is witnessed to prevent tampering.

**Ledger-day** — [Coined] The daily cycle of the band, as
structured by the ledger's rhythms: morning count, midday
march, evening fire, night watch.

**Lie-price** — [Coined] The cost of discovering a broken
promise. When a jarl's terms do not match his actions, the lie-
price is what the band demands to correct the shortfall. Usually
double the original amount. "The lie-price is steep" is a
warning that the band knows it was cheated.

**Lime-bast** — [Historical] See _bast_. Specifically from
linden (lime) trees. The strongest plant-fiber cordage locally
available.

**Line-fighter** — [Coined] A man whose job in combat is to
hold a position and fight in formation (such as it is). Kell
is a line-fighter. Not skilled enough for skirmishing, not
smart enough for scouting. But he stands where you put him and
he does not move.

**Lodestone** — [Historical] Naturally magnetic iron ore. Used
(rarely) for compass-like navigation in Norse culture. In the
Rimevegr, lodestones are superstitiously valued -- they "point
to the truth," which is to say, they point north.

**Loft** — [Historical] An upper floor or sleeping platform
inside a longhouse. Usually reached by ladder. Warmer than
ground-level because heat rises. The loft is where children
sleep and grain is stored.

**Long Dark** — [Coined] The 300-day winter season of the
Rimevegr. Cold, snow, diminishing light, The Veil at its
thickest. Everything costs more energy. Fire is the center of
life. The Long Dark is not a season but a state of existence.

**Long Summer** — [Coined] The 60-day warm season. Near-constant
daylight. Aggressive growth. Mud replaces ice. Insects are
vicious. Not summer as the Norse knew it -- compressed, violent,
exhausting in its own way.

**Longhouse** — [Historical] The standard Norse dwelling. Long,
narrow, turf-roofed. Central fire pit. Animals at one end,
people at the other. In the Rimevegr, longhouses cluster on
fjord slopes. The band does not own one. They shelter in
abandoned ones when lucky.

**Loot-share** — [Coined] The contractual division of plunder.
Percentages set at band formation: captain's share, Named Men's
share, veterans' share, commons' share. Changing the loot-share
mid-contract is a band-break trigger.

**Loom** — [Historical] A frame for weaving cloth. Settlement
technology. The vertical warp-weighted loom is the Rimevegr
standard. A woman at a loom produces wool cloth at about one
yard per day. The sound of a working loom is the sound of a
functioning settlement.

**Lopt** — [Coined] The Scar-Mouth. God of lies that are true,
truths no one wants to hear, and fire that burns the house so
the family escapes. His lips are sewn shut. His eyes laugh.
Invoking Lopt by name requires spitting afterward.

**Lopt's fingers** — [Slang] Spies, poisoners, quartermasters
who cook the books. Anyone who serves through deception. Not a
compliment. An acknowledgment.

**Lopt's mouth** — [Slang] A liar. Someone hiding something.
"That is Lopt's mouth talking" means the speaker is not to be
trusted.

## M

**Mace** — [Historical] A heavy weapon with a weighted head
on a wooden haft. Crushes armor and bone without needing an
edge. Effective against mail but slow. In the Rimevegr, maces
are settlement-guard weapons, not march weapons -- too heavy
for their utility.

**Maggot-flour** — [Slang] Flour so old it has weevils. The
band picks out the largest ones and grinds the rest. Protein
is protein.

**Mail** — [Historical] See _brynja_. Chainmail. Interlocked
iron rings forming a flexible metal fabric. The best armor
available in the Rimevegr. Wearing mail is a statement of
wealth and professional status.

**Mail-coif** — [Historical] A hood of chainmail worn over
a padded cap. Protects the head and neck. Heavy enough to
cause neck strain on long marches. Worn only when combat is
expected.

**Mallet** — [Historical] A wooden hammer. Used for driving
tent pegs, setting rivets on an anvil, butchering, and
discipline (a blow from a mallet's flat face across the
shoulders). Less lethal than a metal hammer. More available.

**Mantle** — [Historical] A large cloak or wrap, often of
heavy wool, sometimes fur-lined. Worn over everything else.
The outermost layer of protection. A man's mantle determines
how cold he sleeps.

**March-bread** — [Coined] See _hard-bread_. The hard, dry
bread carried on the march. Lasts weeks. Must be soaked in
water or broth to chew. The taste is absence -- it tastes of
nothing at all, which is better than tasting of mold.

**March-order** — [Coined] The sequence in which the band
walks. Point man, scouts flanking, main body, rear guard.
March-order determines who gets ambushed first and who has
time to react. Sloppy march-order kills bands.

**Mare** — [Historical] A female horse. More docile than
stallions, cheaper to keep. The band's few horses are mares.
A mare that can carry packs through snow is worth more than
most men.

**Mark** — [Historical] A unit of weight for precious metals.
About 200 grams. "A mark of silver" is a substantial sum --
enough to buy a horse or a season's grain.

**Mast-light** — [Coined] A lamp mounted at the top of a ship's
mast. On coastal fjords, mast-lights are the first sign of
approaching vessels. On land, metaphorical: the first visible
sign of anything approaching.

**Mattock** — [Historical] A digging tool with a heavy blade
at right angles to the handle. Used for breaking frozen ground,
cutting roots, digging latrines. The band carries one. It is
also a credible skull-crushing weapon.

**Meadow-sweet** — [Historical] A wild herb used to flavor
mead and ale. Also a mild pain-reliever when brewed as tea.
Contains salicylates -- the band does not know this word but
knows the effect.

**Mead** — [Historical] Honey wine. The prestige drink of Norse
culture. In the Rimevegr, mead is a settlement luxury. The
band cannot produce or carry it. Tasting mead means the band
has reached civilization, however briefly.

**Mead-debt** — [Slang] The social obligation incurred by
accepting someone's hospitality. "We owe a mead-debt at Ashen
Reach" means the band received hospitality and has not
reciprocated.

**Mannát** — [Coined] Man-eating. The first soulbane: the
deliberate eating of human flesh. More than starvation or
murder -- it marks a willing crossing that most settlements
regard as spiritually final.

**Meal-time** — [Coined] The band eats twice: morning porridge
and evening stew. Meal-time is a social institution as much as
a nutritional one. Disputes surface at meal-time. Alliances are
visible in seating.

**Metal-rot** — [Coined] Generic term for corrosion of any
metal. "The chest has metal-rot" means the iron bands are
failing. Everything metal rots in the Rimevegr's damp cold.

**Midden** — [Historical] A garbage dump. Every settlement has
one. Archaeologically valuable; practically disgusting. The
smell announces it. In the Rimevegr, middens are downwind and
away from the water source, same as latrines.

**Midwinter** — [Historical] The darkest point of the Long Dark.
The shortest day. In historical Norse culture, a time of feasts
and ceremony. In the Rimevegr, midwinter is survival. Feasts
happen in halls that can afford them. The band builds a bigger
fire and tells stories.

**Minnidraugr** — [Coined] Memory-ghost. Not the dead person,
but the dead event lingering -- a loop of fear, labor, grief,
or violence impressed into place.

**Minnisvarf** — [Coined] Memory-pull. The drag by which a
Veil-thinned place catches the mind and forces it into old
experience.

**Milk-tooth** — [Slang] A recruit so young he has not seen
a real fight. "He has milk-teeth" means the man is green and
will panic when steel comes out.

**Mortar** — [Historical] 1. A bowl for grinding herbs or grain
with a pestle. 2. A mixture of lime, sand, and water used for
stone construction. The Rimevegr has little stone construction.
Mortar means the grinding bowl here.

**Moss-lamp** — [Coined] Sphagnum moss soaked in fat and lit.
A crude emergency light source. Burns for minutes, not hours.
Smells terrible. Better than barrow-black.

**Mouldy-bread** — [Slang] Food that is technically edible but
barely. "It is mouldy-bread" means the situation is survivable
but unpleasant.

**Mule** — [Historical] A horse-donkey cross bred for carrying.
The band's mule carries the strongbox, surplus supplies, and
occasionally a wounded man. The mule has no name. It does not
need one. It is the most reliable member of the band.

**Mule-price** — [Coined] A medium-large expense. Less than a
horse-price, more than what most commons carry. Losing a mule
costs the equivalent of several weeks' wages, plus whatever the
mule was carrying.

## N

**Nail** — [Historical] Iron nails are trade goods. Each one is
hand-forged. A pouch of nails has purchasing power. Ship nails,
coffin nails, construction nails -- different shapes for
different purposes. The band carries a small supply for repairs.

**Námsvisur** — [Coined] Learning-songs. How children memorize
survival knowledge: counting (attached to death-counts),
lineage (sung backward through generations), seasons (storm
signs, ice indicators, edible vs. poisonous plants). The song
is the curriculum. The test is whether the child survives.

**Named Man** — [Coined] A specialist or officer in the band.
Extra pay, voice in decisions, established reputation. Each has
an earned call-name, a trigger, and an agenda. Named Men are
the band's skeleton. Lose too many and the body collapses.

**Nave** — [Historical] The central hub of a wheel. By
extension: the center of anything. "The nave of the problem"
means the core issue. Settlement use -- the nave of the village
is the well or the hall.

**Neck-ring** — [Historical] A torc or rigid ring worn around
the neck. Status marker. In the Rimevegr, neck-rings of twisted
bronze or silver indicate wealth and rank. A jarl's neck-ring is
a badge of office.

**Needle** — [Historical] A bone or iron sewing needle. Without
needles, clothing cannot be repaired. Bone needles are carved
from any long bone. Iron needles are trade goods -- small,
valuable, easily lost.

**Net** — [Historical] Cordage woven into a mesh for catching
fish or birds. Nets are heavy to carry and slow to deploy.
The band carries one small gill-net for river crossings near
fish runs. Gest considers it a luxury.

**Niding** — [Historical] A man without honor. The worst thing
a man can be called. A niding has broken oaths, fled from
fights, betrayed trust. The word is specific and final -- once
applied, it does not wash off.

**Night-sight** — [Coined] The ability some practitioners
develop to see in low light after extended galdr or seiðr
practice. Not supernatural -- the body adapts to working by
firelight and starlight. Galdr-workers who carve in dim
conditions develop it faster. Mechanically irrelevant but
narratively atmospheric.

**Náttmál** — [Coined] Night-speech. The hidden or inward face
of a rune: the aspect used in secrecy, fear, concealment,
coercion, and deep-working rather than public inscription.

**Night market** — [Trade] Illegal trading session outside settlement walls after dark. Emerges when outlaw pressure reaches threatening levels. Offers rare goods, stolen property, and intelligence -- but attending carries legal and disease risks. See §18.14.3.

**Night-soil** — [Historical] Human excrement collected for
fertilizer. Settlement practice. The euphemism is thin. The
smell is not.

**Nithing-pole** — See _niding-pole_.

**Niding-pole** — [Historical] A carved pole set up to curse
an enemy. The head of a horse or other animal is mounted on a
hazel pole, mouth forced open toward the target's home or hall,
with carved runes directing the curse. In the Rimevegr, a
niding-pole is a galdr rank 4 effect with real mechanical
consequences. Legitimacy of the curse is decided at the ting.
See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.3.

**Nock** — [Historical] The notch in an arrow that fits the
bowstring. Also the act of placing the arrow: "nock and draw."
A man who cannot nock in the dark has not practiced enough.

**Noggin** — [Historical] A small wooden cup or mug. Carved
from a single piece of hardwood. Personal drinking vessel. The
band's commons drink from noggins; Named Men from horn cups.

**North-wind** — [Historical] The coldest wind. In the Rimevegr,
the north-wind comes from the Ice-Reach carrying temperatures
that crack stone. "The north-wind is talking" means extreme
cold is coming.

**Norns** — [Historical] The three fate-weavers of Norse myth.
In the Rimevegr, referenced in speech: "The Norns have woven
it" means the outcome was inevitable. Whether Norns literally
exist is never addressed.

**Notch** — [Historical] A cut or nick in wood or bone used
for counting. Kill-notches on an axe-haft. Day-notches on a
tally-stick. Travel-notches on a walking staff. The Rimevegr
counts by cutting, not writing.

**Nub** — [Coined] The short remaining stump of a candle,
torch, or piece of cordage. "Down to the nub" means nearly
exhausted. Also: the core point. "Give me the nub" means state
the essential without padding.

**Nut-oil** — [Historical] Oil pressed from hazelnuts or
walnuts. Used for lamp fuel, leather treatment, and cooking.
A luxury in the Rimevegr -- nut trees are scarce in the deep
north. Coastal settlements trade for it.

## O

**Oaken** — [Historical] Made of oak. The strongest local wood.
Oaken shields, oaken doors, oaken keels. The word implies
quality and permanence. "An oaken man" means someone reliable.

**Oar-bench** — [Historical] The rowing position on a longship.
By extension: a junior position in any hierarchy. "He has not
earned his oar-bench" means the man has not proven himself.

**Oar-mate** — [Historical] The man who sits beside you on the
oar-bench. By metaphor: a trusted companion. More common in
coastal speech than inland Rimevegr.

**Oath-breaker** — [Historical] Self-explanatory and terminal.
An oath-breaker in the Rimevegr is economically dead -- no band
will hire him, no settlement will shelter him, no contract
will bear his name.

**Oath-ring** — [Historical] A sacred arm-ring on which oaths
were sworn. In historical practice, kept in temples. In the
Rimevegr, the oath-ring is a settlement institution -- the
headman or jarl keeps one. Swearing on the oath-ring in front
of witnesses is the strongest form of non-divine contract.

**Oat-cake** — [Historical] A flat bread of oat flour cooked on
a hot stone. Keeps for days. Portable. Crumbles in the pack
but the crumbs are still edible. A step above hard-bread.

**Offal** — [Historical] The internal organs of a butchered
animal. Heart, liver, kidneys, lungs, stomach, intestines. All
are eaten. Nothing is wasted. Liver is the prized cut. Stomach
becomes a water-skin. Intestines become sausage casing.

**Oil-lamp** — [Historical] A small stone or clay dish with
animal fat and a fiber wick. See _tallow-lamp_. Oil-lamps are
slightly less smoky than tallow lamps when burning fish oil.

**Odynn** — [Coined] The One-Hung. God of gallows, knowledge-
through-suffering, and debts that cannot be repaid. Depicted
hanging upside-down from a dead ash tree. The strongest oaths
are sworn in his name. The rope-and-eye mark seals contracts.

**Odynn's rope** — [Slang] The worst oath. Unbreakable by
social convention. "On Odynn's rope" means the speaker has bound
himself to something he cannot escape without destroying his
reputation.

**Omen dependency** — [Coined] What happens when a band
receives too many favorable wyrd-readings in a row. The men
start expecting good fortune. When a negative reading finally
comes, the morale drop is doubled. The wyrd-reader learns to
mix bad news with good -- or stops reading entirely. See
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §6 (omen dependency).

**One-Hung** — [Coined] Epithet for Odynn. He hangs from the
ash tree. One eye gone. The other weeping black.

**Ordovast** — [Coined] Warchief of Grimholt. A named NPC who
controls the High Rime-Moors settlement.

**Osk** — [Historical] A wish or desire. In common speech:
"that is his osk" means that is what the man wants. Used to
identify a person's private agenda without calling it such.

**Óskapaðir** — [Coined] The unshaped ones. The unborn dead,
or forms that almost entered the world but never took full human
shape. Feared around failed births and certain barrows.

**Örlögsgalinn** — [Coined] Fate-mad. A völva or seer so damaged
by too much pattern-reading that she cannot reliably separate
omen from immediate reality.

**Örlögsýn** — [Coined] Fate-sight. The deepest and most costly
level of völva seeing, where long consequence and hidden thread
present themselves as if already decided.

**Ǫldrlög** — [Coined] Drinking-songs. Improvised feast-verses
that begin in boasting, obscenity, or humor and sometimes end
with truths no sober man meant to reveal.

**Otter-skin** — [Historical] Waterproofed pelt. Prized for
its water resistance. Used for covering packages that must stay
dry -- fire-pouches, the ledger, bandage supplies. Otter-skin
pouches are expensive equipment.

**Outlaw** — [Historical] A person expelled from legal
protection. An outlaw may be killed by anyone without penalty.
In the Rimevegr, outlawry is more common than historically --
without divine stigma, it is a practical condition rather than
spiritual exile. Some outlaws join bands. Some form their own.

**Outvegr** — [Coined] The Black Pine Wilds. Literally "the
outer way" -- the forest paths between settlements. Only
svarthird bands regularly cross the Outvegr.

## P

**Pace** — [Coined] The unit of distance in march measurement.
Roughly one yard. "Two hundred paces" is the standard scouting
report distance. Paces are not precise but consistent enough
between experienced men.

**Pack-frame** — [Historical] A wooden frame strapped to the
back for carrying loads. Distributes weight across shoulders
and hips. Better than a sack but requires construction. The
band builds pack-frames from green wood when the mule is
overloaded.

**Pack-train** — [Historical] A line of loaded animals. The
band's "pack-train" is one mule and whatever the men carry on
their backs. Real pack-trains of ten or more animals belong
to merchants and armies.

**Padded-jack** — [Historical] See _gambeson_. A quilted fabric
armor jacket. The poor man's protection. Better than nothing.
Absorbs some impact, stops some cuts. Worn under mail by those
who have both.

**Pale Sun** — [Coined] A rare weather event. The sun appears
blood-red and radiates no warmth. Animals refuse to move. Wise
folk stay indoors. An omen. No one agrees of what.

**Pale Widow** — [Coined] The ruler of Ashen Reach. A widow
with land, grain, and no husband to spend them. A political
power. An employer. Not her name -- her title.

**Palisade** — [Historical] A defensive wall of sharpened wooden
stakes. The primary defense of Rimevegr settlements. Not
impressive but functional -- it stops a casual raid and buys
time against a serious one.

**Pannier** — [Historical] A basket hung on either side of a
pack animal. The mule carries panniers. Balance is critical --
an unbalanced pannier shifts and the mule stumbles. Packing
panniers is a skill.

**Parchment** — [Historical] Treated animal skin used for
writing. Expensive. In the Rimevegr, parchment is rare -- the
charcoal ledger uses cheaper birch-bark or scraped leather.
True parchment is settlement material, used for contracts that
must survive.

**Parley** — [Historical] A formal meeting between opposing
sides to negotiate before or instead of fighting. Parley terms
are announced by horn. Approaching under parley and attacking
is oath-breaking. It happens anyway.

**Patch-wool** — [Slang] A piece of wool fabric saved for
repairs. Every man carries patch-wool. The patches accumulate
on clothing until the garment is more patch than original.

**Pattern-weld** — [Historical] A sword-making technique. Rods
of iron and steel are twisted together and forge-welded,
producing a blade with a visible wavy pattern. The strongest
blades are pattern-welded. In the Rimevegr, where master smiths
are scarce, a pattern-welded sword is an heirloom.

**Pay-ritual** — [Coined] The formal distribution of wages. The
sergeant sits on a flat stone, purse left, ledger right, small
knife stuck in the wood. Each man steps forward, receives
copper, counts openly, walks away. The ritual is sacred --
deviating from it signals crisis.

**Peat** — [Historical] Compressed moss and plant matter found
in bogs. Burns slowly with low heat and dense smoke. The primary
fuel of settlements near moorland. Peat smoke has a distinctive
sweet-sour smell that clings to clothing.

**Pelt** — [Historical] An animal skin, usually with fur
attached. Currency, clothing, and insulation. Pelts are traded
by type and quality: wolf, fox, bear, otter, marten. A good
winter pelt trades for a week's food.

**Pestle** — [Historical] A blunt-ended stick for grinding in a
mortar. Used for crushing herbs, grinding grain, mixing
poultices. Bone, stone, or hardwood.

**Pike** — [Historical] A long spear, usually 10-15 feet. Not
carried on the march -- too long, too heavy. Settlement defense
weapon. A line of pikes behind a palisade makes a gate breach
very expensive for attackers.

**Pin-money** — [Coined] A trivial amount of coin. Less than
the cost of a day's food. "That is pin-money" means the offer
is insultingly low.

**Pitch** — [Historical] Pine resin processed into a thick,
waterproof sealant. Applied to boats, leather, torch-heads,
and wound dressings. Collected from wounded pine trees. Too
useful to leave behind.

**Plank-shield** — [Coined] A round shield of edge-to-edge
planks (linden, poplar). Leather-faced. Iron boss covering the
hand-grip. A consumable in combat -- designed to take damage
so the man behind it does not.

**Pole Star** — [Historical] The fixed point of the night sky.
Everything rotates around it. Finding the Pole Star is the
single most important navigational skill for a pre-compass
traveler. Cloud cover removes this option.

**Porridge** — [Historical] Grain boiled in water. The
foundational camp meal. Barley or oats, depending on supply.
Cooked in the communal pot. A man who has eaten porridge every
morning for three years can identify grain type and water
quality blindfolded.

**Post-rider** — [Historical] A mounted messenger. Expensive
to hire. In the Rimevegr, post-riders run between major
settlements on known routes. Faster than walking, less reliable
than you would think -- post-riders disappear in the Black Pine.

**Pot-hook** — [Historical] An iron hook hung from a cross-bar
over the fire to suspend the cooking pot. Adjustable height
controls heat. The pot-hook is one of Dalla's non-negotiable
camp requirements.

**Pot-luck** — [Historical] Whatever the cook puts in the stew.
Not a choice -- an outcome. "It is pot-luck tonight" means eat
what is available without complaint.

**Practitioner degradation** — [Coined] See **Degradation**.
The slow physical cost of magical practice, tracked per
individual. Hands tremble, trances lengthen, readings blur.
Every galdr-worker over forty knows the feeling. See
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.9.

**Prow-beast** — [Historical] The carved figure at the bow of a
Norse longship. Dragons, serpents, wolves. Removed when
approaching friendly shores (so as not to frighten the land-
spirits). In the Rimevegr's inland setting, prow-beasts appear
only at coastal settlements as cultural decoration.

## Q

**Quarrel** — [Historical] A short, heavy crossbow bolt. Also:
a dispute. Both are sharp and penetrating. The word is used in
both senses. Crossbows are rare in the Rimevegr -- too
expensive to maintain on the march.

**Quarter** — [Historical] 1. A portion (captain's quarter of
the loot). 2. Mercy in battle ("asking quarter" means surrender
and request for mercy). 3. Lodging in a settlement ("quartering
terms"). All three meanings are active.

**Quartering terms** — [Coined] The negotiated agreement for a
band to stay in a settlement. Where they sleep, what they eat,
what it costs, how long. Quartering quality reveals standing:
the hall (honored), the barn (tolerated), the yard (unwelcome),
outside the walls (hostile).

**Quern** — [Historical] See _hand-quern_. A pair of stones for
grinding grain. The simplest machine in the settlement. One
stone rests. One stone turns. Grain becomes flour. An hour
yields enough for twelve mouths.

**Quick** — [Historical] 1. The living flesh beneath a nail
or around a wound. "Cut to the quick" means cut deeply enough
to hit living tissue. 2. Alive. "The quick and the dead" --
everyone. Mercenaries deal with both categories daily.

**Quill** — [Historical] A feather shaft cut for writing. In
the Rimevegr, quills are settlement tools. The band uses
charcoal stubs. Quills require ink, which requires gall-nuts,
iron vitriol, and gum arabic -- none of which travel well.

**Quiver** — [Historical] A container for arrows. Leather or
birch-bark tube, worn on the back or hip. A full quiver holds
twenty to thirty arrows. An archer without a quiver is an
archer for one shot.

**Quota** — [Coined] The assigned amount of a task. Foraging
quota: what each man must bring back. Watch quota: how many
hours each man owes per night. Gest tracks quotas. Fall behind,
and your name moves up the ledger's trouble column.

## R

**Rag-and-bone** — [Slang] A scavenger who collects scraps
from battlefields, middens, and abandoned steadings. Not
respected but tolerated. Rag-and-bone men supply materials
the band cannot produce: needles, rivets, scrap leather.

**Ragnarok** — [Historical] The prophesied end of the gods and
the world. In the Rimevegr, referenced in speech but never as a
current threat. "After Ragnarok" is an idiom meaning "never"
or "when everything is finally over."

**Rasp** — [Historical] A coarse file used for shaping wood,
hoof-trimming, and rough metal work. A rasp and a draw-knife
are the minimum woodworking toolkit.

**Ration** — [Coined] The measured daily food allowance. Gest
determines rations based on supply divided by days to the next
settlement. The calculation is simple. The politics of
enforcement are not.

**Raven** — [Historical] Odynn's birds. Battlefield scavengers.
Seeing ravens after a fight is the only acknowledgment the dead
receive from the natural world. "The ravens are here" means
there are dead nearby.

**Raven-banner** — [Historical] A triangular flag bearing a
raven image. In historical Norse culture, a war-standard. In
the Rimevegr, used by bands that want to advertise themselves.
Most bands do not -- advertisement invites ambush.

**Rúnamunnr** — [Coined] Rune-mouth. The precise mouth-shape,
breath-pattern, and throat placement used in galdr so that a
carved rune is sounded through the body rather than merely read.

**Reeve** — [Historical] A settlement administrator. Below a
jarl, parallel to a headman. Manages daily business, collects
taxes, arbitrates minor disputes.

**Render** — [Historical] To melt fat from animal tissue by
slow heating. Rendering tallow is a camp task: cut fat into
small pieces, heat slowly over low fire, strain through cloth.
The liquid cools into solid tallow. The residue (cracklings) is
eaten.

**Rest-day** — [Coined] A day the band does not march. Not a
choice but a necessity -- rest-days happen when the men are too
exhausted, the weather too severe, or the terrain too broken
to continue. Rest-days cost food without gaining distance.

**Rime** — See _hrime_.

**Rime-fog** — [Coined] A thick, cold fog that combines ground-
level cloud with suspended ice crystals. The most common weather
during the Long Dark. Visibility drops to paces. Sound warps.
Navigating in rime-fog is navigating by memory and luck.

**Rime-Moors** — See _High Rime-Moors_.

**Rime-road** — See _Rimevegr_.

**Rime-wall** — [Coined] A buildup of ice crystals on any
vertical surface during extreme cold. Camp walls, rock faces,
the inside of lean-tos. Rime-wall scrapes off and can be
melted for drinking water in emergencies. It tastes of
whatever surface it formed on.

**Rimevegr** — [Coined] "The Rime-Way" or "The Frost Road."
The name of the land. Originally the name of the old north-south
trade route. Now the word for the entire territory.

**Ring-coat** — [Historical] See _brynja_. Another term for a
mail shirt. The rings in question are individual iron links,
each riveted or twisted closed.

**Rivet** — [Historical] A metal fastener driven through two
pieces and hammered flat on the back. Ship-building and
shield-repair technology. A man who can set a rivet can repair
a shield in the field.

**Road-warden** — [Coined] A patrol that guards trade routes.
Employed by jarls or merchant guilds. Road-wardens are
sometimes former svarthird men. The band encounters them on
major routes and must decide: toll, parley, or avoid.

**Root-cellar** — [Historical] An underground storage chamber
dug into the earth, using the ground's constant temperature to
preserve food through winter. Every settlement has one. A root-
cellar with turnips, dried fish, and grain is the difference
between a living settlement and a dead one.

**Rope** — [Historical] Cordage. Made from hemp, bast, or
horsehair. The band carries several coils. Rope is a universal
tool: tie loads, build shelters, cross rivers, bind prisoners,
hang the condemned. Good rope is worth its weight.

**Rope-and-eye** — [Coined] Odynn's mark. Scratched onto
contract-stones beside contract terms. The visual shorthand for
an oath sworn on the One-Hung. Breaking a contract marked with
rope-and-eye is career-ending.

**Rushlight** — [Historical] The cheapest light source. A peeled
rush soaked in waste fat, clamped in a holder. Burns for fifteen
to thirty minutes with a guttering flame. Settlement lighting.
The band does not carry them.

**Rune** — [Historical] A character of the runic alphabet (the
futhark). In the Rimevegr, runes are both a writing system and
a magical medium. Some carved rune-stones speak when read aloud.
Some bleed when a traitor passes. Runes are the only written
record beyond the charcoal ledger.

**Rune-carver** — [Historical] Anyone who carves runes. In the
Rimevegr, this overlaps with galdr-work (magical rune-carving)
but is not identical -- a rune-carver may simply be literate,
not magical. The distinction is deliberately blurred.

**Rune-flaw** — [Coined] A miscarved rune. May produce the
opposite of the intended effect, attract unwanted attention
from beyond the Veil, or simply fail. Rune-flaws result from
failed galdr rolls (see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11, failure
table). Old rune-workers can identify another carver's flaw by
the angle of the cut.

**Rune-reader** — [Coined] Thorne's self-description of his
work. He "reads" runes rather than performing seidr -- a
deliberate linguistic dodge to avoid the ergi taboo.

**Rune-stone** — [Historical] A standing stone with carved
runes. Marks territory, seals contracts, wards locations. In
the Rimevegr, some rune-stones still resonate with old galdr.
Whispering rune-stones speak in riddles, sometimes in lies.

**Rune-ward** — [Coined] A protective rune-mark carved at a
camp perimeter or doorway. Whether rune-wards actually prevent
anything is deliberately ambiguous. Men feel safer with them.
Ash carves them anyway.

**Runnel** — [Historical] A small stream or rivulet. A runnel
near camp means fresh water without exposing men at a river
crossing. Finding runnels is a scout's skill.

**Rush-mat** — [Historical] A mat woven from dried rushes. Laid
on floors for insulation and comfort. In settlements, rush-mats
cover dirt floors. On the march, a man with a rush-mat sleeps
slightly better than one without.

**Rust** — [Historical] Iron oxide. The enemy of every edged
tool. Keeping rust off a blade requires constant oiling.
Neglected weapons rust overnight in the Rimevegr's damp. A
rusty sword is a dead man's sword.

**Rye** — [Historical] A grain crop hardier than wheat. Grows
in poor soil and cold climates. The primary bread-grain of the
Rimevegr. Rye bread is dense, dark, and sour. It keeps longer
than wheat bread.

## S

**Saddle** — [Historical] A leather seat for mounted riding.
Expensive. The band's few horses are ridden bareback or with
blanket-and-girth. A proper saddle is an officer's luxury.

**Saddle-sore** — [Slang] Chafing and bruising from riding.
Also: tired to the point of uselessness. "The men are saddle-
sore" means they cannot march further today.

**Saga** — [Historical] A prose narrative of historical events,
usually told orally and later written. In the Rimevegr, the
saga tradition is weakened -- with the gods silent, the frame
that gave sagas their authority is gone. Sagas are what they
always secretly were: lies that sound like truth, told by men
who were not there.

**Salted** — [Historical] Preserved with salt. Salt fish, salt
meat. The primary non-fresh food. Requires soaking in fresh
water before cooking. The soaking water is not wasted -- it
contains salt.

**Salt-lick** — [Historical] A natural deposit of mineral salt.
Animals gather there. Hunters know them. A salt-lick near a
camp means fresh meat is findable. Salt itself can be scraped
and collected.

**Sax** — See _seax_.

**Scabbard** — [Historical] A sheath for a sword or large knife.
Leather over a wooden core. The interior is often lined with
oiled wool to protect the blade. Drawing a sword from a wet
scabbard makes a distinctive sound.

**Scaffold** — [Historical] 1. A temporary raised platform for
construction or repair. 2. A platform for the dead -- see
_sky-burial_. The Rimevegr uses both meanings.

**Scour-bag** — [Coined] A leather bag filled with sand and
vinegar for cleaning chainmail. The mail is dumped in, the bag
is rolled and tumbled. The abrasion scours rust. After
scouring: oil the mail, store in oiled cloth.

**Seax** — [Historical] A single-edged knife or short sword.
The universal secondary weapon and utility tool. Every man
carries a seax. Size ranges from belt-knife to short-sword.

**Seidr** — [Historical] Spirit-talking magic. Communion with
the dead, land-spirits, and hidden presences. Practiced mainly
by women (volvas). Deeply stigmatized for men (the _ergi_ taboo).
The cost is sanity and social standing. Prolonged practice
changes a person -- they see things others cannot.

**Seiðr-trance** — [Historical/Coined] The state a seiðr-worker
enters to commune with spirits. Requires one hour minimum,
during which the practitioner is physically vulnerable -- eyes
open but unseeing, body slack, voice not their own. Interrupting
a trance is dangerous: the practitioner may not fully return.
Cost: 2+ Willpower per session.

**Seidr-wife** — [Historical] A female seidr practitioner. Less
stigmatized than a male one. Still feared. A seidr-wife in a
settlement is useful and dangerous in equal measure.

**Sentry** — [Historical] A guard posted to watch for threats.
The band rotates sentries in watches. Falling asleep on sentry
duty is a flogging offense. In deep Long Dark, a sentry may
freeze to death standing upright.

**Serving-order** — [Coined] The hierarchy of food distribution.
Captain first, Named Men, veterans, commons. Never deviated
from. When serving-order changes, the power structure has
changed.

**Shield-boss** — [Historical] The iron dome covering the hand-
grip on a shield. Protects the knuckles. Can be used as a
striking weapon in close quarters.

**Shield-brother** — [Historical] A man who fights beside you.
The bond is forged in combat, not ceremony. A shield-brother
relationship is the mercenary equivalent of kinship. Losing a
shield-brother leaves a debt that weighs the same as blood.

**Shield-maiden** — [Historical] A woman who fights. In the
Rimevegr, not a special category -- just a fighter who happens
to be a woman. Petra and Ash serve in the band without special
comment because the band cannot afford to refuse competence.

**Shield-rim** — [Historical] The edge of a round shield,
sometimes reinforced with leather or iron strip. A blow to the
shield-rim can split the planks. Shield-rim fighting -- using
the edge offensively -- is a close-quarters technique.

**Shield-wall** — [Historical] A massed infantry formation. In
historical Norse warfare, the primary battle tactic. In the
Rimevegr, shield-walls do not form -- no army is large enough.
Fighting is small-unit: ambushes, skirmishes, raids.

**Shingle** — [Historical] A rocky beach. Rounded stones. Bad
footing. Good for beaching boats, terrible for camping.

**Short-count** — [Slang] Underpayment. "The jarl gave us a
short-count" means the agreed payment was not fully delivered.
A grievance, recorded in the ledger.

**Silver-throat** — [Slang] A good negotiator. Gest is the
band's silver-throat. The term is functional, not flattering --
a silver-throat's skill is getting the most payment for the
least risk.

**Sinew** — [Historical] Tendon. Dried and processed, it
becomes thread or bowstring material. Sinew thread swells when
wet, tightening stitches. Used for leather-work, wound
stitching, and bow construction.

**Skinning** — [Historical] The process of removing an animal's
hide. Done immediately after killing -- the skin comes off
easier while still warm. A skilled skinner preserves the pelt
whole. A clumsy one wastes half of it.

**Sky-burial** — [Coined] Exposing the dead on a raised
platform for birds. Used when the ground is too frozen to dig
and fire-wood too scarce to burn. Pragmatic. The dead are
elevated to keep wolves from them. The ravens find them
regardless.

**Sling** — [Historical] A leather strap for hurling stones.
The cheapest ranged weapon. Effective at fifty paces against
unarmored targets. River-stones are ammunition. A good slinger
has practiced since childhood.

**Skald** — [Historical] A poet, specifically one who composes
and recites norse poetry and sagas. Skalds exist in settlements.
They pour mead before performing (invoking Bragi). The band has
no skald. Stories are amateur.

**Skathi** — [Coined] The Bone-Walker. Goddess of winter, the
hunt, and the indifference of high places. Made of ice shards.
No face. She carries a bow strung with sinew from something
that was once a god.

**Skein** — [Historical] A measured length of thread or yarn
wound in a loose coil. Trade goods. "A skein of wool thread"
is a unit of value in settlement barter.

**Sjónarœði** — [Coined] Sight-fury. Acute overload in a völva:
open-eyed panic, tears, shaking, and inability to stop seeing
pattern in every movement and edge.

**Sjónarvillt** — [Coined] Sight-lost. A state where the seer
wanders after lines and openings no one else can perceive,
often needing to be led back by voice or touch.

**Skóggangr** — [Legal] Full outlawry. Permanent exile from all Norse law. The outlaw is "forest-goer" -- stripped of all property, legal protections, and social bonds. May be killed by anyone without legal consequence. The worst punishment short of death. See `data/culture/outlawry_system.yaml`.

**Skull-splitter** — [Slang] A heavy axe. The name describes
the function. Not a compliment to the weapon -- a statement of
what axes do.

**Slag** — [Historical] See _dross_. The waste from smelting.
Also used as an insult: "He is slag" means worthless.

**Smoke-rack** — [Historical] An improvised frame of green
sticks over a low fire for smoking meat. Green wood resists
burning. Four to eight hours of dense smoke produces meat that
keeps for a week. True preservation requires 24-48 hours.

**Snow-blind** — [Coined] Temporary blindness from sunlight
reflecting off snow. The eyes burn, water, and refuse to focus.
Prevented by soot smeared below the eyes or by narrow-slit
goggles carved from bone. Cured by rest in darkness.

**Snow-shoe** — [Historical] A flat frame of bent wood and
woven sinew, strapped to the foot to distribute weight across
soft snow. Without snow-shoes, a man sinks to the knee. With
them, he walks on top. The difference is measurable in miles
per day.

**Snorri** — [Coined] A Named Man in the band. The storyteller.
His bad knees are a weather instrument. He does not know why
they work.

**Spirit-lore** — [Coined] The ranked skill (1-5) governing
seiðr practice. Determines which spirits can be contacted and
whether the contact is counsel or coercion. Rank 1-3: ask the
dead. Rank 4+: compel the dead. The boundary between
consultation and death-seiðr. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.

**Spit** — [Historical] 1. A rod for roasting meat over fire. 2. The act of spitting (Gest's single expressive luxury). 3. A narrow point of land extending into water.

**Spoke-shave** — [Historical] A small draw-knife with handles
at both ends. Used for fine wood-shaping work -- arrow shafts,
tool handles, tent pegs.

**Spoor** — [Historical] Animal tracks and signs. Droppings,
broken twigs, fur caught on branches. Reading spoor is how
hunters find game and how scouts determine whether animals or
men passed through. Fresh spoor is wet and warm. Old spoor is
dry and cold.

**Söngœði** — [Coined] Sung-madness. A communal phenomenon where
a song gains its own life -- voices join without choosing to,
words arrive that no singer composed, knowledge surfaces that no
one present possessed. Escalates in four stages: catch, pull,
shaping, truth. The galdr-workers can initiate it via _ljóðveiðr_
(verse-hunting). Dangerous: extended episodes cause voice
damage, memory gaps, and in rare cases, death from exhaustion
when the singer cannot stop.

**Spruce-gum** — [Historical] Resin collected from spruce trees.
Chewed as a stimulant on the march. Also used to seal small
leaks in water-skins and boats. Tastes of pine and bitterness.

**Stag** — [Historical] A male deer. The largest wild game in
the forests. A stag provides enough meat for the band for three
days, plus hide, sinew, and bone. Hunting a stag in deep snow
is a two-man job with dogs.

**Standing** — [Coined] The band's reputation with a specific
settlement. Measured on a -5 to +5 scale. Standing determines
quartering quality, trade prices, and whether the gate opens
at all.

**Stave** — [Historical] 1. A wooden plank, especially a
curved one for barrels. 2. A staff or pole. 3. A section of a
poem or song ("first stave"). All three meanings are active.

**Steading** — [Historical] A farmstead. Smaller than a village.
One family, their livestock, their fields. A steading along the
road is a possible supply point -- if the farmer is willing to
trade.

**Steel** — [Historical] Iron alloyed with carbon. Harder and
holds an edge better than iron. In the Rimevegr, true steel
is a luxury. Most blades are iron with a steel edge welded on.
A full-steel sword is worth a small farm.

**Stern-post** — [Historical] The vertical timber at the back
of a ship. By extension: the last man in a column, the rear-
guard position. "He was at the stern-post" means he covered the
retreat.

**Stirrup** — [Historical] A foot-support hanging from a saddle.
Allows a rider to mount easily, stand in the saddle, and fight
from horseback. Stirrups arrived late to Norse culture. In the
Rimevegr, they exist but are not universal.

**Stew** — [Coined/Historical] The band's primary meal. A
communal pot managed by Dalla. Everything goes in. The stew's
thickness is a readable supply indicator. The stew is also a
social institution -- stories are told over it, disputes happen
near it.

**Strand** — [Historical] A beach. A landing place. To be
"stranded" originally meant to be left on a beach without a
ship.

**Strongbox** — [Coined] The band's treasury. A locked iron-
bound chest. Contains the silver, the signed contracts, and
the charcoal ledger. The strongbox is carried by the mule.
Losing the strongbox is existential.

**Strop** — [Historical] A strip of leather used for final
edge-honing after a whetstone. Stropping aligns the fine edge
without removing metal. The difference between a sharp blade
and a truly sharp blade is the strop.

**Stump-Armed** — [Coined] Epithet for Tyvr, god of law.
One hand gone, inside a wolf that no longer exists.

**Svarthird** — [Coined] "Black Hird." The generic name for
mercenary bands in the Rimevegr. They wear dark wool and iron
and leave black reputations behind. All bands are called
svarthird by outsiders. Individual bands are known by their
captain's name or a descriptor.

**Sward** — [Historical] A stretch of grass-covered ground.
In the Rimevegr, sward is Long Summer terrain -- green, soft
underfoot, brief. The sward turns to frost in days when the
Long Dark returns.

**Sweat-house** — [Historical] A small enclosed structure heated
with fire-warmed stones. Water poured on the stones produces
steam. Used for bathing, healing, and ritual. Settlements with
a sweat-house are considered civilized. The band has not seen
one in months.

**Sweet-water** — [Coined] Fresh, clean drinking water. As
opposed to brackish, stagnant, or ice-melt water that tastes
of minerals. "Sweet-water here" is a scout's best report.

**Swine** — [Historical] Pigs. Settlement livestock. Swine eat
anything and produce pork, lard, and leather. More difficult
to herd than sheep, more valuable per animal. A settlement's
swine-count is a wealth indicator.

## T

**Tack** — [Historical] The general term for horse equipment:
bridle, reins, saddle, girth, blanket. "Good tack" means well-
maintained riding gear. The band's tack is poor because the
band's horses are few and the budget is thin.

**Tallow** — [Historical] Rendered animal fat. The universal
lubricant, waterproofing agent, light source, and cooking
medium. Tallow lamps, tallow candles, tallow for leather,
tallow for the cook-pot. A camp that runs out of tallow runs
out of light.

**Tallow-lamp** — [Historical] A shallow dish with rendered fat
and a twisted fiber wick. Dim, smoky, smells of rancid fat.
The most available portable light.

**Tang** — [Historical] The extension of a blade that fits into
the handle. A full-tang knife has metal running the entire
length of the grip. More expensive, much stronger. A broken
tang means the blade separates from the handle mid-use.

**Tar** — [Historical] Liquid pitch. Made by burning pine or
birch in a covered kiln. Used for waterproofing everything:
boats, leather, rope, roofs. The smell of tar is the smell of
civilization in the Rimevegr.

**Tenant-farmer** — See _cotter_.

**Thing** — [Historical] A local assembly for settling disputes,
adjudicating feuds, and making communal decisions. Hosted by the
local jarl or headman. Rulings are enforced only by whoever has
the armed men to make them stick.

**Thong** — [Historical] A narrow strip of rawhide or leather.
Used for binding, lashing, and improvised fastening. A belt of
thong is the simplest closure for a pouch or pack. Thong swells
when wet and tightens -- useful for binding tool-heads to hafts.

**Thorn-wall** — [Coined] A defensive barrier of thorn-bearing
bushes, cultivated or natural. Thornwall (the settlement) takes
its name from this feature.

**Three Wolves, the** — [Coined] Hadric's rival mercenary band.
They burn villages. The band's primary antagonist.

**Thrall** — [Historical] An unfree person. Debt-bound or
captured. Can be sold. Children of thralls are born thrall.
Freedom can be purchased -- the price is whatever the owner
names. Thralldom is regarded as bad luck, not injustice.

**Thrall-mark** — [Coined] The social stigma that follows a
former thrall. Not a physical mark -- a reputation. "He has
the thrall-mark" means the man was once unfree, and some people
will never let him forget it.

**Þögulœði** — [Coined] Silent-fury. The numb, withdrawn state
that follows severe sight-strain or spirit-contact, when the
mind shuts doors simply to remain intact.

**Thread** — [Historical] Spun fiber for sewing. Wool, linen,
or sinew. Every man carries a needle and thread. Without thread,
a torn garment stays torn, and a wound stays open.

**Threshing** — [Historical] Beating harvested grain to separate
kernels from stalks. Done with a flail or by driving animals
over the sheaves. Settlement work that happens in the weeks
after harvest.

**Thurr** — [Coined] The Crack-Handed. God of thunder, violence
that solves nothing but must happen anyway, and the last stand.
His eyes are closed because he cannot bear to see what his
hands have done. Never silent -- he was always the god who hit
things.

**Thurr's hands** — [Slang] An oath of frustration. What a man
says when his patience is completely gone.

**Tide-pool** — [Historical] A trapped pool of salt water among
coastal rocks. A foraging source: small crabs, shellfish,
seaweed. Minimal calories, but something is better than nothing.

**Timber** — [Historical] Cut wood for construction. "Good
timber" means straight-grained wood suitable for building. The
Black Pine provides unlimited timber, but cutting, hauling, and
processing it is labor the band cannot spare.

**Tiller** — [Historical] 1. The helm-bar of a ship. 2. A
farmer who works the soil. Both uses are active.

**Tinder-pouch** — See _fire-pouch_.

**Toll** — [Historical] A fee for crossing. Bridges, fords,
gates. Toll collectors are everywhere. The fee is whatever the
collector thinks he can extract without getting killed for it.

**Tongs** — [Historical] A metal gripping tool used by smiths
to hold hot iron. Also useful for moving coals in a fire, which
is the context most band members encounter them.

**Tongue** — [Historical] 1. Body part. 2. A narrow strip of
land between two bodies of water. 3. A flame. "The tongue of
the fire" is common usage.

**Tooth-stick** — [Historical] See _chewing stick_. A twig of
hazel, birch, or willow, chewed at one end until fibrous.
Rubbed against teeth and gums. Willow contains salicin -- the
gums feel better after.

**Tribute** — [Historical] A payment extracted by force or
threat. When the band demands supplies from a settlement, the
demand is tribute. Repeated tribute causes settlement decay.
The word is understood by everyone and never used by the party
extracting it -- they say "contribution" or "provision."

**Trough** — [Historical] A long, shallow container. For water
(animals drink from it), for mixing mortar, for washing clothes.
Carved from a single log or assembled from planks.

**Truce** — [Historical] A temporary cessation of hostilities.
Truces are fragile. They last as long as both sides benefit.
When one side gains advantage, the truce breaks. "The truce
stinks" means it will not hold.

**Truss** — [Historical] 1. A structural frame of timbers for
roofing. 2. A bundle of hay or straw tightly bound. 3. A
medical binding for a hernia. All three uses are active. The
context determines the meaning.

**Tun** — [Historical] A large cask for storing liquid -- ale,
mead, water. Coopered from staves and iron hoops. A full tun
is too heavy for one man. Two men roll it.

**Turnip** — [Historical] A root vegetable. The Rimevegr's most
reliable crop. Stores well in root-cellars through the Long
Dark. Boiled, mashed, added to stew. The taste is bland. The
calories are essential.

**Turnshoe** — [Historical] Medieval boot construction method.
The boot is sewn inside-out and then turned right-side out. The
seam contacts the inside of the foot, not the ground.

**Tyvr** — [Coined] The Stump-Armed. God of law, justice-that-
costs-a-hand, and the thing-assembly where grudges are weighed
in silver and blood.

**Tyvr-handed** — [Slang] Describes a jarl who rules justly.
The opposite is "wolf-fed" -- a jarl who rules by appetite.

**Tyre** — [Historical] An iron band shrunk onto a wooden wheel
rim to prevent splitting. Making a tyre requires a smith, forge,
and precision. A wheel without a tyre cracks on stone roads
within days.

## U

**Ullr** — [Historical] A god associated with hunting, skiing,
and winter sport. In the Rimevegr, Ullr is a minor invocation --
hunters may speak his name, but he has no cult or formal
worship. One of the gods who went quiet without anyone noticing.

**Umber** — [Historical] A brownish earth pigment. Used for
dyeing leather and marking camp boundaries. Mixed with fat,
it makes a brown paste for weatherproofing exposed wood.

**Underbelly** — [Slang] The vulnerable part of anything. "The
settlement's underbelly" is its weakest defensive point. "The
contract's underbelly" is the clause that will hurt the band.

**Undercroft** — [Historical] A storage room beneath a hall,
sometimes partially underground. Cooler and drier than a root-
cellar. A luxury of stone-and-timber construction.

**Underkeel** — [Slang] The hidden cost of something. "Check
the underkeel" means look for the trap in the contract.

**Unfree** — [Historical] See _thrall_. A person without legal
autonomy. Unfree persons cannot own property, bear witness at
the Thing, or leave without permission. The largest social
class in many settlements.

**Upwind** — [Historical] From the direction the wind is blowing.
"Camp upwind of the midden" is basic sanitation sense.

**Urn-burial** — [Historical] A cremation burial where the ashes
are placed in a clay or stone urn. Higher status than a shallow
grave. Lower status than a barrow. Most settlements cannot
afford more.

**Usurer** — [Historical] A money-lender who charges interest.
Considered low but necessary. In the Rimevegr, usurers exist in
larger settlements. Their rates are extortionate because default
risk is extreme. No one likes them. Everyone uses them.

**Útlagi** — [Legal] Outlaw. One declared outside the law by ting ruling. From "út" (out) and "lög" (law). Encompasses both lesser (fjörbaugsgarðr) and full (skóggangr) outlawry.

**Utmark** — [Historical] The uncultivated land beyond a
settlement's worked fields. Common grazing, hunting ground, and
timber source. Beyond the utmark lies true wilderness. The
utmark is shared by the community; disputes over its use are
settled at the Thing.

**Utter-dark** — [Coined] Darkness so complete that a man
cannot see his own hand. Cave interiors, barrow passages,
deep-winter nights under cloud cover. In utter-dark, sound
becomes the primary sense. Men speak to locate each other.

## V

**Valgrim** — [Coined] Described as a common name for war
chiefs who rule through martial reputation rather than land
ownership. Etymologically: "the grim chosen ones."

**Valkyrie** — [Historical] In Norse myth, divine women who
collected the worthy dead for Odin's hall. In the Rimevegr,
referenced in camp-talk and older sagas but never confirmed.
"Waiting for the valkyrie" is dark humor for expecting to die.

**Vambrace** — [Historical] Forearm armor. Leather or iron.
Protects the arm from wrist to elbow. Worn on the weapon arm
or both arms. Leather vambraces are common; iron ones are
expensive and hot.

**Vanrgrim** — [Coined] "The Bitter Gods." The Low Court of the
pantheon: Tyvr, Skathi, Bragi, Heimr. The gods of law, winter,
poetry, and warning. Less invoked than the Aesrgrim but deeper
in the culture's foundation.

**Vargheim** — [Coined] A large village deep in the Black Pine.
Population 165. Jarl Ulf Vargson. Wolf-warden kennels and
charcoal kilns.

**Vargr** — [Legal/Insult] Wolf. Applied to full outlaws, naming
them as beasts beyond human society. The source of "wolfshead"
in English. A vargr has no legal standing -- killing one is no
more murder than killing a wolf.

**Vébǫnd** — [Historical] Sacred boundary ropes marking lawful
or holy ground. To cross the vébǫnd armed or polluted is a grave
violation even in a world where the gods no longer answer.

**Villimaðr** — [Coined] Wild-man. One who has passed through
villimennska and returned only in body -- human-shaped, but
socially and spiritually emptied.

**Villimennska** — [Coined] The Wilding. The third and final
soulbane: the stripping away of law, kin-bond, and human shape
of mind until appetite is all that remains.

**Veil, the** — [Coined] The boundary between the living world
and whatever lies beyond. Unstable since the Cracking. When it
thins: shadows at wrong angles, sounds that should not be,
distances that shift, fire burns blue at the edges. The air
tastes of old metal.

**Veil Ceiling** — [Coined] The self-imposed cap on supernatural
content. Supernatural elements stay rare, grounded, ambiguous --
always deniable. The effect is dread without certainty. The
reader decides. The novel does not.

**Veil-Thinning** — [Coined] A rare event when the Veil thins
unpredictably. Reality becomes unreliable. Correlated with
season (worst in Long Dark) and location (barrows, old temples).
Unknown triggers. Characters cannot predict them.

**Veil-Touched** — [Coined] People born with pale eyes and
affinity for wyrd-reading. They sense the threads of fate.
Feared as much as respected. Volvas and rune-carvers fall into
this category.

**Veil-thinning (intentional)** — [Coined] The forbidden act of
deliberately weakening the Veil barrier. Classified as the most
serious magical crime. Triggers settlement panic and feud +3
with all neighboring settlements. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`
§11.8.4.

**Venom** — [Historical] Poison from snakes, spiders, or
plants. In the Rimevegr, venom is rare as a weapon -- too
unreliable and too hard to obtain. But baneberry and wolfsbane
grow in the Black Pine. Someone with knowledge could extract a
lethal dose.

**Venture** — [Coined] A speculative contract where the band
accepts risk for a share of potential reward rather than a fixed
fee. "Going on venture" means the band may earn nothing or may
earn a fortune. Gest hates ventures.

**Vermin** — [Historical] Rats, mice, fleas, lice. The permanent
unwanted company of every camp and settlement. Vermin eat the
stores and spread sickness. The battle against vermin is never
won, only managed.

**Verklög** — [Coined] Work-songs. Rhythmic songs tied to
repetitive labor: rowing, grinding, splitting, smithing,
carding. The beat times the work; the verses encode technical
knowledge (knot sequences, brine-soak timing, beam-count per
raise). Never taught -- caught by proximity and repetition.

**Vǫgguvísur** — [Coined] Cradle-songs. Death-counting lullabies
sung to infants -- catalogues of dangers set to rocking rhythm.
The melody writes survival instincts into the body before the
mind can parse the words. Also preserves the names of dead
children the sagas will never mention.

**Veteran** — [Coined] A proven fighter with time in the band.
Above common, below Named Man. Veterans are reliable enough to
not need watching. They eat after Named Men and before commons.

**Volva** — [Historical] A female seidr-practitioner. A seeress.
Volvas occupy a complex social position -- feared, needed,
isolated. They see what others cannot. The cost is visible on
their faces.

**Vow** — [Historical] A solemn promise, often with divine
witness. Distinct from an oath in that a vow is private -- a
man makes a vow to himself or to one other person. An oath is
public. Breaking a vow has no legal consequence. The personal
consequence is another matter.

## W

**Wading** — [Historical] Crossing a river on foot. Current,
depth, footing, and cold are the four hazards. A mid-thigh
ford in winter can stop a man's heart from cold shock. The
band crosses in a linked chain, gripping each other's belts.

**Wainwright** — [Historical] A cart-builder. A settlement
specialist. The band has no cart -- carts require roads, and
roads are intermittent in the Rimevegr.

**Ward** — [Historical/Coined] 1. A rune-carved protective
mark. Galdr-workers lay wards at camp perimeters, on
doorposts, around sleeping areas. Whether they work is
deliberately ambiguous. 2. A person under protection.
"Thorne's ward" means someone he has taken responsibility for.

**Ward-right** — [Coined] A political appointment at the
Allthing that determines whose galdr-worker wards the assembly
stones. The ward-holder controls magical safety for all
participants and gains +1 modifier to all Allthing actions.
Appointment resolved on Allthing Day 1 by `ward_influence`
formula. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.1.

**Ward-stone** — [Coined] A rune-carved stone set at a
settlement boundary or building entrance to provide magical
protection. Must be maintained by a galdr-worker -- ward-stones
degrade over time. Cost: 250-450 copper per stone. Intact
ward-stones give +1 morale to bands camping nearby. See
`08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §7.

**Wasting asset** — [Coined/Slang] What a galdr-worker is to a
mercenary band. The practitioner degrades with use and cannot
be replaced. Unlike swords or shields, a ward-worker ages out
of usefulness and there are not enough young practitioners
entering the trade. See `08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §7 and §8.

**Watch-fire** — [Coined] A small fire maintained during the
night watch. Just enough light to see the camp perimeter.
Different from the sleeping fire -- the watch-fire is
positioned for visibility, not warmth.

**Watch-rotation** — [Coined] The schedule of night sentries.
Four watches per night. Each man serves one watch in rotation.
Named Men are sometimes exempt. The fourth watch (pre-dawn)
is the worst -- the cold is deepest and the body wants sleep
most.

**Water-skin** — [Historical] A container made from animal
stomach, bladder, or sewn leather sealed with pitch. Holds
about a liter. Imparts a taste -- leather and whatever fat was
used to seal it. A man needs two liters at rest, four on the
march.

**Weak Link** — [Coined] The slowest, clumsiest, or least
skilled member of the band. Contempt is constant but rarely
spoken. The weak link carries weight because the band cannot
afford to carry nothing. This is Lump's starting position.

**Wergild** — See _weregild_.

**Wet-nurse** — [Historical] A woman who breast-feeds another's
child. In the Rimevegr, wet-nurses are settlement women. The
band has no use for the term except as an insult: "He needs a
wet-nurse" means the man is helpless.

**Weregild** — [Historical] Blood-price. The silver value of a
human life, paid to end a feud or compensate a killing. Not
fixed in the Rimevegr -- negotiated. What a man's life is worth
depends on who is asking and who is answering. Common: 15-25
silver. Named Man: 50+ silver.

**Whet** — [Historical] To sharpen by rubbing on a stone. The
sound of a whetstone on steel at dusk is one of the novel's
ambient textures.

**Whetstone** — [Historical] A flat stone of fine-grained
sandstone or slate used for sharpening blades. Wetted with water
or spit. Every fighting man carries one. The whetstone and the
fire-pouch are the two things a man checks before sleep.

**Whey** — [Historical] The liquid remaining after milk is
curdled for cheese. Thin, sour, slightly nourishing. Drunk
as a beverage in settlements. The band encounters whey only
in halls that keep cattle.

**Wicker** — [Historical] Woven thin branches, usually willow.
Used for baskets, fish-traps, temporary fences, and the frames
of lightweight shields. Quick to make but not durable.

**White-out** — [Coined] A rime storm severe enough to erase
all visual reference. Sky and ground merge into uniform white.
Navigation becomes impossible. A man ten paces from shelter
cannot find it. White-outs kill without malice.

**Widow's-portion** — [Historical] The property a widow retains
after a husband's death. In the Rimevegr, widows with property
are political powers. The Pale Widow rules Ashen Reach not
through war but through grain.

**Wind-break** — [Historical] A barrier against wind. A wall of
stacked brush, a stretched hide, a snow-wall. Reduces wind-chill
dramatically. Radiant fire-heat bounces off the wind-break
toward the sleepers.

**Windlass** — [Historical] A winding mechanism for raising
heavy loads. Used in wells, harbor cranes, and crossbow cocking.
A mechanical advantage device. Settlement technology.

**Winter-camp** — [Coined] A semi-permanent camp established
for the worst of the Long Dark. Better constructed than a
marching camp. Earthen walls, deeper fire-pits, thicker
shelters. The band digs in and waits for conditions to improve.

**Winter-hall** — [Coined] A semi-permanent shelter large enough
for the entire band. Reaching a winter-hall before deep Long
Dark is a survival objective. A winter-hall has fire, walls,
and a roof. Everything else is luxury.

**Wolf-fed** — [Slang] Describes a jarl who rules by appetite,
not justice. The opposite of "Tyvr-handed." A wolf-fed jarl
takes what he can because he can.

**Wolf-trap** — [Historical] A covered pit or spring-loaded
mechanism for catching wolves. Wolves prey on livestock and
lone travelers. Settlements maintain wolf-traps. Walking into
one in the dark is not abstract -- it is a real hazard of
off-trail travel.

**Wolfshead** — [Legal] English equivalent of vargr. A person under full outlawry, declared a wolf in human form. May be killed without legal consequence. Wolfshead bands are groups of outlaws who survive by raiding, trading, or selling their combat skills. See `data/wolfshead_bands.yaml`.

**Woof** — [Historical] See _weft_. The horizontal threads in
weaving that cross the warp. "Warp and woof" means the
fundamental structure of something.

**Work-song** — [Historical] A rhythmic song sung during
repetitive labor: rowing, marching, grinding, threshing. The
rhythm coordinates effort. The band has work-songs for
marching. They are not pretty. They keep pace.

**Worsted** — [Historical] A type of yarn or fabric made from
long-staple wool, tightly spun. Smoother and stronger than
regular wool cloth. More expensive. Named Men might own a
worsted tunic. Commons do not.

**Wound-dew** — [Historical] Kenning for blood. One of the
language's fossilized metaphors. Characters use it without
thinking of it as poetry.

**Wyrd** — [Historical] Fate. The sense that the outcome is
already woven. Wyrd is not predestination in a theological
sense -- it is the cumulative weight of all past actions
pressing on the present. What will happen has already been set
in motion. You just cannot see the thread yet.

**Wyrd-reading** — [Historical] Fate divination. Casting lots,
reading entrails, interpreting omens. The most common and least
stigmatized magical tradition. Reveals the threads already
woven. The knowledge is always true but never complete.

**Wyrm** — [Historical] A serpent or dragon in Norse myth.
In the Rimevegr, wyrms are saga creatures -- no one living
has seen one. The word survives in place-names (Wyrm-teeth
Pass) and metaphor. "That contract is a wyrm" means it has
teeth you cannot see.

## Y

**Yardarm** — [Historical] The horizontal spar on a ship's mast
from which sails hang. By extension: the crossbar of any
structure. "Strung from the yardarm" means publicly hanged as
a warning.

**Yarn** — [Historical] 1. Spun fiber ready for weaving or
knitting. Wool yarn is the Rimevegr's primary textile thread. 2. A story, usually exaggerated. "Spinning a yarn" means
telling a tall tale. Snorri spins yarns. Some of them are even
true.

**Yearling** — [Historical] An animal in its second year. A
yearling calf, a yearling foal. Also slang for a second-year
recruit -- someone who has survived one Long Dark but is not
yet experienced.

**Yew** — [Historical] A wood prized for bows. Yew heartwood
compresses and sapwood stretches -- a natural composite. The
best bows are made from yew staves where heartwood and sapwood
meet. Yew is also poisonous. The berries, needles, and bark
can kill livestock and men.

**Yoke** — [Historical] 1. A wooden frame for carrying buckets
on the shoulders. 2. Metaphor for any burden. "Under the yoke"
means burdened with obligation.

**Yore** — [Coined] The time before the Cracking. The old
world. Songs reference yore as a golden period, but golden
periods always exist only in songs. No one living has reliable
information about yore. The sagas contradict each other.

**Youngblood** — [Slang] A new recruit, especially one who is
aggressive and untested. Youngbloods volunteer for point
position and regret it. The term is not affectionate. A
youngblood is a liability until he has been scared enough to
think before acting.

## Z

**Zeal** — [Historical] Fierce determination. In the Rimevegr,
zeal is mistrusted. A man with zeal has lost perspective. Cool
competence is preferred. "He has too much zeal" is a warning
that someone will do something reckless.

**Zinc** — [Historical] A grey metal. Too soft for weapons, too
brittle for tools. Used for cheap coinage when silver runs out.
Zinc coins turn fingers grey and do not ring when dropped.

**Zincs** — [Coined] A camp-slang corruption of "zinc" -- dull,
grey, worthless coins from dead settlements where the mint had
degraded. Zincs trade at a fraction of proper silver. "Paying
in zincs" is an insult.

**Zoning** — [Coined] The tactical division of a defensive
perimeter into sectors. Each man on watch owns a zone. He does
not leave it, even if action occurs in an adjacent zone. Zoning
prevents the fatal clustering of defenders during an attack.

## Appendix: Common Oaths, Curses, and Idioms

This appendix is for fast phrase-loading during drafting. These are not
rare poetic kennings. They are the common verbal tools characters reach
for under stress, drink, grief, bargaining, boredom, and bad weather.

### Formal Oath Gradations

| Phrase | Weight / Use |
| --- | --- |
| "You have my word." | Lightest promise. Reputation-backed only. |
| "On the fire between us." | Sworn over a shared hearth. Breaking it loses fire-right. |
| "My steel answers for it." | Serious martial guarantee. Blade stands as collateral. |
| "By the rime on my bones." | The land and cold are called to witness. |
| "Cut and clasp." | Blood-oath between equals or oathmates. |
| "My name on the ledger for it." | Written guarantee. Permanent if broken. |
| "Before Odynn's rope, I am held." | Sacred oath. Breaking it is social death. |
| "I give it to the Veil." | Absolute oath. Rare, feared, almost never spoken casually. |
| "By the Aesrgrim." | Serious general invocation when stakes are real. |
| "On Odynn's rope." | Contract-binding oath with maximal weight. |
| "Tyvr weigh it." | Let law, right, and consequence decide. |
| "Heimr saw nothing." | Bitter line used when no warning came and none can be blamed. |

| "Salt in the boot." | Travel ward against bad luck and river death. |
| "The third owl is for you." | Death omen. Leave immediately. |

### Fast-Use Guidance

- **Named Men** use fewer oaths, and theirs carry more weight.
- **Commons** burn through camp phrases, complaints, and dark humor.
- **Quartermasters** think in ledger, weight, count, and price idioms.
- **Old campaigners** use death euphemism casually and sincerely in the same breath.
- **Settlement elders** prefer indirect warnings and law-soaked phrases over open threats.
- **Do not over-season dialogue.** One strong phrase in a scene does more work than five.
