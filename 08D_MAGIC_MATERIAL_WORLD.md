# Magic in the Material World

## Magic and the Material World

Magic has a price list. Not the mechanical costs — those are in
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, measured in Willpower and blood and
ranked in tables. This section covers the other price. The silver
price. What a ward costs when you are paying a galdr-worker to
carve it. What a seiðr consultation costs when you need to know
what the dead know. What rune-marked goods sell for in the
Skaldhaven market and why barrow dust costs more than iron nails.

The Rimevegr runs on barter, copper, and silver. Magic sits inside
that economy the way a blacksmith sits inside it — as a service
provider with rare skills and high material costs. The difference
is that a blacksmith makes things people understand. A galdr-worker
makes things people need but cannot evaluate. You know whether a
sword is sharp. You do not know whether a ward is strong until the
night it fails, and by then the galdr-worker is in another
settlement and the dead are in your grain-store.

This asymmetry — the buyer cannot judge quality, the seller cannot
prove quality in advance — shapes everything about how magic trades.
Prices are set by reputation, not by measurement. And reputation is
set by the last catastrophic failure that everyone remembers, not
by the thousand quiet successes that nobody noticed.

### The Economy of Galdr

A galdr-worker's time is the most expensive service in the Rimevegr
that does not involve killing someone. The baseline rate at
Skaldhaven — the closest thing to a standard — is 30 copper for a
rune consultation. That is a conversation. Not a carving. Not a
ward. A galdr-worker looks at your problem, tells you what rank of
work it needs, and quotes you a price for the actual job.

Thirty copper is one and a half days of a mercenary's retainer. It
buys three days of food. It is the price of an hour's conversation
with someone who can see things you cannot, and most people in the
Rimevegr will never pay it. They cannot afford it. They salt their
doors and sew their dead and hope the old wards hold.

The actual work scales by rank, and the scaling is steep:

**Rank 1 galdr — trail-marks, single-use wards, rune-marks that
last one night.** Cost: 15-25 copper, depending on materials. A
competent apprentice can do this work. The materials are cheap —
charcoal, ash-paste, a knife with a clean edge. The work takes
half a day. Any band with a galdr-worker handles rank 1 in-house.
Ash does not charge the band for trail-marks. He carves them because
not carving them means sleeping without a perimeter, and sleeping
without a perimeter means trusting the dark.

**Rank 2 galdr — seasonal wards, binding-marks on storage,
rune-carved tools.** Cost: 40-80 copper. This is where the work
gets real. A seasonal ward on a grain-store — the kind that keeps
vermin and spoilage out through the Long Dark — costs 60 copper at
Skaldhaven. Materials matter now: the carving needs to be done in
finished wood or dressed stone, and the activation requires the
carver's blood. Not much. A finger-cut. But the blood means the
ward is keyed to a living person, and if that person dies the ward
starts to degrade. A farmer who pays 60 copper for a grain-ward
is investing in the continued survival of the man who carved it.

**Rank 3 galdr — annual wards, structural protections, rune-work
that holds through the Long Dark without maintenance.** Cost:
150-300 copper. Fifteen to thirty silver. A jarl's seasonal income
from a single farmstead might be 50 silver. A rank 3 ward on the
settlement's main hall costs a visible fraction of that income. The
materials are expensive — silver wire from Deepholm, standing stone
chips, sometimes veil-touched water — and the galdr-worker pays in
blood and will. Rank 3 costs 2 Willpower and 1 HP per the
mechanical tables. The silver price reflects the fact that few
galdr-workers can do rank 3 work and survive it often enough to
make it a profession.

Ash is rank 3. He can set annual wards. He can bind a space against
the dead so thoroughly that draugr will walk around the warded
building rather than approach it. He has done this four times in
the last two years. Each time he was slower to recover. Each time
the runes took a little more. He does not discuss the recovery
time with Voss.

**Rank 4 and above — fortress wards, Allthing protections,
permanent bindings.** Cost: 500 copper and up. Often paid in land-
rights or political favors rather than silver, because no one
carries that much coin on a road where bandits know the price of
galdr. The material components alone — rare standing stone chips,
veil-touched water from specific springs, silver wire that has
been through the Deepholm forge three times — can cost 200 copper
before the galdr-worker touches the stone. Nobody in Voss's band
does rank 4 work. Ash could attempt it. The attempt would cost 3
Willpower and 3 HP and require a component that the band does not
carry. The failure cascade at rank 4 includes permanent stat
damage. Ash knows this. He has seen what rank 4 failure looks
like. He will not discuss it.

The material costs are not arbitrary. Silver wire from Deepholm
is galdr-grade — smelted with rune-marked crucibles, drawn through
dies that have been carved with binding-marks since the foundry
was built. Regular silver wire does not work. The difference is
not visible. A merchant who substitutes regular wire for Deepholm
wire is selling a ward that will fail, and when it fails people
die, and when people die the merchant's family pays the blood-price
or the merchant's settlement loses its trade reputation. This has
happened three times in recorded memory. The punishments were
severe enough that Deepholm's monopoly is self-enforcing.

A galdr-worker's kit — the tools, not the materials — costs
roughly 200 copper to assemble and 50 copper per season to
maintain. Carving knives with edges fine enough for rune-work.
Whetstones. Chalk for marking. A set of templates for the
standard forms. Blood-lancets. Binding cord. A pouch of clean
ash. The kit fits in a satchel the size of a man's head. Ash
carries his on his left hip, opposite his hand-axe, and he
checks it every morning the way Voss checks the camp perimeter.

This is why a good galdr-worker is worth more alive than a good
swordsman. A swordsman kills people. A galdr-worker keeps
everyone in the settlement alive through the Long Dark. You can
replace a swordsman with any fit man and a week of drilling. You
cannot replace a galdr-worker with anything except another
galdr-worker, and there are perhaps forty in the entire Rimevegr
who can work at rank 3. This scarcity makes galdr-workers
political targets rather than employees. A jarl does not hire a
galdr-worker the way he hires a mercenary. He binds one — with
land, with protection, with the implicit threat that a galdr-worker
without a patron is a galdr-worker that someone else will recruit
or kill to prevent the recruitment.

Ash works for Voss because Voss does not bind. The band is not a
settlement. Ash is not a court galdr-worker tending the same ward-
stones year after year. He is on the road. He uses his skills when
the contract requires it and Voss pays him a standard share. This
arrangement makes Ash unusual and, in the eyes of every jarl who
has ever offered to buy his contract, dangerously free.

_The economics of galdr are the economics of irreplaceability.
Every other service in the Rimevegr — smithing, carpentry,
healing, fighting — has a supply curve. More people can learn it.
The pool grows. Galdr supply is shrinking. The old works are
stronger than the new ones. The masters are not training enough
apprentices. The runes take more blood each decade. Ash is not
getting better. He is getting used up. The price of galdr rises
because the supply falls, and the supply falls because the
cost of producing it — the human cost, the cost measured in
shaking hands and nightmares and years shaved off the back end
of a life — rises faster than any market can compensate._

### Ward-Stones and Ward-Rights

A ward-stone is a standing stone carved with galdr at rank 3 or
higher and set into the earth at a settlement's boundary. The
stone anchors a perimeter. Inside the perimeter, the dead cannot
cross uninvited. Outside, they do as they please. The ward does
not kill draugr. It does not banish spirits. It says _not here_
and the dead, for reasons that galdr-workers can describe
mechanically but not explain, listen.

A ward-stone that holds through the Long Dark — the full 100-day
winter when the Veil is thinnest and the dead are most active —
is worth more than the building it protects. This is not metaphor.
A farmstead's timber hall can be rebuilt in a season with enough
hands. A ward-stone that actually works through the Long Dark
requires a rank 3 galdr-worker, Deepholm silver wire, standing
stone chips from a quarry that has not been mined out, and a
month of carving. The materials alone cost 100-150 copper. The
galdr-worker's fee adds another 150-300. The total investment
is 250-450 copper for a single stone that protects one boundary
segment.

A settlement needs four to eight ward-stones for a full perimeter,
depending on size. A small farmstead cluster — thirty people, four
buildings — needs four stones at minimum. That is 1,000-1,800
copper. One hundred to one hundred eighty silver. A jarl whose
annual income from the cluster is 200 silver is spending half of
it to a full year on ward-stones alone. This is why most
settlements run partial perimeters — two stones covering the
approaches from the nearest known barrow, and salt-lines and
threshold iron on the remaining sides. It is also why ward-
failure is a political crisis rather than a maintenance problem.

Ward-stones fail. Not all at once. The runes fade. The binding
weakens. A stone that was carved five years ago by a competent
rank 3 galdr-worker is operating at perhaps seventy percent
efficacy. A stone carved fifteen years ago is at thirty percent
or less. A stone carved before the Cracking — the old wards
around Frostfjord Hollow, the Grimholt perimeter, the Deepholm
foundry boundary — still operates at full strength three centuries
later. Nobody alive knows how. Nobody alive can replicate it.
This is the core terror of the ward-stone economy: the best
products are irreplaceable antiques, and every replacement is
worse than the thing it replaces.

Ward-stone maintenance is a political tool. At the Allthing
(reference `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.1), ward-rights are
among the most contentious agenda items. Who provides the galdr-
worker for the Allthing ward-stones? Currently Skaldhaven, under
Sigrun's authority. This means the Fjord Compact controls who is
safe at the assembly and, by quiet implication, who is not. The
galdr-worker could be instructed to leave a gap. The galdr-worker
could be told to weaken one section. Nobody has done this — the
Allthing's neutrality is too valuable to burn for a single-season
advantage — but the capability is noted by everyone who stands
inside the wards and knows who carved them.

Jarls use ward-control as leverage in three ways. First, they
control the schedule. A jarl who employs the settlement's galdr-
worker decides which buildings get warded first. The jarl's hall.
The grain-store. The livestock barn. The common houses come last.
If the galdr-worker runs out of season before the common houses
are warded — and this happens most years — the common folk spend
the Long Dark with salt and threshold iron and prayer. The jarl
sleeps behind rune-carved stone. This is not cruelty. It is
triage. But the people who sleep outside the wards know the
difference between triage and privilege when the screaming starts
at night.

Second, jarls control access to galdr-workers. A galdr-worker who
is bound to a jarl's household does not do freelance work. If a
farmer outside the jarl's favor needs a ward repaired, the farmer
petitions the jarl, and the jarl decides whether the farmer has
been loyal enough to deserve protection. This turns ward-access
into a loyalty test. Pay your taxes. Send your sons to the muster.
Do not speak against the jarl at the ting. Do these things and
your grain-store will be warded. Fail, and your family spends
the dark season at the mercy of whatever walks.

Third, and most dangerously, jarls can threaten ward-withdrawal.
A settlement that defies its jarl — refuses a tax levy, shelters
an outlaw, speaks against the jarl at the Allthing — can have its
wards suspended. The galdr-worker does not come. The stones are
not maintained. The runes fade at their natural rate and inside
three years the perimeter is open. Nobody needs to swing a sword.
The Long Dark does the work.

When a settlement's wards fail completely, the consequences are
immediate and measurable. The first Long Dark without wards costs
livestock — the dead are drawn to animal heat and animal fear
before they turn to humans. The second Long Dark costs people.
Not the strong ones. The ones who go out to check the livestock.
The ones who step outside to relieve themselves at night. The
ones who hear a voice they recognize calling from beyond the salt
line and make the mistake of answering. A settlement that loses
its wards for three consecutive Long Darks either relocates,
submits to the nearest jarl on whatever terms are offered, or
ceases to exist. There are abandoned farmsteads throughout the
Rimevegr. Not all of them were abandoned by choice.

_The ward-stone economy is a protection racket with runes instead
of muscle. The jarl does not threaten violence. He threatens
absence — the absence of the one thing standing between his
people and the dark. The galdr-worker is the instrument, not the
agent. Ash understands this. Every galdr-worker understands this.
The question is not whether your skills will be used as leverage.
The question is whose leverage, and whether they will let you
sleep at night afterward. Ash sleeps poorly. He has for years.
The band assumes it is the galdr that costs him sleep. They are
partly right._

### Seiðr Services and the Price of Speaking with the Dead

A seiðr-wife charges 50 copper for a basic consultation. That is
a half-silver coin, two and a half days of a mercenary's retainer,
five days of food. It buys a trance, a reaching through the Veil,
and a conversation with someone who is dead. The price has been
stable at Skaldhaven for two generations. Nobody remembers who set
it. Nobody argues.

The fifty-copper rate covers a standard reaching — one dead
person, one question, one answer. The seiðr-wife sits. She
breathes. She goes still in a way that makes the living uneasy
because the stillness is not sleep and it is not wakefulness and
it is not anything else that living bodies do. She speaks, and the
voice that comes back is not hers. Sometimes the voice sounds like
the dead person. Sometimes it sounds like something imitating the
dead person. The seiðr-wife does not guarantee authenticity. She
guarantees contact.

The social cost sits on top of the silver cost. A man who consults
a seiðr-wife is a man who could not find his answer among the
living. In the Iron Grip, this is weakness. Ordovast's settlements
do not officially have seiðr-wives. They have healers, herb-
women, midwives — women who do the practical work of keeping
bodies alive without admitting that any of it involves reaching
through the Veil. A huscarl who visits a seiðr-wife in Iron Grip
territory does it at night, alone, and does not speak of it
afterward. The information he receives is usable but its source
is unspeakable.

In the Fjord Compact, the calculation is different. Sigrun permits
seiðr testimony at the ting (reference `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`
§11.8.2). A dead man's oath-testimony is admissible. This makes
seiðr consultation a legal service, not a spiritual one, and legal
services carry legal prices. Testimony-grade seiðr — a reaching
conducted in the presence of witnesses, with the seiðr-wife bound
by truth-oath before and after the trance — costs 100 copper.
Ten silver. That is a week's retainer for an entire mercenary
band of eight. The price reflects the risk. A seiðr-wife who lies
under truth-oath faces outlawry. A seiðr-wife who contacts the
wrong dead — or whose contact is contaminated by something on the
other side of the Veil — faces worse than outlawry. She faces the
certainty that next time she reaches through, whatever she touched
will reach back.

The Whispering Circle uses seiðr differently. The Pale Widow does
not bring her information to the ting. She uses it beforehand —
to know what the dead know about living men's secrets, to hear
what was said in rooms that no living witness will report on.
This is intelligence work, not legal work, and it has no standard
price because it is not officially for sale.

The underground market exists in every settlement large enough to
have a seiðr-wife. People want answers the ting cannot provide.
Was my husband faithful? Did my brother die well or badly? Where
did my father bury the silver he told no one about? These are
private questions, and private questions cost more because the
seiðr-wife is taking a social risk. A consultation discovered by
the wrong person — a priest, a jarl's man, a neighbor with
grudges — can destroy a seiðr-wife's standing. The underground
rate is 80-150 copper depending on the question and the risk.
Some seiðr-wives do this work exclusively. They have no public
practice. They are known by word-of-mouth and they move between
settlements the way Voss's band moves — never staying long enough
to be pinned down.

Dalla's position in the band is unusual and, from an economic
perspective, extraordinary. She is a competent seiðr-wife —
Spirit-lore trained, Wyrd-reading 2, WYR 3. In any settlement
she would command 50 copper per consultation and the social
deference that comes with being the person who talks to the dead.
In the band she is the healer. She sets broken bones. She mixes
wound-paste. She tells Voss when a man is too hurt to march and
argues with him when he disagrees, which is always. Her seiðr is
used when the band needs it — when Voss needs to know what a dead
jarl promised before he died, when a client's story does not match
and the dead man's version might differ. She is not paid extra
for this. She receives a standard share.

This makes Dalla an unpaid strategic asset worth, conservatively,
200 copper per week if she were selling her seiðr services at
market rate. She works for a share worth 20 copper. Voss knows
this. Dalla knows this. The arrangement holds because the band is
not a market. It is a family that pretends to be a business, and
in families the most valuable person is rarely the best
compensated.

_The price of speaking with the dead is not the silver. The silver
is what everyone counts and what no one remembers. The price is
the knowledge. The dead do not lie, but they do not tell you what
you want to hear, either. They tell you what happened. A wife
learns her husband died calling another woman's name. A jarl learns
his father murdered the man whose seat he inherited. A mother
learns her drowned son was alive for longer than she hoped,
trapped under the ice, scratching. Every seiðr-wife has seen
what truth does to the people who pay for it. Dalla carries a
catalogue of ruined faces — the expressions people make when
they hear the answer they asked for and realize they did not want
it. She does not charge the band. If she did, Voss could not
afford the rate. Not in silver. In what it costs Dalla to reach
through the Veil and come back carrying other people's grief._

### Rune-Marked Goods

The galdr economy runs on materials the way a forge runs on coal.
The runes are knowledge. The will is the galdr-worker's to spend.
But the physical substrates — the things into which the runes are
carved, the things that anchor the galdr to the material plane —
have to come from somewhere, and somewhere has a price.

The trade goods database (`data/economy/trade_goods.yaml`) tracks
five core seiðr-and-galdr trade materials:

**Rune-carved birch staves (TG_SDR_001, 20 copper).** The lowest-
tier prepared material. A birch branch, thumb-thick, stripped and
dried, with blank rune-positions pre-carved by an apprentice. The
buyer carves the actual rune. The stave provides the substrate.
These are the parchment of galdr — cheap, disposable, good for
rank 1 and rank 2 one-shot work. Trail-marks, temporary wards,
nightfire-keeping. Ash goes through four or five per week when the
band is on the road. He carves his own when time allows. When time
does not allow, he buys them at Skaldhaven for 20 copper each and
considers it robbery, because the wood costs nothing and the
apprentice who carved the blank positions is paid in food.

**Barrow dust (TG_SDR_004, 30 copper).** Soil taken from inside a
barrow. Not any barrow — a barrow where something walked and was
put back down. The dust carries a trace of Veil-contact that makes
it useful as an amplifier in seiðr work and as a binding-agent in
higher-rank galdr. The collection is dangerous. The people who
collect it — barrow-runners, mostly young men with more courage
than income — sell to middlemen in Skaldhaven who sell to galdr-
workers and seiðr-wives who do not ask where it came from. A
pouch of barrow dust weighs nothing and costs 30 copper because
the collection risk is priced in. One in ten barrow-runners does
not come back. The other nine split the dead man's share.

**Silver wire (TG_SDR_005, 25 copper per arm's-length).** Galdr-
grade silver wire, drawn at the Deepholm foundry. This is the
critical bottleneck. Rune-work at rank 3 and above requires silver
inlay — the rune is carved, and silver wire is pressed into the
grooves to hold the binding. Regular silver does not work. The
difference is in the smelting: Deepholm's crucibles are rune-
marked, the forging temperature is maintained by galdr-workers
who keep the furnace within tolerances that no untouched forge
can match, and the drawing dies have been in continuous use since
before the current master-smith's grandfather was born.

Deepholm's monopoly on galdr-grade silver wire is absolute and
uncontested. There is no alternative supply. During the Long Dark,
when roads close and caravans stop, galdr-workers pay double or
triple for stockpiled wire. A settlement that enters the Long Dark
without wire reserves cannot repair ward-stones until spring.
Deepholm knows this. Deepholm's pricing reflects this. The Fjord
Compact periodically pressures Deepholm to share the smelting
technique. Deepholm periodically declines. The monopoly is their
insurance policy — the reason no jarl can threaten a settlement
that controls the one thing every other settlement needs.

**Standing stone chips (TG_SDR_007, 40 copper).** Fragments from
pre-Cracking standing stones. Not quarried. Collected — chipped
from the bases of the old mono-liths, carefully, by specialists
who know which stones can be harvested and which cannot. The chips
carry resonance from the original carving. A ward carved on
standing stone chip substrate is measurably stronger than one
carved on fresh-quarried rock. The mechanism is unknown. The effect
is consistent. Galdr-workers pay 40 copper for a chip the size of
a man's thumb because that chip can be the difference between a
ward that holds for three years and one that holds for five.

The supply is finite. The standing stones are not being replaced.
Every chip taken weakens the stone it came from, and some of those
stones are themselves ward-anchors for settlements that have relied
on them for centuries. The tension between preservation and harvest
is a running argument at the Allthing. Skaldhaven maintains a
register of standing stones and their condition. The register is
incomplete, because not every jarl reports his stones, and
not every stone is in a jarl's territory.

**Veil-touched water (TG_SDR_008, 50 copper per flask).** Water
from springs that emerge near Veil-thinning sites. The water is
cold — colder than groundwater should be — and it does not freeze
at normal temperatures. It is used in seiðr work as a medium for
scrying and in galdr as a quenching liquid for silver wire that
has been rune-carved. The cost is not the water. The cost is the
collection. Veil-thinning sites are by definition places where the
barrier between the living and the dead is weak. Spending time
near them carries risk. The collectors — often the same barrow-
runners who gather dust — work in pairs, at midday, and never for
longer than it takes to fill the flasks and leave. Fifty copper
per flask is the price for water that might contain a trace of
whatever presses against the world from the other side. Nobody
discusses what that trace might do over time to the person who
drinks it. Nobody drinks it. But the water sits in pouches on
galdr-workers' belts, inches from their skin, day after day.

The material component market functions through a chain of
collection, transport, and resale that mirrors any other commodity
trade. Barrow-runners and spring-collectors sell to middlemen in
Skaldhaven and Deepholm. Middlemen add margins and sell to galdr-
workers and seiðr-wives. The margins are steep — a barrow-runner
sells dust for 15 copper and the middleman resells for 30 — but
the runners do not complain because the middlemen provide
something the runners cannot: anonymity. A man known to raid
barrows is a man the dead might follow home.

Skaldhaven's rune archive adds a dimension that no other
settlement can match. The archive — sealed, accessible only to
credentialed galdr-workers and by special petition to Sigrun —
contains rune-forms that the public tradition has lost. Templates
for rank 4 and rank 5 work. Combinations that the galdr-workers
who use birch staves on the road have never seen. Access to the
archive is not sold. It is granted, as political favor, and
withdrawn as political punishment. A galdr-worker who loses
archive access loses the ability to advance. Sigrun does not need
to threaten. She withholds.

_The material world of galdr is a supply chain with death at one
end and silver at the other. The barrow-runner dies collecting
dust. The galdr-worker dies slowly, spending health for rune-
power. The silver wire comes from a foundry that will never share
its process. The standing stones crumble a little more each
harvest. The springs near the Veil run colder each year. Nothing
in this chain is renewable. Nothing is getting easier to obtain.
The Rimevegr's magical economy is an inheritance being spent by
people who cannot earn more, and every ward-stone that fails and
every rune that fades is a withdrawal from a balance that shrinks
and never grows._

### Cursed Items and Forbidden Trade

A cursed item is a piece of material that carries galdr with
hostile intent. The rune-work is the same — same forms, same
substrates, same silver wire. The direction is inverted. Where a
ward says _not here_, a curse says _you specifically, yes you,
suffer_. The craft is identical. The law is not.

The most common cursed item in the Rimevegr is the niding-pole.
A wooden post, head-height, with a horse's skull mounted on top
and turned to face the target's homestead. Runes are carved down
the shaft. The carving takes a day, the activation requires the
carver's blood, and the result is a directed curse that draws
misfortune — sick livestock, spoiled grain, sleepless nights,
a wrongness in the house that everyone feels and nobody can
identify — toward the target until the pole is found and pulled
down or until the runes fade. Rank 2 niding-poles fade in weeks.
Rank 3 poles can last a season.

The legal status of niding-poles depends on the union (reference
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.3, curse arbitration). The Iron
Grip treats them as assault: the response is to find the carver
and apply violence until the carver discloses the counter-mark or
dies, whichever comes first. The Fjord Compact treats them as a
grievance claim — the curse may encode a legitimate complaint, and
the Compact's process requires investigation before punishment.
The Whispering Circle raises niding-poles as a matter of policy
and pretends otherwise.

Curse arbitration at the Allthing is one of the few issues that
produces shouting in an assembly that conducts most of its business
at speaking volume. A niding-pole raised against a jarl is an act
of war or an act of justice depending on who is speaking. The Iron
Grip wants them banned outright. The Fjord Compact wants case-by-
case adjudication. The Whispering Circle abstains from the vote
and smiles.

Curse-marked weapons are rarer and more dangerous. A blade that
has been used to kill and then rune-carved with binding-marks
carries blood-memory — a trace of the death it dealt, locked into
the metal by the galdr-work. The carrier of such a weapon feels
the deaths. Not vividly. Not like a vision. Like a weight. Like
holding something heavier than iron should be. Warriors who carry
curse-marked blades report poor sleep, irritability, and a
persistent impulse to unsheath the weapon that has nothing to do
with their own intentions.

These weapons sell. That is the uncomfortable fact. A blade that
has killed a named jarl and been rune-marked to remember the kill
is a status item. Not for decent people. For the kind of people
who buy weapons the way other men buy jewelry — to signal what
they are capable of, or what they aspire to. The market is small,
private, and concentrated in the same trading posts that move
barrow dust and veil-touched water. Prices vary wildly. A curse-
marked hand-axe — one kill, minor name — might sell for 100
copper. A sword that carries the memory of a jarl's death can
fetch 500 copper or more, paid by someone who will never use it
in combat but will hang it on a wall and tell the story to every
guest who notices the rune-work on the blade.

Items taken from barrows occupy a separate category. Barrow loot
is not inherently cursed, but it is inherently risky. A sword
pulled from a draugr's grip may be perfectly clean — good steel,
old make, worth its weight in reputation. Or it may carry a trace
of whatever the barrow held down. Or the draugr who held it may
remember the grip and follow the trail of the thing that was
taken. There is no way to know in advance. A galdr-worker can
examine barrow loot and assess the risk — is there residual Veil-
contact in the metal, does the rune-work on the blade seem active
or dormant — but the assessment is probability, not certainty.

This uncertainty drives the barrow-loot market in a direction that
defies common sense: premium prices despite high risk. A weapon
confirmed as barrow-origin sells for two to three times its
material value. A shield with pre-Cracking rune-work sells for
five to ten times. Buyers are paying for history, authenticity,
and the status of owning something that came out of the dark. The
risk is part of the appeal. A man who carries a barrow-sword is
advertising that he is the kind of man who buys things from places
where sane men do not go. Whether this is courage or stupidity
depends on whether the sword's previous owner stays in the ground.

The black market for seiðr components is smaller, quieter, and
more dangerous. The standard materials — barrow dust, veil-touched
water — are legal to trade. The restricted materials are not.
Human bone taken from a barrow where the dead were restless. Blood
drawn from a living person without consent. Hair from a drowned
corpse that has not been given funeral rites. These materials are
used in seiðr work that the Allthing's prohibition on "death-
seiðr" is specifically designed to prevent, and they circulate
anyway, because prohibition has never stopped a market that
serves a need.

The prices are set by risk, not rarity. Human bone from a restless
barrow is not hard to obtain — you dig, you take, you leave before
whatever is in there wakes up. The price, 100-200 copper depending
on provenance, reflects the legal consequence of being caught with
it. Outlawry. Not a fine. Not compensation. Outlawry — the
dissolution of every legal protection, every oath-bond, every
relationship that makes life in the Rimevegr survivable. A man
caught carrying death-seiðr components is a man who has bet his
entire social existence on not being caught.

The worst consequence of cursed items in circulation is not the
immediate damage. It is the contamination. A ward-stone carved
with silver wire that was, unknown to the galdr-worker, previously
used in a niding-pole. A grain-ward set with barrow dust from a
site that was more active than the middleman disclosed. The curse
does not announce itself. It sits inside the material like rot
in a beam — invisible until the weight comes and the beam snaps.
A ward that was supposed to protect becomes the vector for the
thing it was warding against. The grain-store rots from inside.
The livestock sicken. The family sleeps poorly and then does not
sleep at all and then one night the door is open and nobody
remembers opening it.

This is why Deepholm guards its supply chain with the rigidity of
a fortress. This is why Skaldhaven's archive access is restricted.
This is why galdr-workers of reputation buy materials only from
sources they have verified personally and charge their clients for
the verification. Trust in the material chain is the invisible
foundation of every ward in the Rimevegr. When that trust breaks,
the wards do not fail mechanically. They fail morally — doing the
opposite of what they were built to do, turning protection into
pathway, turning the safe place into the dangerous one.

_The cursed item trade is the shadow of the ward-stone trade.
Same materials. Same skills. Same supply chain. Different
direction. Every silver wire that becomes a blessing could have
become a weapon. Every barrow dust that strengthens a ward could
have weakened one. The market does not distinguish between
protection and predation. It sells to both. Ash knows this. He
buys his materials from a single contact in Deepholm — a woman
named Bryn who has sold clean wire for eleven years and has never
once supplied something that carried a trace. Eleven years of
trust purchased with eleven years of vigilance. If Bryn dies, Ash
loses more than a supplier. He loses the ability to guarantee that
the wards he carves are doing what he tells his clients they do._

### The Band's Magic Inventory

What a jarl buys when he hires Voss's Black Axes is not twelve
fighters. Any jarl can muster twelve fighters from his own
household. What a jarl buys is capability that his household
cannot replicate — tactical mobility, contract flexibility, the
willingness to do ugly work without political entanglement, and
magic. The magic is the part that closes the deal, and it is the
part that Voss does not price accurately, because Voss thinks in
swords and Voss undercounts what he carries.

**Ash — Rune-lore 3, WYR 3.** The band's primary galdr-worker.
Rank 3 capability means Ash can set annual wards, create structural
protections that hold through the Long Dark, and carve binding-
marks that will turn draugr away from a building or a camp
perimeter. In settlement terms, Ash alone is worth a jarl's
galdr-worker retainer — 150-300 copper per major ward, 30 copper
per consultation, plus the ongoing maintenance that keeps ward-
stones functional between the annual recarvings.

His kit is complete: carving knives, templates, blood-lancets,
chalk, binding cord, clean ash, and a reserve of Deepholm silver
wire that costs the band 50 copper per season to maintain. He
carries standing stone chips when the band can afford them.
Currently the band carries three chips, valued at 120 copper,
and one flask of veil-touched water at 50 copper. Total galdr
material inventory: roughly 220 copper in consumables, plus the
kit at 200 copper replacement value. Four hundred and twenty
copper strapped to one man's hip.

Ash's limitation is biological. Rank 3 work costs 2 Willpower
and 1 HP per use. In a week of active contract work — setting
wards, marking perimeters, carving protections for a client's
holdings — Ash can do major galdr work three times before he
needs rest. His recovery rate has slowed over the past year.
Cross-reference: MAGIC_TRUTH_003 in the mystery files. Ash burns
faster than he knows. His rune-work is drawing more than the
standard mechanical cost. The surplus is coming from somewhere
that is not on his character sheet, and when the account is
empty the deficit will come due all at once.

**Dalla — Spirit-lore (seiðr), Wyrd-reading 2, WYR 3.** The
band's healer and seiðr-wife. Dalla's seiðr capability gives the
band access to information that no amount of scouting can provide.
She can speak with the dead. She can read the shape of what is
coming. She can determine whether a barrow is active before the
band walks into it. In market terms, Dalla's consultation rate
would be 50 copper per session — standard Skaldhaven pricing —
and her testimony-grade seiðr would fetch 100 copper per session
under Fjord Compact law.

The band pays her a standard share: 20 copper per week. The
disparity between her market value and her compensation is the
single largest unpriced asset on the band's books. Voss knows.
Dalla knows. Neither discusses it, because the discussion would
require admitting that Dalla stays with the band for reasons that
have nothing to do with money, and those reasons are the kind
that make a mercenary commander uncomfortable, because they
cannot be renegotiated when the contract terms change.

Dalla's healing work is her visible function. Her seiðr is her
invisible one. The band uses it perhaps once per contract — one
reaching, one conversation with a dead man or a reading of what
the wyrd shows ahead. In between, Dalla sets splints and mixes
wound-paste and argues with Voss about march pace when someone
is injured. The band sees the healer. The clients see the
seiðr-wife. Voss sees both and prices only the healer, which is
why the band's contract rate has been too low for the past three
years.

**Petra — Wyrd-reading 1, WYR 2.** The band's bone-lot caster.
Her capability is minimal and unreliable. Rank 1 wyrd-reading is
a coin flip dressed in ritual — she shakes the lots, she reads
the fall, and the reading is right slightly more often than chance
would predict. Slightly. Not reliably enough to base a tactical
decision on, but enough that Voss listens when Petra says "bad
road" and adjusts the march route.

Petra's market value as a wyrd-reader is negligible — a few
copper per reading, if anyone would pay. Her value to the band
is not economic. It is psychological. A band that has someone
reading the lots before a march feels prepared. The preparation
may be illusory. The feeling is real. Morale matters more than
accuracy when you are three days from the nearest settlement and
the nights are getting longer.

**Thorne — WYR 1, rune craft life skill.** Thorne carves runes on
tent-poles and checks his rune-amulet when danger is near. He is
not a galdr-worker. His WYR 1 means he has a knack — an instinct
that might be magical sensitivity or might be a veteran's hard-
earned paranoia. His rune-carving is cosmetic, not functional. The
marks he cuts do not activate. They are habit, or prayer, or
something between the two that Thorne himself cannot distinguish.

His value as a magical asset is zero copper. His value as a
magical indicator is harder to price. Thorne's unease — the
specific way he goes quiet, the way his hand moves to the amulet
— is a signal the band has learned to read. When Thorne is
uncomfortable, the band is more cautious. Whether this is because
Thorne is sensing something real or because the band has
superstitiously linked Thorne's mood to danger is a question
nobody asks. The correlation is good enough. On the Rimevegr,
good enough keeps people alive.

**Combined assessment.** If a jarl inventoried the Black Axes'
magical capability the way he would inventory a weapons stockpile,
the assessment would read: one rank 3 galdr-worker with
deteriorating recovery, one seiðr-wife with testimony-grade
capability being compensated at healer rates, one wyrd-reader of
negligible reliability, one rune-sensitive non-practitioner, and
approximately 420 copper in galdr materials. Total magical asset
value: 500-800 copper per contract period, depending on how many
times Ash and Dalla are used.

This makes the band significantly more capable than a standard
mercenary company. Most bands carry no magic at all. A company
with a rank 2 galdr-worker is considered well-equipped. A company
with both a galdr-worker and a seiðr-wife is rare enough that
clients notice and contracts improve. Voss's band should be
commanding premium rates. They are not, because Voss prices by
blade count and Voss prices by his own understanding of what the
band does, and Voss's understanding stops at the sword.

The problem is that the band's magic is a wasting asset. Ash is
burning faster than the mechanical tables predict. Dalla's seiðr
work takes a toll that does not appear on any stat block — the
accumulation of other people's dead, the weight of conversations
that no living person should carry, the slow erosion of the
boundary between her own thoughts and the things she hears through
the Veil. Petra's readings grow no more reliable. Thorne's knack
does not develop. The band's magical capability peaked sometime in
the last two years and is now declining at a rate that none of its
members have measured and none of them want to face.

A jarl who hires the Black Axes today is hiring a band at the top
of a curve that is already bending downward. The wards Ash carves
this season will be weaker than the wards he carved last season.
The reachings Dalla performs this year will leave her hollowed out
for longer than they did last year. The bone lots Petra throws
will tell her nothing she does not already suspect. And Voss will
set the contract price at the same rate he set it two years ago,
because Voss does not count magic as an asset and therefore does
not notice when the asset depreciates.

_The band carries magic the way a rich man carries inherited coin —
spending freely because the balance looks large, never noticing
that the income stopped and the withdrawals continue. Ash is the
coin. Dalla is the coin. They spend themselves and the balance
drops and nobody is earning more. A galdr-worker's recovery does
not improve with age. A seiðr-wife's tolerance for the dead does
not grow. These are depreciating assets in human form, and the
band prices them at a standard share — twenty copper a week, the
same rate they pay the man who carries the supply packs. The gap
between what the magic is worth and what the band charges for it
is the gap between what Voss understands and what Voss refuses to
count. When the gap closes — when Ash cannot carve and Dalla
cannot reach and the band is twelve swords with no magic at all —
the contract rate will not change. The clients will. They will
stop coming. And Voss will stand in an empty camp wondering what
changed, because from where Voss stands, nothing changed. His
people just stopped being worth what they used to be, and he never
knew what that was._

## The Practitioner's Lifecycle

Every practitioner has the same story. The details change — runes or
spirits or bone lots, Skaldhaven or Icebreak or some unnamed
settlement on the ice — but the arc does not. Something wakes in
childhood. Someone notices. Training begins, and with it the cost.
The working years burn through body and mind at a rate that only
becomes visible in retrospect. Decline arrives earlier than anyone
expected. Death comes in forms specific to the tradition but
universal in their inevitability.

The mechanical framework is in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`. The stat
blocks are in `22_MEMBER_STATBLOCKS.md`. This section covers what
neither of those documents can hold: the lived shape of a
practitioner's life from the first sign through the last breath.
What it looks like from the inside. What it costs in years, in
relationships, in the slow accumulation of damage that no healer can
reverse because the damage is the practice.

Magic in the Rimevegr is not a gift. It is a condition. And like all
conditions, it has a progression.

### Manifestation — How Magic Finds Its Practitioners

Nobody chooses to be a practitioner. The capacity arrives uninvited,
manifests without permission, and cannot be returned. It shows up in
childhood, during the years when the body is still forming and the
mind has not yet learned to filter out what it should not perceive.
The traditions differ in how the talent presents, but they share one
truth: it always looks wrong before it looks right.

**Galdr manifestation** typically surfaces between ages eight and
twelve. The child hears something. Not voices — galdr-workers are
careful to distinguish this from what seiðr-wives experience. The
runes hum. A piece of carved wood on a doorframe makes a sound that
nobody else hears, and the child cannot stop listening. They trace
shapes on surfaces — the dirt floor of a longhouse, the bark of a
birch, the flat of their own thigh — without knowing the shapes have
names. To their parents, it looks like restlessness. A child who
cannot sit still, who scratches and scrapes at everything, who stares
at carved posts the way other children stare at fire.

Not all strange children become galdr-workers. Many children in the
Rimevegr are strange — the Long Dark does that, the isolation does
that, the cold and the hunger and the dark do that. But every
galdr-worker was a strange child. Ash was scratching rune-shapes into
his sleeping bench at age nine. He did not know what they were. His
mother thought he was carving his name. He was not carving his name.
He was carving Isa, the ice-rune, over and over, because he could
hear it in the wall and the sound would not stop until the shape was
right.

Some families recognize this. In settlements with a working
galdr-worker — someone who carves wards and charges for the service —
a parent might bring the child forward. "My boy scratches at things.
Is it the talent or is he bored?" The galdr-worker takes one look at
the scratches and knows. The shapes are too precise for boredom. A
bored child carves lines. A galdr-child carves runes they have never
been taught.

In settlements without a galdr-worker — and there are more of these
every decade — the talent goes unrecognized. The child grows up,
the humming fades to background noise, the urge to carve becomes a
habit that nobody connects to anything larger. Another potential
practitioner lost. Not to death, not to violence, but to the simple
fact that nobody was there to say: "That sound you hear is your
life's work."

**Seiðr manifestation** arrives earlier, between ages five and ten,
and it arrives louder. The child does not hear shapes. The child
sees things. Talks to things. Stares at empty corners and describes
what is there to adults who see nothing.

In a settlement that respects the seiðr tradition — one with a
working seiðr-wife, one where the women who keep the old knowledge
have not been driven out or frightened into silence — this is
recognized. "The girl talks to the air." The seiðr-wife visits.
Watches the child. Asks questions. "What does the air look like
when it talks back?" If the answers are right — if the child
describes shapes and colors and temperatures that match what the
seiðr-wife herself perceives in trance — the talent is confirmed.
The child is not mad. The child is seeing through the Veil without
the training that is supposed to come first.

In a settlement that fears seiðr — and by the late Rimevegr period,
that is most settlements — the child is punished. Stop staring at
nothing. Stop talking to the wall. Stop frightening your sister.
The punishment works in the sense that the behavior becomes less
visible. The child learns not to speak about what it sees. It does
not learn not to see it. The talent does not stop because someone
hit the child. It hides. It goes underground. It surfaces at night,
in sleep, in moments of crisis when the conscious mind loses its
grip on the filters it has built.

A punished seiðr-child grows into an adult who sees the dead and
tells nobody. This is more common than anyone in the Rimevegr
would admit.

**Wyrd-reading manifestation** is the most common and the least
dramatic. It does not announce itself. It seeps in. The child has a
sense for weather before it turns. They know who is coming to the
door before the knock. They pick up the bone lots at a festival and
read them correctly on the first throw — not perfectly, but close
enough that the old woman running the lot-game gives them a second
look.

This is the talent that passes most easily as mundane wisdom. A
child who reads weather well is a useful child, not a strange one.
A child who guesses visitors is observant, not magical. Only the
bone lots raise questions, because the lots are supposed to require
training, and when a ten-year-old reads them correctly three times
running, the adults in the room start exchanging glances.

Petra did not know she was a wyrd-reader until she was fourteen. She
thought she was good at guessing. The shepherd who taught her —
a man named Torben who read weather and fate from the same set of
signs — was the one who told her the guessing had a name. "You
are not guessing," he said. "You are reading. The world is telling
you things and you are listening without knowing it." He gave her
a set of carved elk-bone lots and told her to practice until the
reading was deliberate instead of accidental. She practiced. The
reading became deliberate. The guessing stopped being guessing.

**How many never manifest at all.** This is the number nobody knows
and everybody should. For every Ash who was recognized, there are
five children whose galdr-talent was beaten out of them by
exhaustion, labor, suspicion, or the simple absence of anyone who
could name what was happening. For every Dalla who found a teacher,
there are ten seiðr-children who grew up believing they were
broken rather than gifted. The Long Dark kills the weak. It also
kills the talented, because talent that is not trained is talent
that eats its host.

The Rimevegr's magic is not scarce because the world ran out. It is
scarce because the world keeps killing the supply before it matures.
Every settlement that punishes a seiðr-child, every family that
ignores a galdr-child's scratching, every community that lets its
wyrd-reader die without passing on the craft — they are all burning
seed corn and calling it discipline.

_The cruelest part is that the children who manifest are already
paying the cost. The humming that Ash heard at nine was not free.
The shapes the unnamed seiðr-children see through the Veil are not
free. The talent is a door that opens from the other side, and the
draft comes through whether or not anyone teaches the child how to
close it. An untrained galdr-child hears the runes until the hearing
becomes tinnitus. An untrained seiðr-child sees the dead until
seeing them becomes indistinguishable from madness. The training
does not create the cost. The training teaches the practitioner to
survive it._

### Training — The Apprentice Years

The gap between manifestation and practice is filled with years of
work that look nothing like the magic they produce. Training in the
Rimevegr is not a school. It is not a curriculum. It is one person
breaking another person's habits and replacing them with discipline,
and the breaking hurts.

**Galdr training** is the most formal of the three traditions. A
galdr-apprentice is bound to a working galdr-worker for a minimum
of two years, often three. During that time, the apprentice does
not carve runes. The apprentice learns to hear them.

The first year is listening. The master carves. The student watches.
Not the hands — the experienced masters insist on this — but the
wood. Watch the wood. Listen to the grain. A rune carved against the
grain splits the mark and splits the power. A rune carved into a
knot concentrates force where force should not concentrate. The
apprentice sits for hours beside a carving bench, watching chisel
meet wood, learning to read the material the way a butcher reads a
carcass — along the grain, counting the flaws, knowing where the
resistance lives.

The first mark comes at the end of the first year, or early in the
second. The apprentice picks up the chisel. Their hands are shaking.
Not from cold — from the humming, which has grown louder during the
training because the training has opened what little filter the child
had built. The first mark is Isa. Always Isa. The simplest rune, the
straightest line, the vibration that a first-year apprentice can hold
without the hold breaking them.

The chisel cuts into the wood. Blood follows. Always blood, from the
first mark onward. Not ritual blood. Incidental blood. The chisel
slips because the rune pulls, because the vibration in the hand
interacts with the vibration in the wood, and the body pays for the
interference. White scar-lines appear on the apprentice's hands
within the first month of carving practice. Thin lines, like thread
laid under the skin. By rank 1 — which comes at the end of the
second or third year — the hands are mapped with them. Ash's hands
are "scarred with white lines no blade made" (see
`22_MEMBER_STATBLOCKS.md`). Those scars began before he was twelve.

The physical cost starts immediately and never stops accumulating.
A rank-1 galdr-worker has scarred hands. A rank-2 worker has scarred
hands and forearms, and the scars have begun to branch, following the
paths of the nerves. By rank 3, the scarring extends to the
shoulders, and the hands have a faint, persistent tremor at rest that
calms only when holding a chisel. The body learns to associate the
tool with the work, and the tremor is the body shaking off the
excess vibration that builds when the rune-channel is open but not
in use. It is not a disease. It is a side effect of being a
conduit. See MAGIC_TRUTH_003 in the encoded truth system for the
full cost accounting.

Higher ranks take longer and cost more. Rank 2 requires an
additional two to three years of practice beyond rank 1. Rank 3
requires three to five years beyond rank 2, and by this point the
apprenticeship is over — the practitioner is working independently,
earning their keep, and the learning is self-directed. Ash reached
rank 3 in his mid-twenties. He is thirty-two now and has not
progressed. The cost curve steepens with each rank, and rank 4
demands a physical investment that rank 3 has already begun to
exhaust.

Regional training centers shape the tradition. Skaldhaven maintains
the largest rune archive in the Rimevegr — scrolls and carved
tablets recording galdr techniques across generations. A
Skaldhaven-trained galdr-worker learns from records as well as from
a master. They tend to be more theoretical, more precise, and more
fragile — because theory without sufficient physical conditioning
produces brittle practitioners. Deepholm takes the opposite
approach: a formal workshop model where apprentices carve
repetitively under supervision, building hand-strength and endurance
before attempting complex marks. Deepholm-trained workers are
physically harder but sometimes lack the theoretical background to
innovate. Most working galdr-workers were trained by neither center.
Most were trained by a single practitioner in a settlement
somewhere, with whatever materials and methods that practitioner
had inherited.

**Seiðr training** is the opposite of galdr in almost every
structural respect. There is no formal program. There is no
workshop. There is a woman — almost always a woman, though the
tradition does not formally exclude men — who recognizes the talent
in a child and says: "Come. I will teach you what I know."

The relationship between a seiðr-wife and her student is closer to
a family bond than a professional apprenticeship. The student moves
into the teacher's home, or the teacher moves into the student's.
They live together. Eat together. The teaching happens in the gaps
between daily work — while preparing herbs, while cleaning, while
walking between settlements. It is oral, personal, and
unreproducible. When a seiðr-wife dies without a student, everything
she knew dies with her. There is no archive. There is no scroll.
There is the woman, and then there is the silence.

The training itself is trance-work first, everything else second.
The student learns to reach through the Veil — not far, not deep,
but enough to feel what is on the other side. The first sessions
are terrifying. The student closes their eyes, the teacher chants,
the herbs burn, and the student's awareness tilts sideways into a
space that does not correspond to anything the waking mind can
process. Shapes that are not shapes. Voices that are not voices.
The dead are there, and they are interested in the newcomer, the
way wolves are interested in a lamb that has wandered into the
tree line.

The teacher is the anchor. The student reaches and the teacher
holds the line and pulls back if the reaching goes too far. Many
students break during the first year. "Break" is a precise word.
The trance opens something in the mind that was not open before,
and some minds are not built to hold the opening. The student comes
back from the trance and cannot stop crying. Or cannot sleep. Or
cannot stop talking to things that are not there — not in the
controlled, deliberate way the training intends, but in the raw,
unfiltered way of a mind that has seen too much and cannot close
the curtain.

The students who break are not abandoned. The teacher pulls them
out, grounds them, helps them close what was opened. Sometimes it
works. Sometimes it does not, and the student lives the rest of
their life half-in-trance, seeing the dead at the edges of their
vision, never quite present, never quite gone. This is one of the
Rimevegr's open secrets: in every large settlement, there is
someone like this. A woman who stares at walls. A man who flinches
at nothing. They were seiðr students who broke, and nobody tells
their story because the story is not useful.

The students who do not break continue. They learn herb-craft —
which plants dull pain, which plants open the trance, which plants
close it. They learn spirit-communion protocols — how to address
the dead, how to set boundaries, how to dismiss a spirit that will
not leave. They learn the geography of the Veil: where it is thin
(old battlefields, barrow-fields, places where many people died),
where it is thick (running water, new-growth forest, places of
active human habitation), and how the thickness changes with the
seasons. The Long Dark thins the Veil. Midsummer thickens it.

At Icebreak, the hermit Ragnhild teaches a stripped-down version of
seiðr that emphasizes survival over skill. Ragnhild's students learn
to reach quickly, gather information, and pull back before the trance
does damage. They are not elegant practitioners. They are fast,
practical ones. Ragnhild herself is the example: she has been
practicing for forty years and she is still alive, which in seiðr
terms counts as an extraordinary achievement.

Emotional resilience is the foundation. Everything else — the
herb-craft, the trance technique, the spirit protocols — rests on
the student's ability to experience something horrifying and continue
to function. The teacher builds this the only way it can be built:
by exposure. Small horrors first, then larger ones. The student sees
a dead child in the trance and the teacher says: "Yes. Now what will
you do?" The student who can answer that question, session after
session, year after year, becomes the next seiðr-wife. The student
who cannot answer it was never going to survive the practice.

**Wyrd-reading training** is the least structured and the most
self-directed. There is no formal apprenticeship. There are no
regional centers. There is a person who reads the signs — bone lots,
cloud patterns, bird-flight, the configuration of spilled ash —
and a person who wants to learn how. The teaching happens wherever
the teacher is, and it looks like conversation.

"What do you see?" The teacher throws the lots. The student
reads them. The teacher corrects. Not the interpretation — the
observation. "You saw the elk-bone crossing the wolf-bone. You
read that as conflict. Look again. The elk-bone is above the
wolf-bone. That is not conflict. That is survival. Conflict is
side by side. Survival is one above the other."

The training is practice. Throw the lots. Interpret. Check against
reality. Adjust. Throw again. Over months and years, the student
builds a grammar of signs that corresponds to a grammar of
outcomes. The grammar is personal — no two wyrd-readers use
exactly the same system — but the foundations overlap. Certain
configurations recur. Certain patterns mean death regardless of the
reader's personal lexicon.

Petra learned from Torben, the shepherd. He read weather and fate
from the same set of signs, because to a shepherd on the ice, they
are the same thing. A storm that arrives when the flock is two days
from shelter is not a weather event. It is fate. Torben taught Petra
to read the sky and the lots together, to cross-reference wind
against bone, to see the pattern where others saw noise. He did not
frame this as magic. He framed it as paying attention. "The world
tells you everything," he said. "Most people cannot be bothered to
listen."

The cost of wyrd-reading training is not physical. It is
psychological. The student who learns to read the lots also learns
that the lots do not lie. When the bones say a man will die this
winter, the man dies this winter. The student must learn to read
that outcome and continue functioning — to throw the lots for
the next person, knowing that the previous person's fate is
already sealed. The emotional numbness that veteran wyrd-readers
develop is not a personality flaw. It is an adaptation. A
wyrd-reader who feels every reading will stop reading within five
years. The practice selects for detachment.

_Training in every tradition is the same transaction dressed in
different clothes. The student gives the teacher their time, their
labor, their trust, and their intact body and mind. The teacher
gives the student a skill that will eat them alive. The kindest
teachers are honest about this. Ragnhild at Icebreak tells her
students on the first day: "This will kill you eventually. The
question is whether you will do enough good before it does." Most
teachers are not that kind. Most teachers were not told either._

### Active Practice — The Working Years

The working years are the years that count. Everything before —
manifestation, training, the slow acquisition of skill — exists to
produce a practitioner who can do the work. The work is seasonal and
relentless and it grinds through human bodies the way winter grinds
through thatch.

**The galdr-worker's round** follows the year the way a farmer
follows the year, but the crop is not grain. The crop is marks
on wood and stone and iron, and the harvest is measured in wards
that hold and bindings that do not break.

Spring is maintenance. The Long Dark is hard on ward-work. Cold
contracts wood. Moisture seeps into carved marks and freezes,
splitting delicate rune-channels. A galdr-worker walks their
settlement's perimeter in spring the way a herdsman counts stock
after winter — expecting losses, cataloguing damage, assessing
what can be repaired and what must be recarved from scratch. In a
well-kept settlement, ten to fifteen percent of the ward markers
need recarving after winter. In a poorly kept one — where the
ward-worker is overextended or underequipped — the number is
higher. Thirty percent damage is the point where the ward network
has functional gaps. Things come through gaps.

Summer is new construction. Building season means new longhouses,
new storehouses, new animal pens, and every structure needs ward
markers. A skilled settlement galdr-worker carves forty to sixty
new ward markers in a summer. Each one takes half a day to a full
day depending on complexity. The work is physically brutal — hours
of focused carving in a state of partial rune-contact that drains
willpower and steadiness. By the end of a full summer season, the
galdr-worker has given eight to ten weeks of concentrated
rune-work, and the body shows it. Weight loss. Exhaustion.
Tremor increase in the rest hand. Two weeks of recovery before
autumn, and autumn is not restful.

Autumn is emergency work and preparation. The Veil begins thinning
as the days shorten. The dead become more active. Ward networks that
survived spring and summer in adequate condition begin to strain
under increased pressure. The galdr-worker patches, reinforces,
and prays — an ironic act for a tradition that accepts the gods
are gone, but the word persists even when the faith does not. Last
preparations before the Long Dark: stockpiling carving materials,
pre-cutting wood to the right grain, setting aside the reserve
supply of iron chisel-heads because a chisel that breaks during
the Long Dark cannot be replaced until spring.

Winter is the vigil. The Long Dark presses on the wards, and the
galdr-worker maintains them through months of dark and cold and
the relentless hum of dead things pressing against marks that are
slowly weakening. Sleep becomes difficult. The runes are louder
in winter — the same thinning of the Veil that empowers the dead
also amplifies the rune-channels, and the galdr-worker hears them
through the walls at three in the morning when everyone else is
asleep. Ash describes winter as "the season when the work does not
stop and the body does not recover." He sleeps in fragments. He
eats when reminded. He carves, and checks, and carves again, and
the winter is dark and the wards are bright and neither of those
facts makes the other easier.

In a mercenary band, the cycle compresses. Ash does not have the
luxury of a settled galdr-worker's year. The band moves. Every
relocation means new camp wards — a full day's work to raise a
temporary perimeter that will last weeks, not seasons. Trail
marking for scouts requires carved path-wards that degrade within
days. Contract requirements — the specific magical services a jarl
is paying for — demand concentrated, high-quality work on a client's
schedule, not the practitioner's. Ash's annual output is lower than
a settlement galdr-worker's, but his work is more varied and more
dangerous because he has no backup, no institutional support, and no
rest season. See `22_MEMBER_STATBLOCKS.md` for Ash's current
capacity and its limitations.

**The seiðr-wife's round** is less seasonal and more responsive.
Healing happens year-round — broken bones do not wait for spring,
fever does not respect the calendar. A seiðr-wife in a settlement
handles three to five significant consultations per week in the
active months, and the number increases during the Long Dark because
illness and injury increase when the cold deepens.

Spirit consultations are seasonal by nature. The Veil thins in the
Long Dark, which makes reaching easier — the spirits are closer,
the trance is shallower, the information flows faster. A
seiðr-wife who attempts a deep consultation in midsummer may spend
hours in trance trying to reach a spirit that would come in
minutes during winter. Smart practitioners schedule their spirit
work accordingly. Clients who need to speak to the dead learn to
wait for winter, or to pay the premium that a summer consultation
costs in extra recovery time.

Ting season brings testimony work. A seiðr-wife's word carries
evidentiary weight — she can speak to the dead, confirm alibis,
verify inheritance claims through spirit consultation. This is
not comfortable work. The spirits she contacts for ting purposes
are not always cooperative, and the questions she asks are not
always questions the spirits want to answer. A murder victim's
spirit may want justice. It may also want revenge, and
distinguishing the two inside a trance while a lawspeaker waits
for an answer is one of the hardest things a seiðr-wife does.

Dalla's practice in the band is a compressed version of the
settlement round. She heals injuries after combat — not with
magic alone, but with herb-craft supplemented by seiðr insight.
She reads the wounded body through the Veil, seeing where the
damage lies beneath the skin the way a sighted person sees
what a blind person cannot. The information is valuable. The
trance-cost of extracting it is not trivial. Dalla looks worse
after healing three wounded men than the wounded men look
themselves. She does not talk about this. The men do not ask.

**The wyrd-reader's round** is daily rather than seasonal. A
competent wyrd-reader casts the lots every morning. Not for
herself — for the group. "What does the day hold? What should
we avoid? Which direction is safer?" In a settlement, this is a
public service, performed in the meeting hall or on the commons,
available to anyone who brings a question and a small payment —
food, usually, or labor, or the occasional copper piece.

Pre-battle readings are the highest-stakes work a wyrd-reader does.
The band is about to fight. Voss wants to know the outcome. Petra
throws the lots and reads what is there, and what is there is not
always what Voss wants to hear. A wyrd-reader who softens the
reading for the commander's comfort is a wyrd-reader who gets
people killed. A wyrd-reader who delivers the reading raw —
"The lots say three of your twelve will die and two more will take
wounds that do not heal" — is a wyrd-reader who gets blamed for
the deaths. Petra learned early to frame readings in choices rather
than outcomes: "The lots say the west approach is death. The north
is blood but survival." This is not dishonesty. It is translation.
The lots do not care about morale. Petra does.

Ting-season divination fills the rest of her professional calendar.
Legal disputes, inheritance questions, commercial decisions — all
benefit from a reading, and a well-known wyrd-reader can earn
enough in ting season to carry her through the quiet months. Petra
does not yet have that reputation. She is young and her readings
lack the weight of decades. This will change. Whether she wants
it to is another question.

**The physical toll during active years** accelerates with
practice. This is the mechanism that `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`
models abstractly and this section makes concrete.

Galdr scarring accelerates through the working years. The white
scar-lines deepen from threads to cords. The branching pattern
extends from hands and forearms to shoulders and, in advanced
cases, the upper back and neck. The scarring follows the nerve
pathways — it is not random damage but a mapping of the channels
that rune-force uses when it passes through the body. By age
thirty-five, a rank-3 galdr-worker's hands look sixty. By forty,
they look older than that. Ash is thirty-two and his hands are
the oldest part of him.

Seiðr practitioners show premature aging that presents differently.
Hair loss begins in the late twenties for active practitioners —
patchy at first, then general. Dalla is twenty-eight and already
wearing her hair shorter than she used to, because the thinning
is visible and she does not want people counting the cost in her
appearance. Joint problems follow — the hips and knees first, then
the spine. Trance-work involves physical stillness for extended
periods, and the body that holds the trance also holds the
position, and the damage accumulates. Sleep becomes uneven.
The seiðr-wife sleeps when the dead are quiet, which means she
sleeps at unpredictable hours and wakes when no one else does.
The body's rhythms follow the practice, not the sun.

Wyrd-readers develop obsessive patterns and anxiety. The lots
speak constantly — not literally, but the reader cannot stop
reading. Every arrangement of objects becomes a potential sign.
Spilled grain. Dropped tools. The way a flock of birds changes
direction. A veteran wyrd-reader processes the world as a
continuous stream of potential information, and the processing
does not stop. Petra is not yet deeply into this phase, but she
already catches herself reading the arrangement of pebbles on a
riverbank and has to force herself to stop. The anxiety comes
from the gap between what the signs suggest and what the reader
can do about it. Knowing something bad is coming and being
unable to prevent it is the working condition of every
wyrd-reader who has practiced for more than a decade.

**Social position** during the working years is the same across
all three traditions: respected and isolated. The practitioner eats
with the group but is never fully of the group. Dalla heals the
men and they thank her and sit somewhere else. Ash wards the camp
and they sleep soundly and forget who made that possible. Petra
reads the lots and they follow her advice and attribute the
outcome to their own good judgment.

This is not ingratitude. It is proximity aversion. The practitioners
handle forces that the non-practitioners cannot see, cannot measure,
and cannot verify except by the results. A man who watches Ash carve
a ward sees a person cutting marks into wood. He does not see what
the marks do, does not feel the vibration, does not hear the hum.
He knows the ward works because the dead stay outside, but the
connection between the carving and the protection is invisible to
him. Invisible work is easy to forget. Easy to undervalue. Easy to
sit away from, because something about the person who does it makes
the non-practitioner's skin crawl, and the non-practitioner does not
know why.

The exception is ting season, when practitioners are publicly
visible and publicly useful. For a few weeks, the galdr-worker is
the person who wards the Allthing stones. The seiðr-wife is the
person who provides spirit testimony. The wyrd-reader is the person
who predicts the harvest. They are central to communal life, and the
community treats them accordingly — with respect that lasts until
ting season ends and the practitioners return to their normal
position on the edge of things.

_The working years are the years the practitioner lives for and dies
from. The work is the purpose, the identity, the thing that justifies
the cost. A galdr-worker who is not carving is restless. A seiðr-wife
who is not reaching is incomplete. A wyrd-reader who is not reading
is anxious. The practice is the practitioner, and the practitioner is
the practice, and the distinction between self and skill dissolves
somewhere around the fifth year of active work and never re-forms.
This is not metaphor. This is the mechanism by which the cost
accumulates. The body keeps the score, and the score is the life._

### Decline — When the Body Stops Keeping Up

Nobody announces the beginning of decline. It arrives the way
winter arrives — not on the day the first snow falls, but on
the day you realize the last snow never fully melted. By the time
a practitioner recognizes the decline, the decline has been
underway for years.

**Galdr decline** starts in the hands and works inward. The
tremor that was a background hum during the working years becomes
a foreground problem. A galdr-worker who cannot hold a steady
chisel cannot carve a clean rune. An unclean rune is a rune
that fails under stress. A rune that fails under stress is a
ward that breaks when the dead push against it, and that is not
an abstract problem — that is a hole in the perimeter through
which something comes.

The progression is measurable. Tremor intensity increases at a
rate of roughly five to eight percent per year after age forty for
a rank-3 practitioner. This means that the quality gap between a
forty-year-old and a forty-five-year-old is significant. A
forty-year-old rank-3 galdr-worker produces wards at rank-3
quality with moderate effort. A forty-five-year-old produces the
same wards at rank-2-plus quality with extreme effort, and the
effort itself accelerates the tremor, creating a feedback loop
that cannot be broken without stopping work entirely.

Most galdr-workers lose a full rank of effective output by age
fifty. Not a formal rank — the knowledge does not leave. A
fifty-year-old galdr-worker with a lifetime of rank-3 practice
knows everything a rank-3 worker should know. But the hands
cannot execute what the mind holds. The chisel wanders. The
mark is imprecise. The rune activates at rank-2 potency because
the carving carries rank-2 precision, and precision is physical
and physical precision declines with age and accumulated damage.

The exceptional ones hold longer. Audun at Deepholm remains an
effective rank-4 worker at fifty-three, but Audun is the
exception that galdr-workers point to for comfort and that
honest observers point to for scale. If the best galdr-worker
in the Rimevegr starts declining at fifty-three, the ordinary
worker starts at forty-five. "Longer" means fifty-five, not
seventy. The idea of a galdr-worker practicing at full rank in
old age is a story from before the Cracking, when divine power
supplemented the physical cost, and telling that story now is
cruelty dressed as hope.

Ash is thirty-two. He is already showing rank-3 strain — not
decline, not yet, but the leading indicators. The tremor at rest
is slightly worse this year than last year. Recovery time after
a major carving session is half a day longer than it was two
years ago. These are not problems. They are previews of
problems. Ash will not be a rank-3 worker at forty-five unless
something changes. Nothing will change. The gods are not coming
back. The rune-channels are not refilling. The body is not
going to reverse what three centuries of orphaned magic have
set in motion. Ash's best years are the years he is living
right now, and neither he nor Voss has the honesty to say it
aloud.

**Seiðr decline** starts in the mind and stays there. The hands
remain steady — seiðr does not require the physical precision
that galdr demands. What frays is the boundary between the
practitioner and the Veil, and the fraying is not a disorder.
It is a consequence. Push through a membrane often enough and
the membrane thins.

The dead become harder to dismiss after the trance ends. At first,
it is a lingering presence — the sense that the spirit contacted
during the session is still nearby, still watching, for minutes
or hours after the trance closes. Over years, minutes become
hours, hours become days, and the practitioner lives in a state
of permanent partial contact with the dead. Voices linger.
Not auditory hallucinations — no seiðr-wife calls them that,
because they are not hallucinations. The dead are speaking. The
fact that the trance is over does not mean the dead have
stopped.

Boundaries between self and spirit blur. The oldest seiðr-wives
become the strangest people in any settlement. They talk to the
air, answering questions nobody in the room has asked — because
the questions came from someone not in the room, someone not in
the living world at all. They sleep at odd hours because the dead
are quieter at midday and louder at midnight, and the seiðr-wife
has learned to sleep when the noise drops and wake when it rises.
They forget names — living names. They know the dead by name but
call their own grandchildren "the girl" because the living have
become less vivid than the dead, and the dead do not change.

This is not madness. The distinction matters. Madness is the
mind failing. Seiðr decline is the mind succeeding in an
environment it was never meant to sustain. The seiðr-wife sees
accurately. She perceives correctly. She processes what others
cannot process, and the processing wears grooves in her attention
that the living cannot fill. She is not broken. She is eroded.
The river is real. The fact that the river carved a canyon does
not make the canyon a flaw in the rock.

Ragnhild at Icebreak, at sixty-two, is the Rimevegr's best
case study in controlled seiðr decline. She maintains boundaries
through brutal discipline — fixed trance schedules, strict
duration limits, herb protocols that close the Veil contact as
completely as herbs can close it. She still hears the dead
between sessions. She still talks to them when she thinks no one
is listening. But she functions. She teaches. She heals. She is
the proof that managed decline is possible and the proof that
managed decline is not the same as no decline.

**Wyrd-reading decline** is the quietest and the cruelest. It
does not damage the body, does not fray the mind. It damages
something else — the part of a person that believes outcomes
are worth caring about.

Fatalism settles over a wyrd-reader the way sediment settles
over a riverbed. Slowly. Invisibly. Each reading adds a grain.
The lots say a child will die, and the child dies. The lots say
a storm will destroy the roof, and the storm destroys the roof.
The lots say the battle will be lost, and the battle is lost.
Each correct reading confirms that the world moves in patterns
the reader can perceive and cannot change, and the accumulation
of that confirmation produces something that is not quite despair
and not quite acceptance but shares features with both.

Wyrd-readers who practice for decades develop a resigned certainty
about outcomes. They stop being surprised. Then they stop being
invested. Then they stop reading, because the lots tell them
things they do not want to know, and knowing without the ability
to change is a punishment that the practice imposes and the
practice does not relieve.

Petra is years from this. She is young, her readings are still
fresh, and she still cares about what the lots say. She flinches
when the reading is bad. She celebrates internally when the
reading is good. But the trajectory is visible in every old
bone-caster the Rimevegr has produced — the flat eyes, the level
voice, the way they deliver a death reading with the same tone
they use to announce clear weather. That tone is not strength.
It is the absence of something that used to be there.

**What decline means for a band.** A mercenary company whose
galdr-worker is declining is a company whose contract value is
dropping. The wards weaken. The binding-marks hold for shorter
periods. The clients notice before the band does, because the
clients are paying for results, not for effort, and the results
are measurable. A ward that lasted a year now lasts eight months.
A binding-mark rated for a barrow-hold now fails at the six-month
check. The jarl who hired the band last season hires a different
band this season, and the reason is not personal. The reason is
rank-2 work at rank-3 prices.

Voss does not understand this. The analysis from the previous
section — the wasting-asset framework from Section 7 —
applies here in its most concentrated form. Voss sees Ash
carving. The carving looks the same. But the output quality is
declining, and the decline is invisible to anyone who does not
measure it, and Voss does not measure it because Voss does not
count magic as a quantifiable asset. By the time the decline
produces a contract failure — a ward that breaks, a binding that
does not hold, a client who walks away — the decline will have
been underway for years. The correction will arrive all at once,
and it will look like a sudden catastrophe rather than what it is:
the end of a gradual process that nobody tracked because nobody
knew how.

_Decline is not the end of practice. It is the end of effective
practice, which is worse, because the practitioner is still
there. The knowledge is still there. The desire is still there.
What is missing is the capacity, and the gap between what the
practitioner knows and what the practitioner can do is a gap
that fills with bitterness or resignation or, in the best cases,
teaching. The galdr-worker who cannot carve can still teach. The
seiðr-wife who cannot close the Veil can still counsel. The
wyrd-reader who cannot care can still throw the lots. But the
work they do in decline is not the work they were — it is the
shadow of the work, and they know the difference, and the
knowing is its own cost._

### Death — How Practitioners Die

Practitioners die the way everyone in the Rimevegr dies — badly,
and earlier than they should. The difference is specificity. A
sword-carrier dies of steel. A farmer dies of cold. A practitioner
dies of the practice, and the practice kills in ways that are
particular to each tradition and unmistakable to anyone who has
seen the pattern before.

**Galdr-workers' deaths** follow two tracks. The first is sudden:
accidental galdr-backlash. A rank-4 or rank-5 effect that fails
in cascade — the rune-channel opens, the mark does not hold, the
force that should have been directed into the wood or stone or
iron redirects into the body. The result depends on the rank. A
rank-4 backlash can shatter the carving hand. A rank-5 backlash
can stop the heart. The failure rates at rank 4 are documented in
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, and those rates apply every time a
rank-4 effect is attempted, with no allowance for experience.
A galdr-worker who has cast a thousand rank-4 effects is no
safer than one who has cast ten. The rune does not care who
holds the chisel.

The second track is slow: accumulated physical toll. The scarring
that begins in the hands and arms in the working years continues
to spread through the body in later life. The nerve damage that
produces tremor in the hands extends to the arms, the shoulders,
the spine. White-haired at forty. Hands shaking at forty-five.
The body ages faster than the years — a fifty-year-old
galdr-worker has the body of a sixty-five-year-old, and the
discrepancy widens each year. Most galdr-workers die before
sixty. Heart failure is common — the rune-channels stress the
cardiovascular system in ways that no healer fully understands.
Stroke is common. Simple collapse after a major working is common.

Stopping practice is not an escape. A galdr-worker who stops
carving is a galdr-worker who has removed the purpose from a body
that has been shaped by that purpose since childhood. The
rune-hunger does not end when the practice ends. The hands shake
not because they are carving but because the channels are open
and there is nothing flowing through them. The body was built to
conduct rune-force, and without that conduction, it shakes itself
apart. Stopping is its own kind of death — slower, quieter, and
arguably crueler, because the galdr-worker is conscious for all
of it. A galdr-worker who stops is nobody. The name stays. The
skill stays as knowledge. The identity leaves, because the
identity was the carving, and without the carving there is a
person-shaped absence where a practitioner used to be.

**Seiðr-wives' deaths** are stranger and harder to count. Many die
during trance. The reaching goes out and does not come back. The
spirit — the seiðr-wife's awareness, her self, whatever word fits
the thing that goes through the Veil during trance — goes out and
stays out. The body continues. This is the detail that makes
seiðr-death different from any other kind: the body does not stop
immediately. It continues breathing. It remains warm. The heart
beats. For hours, sometimes days, the body maintains itself the
way a fire maintains itself after the person who built it has
left. And then it stops. No spasm, no struggle. The breath
spaces out and ceases. The heart slows and stops. The body cools.
The seiðr-wife has been dead since the moment the spirit did not
return; the body simply took time to notice.

Others die of accumulated spirit-damage — the gradual erosion of
the boundary between self and not-self that Section 8's decline
subsection describes in its early stages. In its late stages, the
erosion is total. The seiðr-wife becomes the dead. Not a specific
dead person — the dead in general. She walks among the living
and does not recognize them. She speaks, but the words are
answers to questions the living did not ask. She eats if food is
placed before her. She sleeps if led to a bed. But the person
who inhabited the body has thinned to a membrane, and the
membrane has torn, and what comes through is not any identifiable
spirit but the aggregate presence of everything on the other side.
She walks until she forgets to walk. She breathes until she
forgets to breathe. It takes weeks. It is the worst way a
practitioner can die, because nobody can tell when the dying
started, and the woman who is dying does not know she is dying,
because the woman is no longer there to know anything.

**Wyrd-readers' deaths** are the most mundane. They die of the
same things everyone in the Rimevegr dies of — cold, steel,
sickness, age. The practice does not kill them directly. The
bone lots do not scar the hands. The readings do not thin the
mind. A wyrd-reader's body is no more damaged at fifty than a
non-practitioner's body.

But they see it coming. A wyrd-reader who has practiced for thirty
years reads their own death in the lots. Not the exact day, not
the specific mechanism — but the season. The direction. The shape.
They throw the lots one morning and the configuration says winter,
and they know this is the last autumn, and they have weeks to
prepare. Whether preparation helps depends on whether the death
allows preparation. A death by sickness can be prepared for —
affairs settled, final words spoken, the body positioned for proper
rites. A death by steel comes too fast for preparation to matter.
A death by cold means the preparation failed, because preparation
for cold death is survival, and survival means not dying.

Most deaths do not allow preparation. The wyrd-reader knows this.
The lots told them that too.

Torben — Petra's teacher, the shepherd who read weather and fate
from the same set of signs — died in a late-spring blizzard when
Petra was nineteen. He had read the blizzard two days before it
arrived. He told Petra to take the flock south. He did not go
with them. She asked why, and he said the things an old man says
when the lots have told him what they have told him. She took
the flock. He stayed. They found his body when the snow melted.
He was sitting upright against a rock, facing north, with his
bone lots arranged in front of him in a pattern Petra has never
been able to read. She kept the lots. She has never thrown that
particular configuration. She is not sure she wants to learn
what it says.

**Practitioner funerals** are more elaborate than ordinary
death-rites, and the reason is practical, not ceremonial. The
Rimevegr's standard death-rites — see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`
for the rune-marking protocols — are designed to prevent the
dead from returning. For ordinary dead, the rites work reliably.
For practitioners, they are insufficient.

A galdr-worker's body carries residual rune-force. The channels
that conducted power during life do not close cleanly at death.
The body hums for days after death, producing faint vibrations that
any trained galdr-worker can detect and that some untrained people
can feel as a wrongness in the air near the corpse. If the standard
death-rites are applied, the residual force interferes with the
binding marks, weakening them at exactly the points where they need
to be strongest. A galdr-worker buried with standard rites has a
significantly higher return rate than an ordinary corpse — estimated
at three to five times higher in the Deepholm records, though the
data is sparse.

A seiðr-wife's spirit is already half through the Veil at the time
of death. The trance-work of a lifetime has thinned the barrier to
the point where death — which is itself a passage through the
Veil — is barely a transition. The spirit does not settle on the
far side. It lingers. It drifts. It returns to the places it knew
in life, not out of malice but out of habit, the way a living person
returns to a familiar chair. If the death-rites are insufficient, the
seiðr-wife's spirit returns not as a draugr — not as a mindless
revenant — but as something closer to a living presence. Aware.
Purposeful. Capable of the work she did in life, including
reaching through the Veil and pulling others through.

A returned practitioner is worse than a returned warrior for one
specific reason: the returned practitioner still remembers how to
work. A returned warrior has strength and rage and the residue of
combat skill. A returned galdr-worker has rune-knowledge, and
residual rune-force, and the ability to carve, and the marks a
dead galdr-worker carves are not wards. They are invitations.
A returned seiðr-wife has the ability to reach, and a dead
seiðr-wife who reaches does not pull information from the other
side of the Veil. She pulls people.

The enhanced death-rites for practitioners involve triple the
standard rune-marking — every binding mark carved and then
cross-bound with a secondary set of marks that specifically target
the residual force. The body is treated with salt and specific
herbs before the rites are applied. The burial site is warded
independently of the settlement ward network, creating a contained
zone that any future galdr-worker can check without approaching
the body. These rites take a full day to perform. They require a
galdr-worker — which means that a settlement whose only
galdr-worker has died is a settlement that cannot properly bury
its own practitioner. The implications of this are left to the
reader to calculate.

_Practitioners die the way they lived: specifically, painfully,
and in ways that make the non-practitioners around them
uncomfortable. The deaths are not tragic in the narrative sense —
the Rimevegr does not have room for tragedy, which requires the
expectation of something better. They are factual. The body kept
the score, and the score came due, and the books are closed.
What remains is the work the practitioner did while they could
still do it. The wards Ash carves this season will outlast him.
The healings Dalla performs today will keep men alive who will
forget her name. The readings Petra gives this morning will
inform decisions whose consequences will unfold without her.
The practitioner's lifecycle is the lifecycle of all useful
things: made, used, worn out. The Rimevegr does not mourn its
tools. It replaces them. And when it cannot replace them, it
does without, and the gap where the tool was becomes a hole that
the cold comes through._

### The Unnamed Problem — What Happens When There Are Not Enough

The numbers are not written down because nobody in the Rimevegr
counts practitioners the way they count livestock or silver. There
is no census. There is no registry. A settlement either has a
galdr-worker or it does not, and the jarl who runs the settlement
knows which it is, and that is the extent of the data collection.

But the pattern is visible to anyone who looks. And a few people
have looked.

**The decline is real.** The Rimevegr's practitioner population is
shrinking. Not dramatically — not a collapse, not yet — but a
slow, generation-over-generation contraction that has been
underway since the Cracking and has accelerated in the last
century.

The galdr tradition keeps the best records because Deepholm keeps
records. The archive scrolls show that three centuries ago, before
the Cracking, approximately one in four hundred people in the
Rimevegr had some galdr talent. One in eight hundred was a trained,
practicing galdr-worker. Today, the best estimate — and it is an
estimate, compiled from the observations of galdr-workers who
travel and compare notes — is one in six hundred for talent and
one in fifteen hundred for trained practitioners. The trained
population has halved. The talent pool has contracted by a third.

For seiðr, the data is worse because the data barely exists.
Seiðr-wives do not maintain archives. They maintain relationships,
and when the relationships break — when a teacher dies without a
student, when a community drives out its seiðr-wife, when the
knowledge that would have been passed on is not passed on — the
loss is total and invisible. Nobody writes it down because nobody
who remains knows what was lost. The best estimate is that the
number of practicing seiðr-wives in the Rimevegr has dropped by
more than half since the Cracking. Some regions have none. The
Icebreak coast, which once supported a seiðr tradition strong
enough to sustain three or four active practitioners per major
settlement, now has Ragnhild. When Ragnhild dies, the Icebreak
seiðr tradition dies with her unless one of her current students
survives the training and stays in the region. The odds of both of
those conditions being met are not favorable.

Wyrd-reading has contracted less sharply because the training cost
is lower and the manifestation rate is higher. But even wyrd-reading
is declining, because the pipeline that produces wyrd-readers depends
on older readers recognizing talent in younger people and teaching
them, and the number of older readers is dropping for the same
reason everything else is dropping: the Cracking broke the
infrastructure that used to support magical practice, and nothing
has replaced it.

**The reasons are structural.** Three forces are killing the supply.

First: the physical cost is too high. Before the Cracking, divine
power supplemented the personal cost of magical practice. A
galdr-worker channeled rune-force through channels that the gods
maintained. The channels are still there, but the maintenance
stopped three centuries ago, and the deterioration is cumulative.
Every year, the channels carry less and the body pays more. A
rank-3 galdr effect that cost a moderate physical toll in the age
before the Cracking now costs a significant one. The cost increase
discourages new practitioners. A parent who watches the village
galdr-worker age twenty years in ten is not eager to send their
talented child into the same trade. The talent goes untrained.
The supply drops.

Second: the social position is too dangerous. Practitioners occupy
a space in Rimevegr society that is necessary and feared, useful
and suspected, paid and despised. The galdr-worker wards the
settlement and the settlement cannot do without the wards, but the
galdr-worker is still the person who handles forces that nobody
else can see. The seiðr-wife is the healer and the spirit
speaker, and she is also the woman who talks to the dead, and
the dead are not popular. The wyrd-reader tells the future, and
nobody wants to know the future when the future is likely to be
worse than the present. A child who manifests in a supportive
community may be trained. A child who manifests in a suspicious
community will be suppressed. Most communities are suspicious.
The pipeline loses candidates at the intake.

Third: the Cracking broke the pipeline itself. Before the gods
went silent, magical training was supported by institutions —
the temple complexes, the formal rune-schools, the seiðr
networks that linked practitioners across the Rimevegr. Those
institutions depended on divine patronage for legitimacy and
divine power for resources. When the patronage ended and the
power diminished, the institutions collapsed. What remains are
fragments: Deepholm's workshop, Skaldhaven's archive, isolated
teachers like Ragnhild. These fragments cannot produce
practitioners at the rate the old institutions did. They produce
a few per generation, where the old system produced dozens.

**What happens when a settlement loses its last practitioner.**
This is not a hypothetical. It happened to three settlements in
the Uppmark region within the last fifteen years. The pattern is
consistent.

The galdr-worker dies or leaves. The wards are not maintained.
Within two to three years, the perimeter weakens to the point
where the dead begin testing it. Small incursions first — a
shade at the edge of the settlement, a draugr at the outlying
barrow that was supposed to stay sealed. The settlement cannot
address the incursions because addressing them requires a
galdr-worker, and there is no galdr-worker. They hire a traveling
one, if they can find one and if they can afford one. The
traveling galdr-worker patches the worst gaps and leaves, because
they have a home settlement that is also losing wards. The patches
hold for a season. Then they fail. The settlement either
abandons or contracts — pulling the inhabited area inward, leaving
the outer buildings to the dark, accepting that the perimeter is
smaller and the margins are tighter and the next loss will be
harder to absorb.

The same pattern applies to unions and regional governance. An
Allthing requires warded stones — the assembly site must be
protected from spiritual interference because legal proceedings
conducted in the presence of the unquiet dead are not valid.
A union whose Allthing stones are not properly warded is a union
whose legal system is degrading. This has already happened in
two minor unions in the eastern Rimevegr. The lawspeakers do
not speak about it publicly. They continue holding ting, but
they know the wards are thin, and they know that the next time
a difficult case draws the attention of the dead, the protections
may not hold.

**The timeline** is the part nobody has calculated, because nobody
wants to know the answer.

At current rates of attrition — practitioner deaths exceeding new
practitioner training by roughly three to two — and current rates
of recruitment — one new galdr-worker for every two who die, one
new seiðr-wife for every three, wyrd-readers roughly sustaining
but not growing — the Rimevegr has perhaps two generations before
the magical infrastructure fails irreversibly.

Two generations. Forty to sixty years. That is the window.

After that window closes, the ward networks will not be
maintainable. Not because the wards have failed — the physical
marks will still be there — but because there will not be enough
practitioners to maintain them. A settlement whose wards decay and
cannot be restored is a settlement open to the dead. A region
whose settlements are open to the dead is a region that is no
longer habitable. The dead do not push in all at once. They
seep. They accumulate. They build in the spaces where the wards
used to be, the way water builds behind a dam that is cracking
one fissure at a time.

The data exists to prove this. The Deepholm archive contains
three centuries of records — practitioner counts, ward-failure
rates, recruitment figures. Skaldhaven's sealed records hold the
theoretical framework — the mathematical models that the early
post-Cracking galdr scholars built to predict the decay curve.
Nobody has compiled the data from Deepholm and the models from
Skaldhaven. Nobody has run the calculation. The archives are a
thousand miles apart and neither institution shares willingly.

But the calculation does not require both archives. Anyone with
access to either set of records, and the mathematical competence
to project the curves, would arrive at the same answer within a
margin of a few years. Two generations. Three at the outside, if
recruitment improves. One and a half, if a major event — a war,
a plague, a particularly bad Long Dark — kills a significant
fraction of the current practitioner population in a single blow.

Nobody has done this calculation because nobody wants to know the
answer. The jarls do not want to know because the answer means
their grandchildren will inherit indefensible settlements. The
practitioners do not want to know because the answer means their
work is a rearguard action against an outcome that is already
determined. The lawspeakers do not want to know because the answer
means the legal infrastructure they maintain is temporary.

The people who are closest to understanding the problem are the
practitioners themselves — the Ashes and Dallas who feel the cost
increasing year by year, who sense something draining that cannot
be refilled, who do the work and watch the work diminish and do
not need an archive scroll to tell them what the body already
knows. But practitioners are not analysts. They are workers. They
solve the problem in front of them — the next ward, the next
healing, the next reading — and they do not step back to see the
pattern because the pattern is too large and too frightening and
because seeing the pattern does not change the pattern.

---

The encoded mysteries track this in acute form. MAGIC_TRUTH_003
documents the accelerating cost. The Cracking broke the world
three centuries ago. The practitioner shortage is the world
noticing — reacting, adjusting, failing — three centuries late.
The acute crisis, if it comes, will not look like the Cracking. It
will look like a long winter during which the wards do not hold
and there is nobody left to repair them, and the dead walk through
every door, and the living discover that the barrier between their
world and the next was always maintained by a handful of strange,
damaged people who heard things and saw things and read things that
nobody else could, and those people are gone.

_The practitioner's lifecycle is the Rimevegr's lifecycle,
compressed and accelerated. Birth, growth, the working years,
decline, death. The Rimevegr went through these stages. The gods
were born into the world. The runes grew. The working years
produced the civilization that exists now — the wards, the unions,
the laws, the trade routes, the settlements that survive in a
land that should not support survival. The decline began with the
Cracking and has continued for three hundred years. The death has
not arrived yet. But the lots have been thrown, and the
configuration is readable, and the reading is not ambiguous. The
Rimevegr's magic is dying the way its practitioners die: slowly,
specifically, in ways that are visible to anyone willing to look
and invisible to everyone who is not. And the ones who are willing
to look are the same ones who are dying. And they are not
being replaced._
