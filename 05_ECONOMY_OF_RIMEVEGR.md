## The Shape of Wealth

The Rimevegr is poor. Not poor the way a bad harvest makes a kingdom poor
-- poor the way three centuries of failed summers, broken trade routes, and
silent gods make a civilization poor. The Cracking did not destroy wealth.
It contracted it. What remains circulates in small, hard loops: settlement
to jarl to mercenary to settlement again. Every copper piece in the
Rimevegr has changed hands a thousand times. The metal is worn smooth. The
stamping is illegible. Nobody cares whose face was on it. The weight is
what counts.

### What Wealth Looks Like

A jarl's wealth is visible: arm-rings, a hall with a proper high seat, a
cloak trimmed in wolf fur, a sword that someone named. A bondi's wealth is
invisible: grain in the pit, a second cow, enough firewood to last until
thaw without rationing. A thrall's wealth does not exist. A mercenary's
wealth is portable: the silver in his purse, the iron on his back, the
boots on his feet.

The richest settlement in the Rimevegr -- Skaldhaven, the nearest thing to
a town -- might hold two hundred silver in total circulation across all its
households, shops, and the jarl's treasury. A village like Frostfjord
Hollow holds twenty-four silver in its treasury and perhaps another fifteen
hidden across eighty-seven households. A hamlet like Stonebay holds almost
nothing. The three coppers buried under Widow Yrsa's hearthstone are the
hamlet's entire coin reserve.

## Currency

### Copper

The coin that people touch. Copper pieces are small, roughly
thumbnail-sized, and made by local smiths hammering flat blanks into rough
discs. Some bear a jarl's mark. Most do not. Weight varies by five to ten
percent between settlements -- a Grimholt copper is heavier than a
Frostfjord copper, and the traders at Skaldhaven know this and price
accordingly.

A common man handles copper. A farmer who sells surplus grain at market
earns a handful of coppers that will buy salt, ale, or a small tool repair
before the walk home. The coppers go back into circulation the same week.
They do not accumulate.

### Silver

Silver is weighed, not counted. A "silver piece" means a standardized
weight of silver roughly equivalent across the Rimevegr -- not because any
authority enforces the standard, but because traders refuse light coin. The
enforcement mechanism is the folding balance: a small set of scales carried
in a leather pouch by anyone who handles silver regularly. Merchants,
sergeants, jarls' stewards, and mercenary captains all carry them.

Hack-silver is common. Arm-rings, ingots, and foreign coins are cut, filed,
or snapped to the needed weight. A man paying three silver and four copper
might hand over two whole coins, half an arm-ring segment, and a filed
sliver balanced on the scale to make weight. The aesthetic of payment is
irrelevant. The weight is everything.

Silver concentrates in four places: jarl treasuries, mercenary purses,
merchant scales, and the earth under hearthstones. Everywhere else, copper
and barter do the work.

### The Exchange Rate

One silver equals ten copper in the Rimevegr. This ratio holds in stable
conditions -- at market towns during calm weeks, in contracts between
literate parties, in the simulation's mechanical economy. In practice, the
rate floats. After a bad harvest, a silver piece buys fifteen coppers'
worth of goods because silver becomes the only thing people trust. During
the brief Long Summer glut, copper regains value and the rate tightens
toward eight-to-one. Nobody publishes rates. A man who trades knows the
current ratio by feel, the way a farmer knows when rain is coming.

### Gold and High-Value Exchange

Gold is not everyday currency in the Rimevegr. Most people never touch it.
When gold appears, it appears as a ring-piece, foreign coin, reliquary scrap,
or oath-jewelry melted back into trade weight. It is used for dowries, ransom,
high diplomacy, temple gifts, and compact payments large enough that hauling
silver becomes stupid.

For contracts and treasury reckoning, use this accounting standard:

- 1 silver = 10 copper
- 1 gold = 10 silver by clean weight

That is a ledger ratio, not a universal moneychanger's promise. Gold is
broken locally, by men with scales, leverage, and a reason to shave the deal.
A trader is not exchanging abstract units. He is deciding how much risk, assay
work, and local scarcity he will charge for.

| Settlement | Who breaks gold | Usual offer for 1 gold | Lore and practice |
| ---------- | --------------- | ---------------------- | ----------------- |
| Deepholm | Harbor traders, reeve's scales, merchant houses | 9 silver 8 copper to 10 silver | The cleanest market in the Rimevegr. Standardized weights, daily trade, and competition keep the skim narrow. Foreign coin with known stamp may pass at the full ten silver. |
| Thornwall | Wool factors, market reeves, wealthy bondi | 9 silver 5 copper to 9 silver 8 copper | Honest enough, but the buyer prefers to keep some silver in hand for flock purchases and winter grain. Better terms at autumn fairs than in the lean months. |
| Ashen Reach | Timber factors, tar merchants, the Pale Widow's agents | 9 silver to 9 silver 6 copper | Traders often shave the exchange and make up the difference in store credit, timber rights, or tar contracts. Best used when buying bulk goods rather than demanding loose silver. |
| Kolvik | Harbor brokers, ship captains, visiting merchants | 9 silver 2 copper to 9 silver 6 copper | Coin moves through the harbor, but captains prefer mixed payment in silver, rope, sailcloth, or salt cargo. A clean exchange exists, but rarely with generous change. |
| Skaldhaven | Innkeepers, scribes, temple stewards, traders | 9 silver 4 copper to 10 silver | Literate weighing and temple gifts make this one of the few places where gold is not treated as suspicious loot. The full ten silver usually requires status, witnesses, or patience. |
| Grimholt | Ordovast's factors, mine paymasters | 9 silver to 9 silver 5 copper | Ore wealth makes silver available, but Ordovast's people take their cut hard. Gold is welcomed if it settles arms, ore, or mercenary accounts. |
| Frostfjord Hollow | Hrothgar's reeve, one or two visiting merchants | 8 silver 5 copper to 9 silver | Fish village, thin silver pool. Hrothgar's tax and the risk of making change both come out of the seller's hide. Better to spend gold on cargo here than to ask for coin back. |
| Vargheim | Fur traders, headman's steward, caravan men | 8 silver 8 copper to 9 silver 2 copper | Exchanges follow the fur season. When caravans are in, rates tighten. In hungry months, locals discount gold because nobody has loose silver to break it. |
| Raven's Perch | No routine market exchange | 7 silver to 8 silver, if anyone can pay at all | Gold here is a ransom piece, not a circulating medium. A desperate thane may accept it against tribute or debt, but common folk cannot make change. |

Treat the table above as normal market behavior, not law. A lord's treasury,
a desperate captain, or a temple chest can break gold on exceptional terms.
They do so because the politics suit them, not because the village owes anyone
modern-style currency service.

## The Calibration Anchor

Every price in this document is calibrated against one fact:

**One silver buys one week's food for one person.**

The practical daily anchor beneath that is the **málsverðr** -- an Old Norse
meal term, literally meal-food, used here for the ordinary ale-and-food
serving that keeps a worker on his feet. A málsverðr is not luxury mead. It is
the standard inn or hall meal: porridge or stew, bread, and weak ale safe
enough to drink daily.

**One málsverðr costs 1 copper.**

That is the hand-to-hand anchor. Everything else derives from it. A day's food
costs roughly one copper because the málsverðr is what a person must at least
replace. Two copper buys a day's unskilled labor -- because a laborer must eat,
and his employer must make hiring him worth more than the food he consumes.
Five copper buys a mercenary's daily mission pay -- because a fighter's skills,
equipment, and willingness to die are worth more than ditch-digging but less
than what the contract earns for the band.

| Value      | What it means                                        |
| ---------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| 1 copper   | One málsverðr. A cup of ale. A bowl of pottage. A ferry ride |
| 2 copper   | A day's unskilled labor. A floor pallet              |
| 5 copper   | A mercenary's daily mission pay                      |
| 1 silver   | A week's food. Two days' journeyman pay              |
| 2 silver   | A common fighter's weekly retainer                   |
| 3 silver   | A veteran's weekly retainer                          |
| 5 silver   | A Named Man's weekly retainer                        |
| 10 silver  | A good axe-head and change                           |
| 1 gold     | Ten weeks' food. A serious trader's conversion piece |
| 20 silver  | A sound horse. A year's income for a laborer         |
| 50 silver  | Weregild for an important man                        |
| 100 silver | A jarl's ransom. A small settlement's total reserves |

## Barter

### Why Barter Dominates

Most people in the Rimevegr never touch silver. A bondi in Frostfjord
Hollow pays his rent to Jarl Hrothgar in grain and labor. He pays the smith
in charcoal and grain. He pays the alewife in barley. The alewife pays the
carpenter in ale. The carpenter pays the bondi in a repaired door-frame.
The circle closes without a single coin changing hands.

This is not a primitive system. It is an efficient one. Barter works when
the parties know each other, when transactions repeat, and when the
community enforces fairness through reputation. A settlement of eighty
people has no need for a currency when every debt is tracked in the
collective memory of its inhabitants.

Barter fails at the boundaries -- when a stranger arrives, when a mercenary
band demands payment, when goods must travel between settlements that do
not share a social ledger. That is when coin appears. Coin is the language
strangers speak.

### What People Barter

| Category       | Common goods                                      |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Food staples   | Grain, dried cod, smoked meat, cheese, butter     |
| Preservatives  | Salt, honey, vinegar                              |
| Raw materials  | Timber, firewood, charcoal, wool, hides, iron ore |
| Finished goods | Cloth, rope, shoes, nails, tools, pottery         |
| Animals        | Chickens, goats, sheep, pigs, cattle              |
| Labor          | Days of work: plowing, building, hauling, fishing |
| Services       | Healing, guiding, ferrying, midwifery             |
| Access rights  | Grazing, fishing, timber-cutting, water access    |

### The Favor Ledger

Every settlement runs on an invisible balance of obligation. When Kari
helped thatch Olaf's roof, Olaf owes. When the widow gave the smith's
children cheese during the hard week, the smith owes. Nobody writes it
down. Everybody remembers. The man who takes more than he gives finds his
neighbors slow to help when his own roof leaks.

A newcomer -- a traveler, a mercenary, an outlander -- has no position in
this ledger. He cannot trade on credit. He cannot call in debts. He pays
with coin or goods, at the stranger's premium, and the community pockets
the surplus as insurance against the risk of dealing with someone who might
never come back.

### Three Payment Layers

The economy works cleanly once payments are sorted into three layers:

| Layer | What usually pays | Typical use |
| ----- | ----------------- | ----------- |
| Local daily life | Copper, barter, favors, meals, ale, labor, produce | Food, bedding, ferry crossings, small repairs, neighborly help |
| Outsider and professional exchange | Mixed copper and silver, often food plus coin | Healing a stranger, hiring a guide, renting room space, specialist craft work |
| Military, legal, and long-distance exchange | Silver by weight, sometimes gold | Retainers, tribute, weregild, long caravan deals, ship cargoes, elite gifts |

Within one settlement, all three layers can exist at once. A man may pay 1
copper for his bowl of stew, 6 copper and supper for a guide to the next
valley, and 20 silver by weighed hacksilver to settle a blood-price. The
mistake is thinking there is one money for all three.

### Weregild (Blood-Price)

The legal price of a life. Paid to the family of the dead to prevent
blood-feud. Negotiated at the thing or between families. Not paying
weregild means the dead man's kin have a legal right to kill the debtor or
a member of his family.

| Person                | Price   | Notes                             |
| --------------------- | ------- | --------------------------------- |
| Thrall                | 5-10    | Property damage, not a life-debt  |
| Common karl           | 15-25   | The standard blood-price          |
| Skilled karl          | 25-40   | Smith, healer, shipwright         |
| Mercenary (common)    | 15-25   | Paid by the employer or the band  |
| Named Man             | 50+     | A serious debt                    |
| Jarl's huscarl        | 40-60   | The jarl enforces collection      |
| Jarl or chieftain     | 100-200 | Functional deterrent; rarely paid |
| Woman (free, married) | 20-35   | Paid to her husband and father    |
| Child (free)          | 10-15   | Paid to the father                |

Weregild for outlaws is zero. Killing an outlaw carries no legal
consequence. This is why outlawry is feared more than execution -- an
outlaw's family receives no compensation when he dies.

## Services

### The Smith

The most important craftsman in any settlement. Without a smith, iron
rusts, tools break, horses go unshod, and weapons dull. A settlement
without a smith depends on a neighboring settlement's smith, which means
paying the stranger premium and waiting in line behind locals.

| Service               | Copper | Silver | Barter option                     |
| --------------------- | ------ | ------ | --------------------------------- |
| Sharpen a blade       | 1      | —      | A meal                            |
| Repair a tool (minor) | 2-3    | —      | A day's labor or food equivalent  |
| Repair a tool (major) | —      | 1-2    | Iron stock + charcoal + food      |
| Shoe a horse (all 4)  | —      | 1      | Grain supply or charcoal delivery |
| New axe-head          | —      | 3-5    | A week's food + raw iron          |
| New spear-head        | —      | 2-3    | Iron supply + charcoal            |
| New knife             | —      | 1-2    | — (small enough for coin)         |
| Sword repair (minor)  | —      | 2-3    | Depends on damage                 |
| Sword repair (major)  | —      | 5-8    | Materials + extended labor        |
| New sword (common)    | —      | 15-25  | Weeks of work; advance required   |
| New sword (fine)      | —      | 40-80  | Commission; patron supplies iron  |
| New mail links        | —      | 5-10   | Per section; full shirt is months |
| Helm repair           | —      | 2-5    | — (coin or iron)                  |
| Nails (per dozen)     | 1      | —      | — (always in demand)              |

The smith at Grimholt is the best in the Rimevegr -- Ordovast's iron mines
feed his forge. The smith at Stonebay Hamlet does not exist. When the
fishermen need metalwork, they wait for the seasonal visit of the traveling
smith from Vargheim, who charges double and expects lodging.

In Frostfjord Hollow, the smith is rated "Basic" -- he handles repairs and
simple new work. A common axe-head, yes. A sword, no. For that, the buyer
travels to Ashen Reach, where the smith has hard-to-find steel and the
skill to fold it, or to Grimholt, where the iron is better but Ordovast
takes a percentage.

### The Healer

Healers are paid in food, labor, and obligation -- not coin. A family whose
child survives a fever owes the healer an ongoing debt that manifests as
food deliveries, firewood stacked by the door, and labor when the healer
asks. This debt has no fixed expiration. It is a relationship, not a
transaction.

Mercenaries are different. They are strangers with silver and no community
standing. The healer charges them coin because coin is the substitute for
trust. In a village this often still means copper and food. In a town or
trade-active place, the same service is more likely to be quoted in silver.

| Service | For locals | Village quote for outsiders | Town or trader-settlement quote |
| ------- | ---------- | --------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Stitch a wound | A meal and shelter | 4-6 copper + food | 8 copper to 1 silver |
| Set a broken bone | A chicken or a day's labor | 8 copper + food and lodging | 1-2 silver |
| Tend a fever (3 days) | Food for the healer's family | 8-10 copper + food and firewood | 1-2 silver |
| Treat infection | Rare herbs if available; else food | 6-8 copper + herbs owed if they must be fetched | 1-2 silver |
| Attend a birth | Feed the midwife for a week | 5-8 copper + board for the midwife | 8 copper to 1 silver |
| Chronic condition | Ongoing food and household help | Ongoing food, labor, and gifts; seldom a fixed quote | Not available as a fixed contract |
| Poison treatment | Whatever the healer demands | 2-4 silver, often plus livestock or family debt | 4-8 silver |
| Surgery (amputation) | The family's best animal | 2-4 silver + the best animal in the yard | 4-6 silver |

At Raven's Perch there is no healer. Thane Egil handles minor wounds
himself with boiled water and linen. Serious injuries require a two-day
journey to Vargheim's herbalist. At Bleakwater Landing, the healer is a
fisherman's widow who learned herb-lore from her mother. She is competent
with fevers and childbirth, limited with trauma. At Skaldhaven the healer
is as close to a physician as the Rimevegr produces -- trained,
experienced, and expensive.

### Guide and Scout

A man who knows the terrain between settlements. Essential for bands moving
through unfamiliar country, especially in the Black Pine Wilds or the High
Rime-Moors where paths vanish between seasons.

| Service | Village or frontier quote | Town or trader quote | Notes |
| ------- | ------------------------- | -------------------- | ----- |
| Guide, per day (local) | 2 copper + food | 4 copper | Short-range, known paths |
| Guide, per day (wilderness) | 5-6 copper + food | 8 copper to 1 silver | Black Pine or Rime-Moors |
| Scout ahead (half-day) | 3 copper | 4-5 copper | Check the route; report back |
| Guide, per tenday | 1 silver 5 copper + food | 2-3 silver + food | Extended journey rate |
| Emergency guide (night) | 5-8 copper + food or shelter guaranteed | 1-2 silver | Nobody wants to walk at night |

Guides know things the maps do not show: where the bog swallows horses,
which fords flood in thaw, where the barrows sit, which stretches of forest
go quiet at dusk. A good guide is worth his fee three times over. A bad
guide gets the band lost, and lost in the Rimevegr means dead.

## Goods as Payment

When a settlement cannot pay silver -- or when the payee prefers weight
over coin -- goods change hands instead. Mercenary bands accept goods
payment frequently, especially from villages too small to hold meaningful
coin reserves.

### Common Payment Goods

| Good                | Weight (approx.) | Silver value | Shelf life           |
| ------------------- | ---------------- | ------------ | -------------------- |
| Sack of grain       | 25-30 lbs        | 2-3          | Months if dry        |
| Barrel of ale       | 60-70 lbs        | 2-3          | Weeks; heat kills it |
| Salt (1 lb)         | 1 lb             | 2-3          | Indefinite           |
| Salt (10 lbs)       | 10 lbs           | 15-20        | Indefinite           |
| Smoked meat (5 lbs) | 5 lbs            | 1            | Months               |
| Dried cod (10 lbs)  | 10 lbs           | 2-3          | Months if dry        |
| Honey (1 lb)        | 1 lb             | 1-2          | Indefinite sealed    |
| Hard cheese (5 lbs) | 5 lbs            | 1-2          | Weeks to months      |
| Common pelts (each) | 2-5 lbs          | 1-3          | Store dry; moths     |
| Fine pelts (each)   | 2-5 lbs          | 5-15         | Same risks           |
| Bolt of cloth       | 10-15 lbs        | 3-5          | Keep dry             |
| Iron bar            | 5-10 lbs         | 3-5          | Rust if wet          |
| Amber (small piece) | < 1 lb           | 3-10         | Indefinite           |
| Firewood (cartload) | 200+ lbs         | 2-3          | N/A                  |
| Charcoal (sack)     | 15-20 lbs        | 1-2          | Keep dry             |

### What a Mercenary Captain Evaluates

Four questions before accepting goods instead of coin:

**Weight.** Can the band carry it? Fourteen men on a forced march through
the Black Pine cannot haul a cartload of grain. Salt is light for its
value. Pelts are light. Grain is heavy. Ale is absurdly heavy for what it
is worth.

**Spoilage.** Will it survive the march? Grain rots if it gets wet. Ale
turns in warm weather. Cheese sweats. Smoked meat keeps but attracts
animals. Salt and iron keep indefinitely.

**Liquidity.** Can it be traded at the next settlement? Salt sells
anywhere. Pelts sell at market towns. Iron sells to any smith. Timber
rights are worthless to a band that is sixty miles from the forest in
question. A horse sells, but finding a buyer takes time the band may not
have.

**Title.** Does the payee actually own it? Goods stolen, owed to a third
party, or disputed are worthless -- taking them makes the band a party to
the dispute. A witnessed transfer before the settlement elder protects the
band's claim.

### The In-Kind Payment Clock

The simulation rules (§8) allow in-kind payment for up to three weeks. On
the fourth week without coin, the captain must pass a Persuade or Command
check with rising difficulty, or morale drops. Men tolerate grain-pay when
they believe the captain is working toward silver. Men who suspect the
captain cannot find silver begin to desert.

## The Tavern, the Inn, and the Alehouse

### Village Alehouse

Not a building. A room in someone's house where the goodwife brews. In
Feldwick -- currently occupied by the Three Wolves -- the alehouse is
closed because the alewife fled. In Frostfjord Hollow, Gudrun One-Eye brews
in her kitchen and sells through a shuttered window. In Stonebay Hamlet,
there is no alehouse. There are twenty-three people and they drink from
whatever pot the elder's wife fills.

| Item                       | Copper | Barter              |
| -------------------------- | ------ | ------------------- |
| Cup of ale (weak, watered) | 1      | A chore; haul water |
| Cup of ale (full strength) | 2      | —                   |
| Bowl of pottage            | 1      | Bring your own bowl |
| Bread and cheese           | 1      | A small favor owed  |
| Floor pallet for the night | 1      | Help in the morning |
| Stabling (if space exists) | 1      | Unlikely to be free |

Expect: shared room with the family and their livestock; smoke-filled;
straw on packed earth; the smell of sour ale, sweat, and dung. It is warm
and dry. Everything else is a bonus.

### Road Inn

Purpose-built on a trade route or crossroads. Separate sleeping area. A
proper hearth. Food cooked with intent. At the crossroads between
Frostfjord and Ashen Reach, the Crossroads Inn survives because both
settlements need a neutral meeting point. The innkeeper sells ale, food,
beds, information, and the implicit safety of neutral ground.

| Item | Copper | Silver | Notes |
| ---- | ------ | ------ | ----- |
| Ale (standard) | 2 | — | Better than alehouse |
| Meal (basic) | 2 | — | Bread, stew |
| Meal (good) | 4 | — | Meat, bread, cheese |

| Hot bath | 5 | — | If the inn has a tub |

The innkeeper knows everyone who passes and sells that knowledge freely --
not for coin, but because information is what makes him indispensable. A
man who controls the gossip at a crossroads controls the crossroads.

### Town Tavern (Skaldhaven and Large Settlements)

A full establishment. Multiple rooms. A kitchen. A cellar. Servants. The
innkeeper is a businessman with property and political connections.

| Item | Copper | Silver | Notes |

| Ale (good) | 2 | — | Properly brewed |
| Mead | — | 1 | Honey is expensive |
| Wine (imported) | — | 2-3 | Shipped from far away |

Wine exists in Skaldhaven because a merchant brought it by ship from

## Market Day

### Frequency by Settlement Type

| Settlement        | Market                                     |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Hamlet            | None. Trade at the nearest village or town |
| Village           | None, or monthly during stable conditions  |
| Large village     | Monthly; possibly seasonal fairs           |
| Town (Skaldhaven) | Weekly, fixed day                          |

Market day brings people from a day's walk -- roughly ten miles. They
arrive the evening before, trade at dawn, and walk home before dark. The
market is finished by midday. The afternoon belongs to ale and settling
accounts.

### Seasonal Prices

The grain price drives everything. When grain is cheap after harvest, all
goods are cheap. When grain runs low in spring, everything tightens. A
mercenary band that plans its movements around the harvest calendar saves
silver. A band that arrives at a settlement in late spring pays triple for
provisions.

| Good       | Harvest (×) | Midwinter (×) | Spring (×) |
| ---------- | ----------- | ------------- | ---------- |
| Grain      | 0.5         | 1.0           | 2.0-3.0    |
| Salt       | 1.0         | 1.0           | 1.2        |
| Firewood   | 0.7         | 2.0           | 1.0        |
| Pelts      | 1.0         | 1.5           | 0.7        |
| Dried fish | 1.0         | 1.5           | 1.5        |
| Fresh meat | 1.0         | 2.0           | 2.5        |
| Ale        | 1.0         | 1.0           | 1.3        |
| Iron goods | 1.0         | 1.0           | 1.0        |

Iron goods are the exception -- metal does not rot, and the smith charges
what the smith charges regardless of the season.

## Trade Between Settlements

### What Each Settlement Produces

| Settlement        | Exports                       | Imports                     |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Frostfjord Hollow | Dried cod, pine tar           | Iron goods, salt, grain     |
| Ashen Reach       | Pine tar, iron goods          | Grain, salt, cloth          |
| Feldwick          | Sheep wool, root vegetables   | Iron, salt, everything else |
| Stonebay Hamlet   | Dried fish (small surplus)    | Everything                  |
| Grimholt          | Iron ore, iron goods          | Food, cloth, timber         |
| Raven's Perch     | Mountain goat hides, iron ore | Food, everything else       |
| Vargheim          | Timber, pelts, charcoal       | Iron, salt, grain           |
| Skaldhaven        | Finished goods, trade hub     | Raw materials, food         |
| Bleakwater Land.  | Fish, seal oil                | Iron, grain, salt           |
| Icebreak          | Fresh water (trade advantage) | Food, iron, cloth           |

### Trade Routes

Long-range trade in the Rimevegr is dangerous. The Black Pine Wilds between
settlements are not empty. Rival bands, bandits, outlaws, and things that
are not quite any of those occupy the forest. A merchant traveling alone
between Frostfjord and Ashen Reach is a dead merchant. Merchant caravans
hire escort bands -- one of the most common contract types for a Svarthird.

The Rime Road -- the old north-south trade route that gives the Rimevegr
its name -- still exists as a mud track in summer and an ice path in
winter. It connects the southern market towns (Skaldhaven and surroundings)
to the northern settlements. Traffic is thin. A caravan passes once or
twice a month during the Long Dark, more often during Long Summer when the
road is passable but the bugs are terrible.

Coastal trade by longship is faster and safer than overland routes.
Frostfjord, Bleakwater Landing, and the Inner Fjord settlements rely on
coastal shipping for anything they cannot produce locally. A longship with
a reliable crew can move goods between fjord settlements in days that would
take a week by land.

## The Mercenary Economy

### Why Mercenaries Matter

A Svarthird -- a mercenary band -- is the Rimevegr's most efficient
economic unit for one thing: converting violence into silver. The band
sells security. Security is what every settlement needs and cannot produce
on its own, because a settlement's fighters are also its farmers,
fishermen, and craftsmen. A jarl who sends all his karls to fight leaves
nobody to tend the fields.

The mercenary exists to fill this gap. He fights so the farmer does not
have to. He is paid because the cost of his sword is less than the cost of
what the sword protects.

### The Pay Cycle

Weekly retainer is due every seventh day. The sergeant sits on his stone or
log with the purse on his left, the ledger on his right, and a small knife
stuck in wood. Each man steps forward, receives his copper and silver,
counts it openly, and walks away. Men with advance debts watch their pile
come up short. Nobody argues at the stone. Arguments happen later, in
private, or not at all.

Mission pay is separate -- a daily rate paid during active contracts, on
top of retainer. The contract specifies the rate. The client pays. If the
client defaults, the band has a decision to make: eat the loss,
renegotiate, or take the payment from the client's settlement by force.

### What a Band Spends

A fourteen-man Svarthird burns silver constantly. Rough weekly budget for
Voss's Black Axes:

| Expense               | Weekly cost (approx.) |
| --------------------- | --------------------- |
| Retainer payroll      | 38 silver             |
| Food (if buying)      | 14 silver             |
| Equipment repair      | 2 silver              |
| Stabling (if horses)  | 2 silver              |
| Miscellaneous         | 2 silver              |
| **Total (no forage)** | **~58 silver/week**   |

With successful foraging, the food cost drops to zero. With good equipment
maintenance, repair costs are lower. But the payroll is fixed. Thirty-eight
silver per week. That is the number that keeps the captain awake at night.
Miss it twice, and the non-payment table starts rolling.

### Contract Prices

What clients pay for mercenary services.

| Contract type         | Typical payment     | Notes                            |
| --------------------- | ------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Escort (per week)     | 40-60 silver        | Covers payroll + margin          |
| Hall protection/month | 80-120 silver       | Long-term; discount for security |
| Barrow clearing       | 50-100 silver       | Flat fee; high risk premium      |
| Weregild collection   | 20-40% of collected | Percentage; band keeps a cut     |
| Raid                  | Loot split          | No fixed pay; shared risk        |
| Rune investigation    | 60-150 silver       | Rare; danger premium             |
| Village tribute       | 20-100+ silver      | Extortion, cadence matters more than name |

### Contract Market Pressure

Those price bands describe what a contract is worth when an employer can
actually raise the silver. They are not a guarantee that a settlement can pay
cleanly or that the offer appears in every season.

In practice, contract availability and contract quality are constrained by the
same pressures that govern the rest of the Rimevegr economy:

- route trouble and weak market liquidity push settlements toward escort,
  guard, and cargo-protection work
- scarcity, unpaid tribute, and poor food depth reduce how much silver an
  employer can actually reserve for advances and final payout
- outlaw pressure raises demand for patrols and guards while also thinning the
  local treasury
- feud and political strain can block some contracts entirely or distort the
  pool toward harsher, more desperate work

So a contract market is never just "what jobs exist." It is:

- what work is needed
- what work can be funded
- and what risks a settlement is desperate enough to pay men to touch

### Tribute and Extortion Benchmarks

Tribute is not priced like a tavern bill. A demanding jarl or armed band is
trying to extract surplus, obedience, and fear at the same time. The key
question is not "what can the village physically produce?" but "what can be
taken repeatedly without pushing it into open revolt or visible ruin too soon?"

Historically, the useful Norse frame here is not a tidy monthly rent. It is a
mix of:

- **`skattr`**: tax or tribute
- **`landaurar`**: land-dues or entry-dues paid in a royal orbit
- great coercive silver payments analogous to **Danegeld**, which could reach
  extraordinary state-level sums in England: Britannica notes the famous
  recurrent payments beginning in 991, while later summaries of the Anglo-Saxon
  record give landmark figures such as 10,000 pounds of silver in 991 and
  36,000 pounds in 1007

The first two matter more for Rimevegr village play. The third matters as a
warning: once tribute scales to kingdom politics, the sums cease to look like
village market prices at all.

Use these benchmarks:

| Target | Reasonable demand | Hard demand | Predatory demand |
| ------ | ----------------- | ----------- | ---------------- |
| Hamlet, one-off visit | 5-10 silver equivalent | 10-15 | 15-20 |
| Village, one-off visit | 12-20 silver equivalent | 20-30 | 30-40 |
| Large village, one-off visit | 20-35 silver equivalent | 35-50 | 50-70 |
| Small town, one-off visit | 40-80 silver equivalent | 80-120 | 120+ |

For recurring local tribute, seasonal cadence is usually more historical and
more economically coherent than weekly silver:

| Target | Light recurring `skattr` | Hard recurring `skattr` | Predatory recurring `skattr` |
| ------ | ------------------------ | ----------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Hamlet | 4-8 silver/season or goods equivalent | 8-12/season | 12-18/season |
| Village | 10-18 silver/season | 18-30/season | 30-45/season |
| Large village | 18-30 silver/season | 30-50/season | 50-75/season |
| Small town | 40-70 silver/season | 70-110/season | 110-160/season |

Cadence still matters:

| Cadence | Typical logic |
| ------- | ------------- |
| Weekly | Rare except for occupiers, ferries, garrisons, or market toll extraction |
| Monthly | Viable for direct coercion over nearby dependants with cash or toll flow |
| Seasonal | Best fit for serious Norse-style tribute: silver plus grain, stock, transport, labor |
| Annual | Best fit for formal overlord dues, royal land-dues, and law-wrapped obligations |

**Practical benchmark:** if a fourteen-man band burns roughly 58 silver per
week in hard cash and provisions, then tribute meant to matter must either:

- cover a meaningful fraction of that burden
- arrive from several settlements at once
- or come partly in grain, animals, labor, transport, and winter board

That makes 10-30 silver as a standing "village tribute" too small except for a
small hamlet shake-down or a single frightened payment.

Suggested coercive demands by payer:

| Payer | Credible periodic demand |
| ----- | ------------------------ |
| Bleakwater-sized crossing point | 3-5 silver monthly plus ferry rights, food, and forced carriage |
| Raven's Perch-sized village | 8-12 silver monthly in campaign season, or 24-36 silver seasonal |
| Moor's End-sized hamlet | 1 sheep monthly, or 6-10 silver seasonal, plus peat or labor |
| Trade village like Kolvik or Frostfjord | 10-18 silver monthly or 30-50 silver seasonal |
| Large village under pressure | 18-30 silver monthly or 50-90 silver seasonal |

Whenever tribute is paid in goods, convert by the málsverðr anchor first, then
round up for coercion. A demanded cart of grain is never priced at fair market
rate by the man doing the demanding.

### Loot Division

When a contract yields tangible loot -- weapons, armor, silver, goods --
the division follows the band archetype:

| Archetype          | Captain | Named Men | Veterans | Commons |
| ------------------ | ------- | --------- | -------- | ------- |
| Tyrant Hird        | 60%     | 20%       | 12%      | 8%      |
| Standard Svarthird | 40%     | 25%       | 20%      | 15%     |
| Fraternal Band     | 25%     | 25%       | 25%      | 25%     |
| Kin-Clan           | 20%     | 20%       | 25%      | 35%     |

Voss's Black Axes follow the Standard Svarthird split. This means Voss
takes forty percent. The Named Men split twenty-five percent among
themselves. The veterans share twenty percent. The commons -- including
Lump -- divide the remaining fifteen percent. Nobody pretends this is fair.
It is accepted because the alternative is arguing with Voss, and nobody
argues with Voss.

## The Economics of Survival

### What Poverty Feels Like

A common fighter in Voss's Black Axes earns two silver per week. Ten silver
in a good month. From this he maintains his equipment (boots wear out,
blades dull, cloaks tear), buys occasional ale (because a man who does not
drink with his comrades is not trusted), and hopes the band provides food
through foraging so he does not have to buy it.

After equipment maintenance and the small pleasures that keep a man sane, a
common fighter saves perhaps two to four silver per month. In a year, if he
survives, he might accumulate twenty to forty silver -- the price of a
sound horse, or half a year's food, or a used mail shirt. Most fighters do
not survive a full year in the Rimevegr. The mathematics of mercenary
saving are theoretical for a reason.

### What Wealth Looks Like for a Band

A healthy band's treasury holds enough for three to four weeks of payroll
plus emergency reserves. For a fourteen-man band at standard rates, that
means 150-200 silver in the chest.

Anything less than two weeks' payroll is a crisis. Anything less than one
week means the non-payment table is already rolling or about to. The band's
financial health is, in functional terms, the answer to one question: how
many weeks until the silver runs out?

### What Ruin Looks Like

When the silver runs out:

1. **Week one:** The captain announces delayed payment. The
veterans exchange looks. The commons do not notice yet.
2. **Week two:** The sergeant's ledger shows debt. Named Men
begin talking privately. Someone suggests taking work below the band's
usual standard -- guarding a merchant for half the normal rate, shaking
down a hamlet for tribute.
3. **Week three:** The non-payment table rolls. Morale drops.
Desertions begin. The band that was fourteen men is twelve, then ten.
4. **Week four:** The captain takes the worst contract
available -- the one nobody else would take, the one with the low pay and
the high body count. Because the alternative is no band at all.

## Horse Economy

The horse market in the Rimevegr is small, thin, and unforgiving. There is
no broad civilian riding culture. Most households own no horse at all.
Those that do usually own one animal expected to be useful in three ways at
once: haul light loads, carry a rider when speed matters, and survive on
worse feed than it should. That scarcity is why horse prices stay high even
in a poor land. A sound horse is not a luxury because it is pretty. It is a
luxury because it compresses distance, labor, and response time in a region
where all three can kill you.

### Breed Market Reality

| Breed               | Typical Price (silver) | Winter Keep   | Market Notes                                      |
| ------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Rimefjord pony      | 18-26                  | Moderate      | Commonest reliable riding stock                   |
| Moor-runner         | 22-32                  | Moderate-high | Price spikes when warbands want scouts            |
| Pine-cob            | 16-24                  | Moderate      | Often sold as "just a work beast" and undervalued |
| Spine pony          | 24-36                  | Low-moderate  | Expensive because supply is narrow                |
| Southblood warhorse | 55-110                 | High          | Rare, status-driven, and fragile to bad keeping   |

Rimefjord ponies hold the market together. They are the "sound horse"
behind most common price references. Moor-runners cost more than their body
size suggests because speed and open-ground pursuit create military value.
Pine-cobs are cheaper to buy than they are worth because practical people
want them and proud buyers overlook them. Spine ponies are hard to source
and almost never sold by fools. A man who gets one cheap is either buying a
cripple, buying stolen stock, or buying a feud he does not yet know about.

### Annual Horse Costs

Buying the animal is only the first expense. Keeping it through a Rimevegr
winter is the real test of whether the owner can afford it.

| Cost                             | Typical Price | Notes                                                    |
| -------------------------------- | ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Oats or grain supplement, 1 week | 4-6 copper    | For hard work, sickness, or thin grazing                 |
| Hay, 1 week                      | 5-8 copper    | Higher near thaw-end scarcity                            |
| Full winter keep, one horse      | 10-18 silver  | Hay, shelter, lost grazing, salt, wastage                |
| Shoeing cycle                    | 1 silver      | More for difficult hooves or mountain calks              |
| Saddle and simple tack           | 5-9 silver    | Good tack often costs more than the cheap horse under it |
| Pack harness                     | 2-4 silver    | Pine-cobs and mules use this more than saddles           |
| Stud fee, local hardy stallion   | 1-3 silver    | Often paid in hay, wool, or labor                        |
| Stud fee, proven war line        | 4-10 silver   | Prestige pricing, not just utility                       |

The real economic divide is not horse ownership but horse overwintering. A
household that cannot lay in enough fodder culls, sells, or gambles. In bad
years, the spring horse market fills with thin animals sold below long-
term value because the owner needs mouths off the books immediately. Rich
buyers and disciplined bands make their best acquisitions then.

### Horse Services and Failure Costs

| Service or Loss                              | Typical Price           | Why It Hurts                          |
| -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Farrier visit for cracked hoof or loose shoe | 2-5 copper              | Delay matters as much as the cost     |
| Saddle sore treatment and rest               | 1-2 silver              | Mostly lost time, not medicine        |
| Lamed horse, temporary                       | 3-8 silver equivalent   | Lost travel tempo, missed contracts   |
| Dead riding horse                            | 20-30 silver equivalent | Plus gear loss, delay, and status hit |
| Dead proven scout horse                      | 30-45 silver equivalent | Replacement is not immediate          |
| Dead broodmare                               | 25-50 silver equivalent | Future foals vanish with her          |

In pure accounting terms, horse loss is one of the fastest ways a small
band or farm falls behind schedule. A horse is stored time. When it dies,
the owner must spend labor to get time back.

### Horses as Economic Multipliers

The reason warbands, jarls, and traders keep paying these prices is that a
single horse changes the value of other work.

| Horse Use       | Economic Effect                                                       |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Mounted scout   | Contracts can be accepted farther from camp without blind travel risk |
| Pack horse      | Band carry capacity rises without exhausting fighters                 |
| Messenger horse | Political and military response times shrink from days to hours       |
| Breeding mare   | Turns fodder and patience into future transport or resale value       |
| Warhorse        | Converts silver into intimidation, pursuit, and battlefield shock     |

This is why a settlement with three competent outriders can dominate a
wider radius than a richer settlement with none. Mounted reach is not just
military. It is administrative.

## Dog Economy

Dogs live lower on the value ladder than horses, but that does not mean
they are cheap in functional terms. Most mongrels are nearly free. Proven
working dogs are not. A hall may replace a dead bucket in an afternoon. It
cannot replace a bitch that throws good guard pups, a bog dog that can hold
trail across standing water, or a wolf-warden line dog that will take
winter blood-scent without breaking.

### Dog Market Reality

| Breed               | Typical Price (silver) | Keep Cost    | Market Notes                                   |
| ------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Fjord hound         | 3-5                    | Low          | Common shore and hall warning dog              |
| Bog tracker         | 4-7                    | Low-moderate | Valuable where marsh and disappearance matter  |
| Moor lurcher        | 5-8                    | Moderate     | Prized by hunters and drovers                  |
| Hall mastiff        | 6-10                   | Moderate     | Expensive mostly because size eats silver      |
| Black Pine wolf-dog | 10-18                  | High         | Controlled breeding line, scarce and political |

Untrained pups are often gifted, traded, or dumped. Trained dogs are priced
on proof: can it track, warn, hold, return, and obey? A hall dog that has
already bitten one thief is worth more than a prettier dog that has bitten
none. Working reputation raises value faster than breed names do outside
elite kennels.

### Dog Keep and Breeding Costs

| Cost                              | Typical Price | Notes                                         |
| --------------------------------- | ------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Scrap keep, 1 week                | 1-2 copper    | For ordinary household dogs                   |
| Meat or fish supplement, 1 week   | 2-4 copper    | Needed for hard-working or breeding lines     |
| Winter keep, one working dog      | 1-3 silver    | Depends on size and whether it earns its feed |
| Kennel bedding, season            | 1-3 copper    | Straw, dry moss, hide scraps                  |
| Healer or kennel-master treatment | 2-8 copper    | Bite tears, paw rot, lung-sickness, mange     |
| Stud fee, ordinary proven dog     | 1-4 copper    | Often "pick of litter" instead of coin        |
| Stud fee, wolf-warden line        | 1-3 silver    | Paid only with permission and status          |

The economics of dogs are mostly about conversion efficiency. A good dog
turns scraps into warning, lost time into found time, and one hunter into
two. A bad dog turns scarce food into noise.

### Dog Services and Losses

| Service or Loss                     | Typical Price           | Why It Hurts                                                       |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Track a missing child or lost sheep | 2-6 copper              | Mostly payment to the handler, but the dog makes the work possible |
| Guard dog hire, one night           | 1-3 copper              | Common where theft is seasonal                                     |
| Trained tracking dog lost           | 4-9 silver equivalent   | Knowledge and trust die with it                                    |
| Hall guard dog killed               | 3-6 silver equivalent   | Immediate security gap                                             |
| Wolf-warden line dog killed         | 10-18 silver equivalent | Bloodline, training, and prestige loss                             |
| Proven breeding bitch lost          | 6-12 silver equivalent  | Future litters vanish with her                                     |

In economic terms dogs are force multipliers at household scale. One hound
does not win battles the way a warhorse can shape one. But one hound can
prevent theft, find game, hold track, warn before raiders crest the rise,
and keep a small holding viable with fewer human hands awake.
