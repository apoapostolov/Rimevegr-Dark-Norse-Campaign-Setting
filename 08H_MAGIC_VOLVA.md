# Völva — The Vision-Magic

## Neither Song nor Silence

Galdr is the throat. The rune-song vibrates through the carver's
body, and the price is written in white scars and shaking hands,
and the work is heard. Seiðr is the stillness. The Veil gathers
around the practitioner, and the price is written in shallow
breath and stolen years, and the work is felt as silence. These
are the two poles of the Rimevegr's magical practice — the loud
tradition and the quiet one, the masculine and the feminine, the
fading and the strengthening.

The völva tradition is neither.

A völva does not sing. A völva does not draw silence. A völva
sees. And the seeing is not metaphor, not poetry, not the
figurative language that storytellers use to make magic sound
grander than it is. The völva's eyes are the instrument, the way
the galdr-worker's throat is the instrument and the seiðr-wife's
breath is the instrument. The eyes are where the power enters,
where the cost accumulates, and where the practice eventually
takes everything.

The three traditions form a triad. The galdr-worker channels
rune-force through sound — divine infrastructure, degrading since
the Cracking, carried by a voice that burns the body that
produces it. The seiðr-wife channels the Veil through silence —
the boundary between the living and the dead, thinning every
decade, navigated by a stillness that empties the lungs that hold
it. The völva channels something else through sight — not
rune-force, not the Veil, but the pattern that underlies both.
The seiðr-wives call it _ørlög_, the primal law, the shape of
what was and what will be. The galdr-workers call it nothing,
because they do not have a framework for what the völva perceives,
and frameworks are how galdr-workers understand the world.

The völva does not need a framework. The völva does not need
understanding. The völva opens her eyes and the world tells her
what it is, and the telling is more than any mind was built to
hold.

## The Phenomenology of Sight

### What the Völva Sees

A völva in full practice does not see the way the living see. She
does not see surfaces — the face of a person, the wall of a hall,
the snow on a field. She sees through. Not through in the physical
sense — she cannot see behind a wall. Through in the temporal
sense. She sees the wall and the wall's history — the trees that
were felled for its timber, the hands that raised it, the years of
weather that have worn its surface, and the years of weather that
will wear it further, and the day the wall will fall, and what
will grow in the space where the wall stood. She sees all of it
simultaneously, layered over the present the way smoke layers over
fire.

This is not metaphor. This is not storytelling. This is what the
völva reports, consistently, across every documented practitioner
in the Rimevegr's history. The world, seen through the völva's
eyes, is not a snapshot. It is a palimpsest — every version of
every thing that exists, has existed, and will exist, written over
the same surface, all visible at once.

The human eye is not designed for this. The human brain is not
designed for this. The völva's practice is the practice of forcing
an organ built for surface-perception to perceive depth that
extends across time, and the organ breaks.

### The Stages of Völva Sight

**First sight — Auðsýn.** The initial manifestation, typically
between ages ten and fourteen. The child sees flickers. A face
that is young and old simultaneously — the grandmother sitting
by the fire, and the girl the grandmother was, and the corpse
the grandmother will be, all three visible in a single glance
that lasts a heartbeat and leaves the child nauseated and afraid.
The flickers are involuntary. They arrive without warning,
triggered by strong emotion in the subject — grief, joy, anger,
the heightened states that thin a person's temporal surface and
let the past and future bleed through.

At this stage, the sight is intermittent and uncontrolled. The
child cannot summon it and cannot dismiss it. She learns to flinch.
A particular quality of light, a particular angle of a face,
triggers the layering, and the child looks away, fast, before the
past and future can resolve into clarity. The flinch becomes habit.
The habit becomes personality. Völva-children are the ones who
never meet your eyes, who look slightly past you, who stare at
the ground or the wall or the fire — anything without a past deep
enough to trigger the sight.

**Second sight — Djúpsýn.** Achieved through training, typically
in the late teens or early twenties. The völva learns to control
the sight — to open it deliberately, to direct it, to hold it on
a subject long enough to read what the layering reveals. This is
the working stage, the stage that produces the readings and the
prophecies that the Rimevegr values and fears.

At second sight, the völva can read a person's ørlög — the
pattern of what has been and what is coming. Not specific events.
Not dates and names and the mechanical details that clients
always want. Shapes. Directions. The weight of a life's momentum,
the angle of its trajectory, the places where the pattern bends
and the bending means something — a choice approaching, a change
arriving, a death drawing near. The reading is imprecise by the
standards of someone who wants to know whether their husband will
survive the Long Dark. It is precise by the standards of someone
who understands that the future is not a road but a river, and
rivers have currents, and the völva can see the current.

The control comes at a cost. Opening the sight deliberately is
harder than receiving it involuntarily, the way swimming
upstream is harder than being carried. The völva's eyes strain.
Not figuratively — the muscles that control focus, that adjust
the lens, that track movement across the visual field, are being
asked to process information that exceeds their design parameters.
Headaches follow every deliberate opening. The headaches become
migraines. The migraines become a constant, low-grade pressure
behind the eyes that does not fully resolve between sessions.

Second-sight völvas develop a characteristic squint — not from
poor distance vision, but from the habitual narrowing of the
visual field that reduces the volume of temporal information
entering the eyes at any given moment. A völva who opens her
eyes fully at second sight sees everything at once — every
person in the room, every object, every surface, all layered
with their pasts and futures, all demanding attention
simultaneously. The squint is a survival mechanism. It limits
the input to what the brain can process without breaking.

**Third sight — Örlögsýn.** The full vision. Fate-sight. The
stage that the oldest völvas achieve and that the youngest völvas
aspire to and that neither group discusses honestly, because
honest discussion of örlögsýn requires admitting what it costs.

At third sight, the völva does not choose to see. The sight is
continuous. The layering is permanent. The völva perceives the
temporal depth of everything in her visual field, all the time,
without rest, without the ability to close the sight the way she
can close her eyes. She can close her eyes. The sight does not
stop. The layering is not dependent on light or on the physical
eye — it has migrated inward, into the processing centres of the
brain, and the brain generates the temporal overlay whether the
eyes are open or shut.

This is the stage at which the distinction between sight and
hallucination becomes academic.

A third-sight völva sees the dead. Not through the Veil, not
through seiðr-contact, but directly — the dead are part of the
temporal record, and the temporal record is what the völva
perceives. A person who died in this room ten years ago is
visible to the third-sight völva as a layer in the room's
palimpsest. The dead man sits where he sat. He eats the meal he
ate. He speaks the words he spoke. He is not a ghost. He is not a
spirit. He is a fact — a recorded moment in the room's history,
playing on a continuous loop that only the völva can perceive.

The völva also sees what has not happened yet. Not the future as
a fixed road — the seiðr-wives are correct that the future is
fluid — but the future as a set of weighted probabilities, each
one visible, each one layered over the present like translucent
parchment stacked on a desk. The most probable outcomes are the
most opaque. The least probable are nearly invisible. The völva
reads the stack, and the reading tells her what is likely, what
is possible, and what is almost certain, and the certainties are
the ones that weigh on her the most, because the certainties
include the deaths, and the deaths are always visible, and the
völva cannot look at a living person without seeing the dead
version layered over the breathing one.

## The Cost of Seeing

### The Eyes

The eyes fail first. This is the universal constant of völva
practice. Every völva who achieves second sight begins to lose her
physical vision. The loss is progressive, irreversible, and
directly correlated with the frequency and depth of deliberate
sight-opening.

**Dryness.** The earliest symptom, appearing within the first year
of second-sight practice. The eyes stop producing adequate tears.
The surface of the eye — the cornea — dries, cracks, becomes
vulnerable to infection and irritation. The völva's eyes are
permanently reddened, perpetually uncomfortable, and she blinks
constantly in an attempt to redistribute moisture that is not
there. The dryness is not caused by environment. It is caused by
the sustained, intense focus that sight-opening requires — the
muscles that should be relaxing and releasing tears are locked in
the narrow squint that limits temporal input, and the locking
prevents the normal blink-and-moisture cycle.

Herb-craft helps. A paste of chamomile and rendered fat, applied
to the lids before sleep, reduces the cracking. But the paste
treats the symptom, not the cause. The cause is the practice. The
practice does not stop.

**Narrowing.** The visual field contracts. A second-sight völva
who has practiced for five years sees the world through a
narrowing tunnel. Peripheral vision degrades — not gradually, not
evenly, but in patches. Dark spots appear at the edges of the
visual field, encroaching inward. The spots are not physical
damage to the retina. They are the Veil.

This is the detail that separates völva sight-loss from any
ordinary condition. The darkness that eats the visual field is
not blindness. It is the Veil, which has found a new surface to
press against. The völva's practice has thinned the boundary
between her perception and the temporal record, and the Veil —
which is the boundary between the living and the dead, between
the present and all other times — has moved inward. It sits
behind her eyes. It presses against the visual cortex from the
inside. And where it presses, the ordinary world goes dark.

But the dark is not empty.

The völva who loses peripheral vision to the Veil's encroachment
does not see nothing in the dark patches. She sees the things that
the Veil carries — the dead, the past, the future, the temporal
depth that the central vision still struggles to process. The
dark becomes a window. The narrowing of physical sight is the
widening of völva sight. What the eye loses, the practice gains.
The tunnel of ordinary vision shrinks while the field of
temporal perception expands, and the trade is not optional and is
not reversible.

This is the mechanism behind the Rimevegr's most persistent
piece of völva lore: that the blind völva sees the most. It is
not mysticism. It is not romance. It is the observation that a
völva whose physical vision has been entirely consumed by the
Veil's encroachment has had her ordinary sight fully replaced
by the temporal sight, and the temporal sight does not require
functioning eyes. It requires functioning neural pathways, and
the Veil, pressing inward, has rewired those pathways to receive
the signal that the practice was always training them for.

**Glaucoma.** The pressure builds. The Veil's encroachment is
not just perceptual — it is physical. The intraocular pressure in
a völva's eyes increases measurably over years of practice. The
pressure compresses the optic nerve. The compression causes pain
— a deep, drilling ache behind the eyes that does not respond to
herbs or cold compresses or rest. The pain is constant. It varies
in intensity but never reaches zero. A working völva lives with
a headache that has lasted years and will last until she dies.

The pressure causes halos. The völva sees rings of light around
every light source — fire, sunlight through cloud, the pale glow
of snow-reflected moonlight. The halos are beautiful and they are
a symptom. They mean the optic nerve is being crushed, slowly,
by the same force that is eating the visual field, and the
crushing is permanent.

The progression from narrowing to tunnel vision to blindness
takes ten to twenty years in a practicing völva. A woman who
achieves second sight at eighteen is squinting at twenty-five,
tunnel-visioned at thirty-five, and blind by forty. Not every
völva follows this schedule — Ragnhild's völva contact at the
Allthing was still sighted at fifty, though her visual field
was reduced to a coin-width of central vision — but the schedule
is the median, and deviations from it are measured in years, not
decades.

### Bleeding

The eyes bleed. Not from injury. Not from infection. From the
practice.

A völva who opens the full sight — second or third stage, a
deliberate deep reading rather than a passive observation —
produces a characteristic physical response: the capillaries in
the sclera rupture. The white of the eye fills with blood. The
effect is bilateral and immediate — both eyes, simultaneously,
turning red from the outer edge inward. The blood does not
obscure the vision (the völva's vision at this point is not
dependent on the physical eye in the normal sense). It collects
in the lower lid. It runs down the cheeks.

The bleeding is the Veil's pressure exceeding the structural
tolerance of the eye's blood vessels. The temporal information
that the völva is processing is not massless. It carries weight —
not physical weight, but the same kind of force that the Veil
exerts when it presses on the living. The force enters through
the eyes and the eyes are not built to contain it, and the
smallest, most fragile structures fail first.

A völva who has practiced for a decade bleeds during every major
reading. The bleeding has become part of the ritual's visual
vocabulary — a client who watches the völva's eyes fill with red
knows the reading is real, knows the sight has opened, knows that
the woman sitting before them is paying in blood for the
information she is about to deliver. The blood is not theatre. The
blood is the receipt.

Young völvas, in the early years of second sight, are frightened
by the bleeding. They wipe their eyes and see red on the cloth and
they are afraid. The teacher says what every teacher says: "The
eyes weep because they see too much. The tears of a völva are
red because the sight is not free. When the bleeding stops, the
sight has stopped. Pray the bleeding never stops."

### Hallucinations

At third sight — and sometimes at advanced second sight, in völvas
who have pushed the practice harder than their training supports —
the boundary between perception and hallucination dissolves.

The völva sees the temporal layering continuously. Past and future
are overlaid on the present at all times. In a controlled
practitioner with adequate training and rest, the layering is
manageable — the völva can distinguish the present layer from the
historical layers, can identify which version of a person is the
living version and which is the dead version, can navigate the
palimpsest without losing her place in the current moment.

When control degrades — through exhaustion, through insufficient
rest between readings, through the accumulated neural damage of
years of Veil-pressure on the optic pathways — the layers merge.
The völva cannot tell which version of the room is real. The dead
man sitting by the fire is as vivid as the living man sitting
beside him. The wall that fell ten years ago is as solid as the
wall that stands now. The child who died last winter is as present
as the child who lives, and the völva reaches for the dead child
with the same hand she would use to reach for the living one, and
the reach closes on nothing, and the nothing is the moment when
the völva realizes she has lost the thread.

The hallucinations are not random. They are accurate. Every
vision the völva sees is something that happened or something that
will happen. The hallucination is not a failure of perception — it
is a failure of temporal sorting. The information is correct. The
temporal tag is missing. The völva sees a true thing and cannot
determine when it is true, and in a life where past and future
and present are equally real, the inability to sort them is not
confusion. It is a different kind of madness — the madness of
seeing everything and knowing nothing about when.

The Rimevegr calls this condition _sjónarvillt_ — sight-lost, but
not in the sense of blindness. Lost in the sight. Wandering
inside one's own perception. A sjónarvillt völva is present in
the room and absent from the moment. She answers questions that
were asked ten years ago. She warns of dangers that passed last
winter. She greets people who have not arrived yet, and when they
arrive — days or weeks later — the greeting she gave is the
greeting she gives, and the witnesses who heard both are unsettled
not by the repetition but by the implication: that the völva, in
her wandering, touched the real future and spoke to it, and the
future answered.

The sjónarvillt episodes are intermittent in the early stages.
The völva drifts, returns, drifts again. The intervals between
episodes shorten over years. A völva who experiences one episode
per month at forty experiences one per week at fifty and one per
day at sixty, and by that point the episodes are not episodes.
They are the baseline. The völva lives inside the sight and
visits the present occasionally, and the visits become shorter,
and the sight becomes everything, and the woman who was there
is somewhere else — not dead, not gone, but distributed across
the temporal record like water poured into sand, present
everywhere and graspable nowhere.

## The Rites of Motionlessness — Kyrrseta

The body is the instrument's case. The eyes are the instrument.
And the case, in the völva tradition, is a hindrance.

A völva who wishes to deepen her sight must reduce the body's
demands on her attention. Physical sensation competes with
temporal perception. Hunger, thirst, the ache of a knee, the itch
of a healing wound — these are anchors to the present moment, and
the present moment is the one temporal layer that the völva is
trying to see past. To see deeper, the völva must become less
present. To become less present, the völva must become less
physical. To become less physical, the völva sits.

The _kyrrseta_ — the sitting-still — is the völva tradition's
central practice, and it is the practice that produces the most
recognizable and most disturbing physical consequence of the völva
path: the wasting.

### First Rite — Halda Kyrð (Holding Stillness)

The first rite is learned during training and practiced throughout
the working years. The völva sits in a fixed position — cross-
legged on the ground, on a stone seat, in a carved chair if the
settlement provides one — and she does not move. Not for an hour.
Not for a day. For the duration of the reading, which at second
sight can extend from hours to days depending on the depth and
complexity of the ørlög being read.

During the first rite, the body is maintained. Attendants — young
völvas, students, settlement women who have taken on the
caretaking role — bring water and broth to the practitioner's
lips. They adjust her clothing when the cold threatens. They
cover her eyes with damp cloth when the bleeding begins. They
maintain the body because the völva has left it, not in the
seiðr-wife's sense of the word — the völva is still present, still
conscious, still perceiving — but in the sense that the body's
needs have become irrelevant to the mind's activity, and the mind
will not return to attend to those needs until the reading is
complete.

The first rite's cost is discomfort, stiffness, and the muscle
atrophy that comes from extended immobility. A völva who performs
a three-day reading at first rite returns to movement with
legs that barely support her, hands that shake from disuse rather
than from Veil-damage, and a hunger that takes days to satisfy.
Recovery takes a week. The damage is temporary.

### Second Rite — Ganga Innað (Going Inward)

The second rite is the first deliberate sacrifice. The völva
chooses to reduce her physical capacity in order to increase her
temporal sight, and the reduction is permanent.

The practice: extended immobility, measured in weeks rather than
days. The völva assumes the sitting position and does not leave it.
She is fed. She is watered. She is cleaned by her attendants. She
does not stand. She does not walk. She does not use her legs for
any purpose, because every signal the legs send to the brain — the
proprioceptive feedback of weight-bearing, the stretch of tendons,
the pressure on joints — is a signal that anchors the mind in the
present body, and the present body is what she is trying to
transcend.

The result is predictable and deliberate: muscle atrophy in the
lower limbs. The legs weaken. The ankles lose their range. The
knees stiffen. After a sustained second rite — three weeks, four
weeks, the duration varies by practitioner and by the depth of
sight being sought — the völva cannot walk unaided. She must be
carried, or she must be supported by her attendants, or she must
use a staff, and the staff becomes the symbol that the Rimevegr
associates with the völva tradition — not because staffs are
mystical, but because the women who carry them can no longer stand
without them.

The immobility is not the cost. The immobility is the method. The
cost is what the immobility produces: a body that has been
deliberately reduced, deliberately weakened, deliberately stripped
of the physical capacity that the culture values in every other
context. A woman who cannot walk is a woman who cannot flee. A
woman who cannot stand is a woman who depends on others for every
physical need. The völva who undergoes the second rite has chosen
dependency — the complete, irreversible dependency of a body that
cannot sustain itself — as the price of seeing further than any
intact body can see.

This is not martyrdom. The völvas are precise about this. The
wasting is not sacrifice for its own sake. It is engineering. The
body is a machine that produces sensory input, and most of that
input is noise — the background hum of physical existence that
the brain processes at the expense of the temporal signals the
völva is trying to receive. Reduce the noise, and the signal
strengthens. Reduce the body, and the sight deepens. The
relationship is not symbolic. It is mechanical. Every nerve that
stops sending proprioceptive data is a nerve whose bandwidth
is available for temporal processing, and the brain, freed
from the demands of a walking body, redirects that bandwidth
toward the sight.

The young völvas who tend the seated ones understand this in
theory. In practice, watching a woman they revere waste from a
strong body to a dependent one is an experience that tests their
commitment to the tradition more severely than any reading or any
bleeding. The attendant carries the völva to the privy. The
attendant washes the völva's legs, which are thinning, the muscle
visible through skin that has become translucent from disuse. The
attendant feeds the völva, because the völva's hands are occupied
with the sight — not physically occupied, but attentionally
occupied, the hands forgotten the way one forgets a tool one is
not using. The attendant does this daily, for years, and the
attendant knows that if she progresses in the tradition, she
will be the one sitting, and someone else will be carrying her.

### Third Rite — Aftaka Sjálfs (Unmaking of Self)

The third rite is the final practice. Not every völva attempts it.
Not every völva who attempts it survives. It is the rite that the
tradition's founders — the pre-Cracking völvas whose names are
remembered and whose methods are not — performed in order to
achieve the deepest possible sight, and the price of the deepest
possible sight is the self.

The three unmakings parallel the three soul-components, and the
parallel is deliberate:

**The first unmaking is the unmaking of the hugr.** The mind-soul,
the seat of reason and will, is the last anchor holding the völva
in the present. The first unmaking releases it. The practice is
the most extreme form of the kyrrseta — weeks of absolute
immobility, sensory deprivation, minimal sustenance, the
systematic reduction of every input that the hugr uses to maintain
its grip on the current moment. The völva sits in a dark room.
She is fed enough to sustain life, no more. She does not speak.
She does not listen. She does not engage with the present in any
way, because the present is the hugr's domain, and the hugr must
be emptied.

When the hugr releases — and the seiðr-wives who have witnessed
it describe the moment as "the candle going out behind the eyes"
— the völva enters a state of continuous temporal perception
without rational mediation. She sees everything. She understands
nothing. The pattern is visible but the meaning is not, because
meaning requires the hugr to interpret, and the hugr is gone. The
raw data of past and future floods the mind, and the mind receives
it the way a lake receives rain — without resistance, without
structure, without the capacity to do anything with what it
holds.

**The second unmaking is the unmaking of the hamr.** The shape-soul,
the human form, the composure that keeps the body recognizably
human. The second unmaking releases the völva's attachment to
her physical form. The wasting, already advanced from the second
rite, progresses to its conclusion. The body becomes a framework
— bone and skin and the minimum of flesh needed to maintain
respiration and circulation. The attendants who tend a völva
undergoing the second unmaking are tending a person who no longer
recognizes the body as her own. She does not feel pain, because
pain requires the hamr to locate the body in space, and the hamr
has released its grip. She does not feel cold, because cold is a
signal to the body to act, and the body has stopped acting. She
is present in the sight and absent from the flesh, and the flesh
continues its mechanical functions without her direction, the way
a heart beats without conscious instruction.

**The third unmaking is the unmaking of the fylgja.** The
guardian spirit, the divine inheritance, the last connection
between the individual and the world that made her. The fylgja's
release is the most poorly documented of the three, because the
völvas who have completed it are no longer capable of describing
what happened. The seiðr-wives who have observed it from outside
the trance describe a moment of absolute stillness — not the
Veil's silence, not the seiðr-wife's quieting, but a stillness
that is specific to the völva's tradition. The woman stops. Not
stops breathing, not stops moving — she has not been moving for
weeks. She stops being. The quality of her presence changes. The
seiðr-wife, perceiving through the Veil, sees the spiritual
signature collapse from a point to a line to a plane — the
individual becoming distributed, spread across the temporal
record, no longer located in a single moment or a single body.

The völva who has completed all three unmakings is, by any
definition the living can offer, no longer a person. She breathes.
She maintains biological function. She can be kept alive by
attendants for years, decades — the body is durable, even wasted,
and the autonomic systems continue without the self to direct
them. But the self is gone. Distributed into the temporal record.
Seeing everything, knowing everything, accessible to no one.

The tradition holds that a völva who has completed the three
unmakings has unlocked the secrets of fate. The ørlög, the primal
law, is fully visible to her. She sees the pattern that governs
all things — the past, the present, the future, the connections
between them, the shape of what must be. She sees it because she
has become part of it. She is no longer an observer. She is a
thread in the pattern, woven into the record, experiencing all
times simultaneously because she is no longer bound to any one
time.

Whether this is true cannot be verified. The women who have
completed the three unmakings cannot report what they see. They
cannot speak. They cannot signal. They are alive and they are
gone, and the attendants who care for them — washing the wasted
body, feeding broth through lips that do not close voluntarily,
covering the bleeding eyes that stare at nothing the living can
perceive — do so out of reverence for what the tradition says the
woman has become, not out of certainty that the tradition is
correct.

Two völvas in the Rimevegr have completed all three unmakings and
are still alive — if alive is the correct word for a body that
breathes and a person who is not there. One is at Icebreak, tended
by Ragnhild's network, a woman who sat down eleven years ago and
has not stood since and whose eyes bleed continuously, a thin red
line from each lid that the attendants wipe every hour. The other
is at a settlement in the eastern Rimevegr whose name is not
spoken outside the völva tradition, tended by three women who have
dedicated their lives to maintaining the body that houses
whatever the third unmaking produced.

The tradition says these women are the Rimevegr's greatest
practitioners. The tradition says they see fate itself. The
tradition says that if the right question could be asked in the
right way, they could answer anything — the outcome of the
Cracking, the future of the wards, the truth about the gods'
silence, the shape of what is coming.

Nobody has asked. Nobody knows how. And the women who might have
known are distributed across time, seeing everything, answering
nothing, breathing in rooms where the blood runs from their eyes
and the attendants wipe it away and the fire burns low and the
silence is not the seiðr-wife's silence and is not the Veil's
silence and is not any silence that the living have a word for.

## The Blind Völva

Blindness is the practice's destination, and the tradition has
made the destination sacred.

A blind völva — a woman whose physical vision has been entirely
consumed by the Veil's encroachment, replaced by the continuous
temporal sight that third sight provides — is the tradition's
most venerated figure. Not because blindness is holy. Because
blindness is proof. The woman who has lost her eyes to the sight
has paid the full price, and the full price buys the fullest
vision, and the fullest vision is the thing the tradition exists
to produce.

Blind völvas are worshipped. The word is not too strong. In
settlements that maintain the völva tradition — and they are
fewer each generation, as the practitioners age and the students
thin — a blind völva occupies a position that is equal parts
prophet, elder, and living relic. She is consulted on every
significant decision. She is asked to read the ørlög before every
major event — a marriage, a building, a voyage, a war. Her word
carries more weight than a jarl's, because the jarl sees what is
in front of him and the blind völva sees what is behind and what
is ahead, and the thing that is ahead matters more than the thing
that is in front.

The worship is not comfortable. The blind völva is carried to
the consultation by attendants. Her eyes are covered — not for
her benefit, but for the benefit of the client, because the
sight of the Veil-consumed eyes is disturbing. The sclera is
permanently red from capillary damage. The iris has faded to a
pale, clouded grey, the pigment lost to years of Veil-pressure.
The pupils do not respond to light. The eyes are open and they
see nothing that the living can perceive, and the client who
meets the blind völva's gaze — the unfocused, blood-rimmed,
clouded gaze of a woman who is looking at something that is not
in the room — does not forget that gaze, and does not seek a
second consultation unless the need is desperate.

The blind völva speaks in the present tense about the future. She
does not say "it will happen." She says "it is happening." Because
for her, it is. The future is a layer in the palimpsest, as real
as the present, as visible as the past. The distinction between
will-be and is has collapsed, and the language follows the
perception. Clients find this disturbing. A woman who says "your
son dies in the spring" when the spring is six months away is
a woman who has witnessed the death already, and the certainty
in her voice is the certainty of a witness, not a prophet, and
witnesses are harder to argue with than prophets.

## Madness — The Völva's Particular Forms

Every tradition has its madness. Galdr-workers go rigid — the
body locked in the posture of the last carving, the mind fixed
on the last rune, the tremor extending from the hands to the
entire body until the practitioner is a shaking ruin who can no
longer hold a chisel but cannot stop trying. Seiðr-wives go
thin — the boundary between self and dead dissolving until the
woman walks and talks and breathes but the person is gone,
replaced by the aggregate presence of everyone she has ever
contacted through the Veil.

Völva madness is different. Völva madness is vision without
anchor.

### Sjónarœði — Sight-Fury

The most acute form. The völva's sight opens fully, without
control, without the narrowing squint that manages the input.
Every temporal layer of every object in the visual field arrives
simultaneously at full intensity. The past, the present, the
future, all of them real, all of them vivid, all of them
demanding attention. The brain cannot process it. The neural
pathways that have been trained to receive temporal data
overload, and the overload produces not shutdown but
acceleration — the mind racing through the layers at a speed
that the conscious self cannot follow.

The völva in sjónarœði speaks rapidly, continuously, without
pause, describing what she sees. The descriptions are accurate —
verifiably, specifically, horrifyingly accurate. She describes
events that happened a century ago in this room. She describes
events that will happen tomorrow. She describes the death of the
person standing in front of her, and the death of that person's
children, and the burning of the settlement, and the regrowth of
the forest over the ruins, and the people who will build on the
ruins and who will die there and who will be buried in the ground
that was once a floor. She sees it all. She says it all. She
cannot stop.

The sjónarœði can last hours. The body sustains it — the
adrenaline response keeps the heart running, the voice running,
the lungs running. The damage is neural. The overload burns
pathways the way an electrical surge burns wire. A völva who
survives a full sjónarœði episode emerges with permanent
cognitive damage — gaps in the temporal sorting that the practice
depends on, holes in the ability to distinguish past from future,
a degradation of the second sight's precision that turns the
controlled reading into something approximate, unreliable, and
frightening.

### Þögulœði — Silent Fury

The inverse. The völva's sight opens, and the mind, instead of
racing, stops. The temporal layers arrive and the brain refuses
them. Not a conscious refusal — a protective shutdown. The neural
pathways that carry temporal data close like valves, and the mind
goes dark.

The völva in þögulœði does not speak. She does not move. She does
not respond to stimuli. She is conscious — the seiðr-wives who
have examined þögulœði patients confirm that the mind is present,
the hugr is intact, the self has not departed. The self has
retreated. It has pulled back from the sight the way a hand pulls
back from a flame, and it will not come forward again until the
sight subsides.

The þögulœði can last days. During that time, the völva must be
maintained by attendants — fed, watered, kept warm, positioned
to prevent the pressure sores that immobility causes. The
attendants do not know when the episode will end. The völva does
not know when the episode will end. The sight subsides when the
sight subsides, and the mind comes forward when the mind decides
the sight is safe, and the decision is not conscious and cannot
be hastened.

A völva who experiences frequent þögulœði is a völva whose
practice is exceeding her capacity. The episodes are the mind's
refusal to continue receiving what the practice is sending, and
the refusal is not negotiable. The teacher's response is to
reduce the practice — fewer readings, shallower depth, longer
recovery periods. The student's response is often to push harder,
because the sight is addictive, and the addiction does not respect
the mind's limits, and the next episode is deeper and longer and
the recovery is slower.

### Fate-Reading Madness — Örlögsgalinn

The most insidious form, because it does not look like madness
from the outside.

The völva who develops örlögsgalinn functions. She reads. She
reports. She provides accurate, useful information to her clients.
The madness is not in the reading. The madness is in the
relationship to the reading.

The örlögsgalinn völva has lost the ability to care about anything
except the pattern. The ørlög — the shape of fate — has become
the only thing that matters. People are not people. They are
threads in the pattern. Events are not events. They are nodes
in the weave. The death of a child is not a tragedy. It is a
convergence point. The burning of a settlement is not a
catastrophe. It is a pattern completing itself.

The völva who has reached örlögsgalinn watches a family receive
the news that their son has died in a raid, and she sees the
pattern — the choices that led to the raid, the decisions that
placed the son on the road, the ørlög-threads that converged at
the moment of the killing — and she finds it beautiful. Not the
death. The pattern. The terrible, precise, inevitable shape of
cause and effect that produced the death, visible to her in its
full temporal extent, from the grandmother's choice to marry the
grandfather through the father's choice to farm this land through
the son's choice to take the road — all of it visible, all of it
connected, all of it inevitable, and the inevitability is what
the örlögsgalinn mind finds beautiful, because inevitability is
the pattern at its purest, and the pattern is all that the
örlögsgalinn völva can see.

This is not sociopathy. The völva has not lost empathy through
cruelty or damage. She has lost it through replacement. The
pattern has filled the space where empathy lived, and the pattern
is larger than empathy, and the mind that holds the pattern does
not have room for both. The choice was not conscious. The
replacement was not voluntary. The sight grew, and the pattern
grew with it, and the person who cared about the threads shrank
until the threads were all that remained.

The attendants who serve an örlögsgalinn völva are the only
people who see it clearly, because they are the people closest
to the woman and therefore the people who notice when the woman
stops being the woman. She still speaks. She still reads. She
still reports. But the voice has changed — not in the seiðr-wife's
way, not borrowed from the dead, but drained of the quality that
made it human. The örlögsgalinn völva speaks about fate the way a
carpenter speaks about wood grain: with precision, with expertise,
with a craftsman's appreciation for the material, and with no
recognition that the material is made of lives.

_The völva's path is the path of the eye that will not close.
Galdr burns. Seiðr drowns. The völva sees, and the seeing does
not stop, and the cost of not stopping is everything that makes
the seer a person rather than an instrument. The blind völva is
worshipped because she has paid the price. The mad völva is feared
because she has paid a different price — the price of seeing
clearly enough to stop caring what she sees. The Rimevegr needs
its völvas the way it needs its wards and its seiðr-wives and its
bone-lot casters — it needs someone to see what is coming, because
what is coming cannot be avoided but it can be faced, and facing
requires forewarning, and forewarning requires a woman who is
willing to trade her eyes and her body and her self for the
privilege of watching the world's pattern unfold, one thread at a
time, until the threads are all she sees and the woman is gone
and the pattern remains, beautiful and terrible and true._
