# Dictionary of Norse Terms — Iron Ledger

<!-- notion-export:toc -->


**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr

**Purpose:** A comprehensive glossary of terminology, language, idiom, and usage specific to this setting. Covers Old Norse roots, in-world coinages, mercenary slang, trade language, supernatural vocabulary, material culture, and the everyday speech of the Rimevegr. Each entry is a tool for prose -- use it to give characters the right word for the right thing. A man who calls his axe a "brand" knows a different world from a man who calls it a "chopper."

**Usage rules:**

- Characters use these terms naturally. Never define a term in narration. Meaning arrives through context.
- The glossary includes real Old Norse words (adapted for readability), Rimevegr coinages, and mercenary slang that developed in the three centuries since the Cracking.
- Pronunciation does not matter. The reader will sound them in their head however they see fit. Spelling is for the eye.
- Terms marked **[Historical]** derive from attested Old Norse or early medieval Scandinavian usage. Terms marked **[Coined]** are invented for this setting. Terms marked **[Slang]** are mercenary or common speech, often crude.

## M

**Mace** — [Historical] A heavy weapon with a weighted head on a wooden haft. Crushes armor and bone without needing an edge. Effective against mail but slow. In the Rimevegr, maces are settlement-guard weapons, not march weapons -- too heavy for their utility.

**Maggot-flour** — [Slang] Flour so old it has weevils. The band picks out the largest ones and grinds the rest. Protein is protein.

**Mail** — [Historical] See _brynja_. Chainmail. Interlocked iron rings forming a flexible metal fabric. The best armor available in the Rimevegr. Wearing mail is a statement of wealth and professional status.

**Mail-coif** — [Historical] A hood of chainmail worn over a padded cap. Protects the head and neck. Heavy enough to cause neck strain on long marches. Worn only when combat is expected.

**Mallet** — [Historical] A wooden hammer. Used for driving tent pegs, setting rivets on an anvil, butchering, and discipline (a blow from a mallet's flat face across the shoulders). Less lethal than a metal hammer. More available.

**Mantle** — [Historical] A large cloak or wrap, often of heavy wool, sometimes fur-lined. Worn over everything else. The outermost layer of protection. A man's mantle determines how cold he sleeps.

**March-bread** — [Coined] See _hard-bread_. The hard, dry bread carried on the march. Lasts weeks. Must be soaked in water or broth to chew. The taste is absence -- it tastes of nothing at all, which is better than tasting of mold.

**March-order** — [Coined] The sequence in which the band walks. Point man, scouts flanking, main body, rear guard. March-order determines who gets ambushed first and who has time to react. Sloppy march-order kills bands.

**Mare** — [Historical] A female horse. More docile than stallions, cheaper to keep. The band's few horses are mares. A mare that can carry packs through snow is worth more than most men.

**Mark** — [Historical] A unit of weight for precious metals. About 200 grams. "A mark of silver" is a substantial sum -- enough to buy a horse or a season's grain.

**Mast-light** — [Coined] A lamp mounted at the top of a ship's mast. On coastal fjords, mast-lights are the first sign of approaching vessels. On land, metaphorical: the first visible sign of anything approaching.

**Mattock** — [Historical] A digging tool with a heavy blade at right angles to the handle. Used for breaking frozen ground, cutting roots, digging latrines. The band carries one. It is also a credible skull-crushing weapon.

**Meadow-sweet** — [Historical] A wild herb used to flavor mead and ale. Also a mild pain-reliever when brewed as tea. Contains salicylates -- the band does not know this word but knows the effect.

**Mead** — [Historical] Honey wine. The prestige drink of Norse culture. In the Rimevegr, mead is a settlement luxury. The band cannot produce or carry it. Tasting mead means the band has reached civilization, however briefly.

**Mead-debt** — [Slang] The social obligation incurred by accepting someone's hospitality. "We owe a mead-debt at Ashen Reach" means the band received hospitality and has not reciprocated.

**Mannát** — [Coined] Man-eating. The first soulbane: the deliberate eating of human flesh. More than starvation or murder -- it marks a willing crossing that most settlements regard as spiritually final.

**Meal-time** — [Coined] The band eats twice: morning porridge and evening stew. Meal-time is a social institution as much as a nutritional one. Disputes surface at meal-time. Alliances are visible in seating.

**Metal-rot** — [Coined] Generic term for corrosion of any metal. "The chest has metal-rot" means the iron bands are failing. Everything metal rots in the Rimevegr's damp cold.

**Midden** — [Historical] A garbage dump. Every settlement has one. Archaeologically valuable; practically disgusting. The smell announces it. In the Rimevegr, middens are downwind and away from the water source, same as latrines.

**Midwinter** — [Historical] The darkest point of the Long Dark. The shortest day. In historical Norse culture, a time of feasts and ceremony. In the Rimevegr, midwinter is survival. Feasts happen in halls that can afford them. The band builds a bigger fire and tells stories.

**Minnidraugr** — [Coined] Memory-ghost. Not the dead person, but the dead event lingering -- a loop of fear, labor, grief, or violence impressed into place.

**Minnisvarf** — [Coined] Memory-pull. The drag by which a Veil-thinned place catches the mind and forces it into old experience.

**Milk-tooth** — [Slang] A recruit so young he has not seen a real fight. "He has milk-teeth" means the man is green and will panic when steel comes out.

**Mortar** — [Historical] 1. A bowl for grinding herbs or grain with a pestle. 2. A mixture of lime, sand, and water used for stone construction. The Rimevegr has little stone construction. Mortar means the grinding bowl here.

**Moss-lamp** — [Coined] Sphagnum moss soaked in fat and lit. A crude emergency light source. Burns for minutes, not hours. Smells terrible. Better than barrow-black.

**Mouldy-bread** — [Slang] Food that is technically edible but barely. "It is mouldy-bread" means the situation is survivable but unpleasant.

**Mule** — [Historical] A horse-donkey cross bred for carrying. The band's mule carries the strongbox, surplus supplies, and occasionally a wounded man. The mule has no name. It does not need one. It is the most reliable member of the band.

**Mule-price** — [Coined] A medium-large expense. Less than a horse-price, more than what most commons carry. Losing a mule costs the equivalent of several weeks' wages, plus whatever the mule was carrying.

## N

**Nail** — [Historical] Iron nails are trade goods. Each one is hand-forged. A pouch of nails has purchasing power. Ship nails, coffin nails, construction nails -- different shapes for different purposes. The band carries a small supply for repairs.

**Námsvisur** — [Coined] Learning-songs. How children memorize survival knowledge: counting (attached to death-counts), lineage (sung backward through generations), seasons (storm signs, ice indicators, edible vs. poisonous plants). The song is the curriculum. The test is whether the child survives.

**Named Man** — [Coined] A specialist or officer in the band. Extra pay, voice in decisions, established reputation. Each has an earned call-name, a trigger, and an agenda. Named Men are the band's skeleton. Lose too many and the body collapses.

**Nave** — [Historical] The central hub of a wheel. By extension: the center of anything. "The nave of the problem" means the core issue. Settlement use -- the nave of the village is the well or the hall.

**Neck-ring** — [Historical] A torc or rigid ring worn around the neck. Status marker. In the Rimevegr, neck-rings of twisted bronze or silver indicate wealth and rank. A jarl's neck-ring is a badge of office.

**Needle** — [Historical] A bone or iron sewing needle. Without needles, clothing cannot be repaired. Bone needles are carved from any long bone. Iron needles are trade goods -- small, valuable, easily lost.

**Net** — [Historical] Cordage woven into a mesh for catching fish or birds. Nets are heavy to carry and slow to deploy. The band carries one small gill-net for river crossings near fish runs. Gest considers it a luxury.

**Niding** — [Historical] A man without honor. The worst thing a man can be called. A niding has broken oaths, fled from fights, betrayed trust. The word is specific and final -- once applied, it does not wash off.

**Night-sight** — [Coined] The ability some practitioners develop to see in low light after extended galdr or seiðr practice. Not supernatural -- the body adapts to working by firelight and starlight. Galdr-workers who carve in dim conditions develop it faster. Mechanically irrelevant but narratively atmospheric.

**Náttmál** — [Coined] Night-speech. The hidden or inward face of a rune: the aspect used in secrecy, fear, concealment, coercion, and deep-working rather than public inscription.

**Night market** — [Trade] Illegal trading session outside settlement walls after dark. Emerges when outlaw pressure reaches threatening levels. Offers rare goods, stolen property, and intelligence -- but attending carries legal and disease risks. See §18.14.3.

**Night-soil** — [Historical] Human excrement collected for fertilizer. Settlement practice. The euphemism is thin. The smell is not.

**Nithing-pole** — See _niding-pole_.

**Niding-pole** — [Historical] A carved pole set up to curse an enemy. The head of a horse or other animal is mounted on a hazel pole, mouth forced open toward the target's home or hall, with carved runes directing the curse. In the Rimevegr, a niding-pole is a galdr rank 4 effect with real mechanical consequences. Legitimacy of the curse is decided at the ting. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.3.

**Nock** — [Historical] The notch in an arrow that fits the bowstring. Also the act of placing the arrow: "nock and draw." A man who cannot nock in the dark has not practiced enough.

**Noggin** — [Historical] A small wooden cup or mug. Carved from a single piece of hardwood. Personal drinking vessel. The band's commons drink from noggins; Named Men from horn cups.

**North-wind** — [Historical] The coldest wind. In the Rimevegr, the north-wind comes from the Ice-Reach carrying temperatures that crack stone. "The north-wind is talking" means extreme cold is coming.

**Norns** — [Historical] The three fate-weavers of Norse myth. In the Rimevegr, referenced in speech: "The Norns have woven it" means the outcome was inevitable. Whether Norns literally exist is never addressed.

**Notch** — [Historical] A cut or nick in wood or bone used for counting. Kill-notches on an axe-haft. Day-notches on a tally-stick. Travel-notches on a walking staff. The Rimevegr counts by cutting, not writing.

**Nub** — [Coined] The short remaining stump of a candle, torch, or piece of cordage. "Down to the nub" means nearly exhausted. Also: the core point. "Give me the nub" means state the essential without padding.

**Nut-oil** — [Historical] Oil pressed from hazelnuts or walnuts. Used for lamp fuel, leather treatment, and cooking. A luxury in the Rimevegr -- nut trees are scarce in the deep north. Coastal settlements trade for it.

## O

**Oaken** — [Historical] Made of oak. The strongest local wood. Oaken shields, oaken doors, oaken keels. The word implies quality and permanence. "An oaken man" means someone reliable.

**Oar-bench** — [Historical] The rowing position on a longship. By extension: a junior position in any hierarchy. "He has not earned his oar-bench" means the man has not proven himself.

**Oar-mate** — [Historical] The man who sits beside you on the oar-bench. By metaphor: a trusted companion. More common in coastal speech than inland Rimevegr.

**Oath-breaker** — [Historical] Self-explanatory and terminal. An oath-breaker in the Rimevegr is economically dead -- no band will hire him, no settlement will shelter him, no contract will bear his name.

**Oath-ring** — [Historical] A sacred arm-ring on which oaths were sworn. In historical practice, kept in temples. In the Rimevegr, the oath-ring is a settlement institution -- the headman or jarl keeps one. Swearing on the oath-ring in front of witnesses is the strongest form of non-divine contract.

**Oat-cake** — [Historical] A flat bread of oat flour cooked on a hot stone. Keeps for days. Portable. Crumbles in the pack but the crumbs are still edible. A step above hard-bread.

**Offal** — [Historical] The internal organs of a butchered animal. Heart, liver, kidneys, lungs, stomach, intestines. All are eaten. Nothing is wasted. Liver is the prized cut. Stomach becomes a water-skin. Intestines become sausage casing.

**Oil-lamp** — [Historical] A small stone or clay dish with animal fat and a fiber wick. See _tallow-lamp_. Oil-lamps are slightly less smoky than tallow lamps when burning fish oil.

**Odynn** — [Coined] The One-Hung. God of gallows, knowledge- through-suffering, and debts that cannot be repaid. Depicted hanging upside-down from a dead ash tree. The strongest oaths are sworn in his name. The rope-and-eye mark seals contracts.

**Odynn's rope** — [Slang] The worst oath. Unbreakable by social convention. "On Odynn's rope" means the speaker has bound himself to something he cannot escape without destroying his reputation.

**Omen dependency** — [Coined] What happens when a band receives too many favorable wyrd-readings in a row. The men start expecting good fortune. When a negative reading finally comes, the morale drop is doubled. The wyrd-reader learns to mix bad news with good -- or stops reading entirely. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §6 (omen dependency).

**One-Hung** — [Coined] Epithet for Odynn. He hangs from the ash tree. One eye gone. The other weeping black.

**Ordovast** — [Coined] Warchief of Grimholt. A named NPC who controls the High Rime-Moors settlement.

**Osk** — [Historical] A wish or desire. In common speech: "that is his osk" means that is what the man wants. Used to identify a person's private agenda without calling it such.

**Óskapaðir** — [Coined] The unshaped ones. The unborn dead, or forms that almost entered the world but never took full human shape. Feared around failed births and certain barrows.

**Örlögsgalinn** — [Coined] Fate-mad. A völva or seer so damaged by too much pattern-reading that she cannot reliably separate omen from immediate reality.

**Örlögsýn** — [Coined] Fate-sight. The deepest and most costly level of völva seeing, where long consequence and hidden thread present themselves as if already decided.

**Ǫldrlög** — [Coined] Drinking-songs. Improvised feast-verses that begin in boasting, obscenity, or humor and sometimes end with truths no sober man meant to reveal.

**Otter-skin** — [Historical] Waterproofed pelt. Prized for its water resistance. Used for covering packages that must stay dry -- fire-pouches, the ledger, bandage supplies. Otter-skin pouches are expensive equipment.

**Outlaw** — [Historical] A person expelled from legal protection. An outlaw may be killed by anyone without penalty. In the Rimevegr, outlawry is more common than historically -- without divine stigma, it is a practical condition rather than spiritual exile. Some outlaws join bands. Some form their own.

**Outvegr** — [Coined] The Black Pine Wilds. Literally "the outer way" -- the forest paths between settlements. Only svarthird bands regularly cross the Outvegr.

## P

**Pace** — [Coined] The unit of distance in march measurement. Roughly one yard. "Two hundred paces" is the standard scouting report distance. Paces are not precise but consistent enough between experienced men.

**Pack-frame** — [Historical] A wooden frame strapped to the back for carrying loads. Distributes weight across shoulders and hips. Better than a sack but requires construction. The band builds pack-frames from green wood when the mule is overloaded.

**Pack-train** — [Historical] A line of loaded animals. The band's "pack-train" is one mule and whatever the men carry on their backs. Real pack-trains of ten or more animals belong to merchants and armies.

**Padded-jack** — [Historical] See _gambeson_. A quilted fabric armor jacket. The poor man's protection. Better than nothing. Absorbs some impact, stops some cuts. Worn under mail by those who have both.

**Pale Sun** — [Coined] A rare weather event. The sun appears blood-red and radiates no warmth. Animals refuse to move. Wise folk stay indoors. An omen. No one agrees of what.

**Pale Widow** — [Coined] The ruler of Ashen Reach. A widow with land, grain, and no husband to spend them. A political power. An employer. Not her name -- her title.

**Palisade** — [Historical] A defensive wall of sharpened wooden stakes. The primary defense of Rimevegr settlements. Not impressive but functional -- it stops a casual raid and buys time against a serious one.

**Pannier** — [Historical] A basket hung on either side of a pack animal. The mule carries panniers. Balance is critical -- an unbalanced pannier shifts and the mule stumbles. Packing panniers is a skill.

**Parchment** — [Historical] Treated animal skin used for writing. Expensive. In the Rimevegr, parchment is rare -- the charcoal ledger uses cheaper birch-bark or scraped leather. True parchment is settlement material, used for contracts that must survive.

**Parley** — [Historical] A formal meeting between opposing sides to negotiate before or instead of fighting. Parley terms are announced by horn. Approaching under parley and attacking is oath-breaking. It happens anyway.

**Patch-wool** — [Slang] A piece of wool fabric saved for repairs. Every man carries patch-wool. The patches accumulate on clothing until the garment is more patch than original.

**Pattern-weld** — [Historical] A sword-making technique. Rods of iron and steel are twisted together and forge-welded, producing a blade with a visible wavy pattern. The strongest blades are pattern-welded. In the Rimevegr, where master smiths are scarce, a pattern-welded sword is an heirloom.

**Pay-ritual** — [Coined] The formal distribution of wages. The sergeant sits on a flat stone, purse left, ledger right, small knife stuck in the wood. Each man steps forward, receives copper, counts openly, walks away. The ritual is sacred -- deviating from it signals crisis.

**Peat** — [Historical] Compressed moss and plant matter found in bogs. Burns slowly with low heat and dense smoke. The primary fuel of settlements near moorland. Peat smoke has a distinctive sweet-sour smell that clings to clothing.

**Pelt** — [Historical] An animal skin, usually with fur attached. Currency, clothing, and insulation. Pelts are traded by type and quality: wolf, fox, bear, otter, marten. A good winter pelt trades for a week's food.

**Pestle** — [Historical] A blunt-ended stick for grinding in a mortar. Used for crushing herbs, grinding grain, mixing poultices. Bone, stone, or hardwood.

**Pike** — [Historical] A long spear, usually 10-15 feet. Not carried on the march -- too long, too heavy. Settlement defense weapon. A line of pikes behind a palisade makes a gate breach very expensive for attackers.

**Pin-money** — [Coined] A trivial amount of coin. Less than the cost of a day's food. "That is pin-money" means the offer is insultingly low.

**Pitch** — [Historical] Pine resin processed into a thick, waterproof sealant. Applied to boats, leather, torch-heads, and wound dressings. Collected from wounded pine trees. Too useful to leave behind.

**Plank-shield** — [Coined] A round shield of edge-to-edge planks (linden, poplar). Leather-faced. Iron boss covering the hand-grip. A consumable in combat -- designed to take damage so the man behind it does not.

**Pole Star** — [Historical] The fixed point of the night sky. Everything rotates around it. Finding the Pole Star is the single most important navigational skill for a pre-compass traveler. Cloud cover removes this option.

**Porridge** — [Historical] Grain boiled in water. The foundational camp meal. Barley or oats, depending on supply. Cooked in the communal pot. A man who has eaten porridge every morning for three years can identify grain type and water quality blindfolded.

**Post-rider** — [Historical] A mounted messenger. Expensive to hire. In the Rimevegr, post-riders run between major settlements on known routes. Faster than walking, less reliable than you would think -- post-riders disappear in the Black Pine.

**Pot-hook** — [Historical] An iron hook hung from a cross-bar over the fire to suspend the cooking pot. Adjustable height controls heat. The pot-hook is one of Dalla's non-negotiable camp requirements.

**Pot-luck** — [Historical] Whatever the cook puts in the stew. Not a choice -- an outcome. "It is pot-luck tonight" means eat what is available without complaint.

**Practitioner degradation** — [Coined] See **Degradation**. The slow physical cost of magical practice, tracked per individual. Hands tremble, trances lengthen, readings blur. Every galdr-worker over forty knows the feeling. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.9.

**Prow-beast** — [Historical] The carved figure at the bow of a Norse longship. Dragons, serpents, wolves. Removed when approaching friendly shores (so as not to frighten the land- spirits). In the Rimevegr's inland setting, prow-beasts appear only at coastal settlements as cultural decoration.

## Q

**Quarrel** — [Historical] A short, heavy crossbow bolt. Also: a dispute. Both are sharp and penetrating. The word is used in both senses. Crossbows are rare in the Rimevegr -- too expensive to maintain on the march.

**Quarter** — [Historical] 1. A portion (captain's quarter of the loot). 2. Mercy in battle ("asking quarter" means surrender and request for mercy). 3. Lodging in a settlement ("quartering terms"). All three meanings are active.

**Quartering terms** — [Coined] The negotiated agreement for a band to stay in a settlement. Where they sleep, what they eat, what it costs, how long. Quartering quality reveals standing: the hall (honored), the barn (tolerated), the yard (unwelcome), outside the walls (hostile).

**Quern** — [Historical] See _hand-quern_. A pair of stones for grinding grain. The simplest machine in the settlement. One stone rests. One stone turns. Grain becomes flour. An hour yields enough for twelve mouths.

**Quick** — [Historical] 1. The living flesh beneath a nail or around a wound. "Cut to the quick" means cut deeply enough to hit living tissue. 2. Alive. "The quick and the dead" -- everyone. Mercenaries deal with both categories daily.

**Quill** — [Historical] A feather shaft cut for writing. In the Rimevegr, quills are settlement tools. The band uses charcoal stubs. Quills require ink, which requires gall-nuts, iron vitriol, and gum arabic -- none of which travel well.

**Quiver** — [Historical] A container for arrows. Leather or birch-bark tube, worn on the back or hip. A full quiver holds twenty to thirty arrows. An archer without a quiver is an archer for one shot.

**Quota** — [Coined] The assigned amount of a task. Foraging quota: what each man must bring back. Watch quota: how many hours each man owes per night. Gest tracks quotas. Fall behind, and your name moves up the ledger's trouble column.

## R

**Rag-and-bone** — [Slang] A scavenger who collects scraps from battlefields, middens, and abandoned steadings. Not respected but tolerated. Rag-and-bone men supply materials the band cannot produce: needles, rivets, scrap leather.

**Ragnarok** — [Historical] The prophesied end of the gods and the world. In the Rimevegr, referenced in speech but never as a current threat. "After Ragnarok" is an idiom meaning "never" or "when everything is finally over."

**Rasp** — [Historical] A coarse file used for shaping wood, hoof-trimming, and rough metal work. A rasp and a draw-knife are the minimum woodworking toolkit.

**Ration** — [Coined] The measured daily food allowance. Gest determines rations based on supply divided by days to the next settlement. The calculation is simple. The politics of enforcement are not.

**Raven** — [Historical] Odynn's birds. Battlefield scavengers. Seeing ravens after a fight is the only acknowledgment the dead receive from the natural world. "The ravens are here" means there are dead nearby.

**Raven-banner** — [Historical] A triangular flag bearing a raven image. In historical Norse culture, a war-standard. In the Rimevegr, used by bands that want to advertise themselves. Most bands do not -- advertisement invites ambush.

**Rúnamunnr** — [Coined] Rune-mouth. The precise mouth-shape, breath-pattern, and throat placement used in galdr so that a carved rune is sounded through the body rather than merely read.

**Reeve** — [Historical] A settlement administrator. Below a jarl, parallel to a headman. Manages daily business, collects taxes, arbitrates minor disputes.

**Render** — [Historical] To melt fat from animal tissue by slow heating. Rendering tallow is a camp task: cut fat into small pieces, heat slowly over low fire, strain through cloth. The liquid cools into solid tallow. The residue (cracklings) is eaten.

**Rest-day** — [Coined] A day the band does not march. Not a choice but a necessity -- rest-days happen when the men are too exhausted, the weather too severe, or the terrain too broken to continue. Rest-days cost food without gaining distance.

**Rime** — See _hrime_.

**Rime-fog** — [Coined] A thick, cold fog that combines ground- level cloud with suspended ice crystals. The most common weather during the Long Dark. Visibility drops to paces. Sound warps. Navigating in rime-fog is navigating by memory and luck.

**Rime-Moors** — See _High Rime-Moors_.

**Rime-road** — See _Rimevegr_.

**Rime-wall** — [Coined] A buildup of ice crystals on any vertical surface during extreme cold. Camp walls, rock faces, the inside of lean-tos. Rime-wall scrapes off and can be melted for drinking water in emergencies. It tastes of whatever surface it formed on.

**Rimevegr** — [Coined] "The Rime-Way" or "The Frost Road." The name of the land. Originally the name of the old north-south trade route. Now the word for the entire territory.

**Ring-coat** — [Historical] See _brynja_. Another term for a mail shirt. The rings in question are individual iron links, each riveted or twisted closed.

**Rivet** — [Historical] A metal fastener driven through two pieces and hammered flat on the back. Ship-building and shield-repair technology. A man who can set a rivet can repair a shield in the field.

**Road-warden** — [Coined] A patrol that guards trade routes. Employed by jarls or merchant guilds. Road-wardens are sometimes former svarthird men. The band encounters them on major routes and must decide: toll, parley, or avoid.

**Root-cellar** — [Historical] An underground storage chamber dug into the earth, using the ground's constant temperature to preserve food through winter. Every settlement has one. A root- cellar with turnips, dried fish, and grain is the difference between a living settlement and a dead one.

**Rope** — [Historical] Cordage. Made from hemp, bast, or horsehair. The band carries several coils. Rope is a universal tool: tie loads, build shelters, cross rivers, bind prisoners, hang the condemned. Good rope is worth its weight.

**Rope-and-eye** — [Coined] Odynn's mark. Scratched onto contract-stones beside contract terms. The visual shorthand for an oath sworn on the One-Hung. Breaking a contract marked with rope-and-eye is career-ending.

**Rushlight** — [Historical] The cheapest light source. A peeled rush soaked in waste fat, clamped in a holder. Burns for fifteen to thirty minutes with a guttering flame. Settlement lighting. The band does not carry them.

**Rune** — [Historical] A character of the runic alphabet (the futhark). In the Rimevegr, runes are both a writing system and a magical medium. Some carved rune-stones speak when read aloud. Some bleed when a traitor passes. Runes are the only written record beyond the charcoal ledger.

**Rune-carver** — [Historical] Anyone who carves runes. In the Rimevegr, this overlaps with galdr-work (magical rune-carving) but is not identical -- a rune-carver may simply be literate, not magical. The distinction is deliberately blurred.

**Rune-flaw** — [Coined] A miscarved rune. May produce the opposite of the intended effect, attract unwanted attention from beyond the Veil, or simply fail. Rune-flaws result from failed galdr rolls (see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11, failure table). Old rune-workers can identify another carver's flaw by the angle of the cut.

**Rune-reader** — [Coined] Thorne's self-description of his work. He "reads" runes rather than performing seidr -- a deliberate linguistic dodge to avoid the ergi taboo.

**Rune-stone** — [Historical] A standing stone with carved runes. Marks territory, seals contracts, wards locations. In the Rimevegr, some rune-stones still resonate with old galdr. Whispering rune-stones speak in riddles, sometimes in lies.

**Rune-ward** — [Coined] A protective rune-mark carved at a camp perimeter or doorway. Whether rune-wards actually prevent anything is deliberately ambiguous. Men feel safer with them. Ash carves them anyway.

**Runnel** — [Historical] A small stream or rivulet. A runnel near camp means fresh water without exposing men at a river crossing. Finding runnels is a scout's skill.

**Rush-mat** — [Historical] A mat woven from dried rushes. Laid on floors for insulation and comfort. In settlements, rush-mats cover dirt floors. On the march, a man with a rush-mat sleeps slightly better than one without.

**Rust** — [Historical] Iron oxide. The enemy of every edged tool. Keeping rust off a blade requires constant oiling. Neglected weapons rust overnight in the Rimevegr's damp. A rusty sword is a dead man's sword.

**Rye** — [Historical] A grain crop hardier than wheat. Grows in poor soil and cold climates. The primary bread-grain of the Rimevegr. Rye bread is dense, dark, and sour. It keeps longer than wheat bread.

## S

**Saddle** — [Historical] A leather seat for mounted riding. Expensive. The band's few horses are ridden bareback or with blanket-and-girth. A proper saddle is an officer's luxury.

**Saddle-sore** — [Slang] Chafing and bruising from riding. Also: tired to the point of uselessness. "The men are saddle- sore" means they cannot march further today.

**Saga** — [Historical] A prose narrative of historical events, usually told orally and later written. In the Rimevegr, the saga tradition is weakened -- with the gods silent, the frame that gave sagas their authority is gone. Sagas are what they always secretly were: lies that sound like truth, told by men who were not there.

**Salted** — [Historical] Preserved with salt. Salt fish, salt meat. The primary non-fresh food. Requires soaking in fresh water before cooking. The soaking water is not wasted -- it contains salt.

**Salt-lick** — [Historical] A natural deposit of mineral salt. Animals gather there. Hunters know them. A salt-lick near a camp means fresh meat is findable. Salt itself can be scraped and collected.

**Sax** — See _seax_.

**Scabbard** — [Historical] A sheath for a sword or large knife. Leather over a wooden core. The interior is often lined with oiled wool to protect the blade. Drawing a sword from a wet scabbard makes a distinctive sound.

**Scaffold** — [Historical] 1. A temporary raised platform for construction or repair. 2. A platform for the dead -- see _sky-burial_. The Rimevegr uses both meanings.

**Scour-bag** — [Coined] A leather bag filled with sand and vinegar for cleaning chainmail. The mail is dumped in, the bag is rolled and tumbled. The abrasion scours rust. After scouring: oil the mail, store in oiled cloth.

**Seax** — [Historical] A single-edged knife or short sword. The universal secondary weapon and utility tool. Every man carries a seax. Size ranges from belt-knife to short-sword.

**Seidr** — [Historical] Spirit-talking magic. Communion with the dead, land-spirits, and hidden presences. Practiced mainly by women (volvas). Deeply stigmatized for men (the _ergi_ taboo). The cost is sanity and social standing. Prolonged practice changes a person -- they see things others cannot.

**Seiðr-trance** — [Historical/Coined] The state a seiðr-worker enters to commune with spirits. Requires one hour minimum, during which the practitioner is physically vulnerable -- eyes open but unseeing, body slack, voice not their own. Interrupting a trance is dangerous: the practitioner may not fully return. Cost: 2+ Willpower per session.

**Seidr-wife** — [Historical] A female seidr practitioner. Less stigmatized than a male one. Still feared. A seidr-wife in a settlement is useful and dangerous in equal measure.

**Sentry** — [Historical] A guard posted to watch for threats. The band rotates sentries in watches. Falling asleep on sentry duty is a flogging offense. In deep Long Dark, a sentry may freeze to death standing upright.

**Serving-order** — [Coined] The hierarchy of food distribution. Captain first, Named Men, veterans, commons. Never deviated from. When serving-order changes, the power structure has changed.

**Shield-boss** — [Historical] The iron dome covering the hand- grip on a shield. Protects the knuckles. Can be used as a striking weapon in close quarters.

**Shield-brother** — [Historical] A man who fights beside you. The bond is forged in combat, not ceremony. A shield-brother relationship is the mercenary equivalent of kinship. Losing a shield-brother leaves a debt that weighs the same as blood.

**Shield-maiden** — [Historical] A woman who fights. In the Rimevegr, not a special category -- just a fighter who happens to be a woman. Petra and Ash serve in the band without special comment because the band cannot afford to refuse competence.

**Shield-rim** — [Historical] The edge of a round shield, sometimes reinforced with leather or iron strip. A blow to the shield-rim can split the planks. Shield-rim fighting -- using the edge offensively -- is a close-quarters technique.

**Shield-wall** — [Historical] A massed infantry formation. In historical Norse warfare, the primary battle tactic. In the Rimevegr, shield-walls do not form -- no army is large enough. Fighting is small-unit: ambushes, skirmishes, raids.

**Shingle** — [Historical] A rocky beach. Rounded stones. Bad footing. Good for beaching boats, terrible for camping.

**Short-count** — [Slang] Underpayment. "The jarl gave us a short-count" means the agreed payment was not fully delivered. A grievance, recorded in the ledger.

**Silver-throat** — [Slang] A good negotiator. Gest is the band's silver-throat. The term is functional, not flattering -- a silver-throat's skill is getting the most payment for the least risk.

**Sinew** — [Historical] Tendon. Dried and processed, it becomes thread or bowstring material. Sinew thread swells when wet, tightening stitches. Used for leather-work, wound stitching, and bow construction.

**Skinning** — [Historical] The process of removing an animal's hide. Done immediately after killing -- the skin comes off easier while still warm. A skilled skinner preserves the pelt whole. A clumsy one wastes half of it.

**Sky-burial** — [Coined] Exposing the dead on a raised platform for birds. Used when the ground is too frozen to dig and fire-wood too scarce to burn. Pragmatic. The dead are elevated to keep wolves from them. The ravens find them regardless.

**Sling** — [Historical] A leather strap for hurling stones. The cheapest ranged weapon. Effective at fifty paces against unarmored targets. River-stones are ammunition. A good slinger has practiced since childhood.

**Skald** — [Historical] A poet, specifically one who composes and recites norse poetry and sagas. Skalds exist in settlements. They pour mead before performing (invoking Bragi). The band has no skald. Stories are amateur.

**Skathi** — [Coined] The Bone-Walker. Goddess of winter, the hunt, and the indifference of high places. Made of ice shards. No face. She carries a bow strung with sinew from something that was once a god.

**Skein** — [Historical] A measured length of thread or yarn wound in a loose coil. Trade goods. "A skein of wool thread" is a unit of value in settlement barter.

**Sjónarœði** — [Coined] Sight-fury. Acute overload in a völva: open-eyed panic, tears, shaking, and inability to stop seeing pattern in every movement and edge.

**Sjónarvillt** — [Coined] Sight-lost. A state where the seer wanders after lines and openings no one else can perceive, often needing to be led back by voice or touch.

**Skóggangr** — [Legal] Full outlawry. Permanent exile from all Norse law. The outlaw is "forest-goer" -- stripped of all property, legal protections, and social bonds. May be killed by anyone without legal consequence. The worst punishment short of death. See `data/culture/outlawry_system.yaml`.

**Skull-splitter** — [Slang] A heavy axe. The name describes the function. Not a compliment to the weapon -- a statement of what axes do.

**Slag** — [Historical] See _dross_. The waste from smelting. Also used as an insult: "He is slag" means worthless.

**Smoke-rack** — [Historical] An improvised frame of green sticks over a low fire for smoking meat. Green wood resists burning. Four to eight hours of dense smoke produces meat that keeps for a week. True preservation requires 24-48 hours.

**Snow-blind** — [Coined] Temporary blindness from sunlight reflecting off snow. The eyes burn, water, and refuse to focus. Prevented by soot smeared below the eyes or by narrow-slit goggles carved from bone. Cured by rest in darkness.

**Snow-shoe** — [Historical] A flat frame of bent wood and woven sinew, strapped to the foot to distribute weight across soft snow. Without snow-shoes, a man sinks to the knee. With them, he walks on top. The difference is measurable in miles per day.

**Snorri** — [Coined] A Named Man in the band. The storyteller. His bad knees are a weather instrument. He does not know why they work.

**Spirit-lore** — [Coined] The ranked skill (1-5) governing seiðr practice. Determines which spirits can be contacted and whether the contact is counsel or coercion. Rank 1-3: ask the dead. Rank 4+: compel the dead. The boundary between consultation and death-seiðr. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.

**Spit** — [Historical] 1. A rod for roasting meat over fire. 2. The act of spitting (Gest's single expressive luxury). 3. A narrow point of land extending into water.

**Spoke-shave** — [Historical] A small draw-knife with handles at both ends. Used for fine wood-shaping work -- arrow shafts, tool handles, tent pegs.

**Spoor** — [Historical] Animal tracks and signs. Droppings, broken twigs, fur caught on branches. Reading spoor is how hunters find game and how scouts determine whether animals or men passed through. Fresh spoor is wet and warm. Old spoor is dry and cold.

**Söngœði** — [Coined] Sung-madness. A communal phenomenon where a song gains its own life -- voices join without choosing to, words arrive that no singer composed, knowledge surfaces that no one present possessed. Escalates in four stages: catch, pull, shaping, truth. The galdr-workers can initiate it via _ljóðveiðr_ (verse-hunting). Dangerous: extended episodes cause voice damage, memory gaps, and in rare cases, death from exhaustion when the singer cannot stop.

**Spruce-gum** — [Historical] Resin collected from spruce trees. Chewed as a stimulant on the march. Also used to seal small leaks in water-skins and boats. Tastes of pine and bitterness.

**Stag** — [Historical] A male deer. The largest wild game in the forests. A stag provides enough meat for the band for three days, plus hide, sinew, and bone. Hunting a stag in deep snow is a two-man job with dogs.

**Standing** — [Coined] The band's reputation with a specific settlement. Measured on a -5 to +5 scale. Standing determines quartering quality, trade prices, and whether the gate opens at all.

**Stave** — [Historical] 1. A wooden plank, especially a curved one for barrels. 2. A staff or pole. 3. A section of a poem or song ("first stave"). All three meanings are active.

**Steading** — [Historical] A farmstead. Smaller than a village. One family, their livestock, their fields. A steading along the road is a possible supply point -- if the farmer is willing to trade.

**Steel** — [Historical] Iron alloyed with carbon. Harder and holds an edge better than iron. In the Rimevegr, true steel is a luxury. Most blades are iron with a steel edge welded on. A full-steel sword is worth a small farm.

**Stern-post** — [Historical] The vertical timber at the back of a ship. By extension: the last man in a column, the rear- guard position. "He was at the stern-post" means he covered the retreat.

**Stirrup** — [Historical] A foot-support hanging from a saddle. Allows a rider to mount easily, stand in the saddle, and fight from horseback. Stirrups arrived late to Norse culture. In the Rimevegr, they exist but are not universal.

**Stew** — [Coined/Historical] The band's primary meal. A communal pot managed by Dalla. Everything goes in. The stew's thickness is a readable supply indicator. The stew is also a social institution -- stories are told over it, disputes happen near it.

**Strand** — [Historical] A beach. A landing place. To be "stranded" originally meant to be left on a beach without a ship.

**Strongbox** — [Coined] The band's treasury. A locked iron- bound chest. Contains the silver, the signed contracts, and the charcoal ledger. The strongbox is carried by the mule. Losing the strongbox is existential.

**Strop** — [Historical] A strip of leather used for final edge-honing after a whetstone. Stropping aligns the fine edge without removing metal. The difference between a sharp blade and a truly sharp blade is the strop.

**Stump-Armed** — [Coined] Epithet for Tyvr, god of law. One hand gone, inside a wolf that no longer exists.

**Svarthird** — [Coined] "Black Hird." The generic name for mercenary bands in the Rimevegr. They wear dark wool and iron and leave black reputations behind. All bands are called svarthird by outsiders. Individual bands are known by their captain's name or a descriptor.

**Sward** — [Historical] A stretch of grass-covered ground. In the Rimevegr, sward is Long Summer terrain -- green, soft underfoot, brief. The sward turns to frost in days when the Long Dark returns.

**Sweat-house** — [Historical] A small enclosed structure heated with fire-warmed stones. Water poured on the stones produces steam. Used for bathing, healing, and ritual. Settlements with a sweat-house are considered civilized. The band has not seen one in months.

**Sweet-water** — [Coined] Fresh, clean drinking water. As opposed to brackish, stagnant, or ice-melt water that tastes of minerals. "Sweet-water here" is a scout's best report.

**Swine** — [Historical] Pigs. Settlement livestock. Swine eat anything and produce pork, lard, and leather. More difficult to herd than sheep, more valuable per animal. A settlement's swine-count is a wealth indicator.

## T

**Tack** — [Historical] The general term for horse equipment: bridle, reins, saddle, girth, blanket. "Good tack" means well- maintained riding gear. The band's tack is poor because the band's horses are few and the budget is thin.

**Tallow** — [Historical] Rendered animal fat. The universal lubricant, waterproofing agent, light source, and cooking medium. Tallow lamps, tallow candles, tallow for leather, tallow for the cook-pot. A camp that runs out of tallow runs out of light.

**Tallow-lamp** — [Historical] A shallow dish with rendered fat and a twisted fiber wick. Dim, smoky, smells of rancid fat. The most available portable light.

**Tang** — [Historical] The extension of a blade that fits into the handle. A full-tang knife has metal running the entire length of the grip. More expensive, much stronger. A broken tang means the blade separates from the handle mid-use.

**Tar** — [Historical] Liquid pitch. Made by burning pine or birch in a covered kiln. Used for waterproofing everything: boats, leather, rope, roofs. The smell of tar is the smell of civilization in the Rimevegr.

**Tenant-farmer** — See _cotter_.

**Thing** — [Historical] A local assembly for settling disputes, adjudicating feuds, and making communal decisions. Hosted by the local jarl or headman. Rulings are enforced only by whoever has the armed men to make them stick.

**Thong** — [Historical] A narrow strip of rawhide or leather. Used for binding, lashing, and improvised fastening. A belt of thong is the simplest closure for a pouch or pack. Thong swells when wet and tightens -- useful for binding tool-heads to hafts.

**Thorn-wall** — [Coined] A defensive barrier of thorn-bearing bushes, cultivated or natural. Thornwall (the settlement) takes its name from this feature.

**Three Wolves, the** — [Coined] Hadric's rival mercenary band. They burn villages. The band's primary antagonist.

**Thrall** — [Historical] An unfree person. Debt-bound or captured. Can be sold. Children of thralls are born thrall. Freedom can be purchased -- the price is whatever the owner names. Thralldom is regarded as bad luck, not injustice.

**Thrall-mark** — [Coined] The social stigma that follows a former thrall. Not a physical mark -- a reputation. "He has the thrall-mark" means the man was once unfree, and some people will never let him forget it.

**Þögulœði** — [Coined] Silent-fury. The numb, withdrawn state that follows severe sight-strain or spirit-contact, when the mind shuts doors simply to remain intact.

**Thread** — [Historical] Spun fiber for sewing. Wool, linen, or sinew. Every man carries a needle and thread. Without thread, a torn garment stays torn, and a wound stays open.

**Threshing** — [Historical] Beating harvested grain to separate kernels from stalks. Done with a flail or by driving animals over the sheaves. Settlement work that happens in the weeks after harvest.

**Thurr** — [Coined] The Crack-Handed. God of thunder, violence that solves nothing but must happen anyway, and the last stand. His eyes are closed because he cannot bear to see what his hands have done. Never silent -- he was always the god who hit things.

**Thurr's hands** — [Slang] An oath of frustration. What a man says when his patience is completely gone.

**Tide-pool** — [Historical] A trapped pool of salt water among coastal rocks. A foraging source: small crabs, shellfish, seaweed. Minimal calories, but something is better than nothing.

**Timber** — [Historical] Cut wood for construction. "Good timber" means straight-grained wood suitable for building. The Black Pine provides unlimited timber, but cutting, hauling, and processing it is labor the band cannot spare.

**Tiller** — [Historical] 1. The helm-bar of a ship. 2. A farmer who works the soil. Both uses are active.

**Tinder-pouch** — See _fire-pouch_.

**Toll** — [Historical] A fee for crossing. Bridges, fords, gates. Toll collectors are everywhere. The fee is whatever the collector thinks he can extract without getting killed for it.

**Tongs** — [Historical] A metal gripping tool used by smiths to hold hot iron. Also useful for moving coals in a fire, which is the context most band members encounter them.

**Tongue** — [Historical] 1. Body part. 2. A narrow strip of land between two bodies of water. 3. A flame. "The tongue of the fire" is common usage.

**Tooth-stick** — [Historical] See _chewing stick_. A twig of hazel, birch, or willow, chewed at one end until fibrous. Rubbed against teeth and gums. Willow contains salicin -- the gums feel better after.

**Tribute** — [Historical] A payment extracted by force or threat. When the band demands supplies from a settlement, the demand is tribute. Repeated tribute causes settlement decay. The word is understood by everyone and never used by the party extracting it -- they say "contribution" or "provision."

**Trough** — [Historical] A long, shallow container. For water (animals drink from it), for mixing mortar, for washing clothes. Carved from a single log or assembled from planks.

**Truce** — [Historical] A temporary cessation of hostilities. Truces are fragile. They last as long as both sides benefit. When one side gains advantage, the truce breaks. "The truce stinks" means it will not hold.

**Truss** — [Historical] 1. A structural frame of timbers for roofing. 2. A bundle of hay or straw tightly bound. 3. A medical binding for a hernia. All three uses are active. The context determines the meaning.

**Tun** — [Historical] A large cask for storing liquid -- ale, mead, water. Coopered from staves and iron hoops. A full tun is too heavy for one man. Two men roll it.

**Turnip** — [Historical] A root vegetable. The Rimevegr's most reliable crop. Stores well in root-cellars through the Long Dark. Boiled, mashed, added to stew. The taste is bland. The calories are essential.

**Turnshoe** — [Historical] Medieval boot construction method. The boot is sewn inside-out and then turned right-side out. The seam contacts the inside of the foot, not the ground.

**Tyvr** — [Coined] The Stump-Armed. God of law, justice-that- costs-a-hand, and the thing-assembly where grudges are weighed in silver and blood.

**Tyvr-handed** — [Slang] Describes a jarl who rules justly. The opposite is "wolf-fed" -- a jarl who rules by appetite.

**Tyre** — [Historical] An iron band shrunk onto a wooden wheel rim to prevent splitting. Making a tyre requires a smith, forge, and precision. A wheel without a tyre cracks on stone roads within days.

## U

**Ullr** — [Historical] A god associated with hunting, skiing, and winter sport. In the Rimevegr, Ullr is a minor invocation -- hunters may speak his name, but he has no cult or formal worship. One of the gods who went quiet without anyone noticing.

**Umber** — [Historical] A brownish earth pigment. Used for dyeing leather and marking camp boundaries. Mixed with fat, it makes a brown paste for weatherproofing exposed wood.

**Underbelly** — [Slang] The vulnerable part of anything. "The settlement's underbelly" is its weakest defensive point. "The contract's underbelly" is the clause that will hurt the band.

**Undercroft** — [Historical] A storage room beneath a hall, sometimes partially underground. Cooler and drier than a root- cellar. A luxury of stone-and-timber construction.

**Underkeel** — [Slang] The hidden cost of something. "Check the underkeel" means look for the trap in the contract.

**Unfree** — [Historical] See _thrall_. A person without legal autonomy. Unfree persons cannot own property, bear witness at the Thing, or leave without permission. The largest social class in many settlements.

**Upwind** — [Historical] From the direction the wind is blowing. "Camp upwind of the midden" is basic sanitation sense.

**Urn-burial** — [Historical] A cremation burial where the ashes are placed in a clay or stone urn. Higher status than a shallow grave. Lower status than a barrow. Most settlements cannot afford more.

**Usurer** — [Historical] A money-lender who charges interest. Considered low but necessary. In the Rimevegr, usurers exist in larger settlements. Their rates are extortionate because default risk is extreme. No one likes them. Everyone uses them.

**Útlagi** — [Legal] Outlaw. One declared outside the law by ting ruling. From "út" (out) and "lög" (law). Encompasses both lesser (fjörbaugsgarðr) and full (skóggangr) outlawry.

**Utmark** — [Historical] The uncultivated land beyond a settlement's worked fields. Common grazing, hunting ground, and timber source. Beyond the utmark lies true wilderness. The utmark is shared by the community; disputes over its use are settled at the Thing.

**Utter-dark** — [Coined] Darkness so complete that a man cannot see his own hand. Cave interiors, barrow passages, deep-winter nights under cloud cover. In utter-dark, sound becomes the primary sense. Men speak to locate each other.

## V

**Valgrim** — [Coined] Described as a common name for war chiefs who rule through martial reputation rather than land ownership. Etymologically: "the grim chosen ones."

**Valkyrie** — [Historical] In Norse myth, divine women who collected the worthy dead for Odin's hall. In the Rimevegr, referenced in camp-talk and older sagas but never confirmed. "Waiting for the valkyrie" is dark humor for expecting to die.

**Vambrace** — [Historical] Forearm armor. Leather or iron. Protects the arm from wrist to elbow. Worn on the weapon arm or both arms. Leather vambraces are common; iron ones are expensive and hot.

**Vanrgrim** — [Coined] "The Bitter Gods." The Low Court of the pantheon: Tyvr, Skathi, Bragi, Heimr. The gods of law, winter, poetry, and warning. Less invoked than the Aesrgrim but deeper in the culture's foundation.

**Vargheim** — [Coined] A large village deep in the Black Pine. Population 165. Jarl Ulf Vargson. Wolf-warden kennels and charcoal kilns.

**Vargr** — [Legal/Insult] Wolf. Applied to full outlaws, naming them as beasts beyond human society. The source of "wolfshead" in English. A vargr has no legal standing -- killing one is no more murder than killing a wolf.

**Vébǫnd** — [Historical] Sacred boundary ropes marking lawful or holy ground. To cross the vébǫnd armed or polluted is a grave violation even in a world where the gods no longer answer.

**Villimaðr** — [Coined] Wild-man. One who has passed through villimennska and returned only in body -- human-shaped, but socially and spiritually emptied.

**Villimennska** — [Coined] The Wilding. The third and final soulbane: the stripping away of law, kin-bond, and human shape of mind until appetite is all that remains.

**Veil, the** — [Coined] The boundary between the living world and whatever lies beyond. Unstable since the Cracking. When it thins: shadows at wrong angles, sounds that should not be, distances that shift, fire burns blue at the edges. The air tastes of old metal.

**Veil Ceiling** — [Coined] The self-imposed cap on supernatural content. Supernatural elements stay rare, grounded, ambiguous -- always deniable. The effect is dread without certainty. The reader decides. The novel does not.

**Veil-Thinning** — [Coined] A rare event when the Veil thins unpredictably. Reality becomes unreliable. Correlated with season (worst in Long Dark) and location (barrows, old temples). Unknown triggers. Characters cannot predict them.

**Veil-Touched** — [Coined] People born with pale eyes and affinity for wyrd-reading. They sense the threads of fate. Feared as much as respected. Volvas and rune-carvers fall into this category.

**Veil-thinning (intentional)** — [Coined] The forbidden act of deliberately weakening the Veil barrier. Classified as the most serious magical crime. Triggers settlement panic and feud +3 with all neighboring settlements. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.4.

**Venom** — [Historical] Poison from snakes, spiders, or plants. In the Rimevegr, venom is rare as a weapon -- too unreliable and too hard to obtain. But baneberry and wolfsbane grow in the Black Pine. Someone with knowledge could extract a lethal dose.

**Venture** — [Coined] A speculative contract where the band accepts risk for a share of potential reward rather than a fixed fee. "Going on venture" means the band may earn nothing or may earn a fortune. Gest hates ventures.

**Vermin** — [Historical] Rats, mice, fleas, lice. The permanent unwanted company of every camp and settlement. Vermin eat the stores and spread sickness. The battle against vermin is never won, only managed.

**Verklög** — [Coined] Work-songs. Rhythmic songs tied to repetitive labor: rowing, grinding, splitting, smithing, carding. The beat times the work; the verses encode technical knowledge (knot sequences, brine-soak timing, beam-count per raise). Never taught -- caught by proximity and repetition.

**Vǫgguvísur** — [Coined] Cradle-songs. Death-counting lullabies sung to infants -- catalogues of dangers set to rocking rhythm. The melody writes survival instincts into the body before the mind can parse the words. Also preserves the names of dead children the sagas will never mention.

**Veteran** — [Coined] A proven fighter with time in the band. Above common, below Named Man. Veterans are reliable enough to not need watching. They eat after Named Men and before commons.

**Volva** — [Historical] A female seidr-practitioner. A seeress. Volvas occupy a complex social position -- feared, needed, isolated. They see what others cannot. The cost is visible on their faces.

**Vow** — [Historical] A solemn promise, often with divine witness. Distinct from an oath in that a vow is private -- a man makes a vow to himself or to one other person. An oath is public. Breaking a vow has no legal consequence. The personal consequence is another matter.

## W

**Wading** — [Historical] Crossing a river on foot. Current, depth, footing, and cold are the four hazards. A mid-thigh ford in winter can stop a man's heart from cold shock. The band crosses in a linked chain, gripping each other's belts.

**Wainwright** — [Historical] A cart-builder. A settlement specialist. The band has no cart -- carts require roads, and roads are intermittent in the Rimevegr.

**Ward** — [Historical/Coined] 1. A rune-carved protective mark. Galdr-workers lay wards at camp perimeters, on doorposts, around sleeping areas. Whether they work is deliberately ambiguous. 2. A person under protection. "Thorne's ward" means someone he has taken responsibility for.

**Ward-right** — [Coined] A political appointment at the Allthing that determines whose galdr-worker wards the assembly stones. The ward-holder controls magical safety for all participants and gains +1 modifier to all Allthing actions. Appointment resolved on Allthing Day 1 by `ward_influence` formula. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.1.

**Ward-stone** — [Coined] A rune-carved stone set at a settlement boundary or building entrance to provide magical protection. Must be maintained by a galdr-worker -- ward-stones degrade over time. Cost: 250-450 copper per stone. Intact ward-stones give +1 morale to bands camping nearby. See `08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §7.

**Wasting asset** — [Coined/Slang] What a galdr-worker is to a mercenary band. The practitioner degrades with use and cannot be replaced. Unlike swords or shields, a ward-worker ages out of usefulness and there are not enough young practitioners entering the trade. See `08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §7 and §8.

**Watch-fire** — [Coined] A small fire maintained during the night watch. Just enough light to see the camp perimeter. Different from the sleeping fire -- the watch-fire is positioned for visibility, not warmth.

**Watch-rotation** — [Coined] The schedule of night sentries. Four watches per night. Each man serves one watch in rotation. Named Men are sometimes exempt. The fourth watch (pre-dawn) is the worst -- the cold is deepest and the body wants sleep most.

**Water-skin** — [Historical] A container made from animal stomach, bladder, or sewn leather sealed with pitch. Holds about a liter. Imparts a taste -- leather and whatever fat was used to seal it. A man needs two liters at rest, four on the march.

**Weak Link** — [Coined] The slowest, clumsiest, or least skilled member of the band. Contempt is constant but rarely spoken. The weak link carries weight because the band cannot afford to carry nothing. This is Lump's starting position.

**Wergild** — See _weregild_.

**Wet-nurse** — [Historical] A woman who breast-feeds another's child. In the Rimevegr, wet-nurses are settlement women. The band has no use for the term except as an insult: "He needs a wet-nurse" means the man is helpless.

**Weregild** — [Historical] Blood-price. The silver value of a human life, paid to end a feud or compensate a killing. Not fixed in the Rimevegr -- negotiated. What a man's life is worth depends on who is asking and who is answering. Common: 15-25 silver. Named Man: 50+ silver.

**Whet** — [Historical] To sharpen by rubbing on a stone. The sound of a whetstone on steel at dusk is one of the novel's ambient textures.

**Whetstone** — [Historical] A flat stone of fine-grained sandstone or slate used for sharpening blades. Wetted with water or spit. Every fighting man carries one. The whetstone and the fire-pouch are the two things a man checks before sleep.

**Whey** — [Historical] The liquid remaining after milk is curdled for cheese. Thin, sour, slightly nourishing. Drunk as a beverage in settlements. The band encounters whey only in halls that keep cattle.

**Wicker** — [Historical] Woven thin branches, usually willow. Used for baskets, fish-traps, temporary fences, and the frames of lightweight shields. Quick to make but not durable.

**White-out** — [Coined] A rime storm severe enough to erase all visual reference. Sky and ground merge into uniform white. Navigation becomes impossible. A man ten paces from shelter cannot find it. White-outs kill without malice.

**Widow's-portion** — [Historical] The property a widow retains after a husband's death. In the Rimevegr, widows with property are political powers. The Pale Widow rules Ashen Reach not through war but through grain.

**Wind-break** — [Historical] A barrier against wind. A wall of stacked brush, a stretched hide, a snow-wall. Reduces wind-chill dramatically. Radiant fire-heat bounces off the wind-break toward the sleepers.

**Windlass** — [Historical] A winding mechanism for raising heavy loads. Used in wells, harbor cranes, and crossbow cocking. A mechanical advantage device. Settlement technology.

**Winter-camp** — [Coined] A semi-permanent camp established for the worst of the Long Dark. Better constructed than a marching camp. Earthen walls, deeper fire-pits, thicker shelters. The band digs in and waits for conditions to improve.

**Winter-hall** — [Coined] A semi-permanent shelter large enough for the entire band. Reaching a winter-hall before deep Long Dark is a survival objective. A winter-hall has fire, walls, and a roof. Everything else is luxury.

**Wolf-fed** — [Slang] Describes a jarl who rules by appetite, not justice. The opposite of "Tyvr-handed." A wolf-fed jarl takes what he can because he can.

**Wolf-trap** — [Historical] A covered pit or spring-loaded mechanism for catching wolves. Wolves prey on livestock and lone travelers. Settlements maintain wolf-traps. Walking into one in the dark is not abstract -- it is a real hazard of off-trail travel.

**Wolfshead** — [Legal] English equivalent of vargr. A person under full outlawry, declared a wolf in human form. May be killed without legal consequence. Wolfshead bands are groups of outlaws who survive by raiding, trading, or selling their combat skills. See `data/wolfshead_bands.yaml`.

**Woof** — [Historical] See _weft_. The horizontal threads in weaving that cross the warp. "Warp and woof" means the fundamental structure of something.

**Work-song** — [Historical] A rhythmic song sung during repetitive labor: rowing, marching, grinding, threshing. The rhythm coordinates effort. The band has work-songs for marching. They are not pretty. They keep pace.

**Worsted** — [Historical] A type of yarn or fabric made from long-staple wool, tightly spun. Smoother and stronger than regular wool cloth. More expensive. Named Men might own a worsted tunic. Commons do not.

**Wound-dew** — [Historical] Kenning for blood. One of the language's fossilized metaphors. Characters use it without thinking of it as poetry.

**Wyrd** — [Historical] Fate. The sense that the outcome is already woven. Wyrd is not predestination in a theological sense -- it is the cumulative weight of all past actions pressing on the present. What will happen has already been set in motion. You just cannot see the thread yet.

**Wyrd-reading** — [Historical] Fate divination. Casting lots, reading entrails, interpreting omens. The most common and least stigmatized magical tradition. Reveals the threads already woven. The knowledge is always true but never complete.

**Wyrm** — [Historical] A serpent or dragon in Norse myth. In the Rimevegr, wyrms are saga creatures -- no one living has seen one. The word survives in place-names (Wyrm-teeth Pass) and metaphor. "That contract is a wyrm" means it has teeth you cannot see.

## Y

**Yardarm** — [Historical] The horizontal spar on a ship's mast from which sails hang. By extension: the crossbar of any structure. "Strung from the yardarm" means publicly hanged as a warning.

**Yarn** — [Historical] 1. Spun fiber ready for weaving or knitting. Wool yarn is the Rimevegr's primary textile thread. 2. A story, usually exaggerated. "Spinning a yarn" means telling a tall tale. Snorri spins yarns. Some of them are even true.

**Yearling** — [Historical] An animal in its second year. A yearling calf, a yearling foal. Also slang for a second-year recruit -- someone who has survived one Long Dark but is not yet experienced.

**Yew** — [Historical] A wood prized for bows. Yew heartwood compresses and sapwood stretches -- a natural composite. The best bows are made from yew staves where heartwood and sapwood meet. Yew is also poisonous. The berries, needles, and bark can kill livestock and men.

**Yoke** — [Historical] 1. A wooden frame for carrying buckets on the shoulders. 2. Metaphor for any burden. "Under the yoke" means burdened with obligation.

**Yore** — [Coined] The time before the Cracking. The old world. Songs reference yore as a golden period, but golden periods always exist only in songs. No one living has reliable information about yore. The sagas contradict each other.

**Youngblood** — [Slang] A new recruit, especially one who is aggressive and untested. Youngbloods volunteer for point position and regret it. The term is not affectionate. A youngblood is a liability until he has been scared enough to think before acting.

## Z

**Zeal** — [Historical] Fierce determination. In the Rimevegr, zeal is mistrusted. A man with zeal has lost perspective. Cool competence is preferred. "He has too much zeal" is a warning that someone will do something reckless.

**Zinc** — [Historical] A grey metal. Too soft for weapons, too brittle for tools. Used for cheap coinage when silver runs out. Zinc coins turn fingers grey and do not ring when dropped.

**Zincs** — [Coined] A camp-slang corruption of "zinc" -- dull, grey, worthless coins from dead settlements where the mint had degraded. Zincs trade at a fraction of proper silver. "Paying in zincs" is an insult.

**Zoning** — [Coined] The tactical division of a defensive perimeter into sectors. Each man on watch owns a zone. He does not leave it, even if action occurs in an adjacent zone. Zoning prevents the fatal clustering of defenders during an attack.
