## Madness — The Völva's Particular Forms

Every tradition has its madness. Galdr-workers go rigid — the body locked in the posture of the last carving, the mind fixed on the last rune, the tremor extending from the hands to the entire body until the practitioner is a shaking ruin who can no longer hold a chisel but cannot stop trying. Seiðr-wives go thin — the boundary between self and dead dissolving until the woman walks and talks and breathes but the person is gone, replaced by the aggregate presence of everyone she has ever contacted through the Veil.

Völva madness is different. Völva madness is vision without anchor.

### Sjónarœði — Sight-Fury

The most acute form. The völva's sight opens fully, without control, without the narrowing squint that manages the input. Every temporal layer of every object in the visual field arrives simultaneously at full intensity. The past, the present, the future, all of them real, all of them vivid, all of them demanding attention. The brain cannot process it. The neural pathways that have been trained to receive temporal data overload, and the overload produces not shutdown but acceleration — the mind racing through the layers at a speed that the conscious self cannot follow.

The völva in sjónarœði speaks rapidly, continuously, without pause, describing what she sees. The descriptions are accurate — verifiably, specifically, horrifyingly accurate. She describes events that happened a century ago in this room. She describes events that will happen tomorrow. She describes the death of the person standing in front of her, and the death of that person's children, and the burning of the settlement, and the regrowth of the forest over the ruins, and the people who will build on the ruins and who will die there and who will be buried in the ground that was once a floor. She sees it all. She says it all. She cannot stop.

The sjónarœði can last hours. The body sustains it — the adrenaline response keeps the heart running, the voice running, the lungs running. The damage is neural. The overload burns pathways the way an electrical surge burns wire. A völva who survives a full sjónarœði episode emerges with permanent cognitive damage — gaps in the temporal sorting that the practice depends on, holes in the ability to distinguish past from future, a degradation of the second sight's precision that turns the controlled reading into something approximate, unreliable, and frightening.

### Þögulœði — Silent Fury

The inverse. The völva's sight opens, and the mind, instead of racing, stops. The temporal layers arrive and the brain refuses them. Not a conscious refusal — a protective shutdown. The neural pathways that carry temporal data close like valves, and the mind goes dark.

The völva in þögulœði does not speak. She does not move. She does not respond to stimuli. She is conscious — the seiðr-wives who have examined þögulœði patients confirm that the mind is present, the hugr is intact, the self has not departed. The self has retreated. It has pulled back from the sight the way a hand pulls back from a flame, and it will not come forward again until the sight subsides.

The þögulœði can last days. During that time, the völva must be maintained by attendants — fed, watered, kept warm, positioned to prevent the pressure sores that immobility causes. The attendants do not know when the episode will end. The völva does not know when the episode will end. The sight subsides when the sight subsides, and the mind comes forward when the mind decides the sight is safe, and the decision is not conscious and cannot be hastened.

A völva who experiences frequent þögulœði is a völva whose practice is exceeding her capacity. The episodes are the mind's refusal to continue receiving what the practice is sending, and the refusal is not negotiable. The teacher's response is to reduce the practice — fewer readings, shallower depth, longer recovery periods. The student's response is often to push harder, because the sight is addictive, and the addiction does not respect the mind's limits, and the next episode is deeper and longer and the recovery is slower.

### Fate-Reading Madness — Örlögsgalinn

The most insidious form, because it does not look like madness from the outside.

The völva who develops örlögsgalinn functions. She reads. She reports. She provides accurate, useful information to her clients. The madness is not in the reading. The madness is in the relationship to the reading.

The örlögsgalinn völva has lost the ability to care about anything except the pattern. The ørlög — the shape of fate — has become the only thing that matters. People are not people. They are threads in the pattern. Events are not events. They are nodes in the weave. The death of a child is not a tragedy. It is a convergence point. The burning of a settlement is not a catastrophe. It is a pattern completing itself.

The völva who has reached örlögsgalinn watches a family receive the news that their son has died in a raid, and she sees the pattern — the choices that led to the raid, the decisions that placed the son on the road, the ørlög-threads that converged at the moment of the killing — and she finds it beautiful. Not the death. The pattern. The terrible, precise, inevitable shape of cause and effect that produced the death, visible to her in its full temporal extent, from the grandmother's choice to marry the grandfather through the father's choice to farm this land through the son's choice to take the road — all of it visible, all of it connected, all of it inevitable, and the inevitability is what the örlögsgalinn mind finds beautiful, because inevitability is the pattern at its purest, and the pattern is all that the örlögsgalinn völva can see.

This is not sociopathy. The völva has not lost empathy through cruelty or damage. She has lost it through replacement. The pattern has filled the space where empathy lived, and the pattern is larger than empathy, and the mind that holds the pattern does not have room for both. The choice was not conscious. The replacement was not voluntary. The sight grew, and the pattern grew with it, and the person who cared about the threads shrank until the threads were all that remained.

The attendants who serve an örlögsgalinn völva are the only people who see it clearly, because they are the people closest to the woman and therefore the people who notice when the woman stops being the woman. She still speaks. She still reads. She still reports. But the voice has changed — not in the seiðr-wife's way, not borrowed from the dead, but drained of the quality that made it human. The örlögsgalinn völva speaks about fate the way a carpenter speaks about wood grain: with precision, with expertise, with a craftsman's appreciation for the material, and with no recognition that the material is made of lives.

_The völva's path is the path of the eye that will not close. Galdr burns. Seiðr drowns. The völva sees, and the seeing does not stop, and the cost of not stopping is everything that makes the seer a person rather than an instrument. The blind völva is worshipped because she has paid the price. The mad völva is feared because she has paid a different price — the price of seeing clearly enough to stop caring what she sees. The Rimevegr needs its völvas the way it needs its wards and its seiðr-wives and its bone-lot casters — it needs someone to see what is coming, because what is coming cannot be avoided but it can be faced, and facing requires forewarning, and forewarning requires a woman who is willing to trade her eyes and her body and her self for the privilege of watching the world's pattern unfold, one thread at a time, until the threads are all she sees and the woman is gone and the pattern remains, beautiful and terrible and true._
