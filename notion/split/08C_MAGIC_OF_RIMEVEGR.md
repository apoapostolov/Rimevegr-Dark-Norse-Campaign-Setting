A reference document for the supernatural layer of the Iron Ledger setting. Not the mechanical rules (those are in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`) but the lived reality — what magic feels like, what it costs, what people believe, and what nobody knows for certain.

## Magic and the Material World

Magic has a price list. Not the mechanical costs — those are in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, measured in Willpower and blood and ranked in tables. This section covers the other price. The silver price. What a ward costs when you are paying a galdr-worker to carve it. What a seiðr consultation costs when you need to know what the dead know. What rune-marked goods sell for in the Skaldhaven market and why barrow dust costs more than iron nails.

The Rimevegr runs on barter, copper, and silver. Magic sits inside that economy the way a blacksmith sits inside it — as a service provider with rare skills and high material costs. The difference is that a blacksmith makes things people understand. A galdr-worker makes things people need but cannot evaluate. You know whether a sword is sharp. You do not know whether a ward is strong until the night it fails, and by then the galdr-worker is in another settlement and the dead are in your grain-store.

This asymmetry — the buyer cannot judge quality, the seller cannot prove quality in advance — shapes everything about how magic trades. Prices are set by reputation, not by measurement. And reputation is set by the last catastrophic failure that everyone remembers, not by the thousand quiet successes that nobody noticed.

### The Economy of Galdr

A galdr-worker's time is the most expensive service in the Rimevegr that does not involve killing someone. The baseline rate at Skaldhaven — the closest thing to a standard — is 30 copper for a rune consultation. That is a conversation. Not a carving. Not a ward. A galdr-worker looks at your problem, tells you what rank of work it needs, and quotes you a price for the actual job.

Thirty copper is one and a half days of a mercenary's retainer. It buys three days of food. It is the price of an hour's conversation with someone who can see things you cannot, and most people in the Rimevegr will never pay it. They cannot afford it. They salt their doors and sew their dead and hope the old wards hold.

The actual work scales by rank, and the scaling is steep:

**Rank 1 galdr — trail-marks, single-use wards, rune-marks that last one night.** Cost: 15-25 copper, depending on materials. A competent apprentice can do this work. The materials are cheap — charcoal, ash-paste, a knife with a clean edge. The work takes half a day. Any band with a galdr-worker handles rank 1 in-house. Ash does not charge the band for trail-marks. He carves them because not carving them means sleeping without a perimeter, and sleeping without a perimeter means trusting the dark.

**Rank 2 galdr — seasonal wards, binding-marks on storage, rune-carved tools.** Cost: 40-80 copper. This is where the work gets real. A seasonal ward on a grain-store — the kind that keeps vermin and spoilage out through the Long Dark — costs 60 copper at Skaldhaven. Materials matter now: the carving needs to be done in finished wood or dressed stone, and the activation requires the carver's blood. Not much. A finger-cut. But the blood means the ward is keyed to a living person, and if that person dies the ward starts to degrade. A farmer who pays 60 copper for a grain-ward is investing in the continued survival of the man who carved it.

**Rank 3 galdr — annual wards, structural protections, rune-work that holds through the Long Dark without maintenance.** Cost: 150-300 copper. Fifteen to thirty silver. A jarl's seasonal income from a single farmstead might be 50 silver. A rank 3 ward on the settlement's main hall costs a visible fraction of that income. The materials are expensive — silver wire from Deepholm, standing stone chips, sometimes veil-touched water — and the galdr-worker pays in blood and will. Rank 3 costs 2 Willpower and 1 HP per the mechanical tables. The silver price reflects the fact that few galdr-workers can do rank 3 work and survive it often enough to make it a profession.

Ash is rank 3. He can set annual wards. He can bind a space against the dead so thoroughly that draugr will walk around the warded building rather than approach it. He has done this four times in the last two years. Each time he was slower to recover. Each time the runes took a little more. He does not discuss the recovery time with Voss.

**Rank 4 and above — fortress wards, Allthing protections, permanent bindings.** Cost: 500 copper and up. Often paid in land- rights or political favors rather than silver, because no one carries that much coin on a road where bandits know the price of galdr. The material components alone — rare standing stone chips, veil-touched water from specific springs, silver wire that has been through the Deepholm forge three times — can cost 200 copper before the galdr-worker touches the stone. Nobody in Voss's band does rank 4 work. Ash could attempt it. The attempt would cost 3 Willpower and 3 HP and require a component that the band does not carry. The failure cascade at rank 4 includes permanent stat damage. Ash knows this. He has seen what rank 4 failure looks like. He will not discuss it.

The material costs are not arbitrary. Silver wire from Deepholm is galdr-grade — smelted with rune-marked crucibles, drawn through dies that have been carved with binding-marks since the foundry was built. Regular silver wire does not work. The difference is not visible. A merchant who substitutes regular wire for Deepholm wire is selling a ward that will fail, and when it fails people die, and when people die the merchant's family pays the blood-price or the merchant's settlement loses its trade reputation. This has happened three times in recorded memory. The punishments were severe enough that Deepholm's monopoly is self-enforcing.

A galdr-worker's kit — the tools, not the materials — costs roughly 200 copper to assemble and 50 copper per season to maintain. Carving knives with edges fine enough for rune-work. Whetstones. Chalk for marking. A set of templates for the standard forms. Blood-lancets. Binding cord. A pouch of clean ash. The kit fits in a satchel the size of a man's head. Ash carries his on his left hip, opposite his hand-axe, and he checks it every morning the way Voss checks the camp perimeter.

This is why a good galdr-worker is worth more alive than a good swordsman. A swordsman kills people. A galdr-worker keeps everyone in the settlement alive through the Long Dark. You can replace a swordsman with any fit man and a week of drilling. You cannot replace a galdr-worker with anything except another galdr-worker, and there are perhaps forty in the entire Rimevegr who can work at rank 3. This scarcity makes galdr-workers political targets rather than employees. A jarl does not hire a galdr-worker the way he hires a mercenary. He binds one — with land, with protection, with the implicit threat that a galdr-worker without a patron is a galdr-worker that someone else will recruit or kill to prevent the recruitment.

Ash works for Voss because Voss does not bind. The band is not a settlement. Ash is not a court galdr-worker tending the same ward- stones year after year. He is on the road. He uses his skills when the contract requires it and Voss pays him a standard share. This arrangement makes Ash unusual and, in the eyes of every jarl who has ever offered to buy his contract, dangerously free.

_The economics of galdr are the economics of irreplaceability. Every other service in the Rimevegr — smithing, carpentry, healing, fighting — has a supply curve. More people can learn it. The pool grows. Galdr supply is shrinking. The old works are stronger than the new ones. The masters are not training enough apprentices. The runes take more blood each decade. Ash is not getting better. He is getting used up. The price of galdr rises because the supply falls, and the supply falls because the cost of producing it — the human cost, the cost measured in shaking hands and nightmares and years shaved off the back end of a life — rises faster than any market can compensate._

### Ward-Stones and Ward-Rights

A ward-stone is a standing stone carved with galdr at rank 3 or higher and set into the earth at a settlement's boundary. The stone anchors a perimeter. Inside the perimeter, the dead cannot cross uninvited. Outside, they do as they please. The ward does not kill draugr. It does not banish spirits. It says _not here_ and the dead, for reasons that galdr-workers can describe mechanically but not explain, listen.

A ward-stone that holds through the Long Dark — the full 100-day winter when the Veil is thinnest and the dead are most active — is worth more than the building it protects. This is not metaphor. A farmstead's timber hall can be rebuilt in a season with enough hands. A ward-stone that actually works through the Long Dark requires a rank 3 galdr-worker, Deepholm silver wire, standing stone chips from a quarry that has not been mined out, and a month of carving. The materials alone cost 100-150 copper. The galdr-worker's fee adds another 150-300. The total investment is 250-450 copper for a single stone that protects one boundary segment.

A settlement needs four to eight ward-stones for a full perimeter, depending on size. A small farmstead cluster — thirty people, four buildings — needs four stones at minimum. That is 1,000-1,800 copper. One hundred to one hundred eighty silver. A jarl whose annual income from the cluster is 200 silver is spending half of it to a full year on ward-stones alone. This is why most settlements run partial perimeters — two stones covering the approaches from the nearest known barrow, and salt-lines and threshold iron on the remaining sides. It is also why ward- failure is a political crisis rather than a maintenance problem.

Ward-stones fail. Not all at once. The runes fade. The binding weakens. A stone that was carved five years ago by a competent rank 3 galdr-worker is operating at perhaps seventy percent efficacy. A stone carved fifteen years ago is at thirty percent or less. A stone carved before the Cracking — the old wards around Frostfjord Hollow, the Grimholt perimeter, the Deepholm foundry boundary — still operates at full strength three centuries later. Nobody alive knows how. Nobody alive can replicate it. This is the core terror of the ward-stone economy: the best products are irreplaceable antiques, and every replacement is worse than the thing it replaces.

Ward-stone maintenance is a political tool. At the Allthing (reference `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.1), ward-rights are among the most contentious agenda items. Who provides the galdr- worker for the Allthing ward-stones? Currently Skaldhaven, under Sigrun's authority. This means the Fjord Compact controls who is safe at the assembly and, by quiet implication, who is not. The galdr-worker could be instructed to leave a gap. The galdr-worker could be told to weaken one section. Nobody has done this — the Allthing's neutrality is too valuable to burn for a single-season advantage — but the capability is noted by everyone who stands inside the wards and knows who carved them.

Jarls use ward-control as leverage in three ways. First, they control the schedule. A jarl who employs the settlement's galdr- worker decides which buildings get warded first. The jarl's hall. The grain-store. The livestock barn. The common houses come last. If the galdr-worker runs out of season before the common houses are warded — and this happens most years — the common folk spend the Long Dark with salt and threshold iron and prayer. The jarl sleeps behind rune-carved stone. This is not cruelty. It is triage. But the people who sleep outside the wards know the difference between triage and privilege when the screaming starts at night.

Second, jarls control access to galdr-workers. A galdr-worker who is bound to a jarl's household does not do freelance work. If a farmer outside the jarl's favor needs a ward repaired, the farmer petitions the jarl, and the jarl decides whether the farmer has been loyal enough to deserve protection. This turns ward-access into a loyalty test. Pay your taxes. Send your sons to the muster. Do not speak against the jarl at the ting. Do these things and your grain-store will be warded. Fail, and your family spends the dark season at the mercy of whatever walks.

Third, and most dangerously, jarls can threaten ward-withdrawal. A settlement that defies its jarl — refuses a tax levy, shelters an outlaw, speaks against the jarl at the Allthing — can have its wards suspended. The galdr-worker does not come. The stones are not maintained. The runes fade at their natural rate and inside three years the perimeter is open. Nobody needs to swing a sword. The Long Dark does the work.

When a settlement's wards fail completely, the consequences are immediate and measurable. The first Long Dark without wards costs livestock — the dead are drawn to animal heat and animal fear before they turn to humans. The second Long Dark costs people. Not the strong ones. The ones who go out to check the livestock. The ones who step outside to relieve themselves at night. The ones who hear a voice they recognize calling from beyond the salt line and make the mistake of answering. A settlement that loses its wards for three consecutive Long Darks either relocates, submits to the nearest jarl on whatever terms are offered, or ceases to exist. There are abandoned farmsteads throughout the Rimevegr. Not all of them were abandoned by choice.

_The ward-stone economy is a protection racket with runes instead of muscle. The jarl does not threaten violence. He threatens absence — the absence of the one thing standing between his people and the dark. The galdr-worker is the instrument, not the agent. Ash understands this. Every galdr-worker understands this. The question is not whether your skills will be used as leverage. The question is whose leverage, and whether they will let you sleep at night afterward. Ash sleeps poorly. He has for years. The band assumes it is the galdr that costs him sleep. They are partly right._

### Seiðr Services and the Price of Speaking with the Dead

A seiðr-wife charges 50 copper for a basic consultation. That is a half-silver coin, two and a half days of a mercenary's retainer, five days of food. It buys a trance, a reaching through the Veil, and a conversation with someone who is dead. The price has been stable at Skaldhaven for two generations. Nobody remembers who set it. Nobody argues.

The fifty-copper rate covers a standard reaching — one dead person, one question, one answer. The seiðr-wife sits. She breathes. She goes still in a way that makes the living uneasy because the stillness is not sleep and it is not wakefulness and it is not anything else that living bodies do. She speaks, and the voice that comes back is not hers. Sometimes the voice sounds like the dead person. Sometimes it sounds like something imitating the dead person. The seiðr-wife does not guarantee authenticity. She guarantees contact.

The social cost sits on top of the silver cost. A man who consults a seiðr-wife is a man who could not find his answer among the living. In the Iron Grip, this is weakness. Ordovast's settlements do not officially have seiðr-wives. They have healers, herb- women, midwives — women who do the practical work of keeping bodies alive without admitting that any of it involves reaching through the Veil. A huscarl who visits a seiðr-wife in Iron Grip territory does it at night, alone, and does not speak of it afterward. The information he receives is usable but its source is unspeakable.

In the Fjord Compact, the calculation is different. Sigrun permits seiðr testimony at the ting (reference `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.2). A dead man's oath-testimony is admissible. This makes seiðr consultation a legal service, not a spiritual one, and legal services carry legal prices. Testimony-grade seiðr — a reaching conducted in the presence of witnesses, with the seiðr-wife bound by truth-oath before and after the trance — costs 100 copper. Ten silver. That is a week's retainer for an entire mercenary band of eight. The price reflects the risk. A seiðr-wife who lies under truth-oath faces outlawry. A seiðr-wife who contacts the wrong dead — or whose contact is contaminated by something on the other side of the Veil — faces worse than outlawry. She faces the certainty that next time she reaches through, whatever she touched will reach back.

The Whispering Circle uses seiðr differently. The Pale Widow does not bring her information to the ting. She uses it beforehand — to know what the dead know about living men's secrets, to hear what was said in rooms that no living witness will report on. This is intelligence work, not legal work, and it has no standard price because it is not officially for sale.

The underground market exists in every settlement large enough to have a seiðr-wife. People want answers the ting cannot provide. Was my husband faithful? Did my brother die well or badly? Where did my father bury the silver he told no one about? These are private questions, and private questions cost more because the seiðr-wife is taking a social risk. A consultation discovered by the wrong person — a priest, a jarl's man, a neighbor with grudges — can destroy a seiðr-wife's standing. The underground rate is 80-150 copper depending on the question and the risk. Some seiðr-wives do this work exclusively. They have no public practice. They are known by word-of-mouth and they move between settlements the way Voss's band moves — never staying long enough to be pinned down.

Dalla's position in the band is unusual and, from an economic perspective, extraordinary. She is a competent seiðr-wife — Spirit-lore trained, Wyrd-reading 2, WYR 3. In any settlement she would command 50 copper per consultation and the social deference that comes with being the person who talks to the dead. In the band she is the healer. She sets broken bones. She mixes wound-paste. She tells Voss when a man is too hurt to march and argues with him when he disagrees, which is always. Her seiðr is used when the band needs it — when Voss needs to know what a dead jarl promised before he died, when a client's story does not match and the dead man's version might differ. She is not paid extra for this. She receives a standard share.

This makes Dalla an unpaid strategic asset worth, conservatively, 200 copper per week if she were selling her seiðr services at market rate. She works for a share worth 20 copper. Voss knows this. Dalla knows this. The arrangement holds because the band is not a market. It is a family that pretends to be a business, and in families the most valuable person is rarely the best compensated.

_The price of speaking with the dead is not the silver. The silver is what everyone counts and what no one remembers. The price is the knowledge. The dead do not lie, but they do not tell you what you want to hear, either. They tell you what happened. A wife learns her husband died calling another woman's name. A jarl learns his father murdered the man whose seat he inherited. A mother learns her drowned son was alive for longer than she hoped, trapped under the ice, scratching. Every seiðr-wife has seen what truth does to the people who pay for it. Dalla carries a catalogue of ruined faces — the expressions people make when they hear the answer they asked for and realize they did not want it. She does not charge the band. If she did, Voss could not afford the rate. Not in silver. In what it costs Dalla to reach through the Veil and come back carrying other people's grief._

### Rune-Marked Goods

The galdr economy runs on materials the way a forge runs on coal. The runes are knowledge. The will is the galdr-worker's to spend. But the physical substrates — the things into which the runes are carved, the things that anchor the galdr to the material plane — have to come from somewhere, and somewhere has a price.

The trade goods database (`data/economy/trade_goods.yaml`) tracks five core seiðr-and-galdr trade materials:

**Rune-carved birch staves (TG_SDR_001, 20 copper).** The lowest- tier prepared material. A birch branch, thumb-thick, stripped and dried, with blank rune-positions pre-carved by an apprentice. The buyer carves the actual rune. The stave provides the substrate. These are the parchment of galdr — cheap, disposable, good for rank 1 and rank 2 one-shot work. Trail-marks, temporary wards, nightfire-keeping. Ash goes through four or five per week when the band is on the road. He carves his own when time allows. When time does not allow, he buys them at Skaldhaven for 20 copper each and considers it robbery, because the wood costs nothing and the apprentice who carved the blank positions is paid in food.

**Barrow dust (TG_SDR_004, 30 copper).** Soil taken from inside a barrow. Not any barrow — a barrow where something walked and was put back down. The dust carries a trace of Veil-contact that makes it useful as an amplifier in seiðr work and as a binding-agent in higher-rank galdr. The collection is dangerous. The people who collect it — barrow-runners, mostly young men with more courage than income — sell to middlemen in Skaldhaven who sell to galdr- workers and seiðr-wives who do not ask where it came from. A pouch of barrow dust weighs nothing and costs 30 copper because the collection risk is priced in. One in ten barrow-runners does not come back. The other nine split the dead man's share.

**Silver wire (TG_SDR_005, 25 copper per arm's-length).** Galdr- grade silver wire, drawn at the Deepholm foundry. This is the critical bottleneck. Rune-work at rank 3 and above requires silver inlay — the rune is carved, and silver wire is pressed into the grooves to hold the binding. Regular silver does not work. The difference is in the smelting: Deepholm's crucibles are rune- marked, the forging temperature is maintained by galdr-workers who keep the furnace within tolerances that no untouched forge can match, and the drawing dies have been in continuous use since before the current master-smith's grandfather was born.

Deepholm's monopoly on galdr-grade silver wire is absolute and uncontested. There is no alternative supply. During the Long Dark, when roads close and caravans stop, galdr-workers pay double or triple for stockpiled wire. A settlement that enters the Long Dark without wire reserves cannot repair ward-stones until spring. Deepholm knows this. Deepholm's pricing reflects this. The Fjord Compact periodically pressures Deepholm to share the smelting technique. Deepholm periodically declines. The monopoly is their insurance policy — the reason no jarl can threaten a settlement that controls the one thing every other settlement needs.

**Standing stone chips (TG_SDR_007, 40 copper).** Fragments from pre-Cracking standing stones. Not quarried. Collected — chipped from the bases of the old mono-liths, carefully, by specialists who know which stones can be harvested and which cannot. The chips carry resonance from the original carving. A ward carved on standing stone chip substrate is measurably stronger than one carved on fresh-quarried rock. The mechanism is unknown. The effect is consistent. Galdr-workers pay 40 copper for a chip the size of a man's thumb because that chip can be the difference between a ward that holds for three years and one that holds for five.

The supply is finite. The standing stones are not being replaced. Every chip taken weakens the stone it came from, and some of those stones are themselves ward-anchors for settlements that have relied on them for centuries. The tension between preservation and harvest is a running argument at the Allthing. Skaldhaven maintains a register of standing stones and their condition. The register is incomplete, because not every jarl reports his stones, and not every stone is in a jarl's territory.

**Veil-touched water (TG_SDR_008, 50 copper per flask).** Water from springs that emerge near Veil-thinning sites. The water is cold — colder than groundwater should be — and it does not freeze at normal temperatures. It is used in seiðr work as a medium for scrying and in galdr as a quenching liquid for silver wire that has been rune-carved. The cost is not the water. The cost is the collection. Veil-thinning sites are by definition places where the barrier between the living and the dead is weak. Spending time near them carries risk. The collectors — often the same barrow- runners who gather dust — work in pairs, at midday, and never for longer than it takes to fill the flasks and leave. Fifty copper per flask is the price for water that might contain a trace of whatever presses against the world from the other side. Nobody discusses what that trace might do over time to the person who drinks it. Nobody drinks it. But the water sits in pouches on galdr-workers' belts, inches from their skin, day after day.

The material component market functions through a chain of collection, transport, and resale that mirrors any other commodity trade. Barrow-runners and spring-collectors sell to middlemen in Skaldhaven and Deepholm. Middlemen add margins and sell to galdr- workers and seiðr-wives. The margins are steep — a barrow-runner sells dust for 15 copper and the middleman resells for 30 — but the runners do not complain because the middlemen provide something the runners cannot: anonymity. A man known to raid barrows is a man the dead might follow home.

Skaldhaven's rune archive adds a dimension that no other settlement can match. The archive — sealed, accessible only to credentialed galdr-workers and by special petition to Sigrun — contains rune-forms that the public tradition has lost. Templates for rank 4 and rank 5 work. Combinations that the galdr-workers who use birch staves on the road have never seen. Access to the archive is not sold. It is granted, as political favor, and withdrawn as political punishment. A galdr-worker who loses archive access loses the ability to advance. Sigrun does not need to threaten. She withholds.

_The material world of galdr is a supply chain with death at one end and silver at the other. The barrow-runner dies collecting dust. The galdr-worker dies slowly, spending health for rune- power. The silver wire comes from a foundry that will never share its process. The standing stones crumble a little more each harvest. The springs near the Veil run colder each year. Nothing in this chain is renewable. Nothing is getting easier to obtain. The Rimevegr's magical economy is an inheritance being spent by people who cannot earn more, and every ward-stone that fails and every rune that fades is a withdrawal from a balance that shrinks and never grows._

### Cursed Items and Forbidden Trade

A cursed item is a piece of material that carries galdr with hostile intent. The rune-work is the same — same forms, same substrates, same silver wire. The direction is inverted. Where a ward says _not here_, a curse says _you specifically, yes you, suffer_. The craft is identical. The law is not.

The most common cursed item in the Rimevegr is the niding-pole. A wooden post, head-height, with a horse's skull mounted on top and turned to face the target's homestead. Runes are carved down the shaft. The carving takes a day, the activation requires the carver's blood, and the result is a directed curse that draws misfortune — sick livestock, spoiled grain, sleepless nights, a wrongness in the house that everyone feels and nobody can identify — toward the target until the pole is found and pulled down or until the runes fade. Rank 2 niding-poles fade in weeks. Rank 3 poles can last a season.

The legal status of niding-poles depends on the union (reference `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.3, curse arbitration). The Iron Grip treats them as assault: the response is to find the carver and apply violence until the carver discloses the counter-mark or dies, whichever comes first. The Fjord Compact treats them as a grievance claim — the curse may encode a legitimate complaint, and the Compact's process requires investigation before punishment. The Whispering Circle raises niding-poles as a matter of policy and pretends otherwise.

Curse arbitration at the Allthing is one of the few issues that produces shouting in an assembly that conducts most of its business at speaking volume. A niding-pole raised against a jarl is an act of war or an act of justice depending on who is speaking. The Iron Grip wants them banned outright. The Fjord Compact wants case-by- case adjudication. The Whispering Circle abstains from the vote and smiles.

Curse-marked weapons are rarer and more dangerous. A blade that has been used to kill and then rune-carved with binding-marks carries blood-memory — a trace of the death it dealt, locked into the metal by the galdr-work. The carrier of such a weapon feels the deaths. Not vividly. Not like a vision. Like a weight. Like holding something heavier than iron should be. Warriors who carry curse-marked blades report poor sleep, irritability, and a persistent impulse to unsheath the weapon that has nothing to do with their own intentions.

These weapons sell. That is the uncomfortable fact. A blade that has killed a named jarl and been rune-marked to remember the kill is a status item. Not for decent people. For the kind of people who buy weapons the way other men buy jewelry — to signal what they are capable of, or what they aspire to. The market is small, private, and concentrated in the same trading posts that move barrow dust and veil-touched water. Prices vary wildly. A curse- marked hand-axe — one kill, minor name — might sell for 100 copper. A sword that carries the memory of a jarl's death can fetch 500 copper or more, paid by someone who will never use it in combat but will hang it on a wall and tell the story to every guest who notices the rune-work on the blade.

Items taken from barrows occupy a separate category. Barrow loot is not inherently cursed, but it is inherently risky. A sword pulled from a draugr's grip may be perfectly clean — good steel, old make, worth its weight in reputation. Or it may carry a trace of whatever the barrow held down. Or the draugr who held it may remember the grip and follow the trail of the thing that was taken. There is no way to know in advance. A galdr-worker can examine barrow loot and assess the risk — is there residual Veil- contact in the metal, does the rune-work on the blade seem active or dormant — but the assessment is probability, not certainty.

This uncertainty drives the barrow-loot market in a direction that defies common sense: premium prices despite high risk. A weapon confirmed as barrow-origin sells for two to three times its material value. A shield with pre-Cracking rune-work sells for five to ten times. Buyers are paying for history, authenticity, and the status of owning something that came out of the dark. The risk is part of the appeal. A man who carries a barrow-sword is advertising that he is the kind of man who buys things from places where sane men do not go. Whether this is courage or stupidity depends on whether the sword's previous owner stays in the ground.

The black market for seiðr components is smaller, quieter, and more dangerous. The standard materials — barrow dust, veil-touched water — are legal to trade. The restricted materials are not. Human bone taken from a barrow where the dead were restless. Blood drawn from a living person without consent. Hair from a drowned corpse that has not been given funeral rites. These materials are used in seiðr work that the Allthing's prohibition on "death- seiðr" is specifically designed to prevent, and they circulate anyway, because prohibition has never stopped a market that serves a need.

The prices are set by risk, not rarity. Human bone from a restless barrow is not hard to obtain — you dig, you take, you leave before whatever is in there wakes up. The price, 100-200 copper depending on provenance, reflects the legal consequence of being caught with it. Outlawry. Not a fine. Not compensation. Outlawry — the dissolution of every legal protection, every oath-bond, every relationship that makes life in the Rimevegr survivable. A man caught carrying death-seiðr components is a man who has bet his entire social existence on not being caught.

The worst consequence of cursed items in circulation is not the immediate damage. It is the contamination. A ward-stone carved with silver wire that was, unknown to the galdr-worker, previously used in a niding-pole. A grain-ward set with barrow dust from a site that was more active than the middleman disclosed. The curse does not announce itself. It sits inside the material like rot in a beam — invisible until the weight comes and the beam snaps. A ward that was supposed to protect becomes the vector for the thing it was warding against. The grain-store rots from inside. The livestock sicken. The family sleeps poorly and then does not sleep at all and then one night the door is open and nobody remembers opening it.

This is why Deepholm guards its supply chain with the rigidity of a fortress. This is why Skaldhaven's archive access is restricted. This is why galdr-workers of reputation buy materials only from sources they have verified personally and charge their clients for the verification. Trust in the material chain is the invisible foundation of every ward in the Rimevegr. When that trust breaks, the wards do not fail mechanically. They fail morally — doing the opposite of what they were built to do, turning protection into pathway, turning the safe place into the dangerous one.

_The cursed item trade is the shadow of the ward-stone trade. Same materials. Same skills. Same supply chain. Different direction. Every silver wire that becomes a blessing could have become a weapon. Every barrow dust that strengthens a ward could have weakened one. The market does not distinguish between protection and predation. It sells to both. Ash knows this. He buys his materials from a single contact in Deepholm — a woman named Bryn who has sold clean wire for eleven years and has never once supplied something that carried a trace. Eleven years of trust purchased with eleven years of vigilance. If Bryn dies, Ash loses more than a supplier. He loses the ability to guarantee that the wards he carves are doing what he tells his clients they do._

### The Band's Magic Inventory

What a jarl buys when he hires Voss's Black Axes is not twelve fighters. Any jarl can muster twelve fighters from his own household. What a jarl buys is capability that his household cannot replicate — tactical mobility, contract flexibility, the willingness to do ugly work without political entanglement, and magic. The magic is the part that closes the deal, and it is the part that Voss does not price accurately, because Voss thinks in swords and Voss undercounts what he carries.

**Ash — Rune-lore 3, WYR 3.** The band's primary galdr-worker. Rank 3 capability means Ash can set annual wards, create structural protections that hold through the Long Dark, and carve binding- marks that will turn draugr away from a building or a camp perimeter. In settlement terms, Ash alone is worth a jarl's galdr-worker retainer — 150-300 copper per major ward, 30 copper per consultation, plus the ongoing maintenance that keeps ward- stones functional between the annual recarvings.

His kit is complete: carving knives, templates, blood-lancets, chalk, binding cord, clean ash, and a reserve of Deepholm silver wire that costs the band 50 copper per season to maintain. He carries standing stone chips when the band can afford them. Currently the band carries three chips, valued at 120 copper, and one flask of veil-touched water at 50 copper. Total galdr material inventory: roughly 220 copper in consumables, plus the kit at 200 copper replacement value. Four hundred and twenty copper strapped to one man's hip.

Ash's limitation is biological. Rank 3 work costs 2 Willpower and 1 HP per use. In a week of active contract work — setting wards, marking perimeters, carving protections for a client's holdings — Ash can do major galdr work three times before he needs rest. His recovery rate has slowed over the past year. Cross-reference: MAGIC_TRUTH_003 in the mystery files. Ash burns faster than he knows. His rune-work is drawing more than the standard mechanical cost. The surplus is coming from somewhere that is not on his character sheet, and when the account is empty the deficit will come due all at once.

**Dalla — Spirit-lore (seiðr), Wyrd-reading 2, WYR 3.** The band's healer and seiðr-wife. Dalla's seiðr capability gives the band access to information that no amount of scouting can provide. She can speak with the dead. She can read the shape of what is coming. She can determine whether a barrow is active before the band walks into it. In market terms, Dalla's consultation rate would be 50 copper per session — standard Skaldhaven pricing — and her testimony-grade seiðr would fetch 100 copper per session under Fjord Compact law.

The band pays her a standard share: 20 copper per week. The disparity between her market value and her compensation is the single largest unpriced asset on the band's books. Voss knows. Dalla knows. Neither discusses it, because the discussion would require admitting that Dalla stays with the band for reasons that have nothing to do with money, and those reasons are the kind that make a mercenary commander uncomfortable, because they cannot be renegotiated when the contract terms change.

Dalla's healing work is her visible function. Her seiðr is her invisible one. The band uses it perhaps once per contract — one reaching, one conversation with a dead man or a reading of what the wyrd shows ahead. In between, Dalla sets splints and mixes wound-paste and argues with Voss about march pace when someone is injured. The band sees the healer. The clients see the seiðr-wife. Voss sees both and prices only the healer, which is why the band's contract rate has been too low for the past three years.

**Petra — Wyrd-reading 1, WYR 2.** The band's bone-lot caster. Her capability is minimal and unreliable. Rank 1 wyrd-reading is a coin flip dressed in ritual — she shakes the lots, she reads the fall, and the reading is right slightly more often than chance would predict. Slightly. Not reliably enough to base a tactical decision on, but enough that Voss listens when Petra says "bad road" and adjusts the march route.

Petra's market value as a wyrd-reader is negligible — a few copper per reading, if anyone would pay. Her value to the band is not economic. It is psychological. A band that has someone reading the lots before a march feels prepared. The preparation may be illusory. The feeling is real. Morale matters more than accuracy when you are three days from the nearest settlement and the nights are getting longer.

**Thorne — WYR 1, rune craft life skill.** Thorne carves runes on tent-poles and checks his rune-amulet when danger is near. He is not a galdr-worker. His WYR 1 means he has a knack — an instinct that might be magical sensitivity or might be a veteran's hard- earned paranoia. His rune-carving is cosmetic, not functional. The marks he cuts do not activate. They are habit, or prayer, or something between the two that Thorne himself cannot distinguish.

His value as a magical asset is zero copper. His value as a magical indicator is harder to price. Thorne's unease — the specific way he goes quiet, the way his hand moves to the amulet — is a signal the band has learned to read. When Thorne is uncomfortable, the band is more cautious. Whether this is because Thorne is sensing something real or because the band has superstitiously linked Thorne's mood to danger is a question nobody asks. The correlation is good enough. On the Rimevegr, good enough keeps people alive.

**Combined assessment.** If a jarl inventoried the Black Axes' magical capability the way he would inventory a weapons stockpile, the assessment would read: one rank 3 galdr-worker with deteriorating recovery, one seiðr-wife with testimony-grade capability being compensated at healer rates, one wyrd-reader of negligible reliability, one rune-sensitive non-practitioner, and approximately 420 copper in galdr materials. Total magical asset value: 500-800 copper per contract period, depending on how many times Ash and Dalla are used.

This makes the band significantly more capable than a standard mercenary company. Most bands carry no magic at all. A company with a rank 2 galdr-worker is considered well-equipped. A company with both a galdr-worker and a seiðr-wife is rare enough that clients notice and contracts improve. Voss's band should be commanding premium rates. They are not, because Voss prices by blade count and Voss prices by his own understanding of what the band does, and Voss's understanding stops at the sword.

The problem is that the band's magic is a wasting asset. Ash is burning faster than the mechanical tables predict. Dalla's seiðr work takes a toll that does not appear on any stat block — the accumulation of other people's dead, the weight of conversations that no living person should carry, the slow erosion of the boundary between her own thoughts and the things she hears through the Veil. Petra's readings grow no more reliable. Thorne's knack does not develop. The band's magical capability peaked sometime in the last two years and is now declining at a rate that none of its members have measured and none of them want to face.

A jarl who hires the Black Axes today is hiring a band at the top of a curve that is already bending downward. The wards Ash carves this season will be weaker than the wards he carved last season. The reachings Dalla performs this year will leave her hollowed out for longer than they did last year. The bone lots Petra throws will tell her nothing she does not already suspect. And Voss will set the contract price at the same rate he set it two years ago, because Voss does not count magic as an asset and therefore does not notice when the asset depreciates.

_The band carries magic the way a rich man carries inherited coin — spending freely because the balance looks large, never noticing that the income stopped and the withdrawals continue. Ash is the coin. Dalla is the coin. They spend themselves and the balance drops and nobody is earning more. A galdr-worker's recovery does not improve with age. A seiðr-wife's tolerance for the dead does not grow. These are depreciating assets in human form, and the band prices them at a standard share — twenty copper a week, the same rate they pay the man who carries the supply packs. The gap between what the magic is worth and what the band charges for it is the gap between what Voss understands and what Voss refuses to count. When the gap closes — when Ash cannot carve and Dalla cannot reach and the band is twelve swords with no magic at all — the contract rate will not change. The clients will. They will stop coming. And Voss will stand in an empty camp wondering what changed, because from where Voss stands, nothing changed. His people just stopped being worth what they used to be, and he never knew what that was._
