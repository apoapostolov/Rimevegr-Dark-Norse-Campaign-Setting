The gods went silent when the sky cracked. No one knows whether they died, fled, or simply stopped caring. People still sacrifice. Still carve their names on rune-stones. Still curse by them. The temples are cold and the statues stare at nothing.

What follows is what the people of the Rimevegr believe. Whether any of it is true is a question the novel never answers directly.

## The Slow Wolf — Úlfrinn Hljóði

Ragnarök was supposed to be fire. The sagas are explicit: the Wolf swallows the sun, the Serpent poisons the sky, the gods ride out in armor that burns with light, and the world ends in a battle so vast that the earth cracks and the sea boils and everything — everything — is consumed. Then, from the wreckage, a new green earth rises. New gods. New people. Sunlight that does not apologize.

None of that happened.

What happened was fog. What happened was silence. What happened was the slow, grey, suffocating nothing of the Cracking — the sky splitting without fire, the gods going quiet without battle, the world continuing without renewal. The people of the Rimevegr expected an ending and received an erosion. They expected Ragnarök and got weather.

This is the theological wound that will not close. The sagas promised a death worth dying in. The world delivered a life barely worth living through. Three interpretations survive, each one feeding on evidence the others cannot explain, and none of them kind.

### The Wolf Won — The Common View

The common folk believe Ragnarök happened. Not the way the sagas described — not in a single cataclysm of fire and divine war — but slowly, the way a body dies of fever rather than a blade. The Wolf did not swallow the sun in one bite. It swallowed it in increments, one shade of light at a time, over centuries. The dimming was so gradual that no generation saw the full arc. Each winter was slightly darker than the last. Each summer slightly shorter. The old complained that the light was not what it had been, and the young assumed the old were simply old.

The Veil is not fog. It is the Wolf's breath, still hanging in the air after the beast ate everything warm. The cold is the Wolf's body — vast, dead, draped over the world like a pelt over a cage. The people of the Rimevegr live inside the Wolf's belly: dim, cold, slowly dissolving. The dead walk because death itself is broken — the Wolf chewed through the boundary between living and dead the way it chewed through everything else, and now the two leak into each other like blood from a wound that will not clot.

**The broken promise:** The sagas promised rebirth. After the fire, after the flood, after the death of gods and men — green fields, clean water, a sun that shines without the Veil's grey film. The rebirth did not come. Common folk have three explanations for this, and all of them are worse than the question:

First, the gods failed to die properly. A half-death produces a half-rebirth: the stunted, fog-choked, grey-green world they inhabit now, where crops grow grudgingly and the sun is a rumor behind cloud. The world is not dead. It is stillborn.

Second, the rebirth happened somewhere else. The Rimevegr was left behind — forgotten by the cycle, discarded like the offal after a slaughter. Somewhere beyond the Veil, beyond the ice, the new green world exists, with new gods and clean sunlight and people who do not wake to the sound of barrow-stones cracking. The Rimevegr is the afterbirth. The new world does not know it exists.

Third — and this is the one that keeps people awake — the rebirth is still coming, but it requires the old world to finish dying first. The Rimevegr is not post-Ragnarök. It is mid- Ragnarök. The Wolf is still chewing. The dying is still happening. The world is being digested, and the people in it are simply too small to feel the peristalsis. When the digestion is complete — when the last rune fades, the last barrow-ward fails, the last settlement goes dark — then the Wolf will finish, and the new world will come. But no one alive now will see it. They are the food. They are not the future.

In Moor's End, an old farmer named Kjartan tells anyone who will listen that he can feel the Wolf's heartbeat through the ground on still nights. Slow. Patient. Digesting. His neighbors think he is mad. They also notice that the ground does pulse, faintly, during the deepest part of the long_dark, when the Veil presses thick and the barrows groan. They blame it on shifting earth. They do not lie on the ground and listen. They do not want to know.

### The Wolf Is Still Coming — The Rope-Readers' View

The rope-readers hold a different and more dangerous belief: Ragnarök has not happened yet. The Cracking was not the end. It was preparation.

The gods went silent because they are marshalling. The divine court is assembling its forces in the space behind the Veil, arming for the final battle that the sagas promised. The Veil itself is the battlefield being drawn — a vast grey curtain pulled across the world to separate the field of war from the field of the living, the way a jarl clears the hall before a judicial combat. The dead walk because Hael is emptying her domain, sending the worthy dead back through the boundary to arm themselves for the fight. The increasing frequency of draugr-risings, the fading of galdr, the boldness of the things that press against the Veil from the other side — these are not symptoms of collapse. They are mobilization.

The Wolf is out there. Past the Veil. Past the ice. Growing in the dark the way a storm grows over open water — invisible until it arrives, and then too vast to comprehend. Everything wrong with the world is the universe bracing for the impact of teeth the size of mountains closing on a sun that has already started to dim in anticipation of being swallowed.

**The disturbing implication:** If the gods are preparing for Ragnarök, they need soldiers. The dead who walk are not accidents. They are conscripts. Every person who dies in the Rimevegr is potential recruitment for the divine army. The barrow-wards are not protections — they are obstacles, interference with Hael's mobilization order. And the rope-readers who hold this view have begun to act on it.

In Frostfjord Hollow, a rope-reader named Arnbjörn was caught weakening barrow-wards — scratching through the galdr-marks on three barrow-stones so the dead inside could rise and march north, toward whatever the gods are building beyond the Veil. When confronted, Arnbjörn did not deny it. He said: "The gods need every spear. You are hoarding the dead like grain, and the battle is coming." The settlement's barrow-keeper was furious — two of the three barrows cracked open, and one draugr killed a thrall before being driven back into the earth. Arnbjörn was exiled. He walked north, into the Veil, and was not seen again. The rope- readers in Skaldhaven say he went to join the army. The barrow- keeper in Frostfjord says he went to die, and good riddance.

The broader consequence: rope-readers across the Rimevegr are splitting into two factions. The orthodox rope-readers maintain the old practices — oaths, contract-stones, the economy of suffering. The militant rope-readers, who call themselves "Odynn's heralds," believe their function is no longer to preserve the social order but to hasten the divine one. They sabotage barrow-wards. They preach that death in the Rimevegr is not an ending but an enlistment. They encourage young men to seek death in battle — not for glory, which is a lie, but for recruitment, which is a duty. The settlements that harbor heralds find their young men reckless and their barrows restless. The settlements that exile them find their dead quiet but their rope-readers gone, and a settlement without a rope-reader is a settlement without contract-enforcement, which is a settlement where oaths begin to fray.

The worst part: the heralds may be right. The draugr that rise from sabotaged barrows do walk north. They do not attack the settlement. They do not haunt the living. They rise, orient themselves toward the ice, and walk until the Veil swallows them. The barrow-keepers say this is simply the dead losing coherence and wandering. The heralds say the dead know where to go, and the living should be grateful someone opened the door.

### The Wolf Ate Itself — The Scar-Mouths' Whisper

The scar-mouths hold the third view, and it is the one that no settlement wants to hear, which is how you know it belongs to Lopt's followers.

The Wolf was never a creature. It was a truth — the final truth, the one Lopt tried to tell the gods before they sewed his mouth shut. The truth was: you are not necessary. The world does not need gods. The runes work without divine hands. The dead walk without Hael's permission. The crops grow — badly, grudgingly — without Fraeja's tears. The seasons turn without Skathi's breath. Justice proceeds without Tyvr's hum. The sagas endure without Bragi's string. Violence happens without Thurr's fists. The gate is watched without Heimr's eyes. Everything the gods did, the world does by itself, worse and slower and without meaning, but it does it.

The Wolf was the realization that the divine order was decorative, not structural. The gods were not the pillars holding the roof. They were the carvings on the pillars — beautiful, intricate, and bearing no weight. When the gods understood this — when Lopt forced the understanding on them, speaking the truth they sewed his mouth to silence — they simply stopped. Not died. Not fled. Became irrelevant. The Cracking was the sound of the universe adjusting to the removal of an ornamental load. The Veil is the scar tissue where the divine order used to be.

**Why this is the most disturbing:** It means the silence is not abandonment. It is accuracy. The gods are silent because there is nothing for them to say. They were never necessary. The rituals, the sacrifices, the oaths, the generational debts, the barrow- wards, the thing-assemblies conducted on judgment-stones — all of it works because the people make it work, through social pressure and habit and the fear of consequences administered by other people, not by any divine mechanism. The entire religious infrastructure of the Rimevegr is a machine that runs on human fuel and always has. The gods were the story people told themselves about why the machine existed. Remove the story and the machine keeps running, because machines do not need stories. They need operators. And the operators are still here.

The scar-mouths who hold this view are, in effect, atheists. But they are atheists who live in a world where the dead walk and runes glow blue in the dark and the Veil presses against the walls at night like something breathing. Their atheism is not a comfort. It is a horror. If there are no gods, then the dead walk for no reason. The runes work for no reason. The Veil exists for no reason. The world is a machine with no designer and no purpose, grinding forward on momentum alone, and when the momentum runs out — as the galdr-workers insist it is running out, decade by decade, rune-failure by rune-failure — there will be nothing to restart it. No rebirth. No new green world. Just a machine winding down in the fog, and the last human standing in the last cold hall, pressing a palm to the last rune-stone and feeling nothing.

In Vargheim, a scar-mouth named Thórdis speaks this view aloud at the thing-assembly once a year, as is her right. The hall listens in silence. No one argues with her. No one refutes the logic. They simply wait for her to finish, then continue with the day's business — oaths, verdicts, weregild calculations — as though she had not spoken. Thórdis considers this the strongest evidence for her position: the truth has been spoken, the machine continues, and the operators do not even pause to acknowledge that the designer was never there. She is right, or she is mad, or she is both. In the Rimevegr, the distinction has not mattered for three hundred years.

### What the Slow Wolf Means for the Living

The three views do not coexist peacefully. Settlements fracture along eschatological lines the way they fracture along political ones — slower, quieter, but with deeper roots. A family that believes they live inside the Wolf's belly raises children differently from a family that believes the Wolf is still coming. The belly-family teaches endurance: survive the digestion, bear the grey, do not hope for sunlight because sunlight is a memory the Wolf has already swallowed. The coming-family teaches purpose: prepare, fight, die well so the gods can use your death. The no-wolf family teaches nothing, which is the hardest education of all — a child raised without eschatology is a child raised without a story about why suffering exists, and a child without that story must invent one or go mad.

Mercenary bands avoid the question entirely. A band that argues about the Wolf's theology is a band that has too much free time and not enough contracts. But the question surfaces in the quietest moments — around the fire at night, in the pause before a shield-wall closes, in the breath between the decision to fight and the first impact. Every man in the Rimevegr has a private answer to the Slow Wolf, and he keeps it where he keeps his other fears: close to the chest, behind the armor, where it cannot be used against him.

## Forbidden Renderings

1. **Never confirm a god exists.** The novel maintains ambiguity.
2. **Never have a god speak directly.** Rune-stones speak. The dead speak. Dreams speak. The gods do not.
3. **Never use the gods as plot devices.** No divine intervention, no miracles, no deus ex machina.
4. **Never have a character lose faith as a dramatic beat.** In the Rimevegr, faith is not a thing you lose — it is a habit you perform because the alternative is worse.
5. **Never explain the Cracking.** Why the gods went silent is the unanswered question of the setting. Answering it would collapse the ambiguity that makes everything work.

## Temple Architecture and Sacred Spaces

### What a Temple Looks Like

A temple in the Rimevegr is not a place of comfort. It is a stone building — always stone, never wood, because wood burns and gods are supposed to be permanent — with walls thick enough to hold cold the way a cellar holds darkness. The stone is local: grey granite in the fjord settlements, dark basalt near the volcanic flats, pale limestone in the lowlands where the quarries have not yet been exhausted. The walls are undecorated on the outside. A temple announces itself by what it lacks: no smoke from the roof, no light from the windows, no sound from within. A building that radiates absence in a settlement full of smoky, noisy, warm-ish halls.

Inside, the space is a single room. No partitions, no side- chapels, no alcoves for private prayer. Privacy is not a concept the Rimevegr's theology supports — what you say to the gods, you say in front of witnesses, or you say nothing. The ceiling is low, barely head-height for a tall man, which forces everyone who enters to hunch slightly. This is not accidental. The builders understood that a body forced into a crouch remembers its smallness, and smallness is the correct posture before silent gods.

The floor is packed earth, not flagstone. The earth connects the temple to the ground beneath it, and the ground connects to the barrows, and the barrows connect to Hael, and Hael is the only god anyone is certain still functions. A temple with a stone floor is a temple that has cut itself off from the dead. The Rimevegr does not build those.

At the center: the god-post. A timber pillar, roughly carved into the shape of whichever god the temple serves, sunk into the earth floor so that the base is buried and the figure rises from the ground like something growing — or something trying to climb out. The carving is crude. Rimevegr artisans can produce fine work when they choose, but temple carvings are deliberately rough, because finished work implies a finished god, and the gods are not finished. They are ongoing. Or absent. Or both.

The god-post's eyes are always open. In Odynn's temples, one eye socket is empty — a hole bored through the wood, so the wall behind is visible through the skull. In Thurr's temples, the eyes are closed — carved shut, lids heavy, as though the god cannot bear to look. In Fraeja's temples, the eyes are dry — no tears carved, which is the cruelest detail, because Fraeja wept until she couldn't, and the statue commemorates the moment the weeping stopped. In Hael's temples, the right eye is bone — the wood stripped and bleached on one side to show the skull beneath the face.

There is no fire in a temple. This is the rule that separates sacred from domestic space. The hearth is life. The temple is not-life. Entering a temple means leaving warmth behind, and the cold inside is not merely uncomfortable — during the long_dark, when temperatures outside drop below what exposed skin can survive, the cold inside a stone temple with no fire is genuinely dangerous. Services are short because they must be. A priest who speaks too long risks killing his congregation through exposure. Theology in the Rimevegr is concise because hypothermia enforces brevity.

### Temple Variations by God

**Odynn:** Stone building, always. The rope-beam runs across the ceiling — a heavy timber from which the initiation-hanging is performed. The ash-tree motif is carved into the lintel: a dead tree with roots that grip nothing. Crow feathers are pinned to the walls with iron nails, one for each question asked of the god that year. By year's end the walls are black with feathers. At the first thaw they are stripped bare and burned. The cycle restarts empty.

**Thurr:** Thurr has no stone temple. His sacred space is the lightning-tree — a dead trunk blackened by a strike, left standing in the open air, surrounded by a ring of stones placed by anyone who has broken something in his name. The stones accumulate. Old lightning-tree sites have rings three deep, each stone a fist- sized lump of broken iron, cracked pottery, snapped tool-hafts. The ring is never cleared. Thurr does not clean up.

**Fraeja:** The coldest building in the settlement, by design. The Dry-Eyed choose the site for maximum wind-exposure. The interior holds the distaff-post — a standing loom-weight carved with the names of dead children from the community. The names are small, scratched with iron needles, and after a generation the wood is so densely inscribed that no new names fit. At that point the old distaff-post is buried in the temple floor and a new one is raised. The buried posts, layered beneath the earth floor, form a stratigraphy of the settlement's lost children, going down as far as the community has existed. In Skaldhaven, the temple floor has been dug to a depth of three feet and seven posts are buried there. Each one holds approximately two hundred names. The Dry-Eyed know the count exactly.

**Hael:** Not a building but a structure at the barrow-edge — the barrow-keeper's hut, half stone and half sod, pressed against the burial ground like a lean-to against a wall. The hut doubles as the temple. The god-post inside is Hael herself: half-face, half-bone, sitting on a carved stone chair. The chair faces north, toward the ice. A bowl sits in the lap — always empty, always waiting for a meal that will not come. Visitors place a pebble in the bowl when they visit. The barrow-keeper empties the bowl each dawn. The pebbles are placed on the nearest barrow-stone. Over decades, the barrow-stones become cairns, buried in small stones left by the living for the dead.

**Lopt:** No temple. No sacred space. Lopt is worshipped — if that word applies — in the negative spaces: the underside of an overturned boat, the shadow behind a storehouse, the gap between two buildings where the wind howls and no one lingers. The scar- mouths sleep in these places. The Lopt-mark (inverted rune) is scratched wherever scar-mouths have rested, accumulating over years into dense clusters of inverted marks that visitors read as warnings. A building wall covered in Lopt-marks is a building the scar-mouths have used, and therefore a building where many uncomfortable truths have been spoken. People avoid it. The wall does not care.

### Tending and Decay

Temples are tended by whatever priesthood serves the relevant god, but the physical maintenance falls to the settlement as a whole. Re-thatching the roof (Fraeja's and Hael's temples have sod roofs that need replacing every three to five years), re- pointing the stone walls with lime-mud, clearing frost-damage in spring — these are communal tasks performed without enthusiasm but without refusal. A settlement that lets its temple crumble is a settlement that has given up on the possibility that the gods might return. No jarl will be the one to authorize that surrender.

The smell of a Rimevegr temple is distinctive: cold stone, old blood (from palm-cuttings at the god-post), the mineral tang of earth floor, and beneath it all the faint sweetness of rot from the buried distaff-posts or the offerings that seep into the ground. In Deepholm, the temple smells of crow feathers and iron — a metallic, dusty scent that visitors describe as the smell of a question no one answered. In Grimholt, the temple smells of nothing at all, which is worse, because even the stone has stopped holding memory.

Temples that are abandoned — and some have been, in settlements that shrank or were overrun — become barrow-adjacent spaces. The dead gravitate toward empty temples the way water fills a hole. A settlement that reclaims an abandoned temple must re-ward it with galdr before use, and the re-warding always feels weaker than the original. The temple remembers being empty. The gods do not return to spaces they have already left. Or so the rope- readers say, which means the statement is both theological assertion and self-serving job security.

## The Calendar of Faith

The twelve months of the Rimevegr year (see `10_CALENDAR_AND_HIDDEN_EVENTS.md`) each carry religious significance that predates the Cracking. The calendar was built for a world where gods answered. It now operates in a world where they do not, and the dissonance between what the rites expect and what the silence delivers is the central rhythm of religious life.

### Long Sun (Months 1–2: Frostwake and Rimeblood)

**Frostwake (Month 1) — Fraeja's Month:**

The thaw. Ice retreats. The Veil thins to its annual minimum — still present, still grey, but lighter, almost translucent on the best days. Frostwake belongs to Fraeja because fertility follows loss, and the winter is the loss that earns the spring.

Rites: On the first day of Frostwake, the Dry-Eyed pour milk on the still-frozen ground outside the temple. The milk freezes on contact — a white smear on dark ice — and does not melt until the ground warms days later. When the milk-patch finally dissolves into the earth, the planting season has begun. Farmers watch the milk-patch the way other cultures watch the stars.

The Stilling's survivors (the women who sat the all-night vigil at the start of the long_dark) receive their grief-predictions during Frostwake. The Dry-Eyed visit each survivor privately and tell her what loss the coming year will bring. The predictions are vague — "a door will close" or "the thread will snap" — and inevitably come true, because in the Rimevegr, doors are always closing and threads are always snapping. The function is not prophecy but preparation: a woman told that loss is coming begins the emotional work of enduring it before it arrives.

**Rimeblood (Month 2) — Thurr's Month:**

The last month before the long_dark closes back in. Rimeblood is the fighting season — the weeks when the ground is dry enough for armies to march, the fjords clear enough for longships to sail, and the light strong enough to see the man trying to kill you. Mercenary contracts peak in Rimeblood. Raids happen in Rimeblood. Blood-feuds settled in winter are prosecuted in Rimeblood, because nobody fights a war in the dark.

Rites: The fist-to-chest before every engagement (described in Thurr's entry) is performed communally at the start of Rimeblood — the entire fighting population of a settlement gathers at the lightning-tree and strikes their chests in unison. The sound is audible across the fjord. Settlements within earshot answer with their own percussion. On a clear Rimeblood dawn, the fjords echo with the sound of hundreds of fists hitting hundreds of chests, and the bruises last for days, and the men wear them openly, and nobody admits they are afraid.

### The Long Dark (Months 3–12)

**Veilthin (Month 3) — Heimr's Month:**

The Veil thickens. Temperatures drop. Darkness arrives early and stays late. Veilthin is the transition month, the threshold between the brief relative safety of summer and the ten-month siege of winter. It belongs to Heimr because it is the gate — the boundary between light and dark, and the watchman who guards it is the first to see what comes through.

Rites: The gate-offering. The old stool is burned, the new stool placed. The gate-watcher begins his long vigil. Settlements that can afford it slaughter a sheep at the gate and let the blood soak into the threshold-stone — a request that the boundary hold, that what is outside stay outside, that the dark respect the door. Settlements that cannot afford a sheep use a chicken. The poorest settlements use nothing and simply close the gate harder.

**Ashfall (Month 4) — Odynn's Month:**

Deep autumn. The last leaves fall. The sky goes permanently grey. Ashfall belongs to Odynn because it is the month of accounting — debts assessed, oaths reviewed, contracts examined. The rope- readers convene at whatever temple the settlement maintains and read through the year's contract-stones: which oaths were kept, which were broken, which remain pending. The reading is public. The settlement attends. Hearing your name read as an oath-keeper is quiet pride. Hearing your name read as an oath-breaker is the beginning of a very bad winter.

Rites: The Asking (if performed that year) happens during Ashfall, because the questions that require children's divination are the questions about the winter ahead: will the stores last, will the ward-marks hold, will the settlement survive until Frostwake. The Asking is the last resort. By Ashfall, a settlement that has not answered its survival questions through normal means is desperate enough to hang a child by the ribs and watch its face.

**Wolfmoot (Month 6) — Tyvr's Month:**

Mid-winter. The thing-assemblies convene. Wolfmoot is the legal month — disputes that accumulated during Ashfall and Ironmoon are adjudicated, weregild is calculated, boundary-stones are confirmed, and the stump-sworn render verdicts that will hold until next Wolfmoot. The judgment-stone is blood-dark by month's end.

Rites: The Wolfmoot allting — the largest thing-assembly of the year — gathers at Skaldhaven and draws representatives from every settlement within travel distance (which, in the long_dark, is not far). The allting lasts three days. The first day is disputes. The second day is legislation — new rules, new tariffs, new agreements between settlements. The third day is the oath- renewal: every standing oath in the assembly's jurisdiction is re-spoken aloud, the oath-taker placing hand or stump on the judgment-stone. An oath not renewed at Wolfmoot is considered lapsed. Many oaths are deliberately not renewed, which is the only legal way to escape a generational debt — let it expire through silence.

**Barrowrise (Month 7) — Hael's Month:**

The deepest cold. The barrows are most active during Barrowrise — the ward-marks are at their weakest, the Veil at its thickest, and the dead are at their most restless. Barrowrise belongs to Hael because this is when the barrow-keepers earn their keep.

Rites: The Letting Go ceremony takes place on the first night of Barrowrise. Mourners who are ready to release their last memory of a dead person gather at the barrow-field and speak. The barrow-keeper listens. The memories are given to Hael and the mourners walk home lighter — or claim to. The barrow-keeper spends the rest of the month reinforcing ward-marks, re-carving faded runes, and sitting up through the night listening to the sounds the barrows make. Not every barrow speaks. The ones that do say things the barrow-keeper does not repeat.

**Longnight (Month 8) — Skathi's Month:**

The killing cold. Longnight is when the temperature drops below what the human body can sustain outdoors for more than an hour. Exposed skin blisters. Breath crystallizes in the air and falls as ice before it disperses. This is Skathi's month because the mountain has come down to the lowlands — the cold that usually lives above the treeline now covers everything.

Rites: The bone-walkers descend from the mountains (if they are coming down at all — some years they do not, and the settlements take this as a sign that the mountain has nothing to say). Those who descend bring meat — frozen, hard as stone, the product of kills made in the high places where animals still move because animals are better at cold than people. The meat is distributed according to need, not rank. This is Skathi's law: the cold does not recognize hierarchy. A jarl who is starving receives the same share as a freedman who is starving. The only exception is the thinning, when the bone-walker's arithmetic overrides the jarl's authority and mouths are counted against grain.

**Hearthstar (Month 9) — Fraeja's Month (Second):**

Fraeja owns two months because grief is the majority of the year. Hearthstar is the month when the stores are at their thinnest and the darkness is at its heaviest and the question of whether the settlement will survive until spring becomes unavoidable. The Dry-Eyed are busiest during Hearthstar — redistributing grain, fostering children whose families can no longer feed them, performing the cold arithmetic of who eats and who does not.

Rites: The Stilling — the all-night vigil without fire, food, or speech — happens on the first night of Hearthstar. The women who endure it are Fraeja's witnesses for the coming year. The women who leave are not shamed, but they are noted, because a woman who cannot sit one cold night without fire is a woman who may not endure the rest of the winter, and the Dry-Eyed watch for weakness with the same clinical attention a bone-walker brings to counting stores.

**Bloodtide (Month 10) — Lopt's Month:**

Bloodtide is the month of reckoning. Specifically: the month when the secrets that accumulated during the long_dark are exposed, because a settlement that enters spring with unresolved lies is a settlement that will fracture during the stress of the planting season. Lopt owns Bloodtide because Lopt is the god of uncomfortable truths, and Bloodtide is when they are spoken.

Rites: The truth-burning. During Bloodtide, the scar-mouths tend a fire in the center of the settlement — not in a temple, in the open, where everyone can see — and anyone who has a secret writes it on birch-bark and feeds it to the flame. The scar-mouth tends. The scar-mouth reads. The scar-mouth remembers. This is the annual purge, and it is the closest thing to confession the Rimevegr permits. People approach the fire with the expression of someone pulling a tooth — grim, resigned, briefly vulnerable, then done.

**Skaldsong (Month 11) — Bragi's Month:**

The penultimate month. The worst is either over or it is not — Skaldsong is when the settlement takes stock and the skalds compose the year's saga. This is the month of memory: who died, who lived, what happened, what was lost, what was kept. The skald works through Skaldsong, weaving the year's events into a narrative that the settlement will carry forward, and by the end of the month the saga is ready for performance.

Rites: The year-saga is performed on the last night of Skaldsong, in the jarl's hall, with the entire settlement present. The skald speaks. The hall listens. The dead are named. The living are named. The deeds are recounted. If the skald is good, the hall weeps. If the skald is great, the hall is silent — a silence that is not Odynn's emptiness or Fraeja's grief but Bragi's gift: the silence of a room full of people who have just been told the truth about their own year, and recognized it, and found it bearable. After the year-saga, mead is poured on the floor — one pour for each dead person named — and the hall drinks what remains. By dawn, most of the settlement is asleep on the benches, and the skald sits by the fire with bleeding fingers and a cracked voice, and the year is over.

Skaldsong is also the month most prone to söngœði — the sung- madness. The combination of year-end emotion, mead, crowded halls, and the skald's own performance creates the conditions under which a line catches and the hall builds a song no one planned. The jarls tolerate it during Skaldsong because the truths the söngœði surfaces are often relevant to the year-saga itself — lost details, forgotten names, events that the skald missed. The galdr-workers attend every Skaldsong performance with wax tablets ready, watching the room for the moment a second voice joins a line the skald did not compose. If the catch takes, the year-saga pauses. The song takes over. And the skald, who was the authority a moment ago, becomes a scribe — recording what the hall produces through the channel that Bragi's broken string still leaks.

**Yuledeep (Month 12) — All Gods' Month:**

The last month. The deepest dark. The breath before the thaw. Yuledeep belongs to no single god and therefore to all of them. It is the month of waiting — the Veil is still thick, the cold is still killing, the stores are either sufficient or they are not, and there is nothing to do but endure until Frostwake.

Rites: The Yule-fire is lit on the first night of Yuledeep — the one night when fire is brought into the temple. A single flame, carried from the jarl's hearth to the god-post, is held before the statue's face for a count of nine breaths. If the flame flickers toward the god, the year will be better. If it flickers away, the year will be worse. If it goes out, someone in the settlement will not live to see Frostwake. The flame always flickers. The interpretation depends on the priest. The settlement believes whatever the priest says, because the alternative — that a candle's behavior in a drafty stone room means nothing — is a truth no one can afford during the darkest week of the darkest month.

### The Missing Months

Ironmoon (Month 5) has no god. It is the empty month — the slot in the calendar where a god should stand but does not. Some scholars believe Ironmoon was once sacred to a deity whose name was lost in the Cracking. Others believe Ironmoon was always empty, a deliberate gap in the divine calendar to remind people that the gods do not cover everything. The settlements treat Ironmoon as a month of secular labor: repairs, tool-making, weaving, the practical work that sustains life without divine supervision. It is, by coincidence or design, the most productive month of the long_dark, because people without a god to petition have nothing to do but work.

## The Priesthood Ecosystem

Eight types of holy people serve the Rimevegr's nine gods (Thurr and Skathi share the trait of having no formal temples, and their servants operate in the field rather than from a fixed position). These eight types interact with each other the way any group of specialists with overlapping jurisdictions interacts: badly.

### Hierarchy (or Lack Thereof)

There is no supreme priest. No arch-rope-reader, no chief Dry- Eyed, no barrow-keeper-general. Each priesthood is autonomous, answering to its own god's theology and its own god's silence. This means that when two priesthoods disagree — and they disagree constantly — there is no arbiter except the thing- assembly, and the thing-assembly is adjudicated by the stump- sworn, who are Tyvr's people, which means every dispute between priesthoods is resolved by one of the disputants' peers, which means every resolution is suspect.

The practical hierarchy is determined by need. In a settlement under siege, the knuckle-scarred have authority. In a settlement dealing with draugr, the barrow-keeper commands. In a settlement negotiating a trade treaty, the rope-reader holds the contracts. The hierarchy shifts with the crisis, which means the priesthood that benefits most from a particular crisis has an incentive to prolong it. This incentive is not theoretical.

### Turf Wars

**Rope-readers vs. barrow-keepers:** The oldest conflict. The rope-readers want the dead to stay in their barrows so the oath-network remains stable — a dead oath-holder is a ledger entry, not a walking complication. The barrow-keepers want the barrows warded and maintained — their entire function depends on the dead being managed. When a rope-reader weakens a ward to let the dead march north (the herald faction), the barrow-keeper sees sabotage. When a barrow-keeper reinforces a ward that the rope-reader wants open, the rope-reader sees obstruction. In Frostfjord Hollow, this conflict escalated to the point where the rope-reader and the barrow-keeper refused to speak to each other, communicating only through the stump-sworn, who charged both of them a fee for the translation.

**Dry-Eyed vs. scar-mouths:** The Dry-Eyed control fostering and domestic crisis management. The scar-mouths expose secrets. When a scar-mouth reveals that a Dry-Eyed placed a child in a household she knew was failing (the fostering calculus), the Dry-Eyed close ranks and the scar-mouth finds their food-scraps thinner than usual. When a Dry-Eyed discovers that a scar-mouth is reading truth-burnings (the birch-bark secret), the Dry-Eyed does not expose this — she uses the knowledge as leverage. The two priesthoods exist in a state of mutual blackmail that neither can afford to rupture, because the settlement needs both the grief-managers and the truth-speakers, and losing either would create a vacuum the other cannot fill.

**Skalds vs. everyone:** The skalds control memory, which gives them leverage over every other priesthood. A rope-reader who offends a skald finds his contract-keeping omitted from the year-saga. A barrow-keeper who quarrels with a skald finds her barrow-tending described in unflattering terms. The quiet death — the skald's ability to erase a person from the record — is the nuclear option, and everyone knows it, and the skalds do not need to threaten it because the threat is implicit in every verse they compose. This makes the skalds the de facto arbiters of inter-priesthood disputes, not because they are wise but because they are dangerous.

**Gate-watchers vs. bone-walkers:** The gate-watcher monitors the boundary between settlement and wilderness. The bone-walker lives in the wilderness and enters the settlement at will. When a bone- walker crosses the gate without the knock — and bone-walkers do not knock, because the mountain does not recognize thresholds — the gate-watcher marks it as an intrusion. The bone-walker does not care. The gate-watcher does. Over time, gate-watchers develop a particular hostility toward bone-walkers, viewing them as creatures of the outside who refuse to acknowledge the inside. The bone-walkers view gate-watchers as nervous functionaries who have mistaken a wooden post for a cosmic boundary. Both are right.

### The Stump-Sworn's Impossible Position

The stump-sworn — Tyvr's one-handed lawspeakers — sit at the center of every inter-priesthood conflict because they adjudicate the thing-assembly where disputes are formally resolved. This makes them simultaneously the most needed and the most resented members of the priesthood ecosystem. They cannot ally with any priesthood without compromising their neutrality. They cannot refuse to adjudicate without abdicating their function. And they cannot be replaced, because no one else has sacrificed a hand for the law's authority.

The four stump-sworn in the Rimevegr meet once, during Wolfmoot, to align their interpretations. These meetings are private. What is discussed is never shared. The other priesthoods resent this opacity intensely, but they cannot challenge it, because challenging the stump-sworn's privacy would require a case brought before the thing-assembly, which is adjudicated by the stump-sworn, and the circularity is as airtight as it is infuriating.

## God-Waste — Guðtap

### What It Is

God-waste is not doubt. Doubt is a thought. God-waste is a condition — a slow, creeping erosion of the capacity to believe, to remember belief, to feel the weight of the sacred in the presence of sacred things. It is not a philosophical position. It is not the clever skepticism of Thorne, who dismisses the gods with pragmatic clarity. God-waste is what happens when the Veil gets inside a person's head and begins to bleach the faith out of them the way it bleaches color out of the landscape.

The Dry-Eyed diagnosed it first, because the Dry-Eyed are the priesthood most attuned to the slow deterioration of the human interior. They noticed the symptoms in the years after the Cracking — not immediately, not dramatically, but in the accumulating way that damp notices a wall: a woman who forgot which god she prayed to at her child's burial. A man who could not remember the words of an oath he had sworn ten years ago and had recited every Wolfmoot since. A barrow-keeper who stood at the barrow-edge and felt nothing — not grief, not fear, not the cold presence of the dead, but nothing, as though the connection between the living person and the divine architecture of the world had been quietly severed.

### Symptoms

God-waste progresses through three stages. The priesthoods name them differently, but the stages are consistent across the Rimevegr:

**First stage — The Greying:** The world loses its sacred dimension. A temple that once felt heavy with presence now feels like a cold stone room. A prayer that once brought a tightness to the chest now produces nothing. A crow on the crow-pole is just a bird. A barrow-stone is just a rock. The greying is subtle — the sufferer often does not notice it until someone else points out that they stopped leaving porridge at the crow- pole, or stopped knocking before entering their own home, or stopped flinching when someone mentioned Lopt's name. The habits of faith erode before the conscious beliefs do, like a riverbank undercutting before the cliff-face falls.

**Second stage — The Forgetting:** The sufferer begins to lose religious knowledge. Not secular knowledge — they can still count grain and mend a net and remember their children's names. But the sacred architecture falls away. They cannot remember which god owns which month. They confuse Odynn with Tyvr. They forget the Heimr-mark and carve a circle without the vertical line, or a line without the circle, and do not notice the error. They speak the names of the dead after the Letting Go, breaking the taboo without awareness that the taboo exists. The Forgetting is when other people begin to notice, because a man who confuses the gods is a man whose oaths may be sworn to the wrong power, and a missworn oath is a void oath, and a void oath is a broken contract, and a broken contract is a debt, and debt is the language the Rimevegr speaks.

**Third stage — The Hollowing:** The sufferer becomes unable to engage with the sacred at all. Temples provoke no response — not hostility, not doubt, but blankness. Oaths spoken by the hollowed sound correct but carry no weight; witnesses describe the sensation as hearing words with the meaning scooped out, like a bowl that has the shape of fullness but holds nothing. The hollowed do not mourn their loss because the loss includes the capacity to mourn it. They function normally in every secular respect — they farm, they fish, they fight, they trade. They simply do so without the invisible scaffolding of belief that holds Rimevegr society together. They are socially dead while biologically alive: present, productive, and profoundly empty.

### How Each Priesthood Responds

**The rope-readers** attempt to cure god-waste through pain, because Odynn's theology holds that suffering produces knowledge and the absence of knowledge is the core symptom. A rope-reader treating a greying patient will cut the patient's palm and press it to a rune-stone, hoping the shock of blood and cold stone will restart the sacred connection. It sometimes works for first- stage cases. It never works for the hollowed. The rope-readers record a 40% success rate for greying, which means they also record a 60% failure rate, and the failures walk away with a cut palm and the same blankness they arrived with, which is worse than before because now they have evidence that even suffering produces nothing.

**The Dry-Eyed** treat god-waste as grief — specifically, grief for a loss the sufferer cannot name. Their intervention is the vigil: the patient sits with a Dry-Eyed in the temple, in silence, in the cold, for as long as the patient can endure. The theory is that the cold and the silence strip away the secular noise that is drowning the sacred signal, the way clearing brush from a path reveals the stones beneath. The Dry-Eyed report better results than the rope-readers, but their sample is biased — only patients willing to sit in a freezing temple for hours seek their help, and willingness is itself evidence that the faith has not fully eroded.

**The scar-mouths** welcome god-waste. They consider it Lopt's gift — the stripping away of illusion, the removal of the decorative layer so the machinery beneath is visible. A scar- mouth who encounters a hollowed person does not try to cure them. Instead, the scar-mouth asks: "What do you see now?" The answer is usually: "Nothing." The scar-mouth nods and says: "That is what was always there." This is not therapy. It is recruitment. The scar-mouths consider the hollowed to be natural-born truth-tellers, people whose divine blindness lets them see the world as it is rather than as the gods decorated it. The hollowed sometimes become scar-mouths themselves. The transition is seamless — a person who feels nothing in a temple is a person who can stand in a jarl's hall and say the unspeakable without flinching, because flinching requires a belief in consequences, and the hollowed have lost the capacity to believe in anything.

### The Veil Connection

The correlation between god-waste and Veil exposure is undeniable. Settlements close to the Veil-line — the northern boundary where the mist is thickest and most persistent — have higher rates of greying and forgetting than southern settlements. Gate-watchers, who stare into the Veil nightly, develop god- waste at roughly three times the rate of the general population. Bone-walkers, who spend weeks above the treeline where the Veil pools in valleys like water in a basin, are almost universally grey by their fourth decade.

The mechanism is unknown. The rope-readers believe the Veil erodes the runes carved into the human soul — the invisible galdr that connects every person to the divine order. The seidr- wives believe the Veil is Fraeja's tears, and tears dissolve faith the way water dissolves salt. The scar-mouths believe the Veil is simply truth: a grey emptiness that shows people what the world looks like without the gods' decoration, and some people cannot unsee it.

### The Disturbing Case of Einar Hollow-Eye

In Deepholm, a barrow-keeper named Einar served faithfully for twenty-two years. He tended the barrows, re-carved the wards, spoke to the dead, and felt the cold presence of Hael in the earth beneath his palm every morning. In his twenty-third year, the feeling stopped. He did not notice immediately — the change was gradual, like the fading of a sound you have heard so long you forget it is there until it is gone.

By his twenty-fourth year, Einar was hollowed. He still tended the barrows. He still re-carved the wards. But the wards he carved were wrong — subtly, crucially wrong. The rune-strokes were technically correct but spiritually empty, the way a man who has forgotten the meaning of a word can still spell it. The wards failed. Three barrows cracked in a single winter.

The settlement did not blame Einar. They did not know the wards were hollow. They blamed the cold, the age of the barrows, the weakening of galdr. Einar knew. He carved new wards, knowing they were empty, knowing the dead would walk, knowing he was the reason but unable to feel the weight of that knowledge. A man who feels nothing cannot feel guilt. A barrow-keeper who feels no guilt carves wards that hold no power. The dead walk. The settlement suffers. The keeper carves again.

Einar still serves. He is the best barrow-keeper Deepholm has had in living memory — diligent, tireless, precise. His wards hold for months instead of years. No one connects the shortened duration to the keeper. Einar does not connect it himself, because the Hollowing took the part of him that would care. He is the most dangerous man in Deepholm, and he is dangerous precisely because he does his job perfectly, without faith, without feeling, and without the divine spark that makes the job work. He is the god-waste made manifest: the operator running the machine after the designer has left, producing output that looks correct and is, in every way that matters, empty.

## Sin, Taboo, and Transgression

The Rimevegr has no concept of sin as a state of the soul. Sin implies a divine standard that can be violated and a divine judge who notices the violation. The gods are silent. If they judge, the verdict is delivered to an empty courtroom. What the Rimevegr has instead is taboo — acts that the community defines as dangerous because they disrupt the fragile architecture of belief, social order, and barrow-ward that keeps the settlement functional.

Taboo is enforced by people, not gods. The punishment for transgression is social, physical, and immediate. There is no afterlife damnation in Rimevegr theology — the dead go to Hael regardless of their conduct, and Hael does not sort. The only damnation is what the living do to you before you get there.

### Taboos by Domain

**Odynn's taboos:**

- Breaking an oath sworn on his name (punished by three- generation debt inheritance).
- Speaking to a rope-reader without a genuine question (wasting the priest's suffering; punished by public shaming at the thing).
- Cutting the crow-pole (an attack on the settlement's connection to watchfulness; punished by exile).
- Lying at the contract-stone (the oath-stone detects nothing, but witnesses do; punished by erasure of the liar's name from all contract-records, which is commercial death).

**Thurr's taboos:**

- Refusing to fight when called (not cowardice in the abstract — specifically refusing to stand in the wall when the muster is called; punished by exclusion from the common store).
- Drawing a weapon in a Thurr-circle (the bare-fist dispute ring; punished by the knuckle-scarred, who hold the offender down and break the hand that held the weapon).
- Repairing a lightning-tree (the dead trunk is supposed to stay dead; mending it implies Thurr's mark can be undone; the community dismantles the repair and the repairer is made to break it down with bare hands).

**Fraeja's taboos:**

- Speaking Fraeja's name while pregnant (inviting the loss- then-renewal sequence; punished by the Dry-Eyed, who withdraw midwifery services — devastating in a world where childbirth kills one in four).
- Weeping in the temple during the Stilling (the vigil requires dry eyes; weeping means the mourner has not yet earned endurance; the mourner is led out and given a year to return).
- Disturbing the buried distaff-posts (the layered record of dead children; digging up the floor is equivalent to exhuming the community's collective grief; punished by the Dry-Eyed standing outside the offender's door).

**Lopt's taboos:**

- Invoking his name indoors (Lopt is an outside god — speaking his name in a hall or home invites destructive truth into the domestic space; the speaker spits and the household marks the doorpost with the Lopt-mark inverted a second time, cancelling the invocation).
- Approaching the truth-burning fire without a secret to burn (wasting Lopt's attention; the scar-mouths remember the face and watch it for a year, which is punishment enough).
- Harming a scar-mouth (the community refuses food to the attacker for a period matching the scar-mouth's recovery; harming a truth-speaker starves the harmer).

**Hael's taboos:**

- Defacing a name-stone (murder of the dead; punished by blood- feud that outlasts the defacer's lifetime).
- Speaking a dead person's name after the Letting Go (dragging the released memory back into the living world; the barrow- keeper re-performs the Letting Go and the speaker sits vigil at the barrow for three nights).
- Entering a barrow (only the barrow-keeper may approach the sealed entrance; anyone else risks waking the dead through proximity alone; the punishment is that the intruder must sit the barrow for a full month, repairing whatever their presence damaged).
- Burying a body without barrow-gifts (blade, bowl, name-stone; a body without gifts becomes an anchor-less dead, which becomes a roaming thing, which becomes the entire settlement's problem; the person responsible for the incomplete burial provides their own blade, bowl, and stone, taken from their possessions, and buries them with their bare hands in frozen ground).

**Tyvr's taboos:**

- Moving a boundary-stone (already described — public shaming and double restitution).
- Lying at the thing-assembly (not all lies — specifically, lies spoken with a hand on the judgment-stone; the hand is held to the stone by the stump-sworn until the liar confesses or the cold takes the skin off the palm, whichever comes first).
- Refusing weregild payment (the math is the math; refusing to pay is refusing the law; the stump-sworn adds compound interest and the thing-assembly enforces collection, by force if necessary).

### The Grey Areas

The taboo system has gaps that people exploit. A man who wants to escape a generational Odynn-oath can simply fail to renew it at Wolfmoot — the oath lapses, the debt dissolves, and the system treats silence as release. This is legal. It is also cowardly, and a man known for letting oaths expire through silence finds future partners unwilling to swear with him.

A skald who has been bribed to administer the quiet death — to erase a person from the saga — has committed no taboo, because the quiet death is not recognized as a religious crime. It is not recognized as anything, because recognizing it would require admitting it exists, and the skalds' control of the record means the evidence dissolves as it is created.

The most exploitable gap: thralls. Taboos apply to free people. Thralls exist in a theological grey zone — they are present in the divine order but have no standing to invoke it. A thrall who defaces a name-stone is punished, but the punishment is administered by the master, not the thing-assembly, and the master's interpretation of "appropriate response" varies from a beating to a killing. A free person who violates a taboo has rights within the system of punishment. A thrall has whatever the master decides they have, which is often nothing.

## Funerary Practices

### The Death-Breath to Barrow-Sealing Pipeline

Death in the Rimevegr is not an event. It is a process — a sequence of steps that begins with the final exhale and ends with the barrow-stone being rolled into place and the first galdr-ward being carved. Each step is essential. Skipping one risks the dead rising. Doing one badly risks the dead rising angry.

**Step 1 — The Death-Breath:** When a person dies, the nearest witness seals the mouth. Immediately. The jaw is bound shut with a strip of linen or leather, tied tight under the chin and over the crown. The purpose: the last breath carries the dying person's spirit upward, and if the mouth remains open, the spirit can re-enter and animate the body before it cools. A corpse with an open mouth is a draugr waiting to happen. In Grimholt, a man died in his sleep and his wife did not notice until morning. The mouth was open for six hours. The barrow- keeper was called. The body was bound at every joint — wrists, ankles, knees, jaw — and buried under triple ward-marks. The barrow has been quiet. The extra precautions are considered responsible, not excessive.

**Step 2 — The Washing:** The body is washed by the women of the household — never men, because touching a dead body with hands that fight is considered contamination of the blade-hand's luck. The water is heated with hearthfire ash, making it alkaline and slightly caustic. The washing removes the scent of the living from the body, which confuses the spirit — it can no longer identify its own flesh by smell and is less likely to attempt re-entry. The wash-water is poured outside the door, away from the settlement, never toward it.

**Step 3 — The Dressing:** The body is dressed in the person's second-best clothing. Not the best — the best is kept by the heir, because wealth stays with the living. Not the worst — dressing the dead in rags is an insult that invites retaliation. The second-best is the compromise: respectful enough to satisfy the dead, practical enough to satisfy the living. Arm-rings are removed. Silver is removed. Iron stays — belt-buckles, brooches, the small functional iron of daily life — because iron confuses the dead the way ash-water confuses the scent.

**Step 4 — The Wake:** The body lies in the hall for one night. The household sits with it. The fire is kept burning — this is the last fire the dead person will see, because the barrow has none. Talk is not required but is common: stories about the dead, disputes with the dead, the unfinished business that the living need to voice before the barrow-stone closes. In Ashen Reach, wakes are loud — arguments, laughter, weeping, the full range of human noise directed at a body that cannot answer. In Deepholm, wakes are silent. The silence is a final conversation conducted in the language Fraeja taught: the language of nothing left to say.

**Step 5 — The Procession:** At dawn, the body is carried to the barrow-field. The barrow-keeper leads. The family follows. The settlement watches from a distance — only the immediate kin and the barrow-keeper approach the barrow itself. The body is carried on a plank, not a cart, because the dead should be borne by the living's effort, not by wheels that could roll in any direction. The procession is silent. Skalds do not sing. Drums do not beat. The only sound is feet on frozen ground and the creak of the plank under the body's weight.

**Step 6 — The Barrow-Gifts:** Before the body enters the barrow, the gifts are placed: a blade, a bowl, and a name-stone. The blade is laid on the chest, edge up, so the dead can feel the iron. The bowl is placed at the feet, empty, waiting for a meal that the living will never bring and the dead will never eat but that must be there because the dead expect it. The name- stone is placed at the head — a flat stone scratched with the dead person's name, their parent's name, and the date of death. This stone is the anchor. Without it, the dead person forgets who they were, and a dead person without identity becomes a thing that roams.

**Step 7 — The Sealing:** The barrow is sealed. For a new barrow, this means rolling a stone slab across the entrance and packing earth around the edges. For an existing barrow (family barrows hold generations), this means re-sealing the entrance after placing the new body inside. The barrow-keeper carves the first galdr-ward on the sealing-stone — a binding- mark designed to keep the dead inside. The ward glows faintly blue in the dark — or it did, before the Cracking. Now it flickers. The barrow-keeper carves it anyway.

**Step 8 — The First Visit:** Three days after the sealing, the nearest kin returns to the barrow and speaks. The words are standard: "You are in the ground. You are remembered. Be still." This is the first maintenance visit. It establishes the contract between the living and the dead: we remember, you stay put. The visits continue — weekly, then monthly, then seasonally, tapering as the grief fades and the barrow settles into the quiet that means the dead have accepted their position. A barrow that is not visited within a year is a barrow that may open. The dead have a finite patience for neglect, and the Rimevegr has learned this through three centuries of experimental error.

### Class Differences

**Jarls:** Full rites plus a weapon-breaking ceremony — the jarl's primary weapon is broken on the barrow-stone so no one can wield it after him. The barrow is dug deeper. The gifts are richer: silver arm-rings, a drinking horn, the best blade the family owns (broken, but silver-hilted). The galdr-ward is carved by the settlement's best galdr-worker, not the barrow- keeper alone. Multiple wards, overlapping, reinforced. A jarl's barrow is expected to hold for a generation. It sometimes does.

**Karls:** Standard rites as described. The quality of the barrow-gifts varies with wealth. A rich karl gets a good blade and a carved bowl. A poor karl gets a rusted knife and a cracked pot. Both are sufficient. The dead are not particular about quality. They are particular about presence.

**Thralls:** A hole in the ground, dug outside the barrow-field, usually in the refuse-area behind the settlement. No blade, no bowl, no name-stone. The body is placed face-down — a deliberate insult that the thrall-owners justify as practical: a face-down body has more difficulty rising, because it must turn over first, and the delay gives the settlement time to respond. No galdr- ward. No procession. No wake. The master or another thrall digs the hole, places the body, fills it, and walks away. The entire process takes less than an hour.

The consequence: thrall-dead are the most dangerous dead in the Rimevegr. They have no anchor (no name-stone), no containment (no barrow-ward), and no maintenance (no family visits). When they rise — and they rise more often than properly barrow'd dead — they roam without direction, without identity, without the remnants of personhood that make other draugr at least predictable. A karl-draugr returns to its barrow. A thrall- draugr wanders. The settlements that produce the most thrall- dead are the settlements with the most roaming dead, and the connection is so obvious that even the jarls who benefit from thrall-labor have begun to notice, and some — a few — have started providing thralls with name-stones. Not out of kindness. Out of the pragmatic calculation that a named dead thrall stays put, and a nameless one does not, and the cost of a scratched pebble is less than the cost of fighting a roaming thing in the dark.

## Heresy and Dangerous Ideas

The Rimevegr does not have a formal concept of heresy because formal heresy requires a doctrinal authority, and the gods' silence has left doctrine as a consensus exercise. What the Rimevegr has instead is dangerous ideas — beliefs that, if spread, threaten the social order that the religious infrastructure supports. The response to dangerous ideas is not theological correction. It is exile, violence, or the quiet death of being erased from the saga.

### The Ash-Cult

The Ash-Cult worships the dead ash tree. Not Odynn — the tree. Their belief: Odynn is still hanging. He never came down. The Cracking was not the gods going silent — it was Odynn's weight finally cracking the tree, and the tree cracking the world, and the world cracking open to let the Veil in. Odynn hangs still, somewhere above the Veil, in a sky no one can see anymore, suffering without end because no one has the courage to climb high enough to cut him down.

The Ash-Cult's members are mostly old men and women who live near the treeline. They tend dead trees the way barrow-keepers tend barrow-stones — carving runes into the bark, leaving offerings at the roots, sitting beneath the branches and listening for the creak that might be rope. They believe that if someone climbs high enough — above the Veil, above the grey, into the sky where the dead ash tree holds the one-hung god — and cuts the rope, Odynn will fall, and his fall will end the Cracking, and the silence will break, and the gods will return.

No one has climbed high enough. The Ash-Cult sends aspirants into the mountains every Yuledeep, looking for the tree that holds the sky. None have returned with evidence. Some have not returned at all. The settlements consider the Ash-Cult harmless — eccentric old people talking to trees. The rope- readers disagree. They see the Ash-Cult as a direct challenge to their authority: if Odynn can be reached by climbing, then the rope-readers' suffering-based system of divine communication is unnecessary. A farmer who believes he can simply walk to Odynn will not pay a rope-reader for answers. The rope-readers' response has been to quietly discredit the Ash-Cult through the skalds, who compose mocking verses about old men hugging trees. The verses are funny. The Ash-Cult does not care. They are listening for rope-creak, and the laughter of the living is not relevant.

### The Flame-Walkers

Lopt devotees who take the truth-burning to its logical extreme: if burning a secret on birch-bark cleanses the confessor, then burning a lie in the physical world — a building constructed on false pretense, a store of grain hoarded while neighbors starve, a hall where a jarl rules through fear — is also sacrament. The Flame-Walkers set fires. They consider this worship.

The Flame-Walkers are the most actively suppressed group in the Rimevegr, because the settlements are built of wood and thatch and a single deliberate fire can destroy a community's winter stores and kill everyone who depends on them. A Flame-Walker caught in the act is killed immediately — no trial, no thing- assembly, no weregild. Fire is too dangerous for due process.

The Flame-Walkers' counter-argument is disturbing: every fire they set exposes a lie. In Kolvik, before it burned, a Flame- Walker set fire to a storehouse that turned out to contain not grain but weapons — the jarl was arming for a surprise raid on a neighboring settlement. The fire exposed the plot. The neighboring settlement survived. The Flame-Walker was killed. The jarl was deposed. The settlement considers the Flame-Walker a criminal. The neighboring settlement considers them a hero. Lopt, as always, is silent, but the scar-mouths note that the fire did what fire does: it revealed what the darkness hid.

### The Unbinding

A movement — loose, decentralized, deliberately leaderless — of people who seek to cut generational oath-ropes. Their argument: the three-generation binding of Odynn-oaths is unjust because the children and grandchildren who inherit the debt had no voice in the swearing. A man's oath should die with the man. His son should be free.

The Unbinding is popular among young people in settlements where generational debts have become crushing — families that hand over a quarter of their harvest every year to settle a great- grandfather's broken promise, families that owe labor to another household for a slight committed before anyone alive was born. The Unbinding's adherents do not renew oaths at Wolfmoot. They scratch out their ancestors' names on contract-stones. They refuse to acknowledge debts they consider illegitimate.

The rope-readers consider the Unbinding an existential threat, because the entire oath-network depends on the assumption that debts outlast the debtor. If a man can simply refuse to honor his grandfather's oath, then oaths have no long-term value, and if oaths have no long-term value, then the rope-readers — whose authority rests on maintaining the oath-network — are obsolete.

The stump-sworn are divided. Tyvr's law says the math is the math — a debt is a debt, regardless of whether the debtor chose it. But Tyvr's law also says justice costs, and a child born into an oath they did not make has paid a cost they did not agree to, and the math of involuntary cost is not the same as the math of voluntary sacrifice. The stump-sworn have no consensus. Wolfmoot debates on the Unbinding have grown louder each year. No verdict has been rendered. The rope-readers want the Unbinding declared taboo. The young want it declared law. The stump-sworn press their palms to the judgment-stone and wait for the hum that does not come, and rule nothing, and the silence that was supposed to be divine is now also legal.

### The Veil-Touched

People who claim the gods speak to them. They hear voices in the fog. They see figures in the Veil that resolve into faces they recognize as divine. They deliver prophecies, commands, warnings. They say: "Odynn told me." They say: "Fraeja sent me." They say things that the silent gods have not said for three hundred years.

The Veil-touched are universally distrusted. Not because the settlements disbelieve in divine communication — they desperately want to believe — but because the seidr-wives' Devouring theory has poisoned the well: if something ate the gods and wears their faces, then the voices the Veil-touched hear are not divine but predatory. A person who hears Odynn in the fog may be hearing something that killed Odynn and learned his voice. The settlements cannot distinguish genuine prophecy from mimicry, and in the absence of that distinction, they treat all Veil- touched claims as potential infiltration.

The response varies. In generous settlements, the Veil-touched are watched — allowed to live but monitored for signs that the voice is controlling them rather than informing them. In harsh settlements, the Veil-touched are exiled to the treeline. In Vargheim, a Veil-touched woman named Inga claimed Fraeja had given her a message for the settlement. The settlement listened. The message was accurate — it predicted a barrow-breach three days before it happened. The settlement was prepared. Lives were saved. Inga was exiled anyway, because the Dry-Eyed could not determine whether the warning came from Fraeja or from whatever cracked the barrow in the first place. The logic was: if the warning came from the threat, then the threat is learning to use our hope against us. Better to lose the messenger than trust the message.

## Sacred Objects and Relics

### Pre-Cracking Artifacts

Before the Cracking, the gods were not silent. Galdr was stronger. Ward-marks were carved by people who could feel the divine resonance and shape their runes accordingly. The objects that survive from that era — weapons, ward-stones, temple- posts, carved bones — still hum with a power that no living galdr-worker can replicate. These objects are the relics of the Rimevegr, and they are the closest thing to proof of the divine that the setting permits.

**Named relics:**

**The Skaldhaven Judgment-Stone:** The oldest judgment-stone in the Rimevegr, used at every Wolfmoot allting. Pre-Cracking origin. The stump-sworn claim it still hums occasionally — not at every verdict, not reliably, but sometimes, when the math is exactly right, a faint vibration rises through the stone into the palm. Whether this is genuine divine residue or the wishful sensation of a man who has sacrificed a hand for an institution he needs to believe in is the question the stump- sworn cannot answer and do not discuss.

**The Deepholm Archive Scrolls:** Not galdr-touched — simply old. The only continuous written record in the Rimevegr, maintained by the Deepholm barrow-keepers for three hundred years. The scrolls document rune-failure rates, barrow-breach frequencies, Veil-thickness measurements, and galdr potency assessments across generations. They are the closest thing to a scientific record the Rimevegr has produced, and they are stored in the barrow-keeper's hut, which is the worst-maintained building in Deepholm, which means the most valuable document in the Rimevegr is protected by a sod roof that leaks.

**The Frostfjord Ward-Stones:** A ring of twelve ward-stones surrounding Frostfjord Hollow, carved before the Cracking. They still glow blue at night — faintly, flickering, but visible. No living galdr-worker can produce this effect. The ward-stones have held for three centuries. They are the reason Frostfjord Hollow has the lowest draugr-incident rate in the Rimevegr. The settlement guards them with the same ferocity a jarl guards his silver, and visiting galdr-workers who attempt to study the runes are watched carefully, because the rune-patterns on the Frostfjord stones are pre-Cracking knowledge, and pre-Cracking knowledge is the most valuable commodity in the Rimevegr.

**Thurr's Rod:** A blunt iron bar, arm's length, found in the ruins of a temple that predates the Cracking by an unknown margin. The bar is heavier than it should be — as though the iron were denser, or as though something inside the iron added weight. The knuckle-scarred who possess it (it circulates among them, passed from one to the next when the holder dies) claim it vibrates during storms. Others claim the knuckle-scarred are old men with broken hands and chronic tremor who mistake their own shaking for divine resonance. Both explanations are plausible. Neither is provable.

### The Relic Economy

Settlements that possess relics gain pilgrimage income. A farmer who walks three days through the long_dark to touch the Frostfjord ward-stones and feel the pre-Cracking hum does not make that journey empty-handed — he brings silver, or dried fish, or labor. Frostfjord Hollow's economy is disproportionately supported by pilgrimage, which gives the settlement an incentive to maintain the ward-stones' reputation and a counter-incentive to allow outsiders close enough to determine whether the hum is genuine or habitual expectation.

### The Forgery Problem

Where relics are valuable, forgeries follow. Travelling traders sell "pre-Cracking rune-stones" to settlements desperate for divine artifacts. The stones are galdr-carved by competent but contemporary hands, aged with soot and acid, and sold with stories attached — provenance chains that trace back to temples and workshops that may or may not have existed. Most forgeries are detected within a season, when the galdr-ward fades faster than a genuine pre-Cracking artifact would. Some forgeries persist for years. A settlement that has built its identity around a relic does not want the relic to be false, and a community that does not want to see a falsehood can sustain that blindness for a remarkably long time.

In Thornwall, a "pre-Cracking" ward-stone protected the settlement's grain store for seven years before a visiting galdr-worker identified the rune-pattern as contemporary. The settlement expelled the galdr-worker and kept the stone. The stone's galdr failed two months later. The grain store was breached by frost-rot that the ward should have prevented. The settlement blamed the galdr-worker for cursing their stone. The galdr-worker, by then three settlements away, did not know and would not have cared. The stone is still in place. The settlement still considers it sacred. Faith, in the Rimevegr, is not about what is true. It is about what works, and a stone that worked for seven years earned seven years of belief, and the failure at the end does not retroactively cancel the belief that preceded it. The Rimevegr's epistemology is pragmatic to the point of self-deception, and the people who practice it consider self-deception preferable to the alternative, which is Thórdis's truth: there is nothing behind the stone, there never was, and the grain rotted because grain rots.

## Thrall Theology — Þrælasiðr

Thralls do not have religion. That is the official position of every priesthood and every thing-assembly in the Rimevegr. Thralls exist within the divine order the way a cow exists within the divine order — present, accounted for, unaddressed. A thrall is not forbidden from praying. A thrall is simply not equipped for it: no name-stone awaits them, no barrow will hold them, no skald will carry their lineage, no oath-network includes them. They are theologically invisible.

This is the official position. It is a lie.

### What Thralls Believe

Thralls have a religion. They have had one since the Cracking, built in whispers and passed from thrall to thrall in the spaces the masters do not monitor: the byre at night, the latrine trench, the moments between commands when the master's back is turned and the thrall's mouth is free.

Þrælasiðr (thrall-belief) inverts the master's pantheon:

**Hael the Opener:** In the masters' theology, Hael is indifferent — the dead go to her because there is nowhere else. In Þrælasiðr, Hael is the liberator. Death is the only freedom a thrall can guarantee. Hael's domain is not the cold hall where the dead sit in silence — it is the open field beyond the hall, the first time a thrall walks without a chain, the first time a thrall has nowhere they must be. Þrælasiðr does not glorify suicide. It simply reframes death as the moment the collar comes off, and for people who wear the collar from birth to grave, this reframing is enough to make Hael the most beloved god in the Rimevegr.

**Thurr the Master's Fist:** In the masters' theology, Thurr is the god of necessary violence. In Þrælasiðr, Thurr is the name they give to violence that has no necessity — the beating that happens because the master is angry, the kick that comes because the thrall was too slow, the fist that falls because fists fall. Thurr is the curse-word in thrall-speech. "Thurr take him" means: may the master choke on his own violence.

**Lopt the Listener:** Lopt is the thrall's god. This is the most dangerous inversion in Þrælasiðr, because in the masters' theology, Lopt is the reviled truth-teller, the god no one invokes. In Þrælasiðr, Lopt is the god who lives under boats and sleeps in outbuildings and eats scraps — which is to say, Lopt lives the way thralls live. He is the god who sees from below. He knows every secret because he is present in every space the powerful consider beneath notice. Thralls leave no offerings for Lopt. They do not need to. Lopt is already there, in the byre, in the latrine stench, in the shadow behind the storehouse. He is listening. He always was.

### The Whispering

Þrælasiðr's central rite is the Whispering — a gathering of thralls in a dark space (always dark, because darkness is the thrall's only privacy) where each thrall speaks one truth that the masters do not know. The truths are small and devastating: "The master's son steals grain." "The mistress weeps when he is gone." "The barrow-ward on the north field is failing and no one has checked it." The truths are not acted on. The Whispering is not a conspiracy. It is a liturgy — the act of holding truth that the powerful discard, keeping it alive in the space where Lopt listens, because someone should know, even if knowing changes nothing.

### Why Masters Tolerate It

Most masters do not know Þrælasiðr exists. The few who suspect do not care, because a thrall who whispers to Lopt in the dark is a thrall who is not running, not fighting, not poisoning the well. Þrælasiðr keeps thralls patient. It gives them a narrative — you suffer now, Hael frees you later, Lopt hears you always — that makes the suffering bearable enough to endure without rebellion. The masters who understand this (and some do — the clever ones, the ones who manage thralls the way they manage cattle, with attention to the animal's needs as a function of the animal's productivity) tolerate Þrælasiðr because it serves their interests. A thrall with faith is a thrall who endures. A thrall without faith is a thrall who calculates, and a thrall who calculates eventually calculates the value of a knife in the dark.

## The Economics of Temples

### The Offering System

Temples collect. Not as a tithe — the Rimevegr has no formal tithing system because the gods have not asked for one, and taxing on behalf of a silent employer is a harder sell than taxing on behalf of a visible one. But offerings accumulate. A farmer who visits the temple to ask a question brings a bowl of grain. A woman who buries a lock of hair for Fraeja also leaves a measure of dried fish. A man who makes a contract at the oath-stone places a silver bit beside the carved eye. These offerings are voluntary, but the social pressure is not. A man who visits the temple empty-handed is a man who values the gods at nothing, and his neighbors notice.

The rope-readers collect the most, because Odynn's economy of suffering includes a material component. A farmer who asks a question and is asked "What will you pay?" does not pay only in blood. He pays in grain, in silver, in labor. The rope-reader eats from these payments. The temple stores the surplus. Over years, a well-attended Odynn temple accumulates a grain reserve that rivals the jarl's own stores.

### Temple as Storehouse

In lean years, the temple's grain becomes a survival resource. The priesthood can release stores to the settlement — and is expected to. A rope-reader who hoards grain while the settlement starves is not merely selfish — he is declaring that the god's food is more important than the people's survival, which is a theological position no one in the Rimevegr supports, because the gods are silent and the hungry are loud.

But the release is not unconditional. The rope-readers attach obligations: oaths sworn, debts acknowledged, promises made in the desperate clarity of hunger. A family that eats the temple's grain in Longnight owes the temple a return at harvest — with interest, calculated by the rope-reader, and the rope-reader's math is Odynn's math, which means the interest is always more than the borrower expected. The temple functions as a bank, and the rope-readers are the bankers, and the currency is not silver but obligation.

The Dry-Eyed operate similarly but with different collateral. Fraeja's temple stores milk, dried herbs, and wool — the domestic surplus that women contribute. The Dry-Eyed release these stores to families in crisis, but the release creates a fostering-debt: a family that takes from Fraeja's temple implicitly agrees that the Dry-Eyed may claim a child for fostering if the Dry-Eyed deem it necessary. This is not stated aloud. It does not need to be. Everyone knows.

### The Moral Calculus

The temple economy creates a permanent tension between the sacred and the practical. A jarl who empties the temple's grain to feed the settlement has made the pragmatic choice — people over gods — but he has also declared that the divine storehouse is subject to human authority, and that declaration echoes. The next time the rope-reader asks for an offering, the farmer remembers that the last offering was redistributed by the jarl, and the farmer offers less, and the temple's reserves shrink, and the next lean year is harder, and the jarl must empty a smaller store, and the cycle tightens until the temple has nothing to give and the settlement has nothing to give to the temple and the entire system collapses into the flat secular reality that the scar-mouths predicted all along.

In Moor's End, the temple was emptied three winters ago. The settlement survived. The temple has not refilled. The rope- reader is gone — he left after the emptying, considering it a desecration. The settlement has no contract-enforcement now. Oaths are made on a handshake. Handshakes hold until they don't. The settlement is, by Rimevegr standards, a theological ruin: a place where the infrastructure of faith has been spent and not replaced, and the people who remain are operating on the last fumes of a social contract that no longer has a divine guarantor. They are, in the scar-mouths' language, free. In everyone else's language, they are unmoored. The difference is academic. The winter is not.
