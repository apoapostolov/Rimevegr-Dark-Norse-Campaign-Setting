## The Three Soulbanes — Mannsbani Þrír

### What They Are

The Rimevegr recognizes three acts that kill the soul while the body continues breathing. Not crimes — the Thing adjudicates crimes, and crimes exist within the legal framework. These three acts exist outside the framework entirely. They are the things a man does after the framework has been demolished, after the gods have been scorned, after the wards have been inverted on his skin and the name has been abandoned and the bridges have all burned. They are the acts that complete the transformation from man to something else — something the Rimevegr has no proper word for because proper words belong to the ordered world and these acts belong to the space beyond it.

The rope-readers call them _mannsbani þrír_ — the three man- killers. Not killers of men. Killers of the man inside the man. Each one destroys a different component of the tripartite soul that the Rimevegr's theology assigns to every human being:

- The _hugr_ — the mind-soul, the seat of reason and will
- The _hamr_ — the shape-soul, the human form and composure
- The _fylgja_ — the guardian spirit, the divine inheritance

The three soulbanes are ordered. Each one is worse than the last. Each one requires the previous one to have already happened, because the soul must be dismantled in sequence — you cannot amputate the fylgja while the hugr still functions, because the hugr would scream and the man would stop. The sequence ensures that by the time the final act is committed, there is no part of the man left that is capable of objecting.

### The First Soulbane — The Eating

_Mannát_ — the consumption of human flesh.

This is the act that kills the hugr. Not because the meat itself is poison — the Rimevegr is a practical place, and there are records, kept quietly in the Deepholm archive, of besieged settlements and starving crews who ate the dead and lived and were not considered god-scorned for it, because starvation obliterates categories and the living have always eaten whatever was available when the alternative was joining the available. The difference is intention.

The god-scorned man who eats human flesh does not eat because he is starving. He eats because the eating is the point. The act is a communion — not with the gods, who are silent, but with the thing that lives in the silence. The man sits in a cave or a ruin or a hollow in the frozen ground, and he eats what was once a person, and the eating is slow and deliberate and conducted with the attention that a rope-reader brings to a sacrifice: every bite is a statement. The statement is: I am no longer one of you. Your flesh is my food. The boundary between your body and mine is the same as the boundary between a sheep and a man, which is to say it is a social agreement, and I have withdrawn from the agreement.

The hugr — the reasoning mind — cannot survive this. The hugr is the part of the soul that holds the categories: this is human, this is animal, this is food, this is not food, this is permitted, this is forbidden. The god-scorned man who eats the forbidden has forced his hugr to participate in an act that contradicts its fundamental architecture. The hugr does not break. It reorganizes. The categories dissolve. Human and animal become the same category. Food and not-food become the same category. The distinction between the man's body and the bodies around him — the distinction that is the foundation of individual identity — blurs, and in the blur the hugr stops being a mind and becomes a mechanism, still operating, still processing, but no longer organized around the principle of _I am human and that matters_.

What remains after the hugr reorganizes is a thing that thinks but does not reason. It plans but does not reflect. It calculates the distance to a victim the way a wolf calculates the distance to a deer — with perfect spatial intelligence and no moral dimension. The god-scorned men who have committed mannát and been captured (there are four recorded cases in three hundred years, and each case is kept in the Deepholm archive under a seal that the barrow-keepers do not break for casual inquiry) all exhibited the same pattern: total coherence, total functionality, total emptiness. They answered questions. They understood the questions. They did not understand why the questions were being asked, because the framework within which the questions had meaning — the framework that says eating people is wrong and requires explanation — no longer existed inside them.

In the winter of 288, a man was found in a cave above the Ashford valley. He was alive. He was warm, which was unusual for a cave in deep winter. The warmth came from a fire built inside a ring of human bones — femurs and tibiae arranged upright in the frozen ground like fence-posts, the skulls placed on top, facing inward. The fire was small and well- tended and the man was sitting inside the ring with the expression of a man sitting inside his own hall. He did not resist capture. He did not speak. He was brought to the nearest settlement, where the rope-reader examined him and found the inverted wards carved into his chest and arms and the Hael-mark reversed over his heart. The rope-reader asked his name. The man looked at the rope-reader without comprehension — not the incomprehension of a foreigner who does not speak the language, but the incomprehension of a creature that no longer understands what a name is for.

He was burned. The bones from the cave were collected and given to the barrow-keeper, who identified seven individuals. Three were outlaws whose descriptions matched missing wolfsheads from the Skaldhaven bounty-board. Two were thralls, identified by the iron collar-marks on the vertebrae. Two could not be identified. The barrow-keeper gave them name-stones anyway — "Unknown, died in the dark" — because a body without a name- stone is a body that will wander, and these particular dead had suffered enough wandering.

### The Second Soulbane — The Riding

_Dýrríða_ — being ridden by beasts.

This is the act that kills the hamr — the shape-soul, the human form, the aspect of identity that says _this is what I look like, this is how I move, this is the boundary of my body and everything outside it is not me_. The term is the Rimevegr's own, and it does not mean what the settlements assume it means when they whisper it to frighten children.

The Riding is not a sexual act in the way the settlements gossip about it. It is a spiritual surrender. The god-scorned man who has already eaten the forbidden and killed his hugr has one remaining boundary: the boundary of his own skin. The hamr — the shape — is that boundary. The hamr says: I am contained. I am this body. I begin at my skin and end at my skin and what is outside my skin is the world and what is inside is me. The Riding dissolves that boundary.

How: the god-scorned man goes to the places where the Veil is thickest — the hush-zones where sound dies, the frost-hollows where the temperature drops twenty degrees in ten paces, the barrow-fields where the wards have failed and the dead press against the boundary like animals against a fence. He goes to these places and he opens himself the way the inverted wards on his body have advertised: he becomes permeable. The barrier between what is inside and what is outside dissolves, and whatever lives in the Veil — whatever the seidr-wives feel pressing against the world's membrane, whatever the dying dead are fleeing from, whatever wears the gods' silence like a skin — moves through him.

The Rimevegr calls this "being ridden" because the language of horse-breaking is the closest analogy the culture has. A horse that is ridden has not stopped being a horse. It has not been destroyed. It has been _occupied_ — its body directed by a will that is not its own, its movement governed by something sitting on top of it, its strength channeled toward purposes it did not choose. The god-scorned man who has been ridden is still breathing. Still walking. Still wearing a human face. But the face is wrong in ways that are difficult to articulate and impossible to ignore.

The eyes change. Not the color — the focus. A ridden man's eyes track things that are not in the room. They fix on empty corners and follow movement that no one else can see. In conversation — if the ridden man can still be drawn into conversation, which becomes increasingly difficult — his gaze drifts to the left of the speaker's face, as though something more interesting is standing just behind them. Witnesses describe the sensation of being looked at by a ridden man as "being watched through a window" — the man is there, but the thing looking out is not him.

The body changes. Not dramatically — the god-scorned man does not sprout fur or grow claws. The changes are postural, behavioral, incremental. The way he walks shifts: less upright, more fluid, with a rolling gait that does not belong to a bipedal frame. The way he holds his head changes — tilted slightly, one ear forward, in the attitude of an animal listening for movement. The way he eats changes: faster, lower to the ground, with less use of the hands and more use of the teeth. Each change is small. Each change is noticeable. The accumulation is horrifying — not because the man is becoming an animal, but because the man is becoming an imitation of an animal performed by a human body that was not designed for the role, and the performance is getting better, and the audience cannot look away.

The seidr-wives of Icebreak, who have the most experience with Veil-contact, believe the Riding is literal: something from the other side of the Veil has entered the man through the doors he carved into his own skin, and the something is learning to operate the body the way a child learns to operate a puppet — clumsily at first, then with increasing fluency. The rope- readers of Skaldhaven disagree: they believe the Riding is self-inflicted, the final stage of a mind that has dismantled its own identity and is grasping for any organizing principle to replace the one it destroyed. The beast-behavior is not possession. It is mimicry — the hugr-less, hamr-less man copying the only model of functional existence he can still perceive, which is the animal, which does not need gods or names or oaths to know what it is.

Both explanations are consistent with the evidence. Neither is provable. The Rimevegr does not resolve this question for the same reason it does not resolve any theological question: the gods are silent, the seidr-wives disagree, and the man in front of you is walking on all fours and the academic distinction between "possessed" and "broken" does not change the fact that he needs to be put down before he reaches the settlement.

In the spring of 304, a hunting party from Frostfjord Hollow found tracks in the mud above the snowline. The tracks began as bootprints — heavy, deep, a man carrying weight. Over fifty paces, the stride shortened, the heel-strike shallowed, the toe-print deepened. At the end of the track, the prints were not bootprints. They were handprints and footprints together, four points of contact, moving fast, heading north. The hunters followed for two hours and then stopped, because the tracks entered a hush-zone and the hunters knew better. The tracks continued. Whatever made them did not stop at the zone's edge. It went in, and the silence swallowed it, and the hunters went home and told the gate-watcher, who marked it in the log as "large animal, unidentified, northbound." He did not write what the hunting party told him they suspected. He did not want the words in a document that someone might read.

### The Third Soulbane — The Wilding

_Villimennska_ — the severing of the spirit and the merging with the wild.

This is the last act. The act that kills the fylgja — the guardian spirit, the divine inheritance, the last remnant of whatever the gods placed in human beings to distinguish them from the beasts that the gods did not speak to. The hugr is already dead. The hamr is already broken. The fylgja is the final thread, and when it is cut, what remains is not a man and not an animal but something the Rimevegr calls a _villimaðr_ — a wildling — and the word is spoken in the same tone reserved for the Veil itself: not fear, not contempt, but the flat recognition of something that should not exist and does.

The Wilding is not an act the god-scorned man performs. It is an act that happens to him — the final collapse of a structure that has been systematically demolished. The hugr was killed by the Eating. The hamr was dissolved by the Riding. The fylgja — which has been turning its face away since the man's outlawry began, which withdrew further with each taboo shattered and each ward inverted — makes its final departure. The departure is felt. Not by the man, who no longer has the capacity to feel spiritual events, but by the people near him. Witnesses to a Wilding — and there are witnesses, three recorded, two of whom are sane enough to give testimony — describe the moment as a change in the air: a sudden cold, a pressure drop, the sensation of something leaving the room through a door that does not exist. One witness, a bone-walker named Dalla, said: "It was like watching a fire go out. Not the flame — the heat. The flame was still there. The body was still moving. But the warmth left, and what remained was not alive in any way that matters."

What a villimaðr looks like: a human body moving through the wilderness with the behavior of a predatory animal and none of the animal's innocence. The villimaðr does not speak. Does not use tools. Does not build fires. Does not wear clothing — the clothes have been shed over weeks or months, not in one dramatic gesture but in the slow divestiture of a creature that no longer understands what clothing is for. The villimaðr hunts. Eats. Sleeps in hollows. Moves through the Veil-touched landscape with a facility that no sane human possesses — the Veil does not confuse the villimaðr's navigation the way it confuses a normal traveler's, because the villimaðr is no longer navigating with the faculties that the Veil disrupts. It is navigating with something older, something beneath the human overlay, something that the gods' architecture was built on top of and that the gods' silence has uncovered.

The villimaðr is dangerous not because it is aggressive — a bear is aggressive, and the settlements know how to deal with bears — but because it is unpredictable in the specific way that a human body stripped of human motivation is unpredictable. It does not hunt when hungry and rest when full, the way an animal does. It does things that follow no pattern the hunters can read: kills without eating, hoards bones without building, marks territory with inverted ward-signs that it should not remember how to carve but does, because the body's muscle- memory outlasts the mind, and the rune-patterns scored into the skin are templates that the hands follow without a mind to guide them.

The settlements that border the deep waste know the villimaðr by reputation and by rare, devastating encounter. A villimaðr entered the livestock pen at Ashford in the winter of 309 and killed seven sheep without eating any of them. The kills were efficient — throat-cuts, not mauling. The gate-watcher found the bodies arranged in a circle with the heads facing inward and the blood pooled in the center. He reported it as a wolf attack. The barrow-keeper, who saw the arrangement of the bodies and the precision of the cuts, said nothing. She reinforced the ward-marks on the settlement's perimeter and did not sleep for a week.

### What the Villimaðr Means for Women

The god-scorned path is overwhelmingly male — outlawry skews male, the waste skews male, the violence that precedes the guðníðing skews male. But women walk it. Rarely. The cases that exist are recorded in the Deepholm archive with the same sealed notation as the mannát cases, and the archive-keepers handle them with the same reluctance, because the female god-scorned path includes a horror that the male path does not.

A woman who has completed the Eating and the Riding — whose hugr is dead and whose hamr is dissolved — is a body without a human operating principle but with a functional reproductive system. The Riding — the opening of the body to whatever presses through the Veil — has physical consequences that differ by sex. A man who is ridden carries the intrusion in his posture, his gaze, his movement. A woman who is ridden carries it in her body.

The barrow-keepers and the seidr-wives use the same term: _kvikindi-barn_ — the beast-child. Not a literal half-animal offspring — the Rimevegr's biology does not work that way and the people know it. What the term describes is a child conceived by a woman whose spiritual architecture has been demolished and whose body has been opened to the Veil's influence during the period of maximal permeability. The child that results — in the three recorded cases, and the records are sparse because the mothers did not survive long enough to provide testimony — is born wrong.

Not deformed. Not monstrous. Wrong in the way the ridden man's eyes are wrong: the body is human, the proportions are correct, the features are normal, but the thing behind the eyes has never been human. The child does not cry at birth. The child does not root for the breast. The child lies still and watches the ceiling with the fixed, tracking attention of a predator assessing a room, and the women attending the birth — invariably seidr-wives, because no one else will attend a god- scorned woman's labor — describe the sensation of the child's gaze as looking into the Veil through a keyhole made of flesh.

The mother dies. In all three recorded cases, the mother died within hours of the birth — not from hemorrhage or complications that a Dry-Eyed could treat, but from a cessation that the attending seidr-wife described as "the body simply stopping." As though the purpose the body had been serving — the gestation of the kvikindi-barn — was complete, and without that purpose the mechanism that had kept the body functioning through the Eating and the Riding and the Wilding simply turned off. The body cooled. The child lay beside it, still watching, still silent, still tracking movement with eyes that should not have been capable of focused vision in a newborn.

What happens to the kvikindi-barn is the question the Rimevegr has answered three times in three different ways. In the first case (year 261), the child was burned by the settlement that found it. In the second case (year 289), the child was taken by a seidr-wife who said she could study it. She and the child disappeared into the deep waste and neither was seen again. In the third case (year 311 — last year), the child was found by a bone-walker in a cave above the treeline, beside the cooling body of its mother, and the bone-walker — a woman named Yrsa who had spent thirty years in the mountains and feared nothing that walked on legs — carried it down to Frostfjord Hollow and left it at the gate without a word.

The child is at Frostfjord Hollow now. It is ten months old. It does not cry. It eats when fed but does not request food. It watches the barrow-field through the window of the foster-home where the Dry-Eyed placed it, and the foster-mother — who took the child because the Dry-Eyed asked and the Dry-Eyed do not ask without reason — says it is a quiet baby, an easy baby, a baby that requires nothing and gives nothing and watches everything with an attention that makes the other children leave the room.

The barrow-keeper visits weekly. She says the child's presence does not disturb the wards. She says the barrows have been quiet since the child arrived — quieter than usual, quieter than they have been in years. She does not say what she thinks this means. The settlement does not ask.

### How the God-Scorned Are Handled

A settlement that encounters a guðníðing — at any stage of the process — has one response: kill. There is no trial. There is no thing-assembly deliberation. The god-scorned man is not a legal entity. He is not even a wolfshead — a wolfshead was once a man and retains the shape of manhood. The god-scorned man has abandoned that shape deliberately. He is, in the Rimevegr's theological classification, _útgarðr_ — outside the fence. Outside the ordered world. Outside the gods' domain, by his own choice, which means outside the law's protection, outside the settlement's obligation, outside every social contract that says killing requires justification.

The killing is performed by whoever is nearest and willing. The body is burned, never buried. A god-scorned body placed in a barrow is a contamination — the inverted wards on the skin would interfere with the galdr-wards on the barrow-stone, and the barrow-keeper would need to re-ward the entire field. The ash from the burning is scattered in running water. The name — if anyone remembers it — is never spoken again. Not the quiet death of skald-erasure, which is a social punishment. This is something more complete: the person is removed from the record because including them would acknowledge that the god-scorned path exists as a possibility, and acknowledging the possibility is the first step toward normalizing it, and the Rimevegr cannot afford to normalize a path that leads from man to nothing.

The rope-readers maintain that the god-scorned path proves the gods are real — if the gods were irrelevant, then scorning them would have no effect, and the guðníðing would simply be a dirty, violent outlaw rather than a creature that walks on all fours and kills sheep in circles. The scar-mouths counter that the god-scorned path proves the gods were always a cage — the man who breaks the cage becomes something terrible, yes, but the cage was still a cage, and a cage is not a gift, and a god who builds cages is not a god who loves.

The stump-sworn, who adjudicate everything else, refuse to adjudicate this. The god-scorned are outside the law by definition. The law has nothing to say about a man who has dismantled himself. The stump-sworn press their palms to the judgment-stone and feel nothing — not even the occasional phantom hum that some verdicts produce. The stone is silent. The god-scorned are silent. The gods are silent. The silence agrees with itself, and the living are left to handle the consequences alone, which is, the scar-mouths note, exactly what the living have always done.

## Disease, Starvation, and the Cave Camps

### What the Waste Smells Like

A cave camp after three months of habitation smells like a charnel house. The men do not wash because water is scarce and cold and the skin that freezes wet is skin that dies. The wounds do not heal because the filth is everywhere -- in the bandages, in the food, in the water that drips from the cave ceiling through soil that the band uses as a latrine because going outside to relieve yourself at night is how you get a knife in the kidney from a bounty hunter.

Infection takes more outlaws than violence does. A cut that would heal in a warm hall with clean linen and honey-salve festers in the cave camp and the red lines spread up the arm and the arm swells and the fever comes and the man dies over three days while his bandmates decide whether to drag the body outside -- which means exposure -- or leave it in the cave, which means the smell.

Most leave it in the cave.

### Starvation

A band that has been raiding a valley for two seasons has stripped it. The farms are empty or defended. The livestock is gone. The stored grain was eaten or scattered or burned. The band has two choices: range farther -- which means marching through hostile ground with every settlement watching the road -- or starve.

Starvation in the Rimevegr waste is slow and specific. The body consumes itself. First the fat -- and most outlaws have no fat to spare. Then the muscle. The man who was strong enough to swing an axe becomes the man who cannot lift his arm above his shoulder. The man who could march thirty miles in a day becomes the man who sits against a wall and breathes and that is all he can manage. The mind goes strange. Decisions that should be simple become impossible. The starving man fixates on things -- food he remembers eating, a meal his mother cooked, the taste of butter -- and the fixation crowds out everything else until thinking about bread is all he does.

A band at the edge of starvation is capable of anything. The calculations that normally restrain violence -- the awareness that burning a farm generates feud, that killing a farmer generates a bounty, that visibly predatory behavior draws a coalition -- cease to operate because the men are dying and dying men do not fear consequences. The worst atrocities in the Rimevegr are committed by bands that are already finished. The blood eagle at the crossroads, the flayed skin on the frame, the corpse road -- these are the acts of men who are going to be dead within a season and have decided that the season's remaining days will be spent making sure everyone remembers.

### The Last Winter

When the food is gone and the raiding ground is exhausted and the band is too weak to march, the cave camp becomes a tomb. The men sit in the dark because they have no fuel. They do not speak because speaking costs energy they do not have. The sick ones die first and the dying is quiet -- a cough that stops, a body that does not move when the man next to it shifts position.

What happens in the last days of a starving outlaw band is not recorded because no one survives to record it. The bones tell some of the story. The marks on the bones tell the rest.

## Using This in Play and Fiction

### For the Captain

The wolfshead threat is omnipresent. Every hex the band crosses may contain men with nothing to lose. The captain's job is to read outlaw pressure, avoid unnecessary confrontation with desperate men who fight without restraint, and -- if a patron asks -- clear the wolves for coin that rarely covers the risk.

The wolfshead is also the mirror. The captain who looks at an outlaw camp and thinks "that could never be us" is the captain who has not done the arithmetic. Three contracts fail in a row. The treasury empties. The men have not been paid. The Articles fray. The Named Men argue. The captain who does not pay his men becomes the captain whose men do not follow orders, and the captain whose men do not follow orders is already an outlaw in everything but name.

### For the Ledger-Keeper

Track outlaw_pressure per settlement. Track bounties. Track the band's own Standing relative to atrocity consequences. The ledger does not care whether the burning was justified. The ledger records the Standing drop, the Feud Track advance, the bounty posting. The numbers are the judgment.

### For the Fiction

The atrocity is never consequence-free. The prose does not moralize -- the ledger does not moralize -- but the consequences arrive, patient and mathematical. The village burned in chapter three is the coalition that blocks the road in chapter nine. The sheep stolen in the Long Dark are the children who did not survive until spring. The oath broken at the Thing is the contract that no one will sign three seasons later.

Show the farmer's daughter who remembers the name. Show the blind man sitting in the elder's hall, his empty eyes turned toward the door, waiting for the sound of boots that he knows will come eventually because the men who blinded him are still out there and a blind man is not a closed account -- a blind man is a living witness who cannot be silenced because everyone has already seen his face.

Show the corpse road. Show the men who made it. Show that they were men once -- that they had names and plans and someone who loved them -- and show that the waste stripped all of that away and left something that stacks rocks on a ridgeline and drives stakes into the frozen ground and puts heads on them and faces them toward the road and calls it necessary and means it.

The system does not punish evil. It accounts for cost.
