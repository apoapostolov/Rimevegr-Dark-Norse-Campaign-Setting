# Dictionary of Norse Terms — Iron Ledger

<!-- notion-export:toc -->


**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr

**Purpose:** A comprehensive glossary of terminology, language, idiom, and usage specific to this setting. Covers Old Norse roots, in-world coinages, mercenary slang, trade language, supernatural vocabulary, material culture, and the everyday speech of the Rimevegr. Each entry is a tool for prose -- use it to give characters the right word for the right thing. A man who calls his axe a "brand" knows a different world from a man who calls it a "chopper."

**Usage rules:**

- Characters use these terms naturally. Never define a term in narration. Meaning arrives through context.
- The glossary includes real Old Norse words (adapted for readability), Rimevegr coinages, and mercenary slang that developed in the three centuries since the Cracking.
- Pronunciation does not matter. The reader will sound them in their head however they see fit. Spelling is for the eye.
- Terms marked **[Historical]** derive from attested Old Norse or early medieval Scandinavian usage. Terms marked **[Coined]** are invented for this setting. Terms marked **[Slang]** are mercenary or common speech, often crude.

## A

**Aesrgrim** — [Coined] "The Grim Gods." Collective name for the High Court of the pantheon: Odynn, Thurr, Fraeja, Lopt, and Hael. The gods who dealt with war, death, and binding. Used in formal oaths and camp curses. "By the Aesrgrim" is serious. "By every grim god" is not -- it is frustration.

**Aett** — [Historical] A family group or clan. Eight families made an aett. In the Rimevegr the word survives as a unit of social belonging -- "he has no aett" means a man has no kin network. The loneliest thing you can say about someone.

**Ale-mist** — [Slang] Cheapest grade of ale brewed from whatever grain is on hand. Barely alcoholic. Tastes of sour water and old bread. The standard mercenary drink when anything better is unavailable. "Ale-mist and hard bread" is shorthand for subsistence-level marching.

**Ale-price** — [Coined] What a settlement charges for hospitality. The literal cost of food and drink for quartering a band. Negotiated before weapons are set down. "What is the ale-price?" is the first question Gest asks at any gate.

**Allting** — [Historical] A general assembly. Larger than a local thing. Multiple settlements send representatives. In the Rimevegr, alltings are rare and dangerous -- assembling leaders in one place invites ambush.

**Amadou** — [Historical] Fire-starting material made from bracket fungus (Fomes fomentarius). Scraped to a felt-like texture, it catches and holds an ember better than any other tinder. Carried in the fire-pouch. Worth its weight in copper to a man on the march.

**Amber-path** — [Coined] The old southern trade route that carried amber north and silver south before the Cracking. Now a broken series of mud tracks and frozen ruts. Used metaphorically: "He walks the amber-path" means a man is trying to do business the old way, when the old way no longer works.

**Anchor-stone** — [Coined] A heavy flat stone used as a base for a temporary forge. Also: the central stone of a fire-ring in camp. Metaphorically, the person or thing the band cannot do without. "Dalla is the anchor-stone" means the cook holds everything together.

**Anvil-song** — [Slang] The sound of a blacksmith working. Heard at a distance, it means a settlement is alive and producing. A hammer striking nothing (a cold anvil) means the smith is dead or fled.

**Arm-ring** — [Historical] A coiled band of silver or bronze worn on the upper arm. Not decoration -- portable wealth. Arm- rings can be hacked into pieces (hack-silver) for small transactions. A man's arm-rings are his treasury. "He wears three rings" means he has three contracts' worth of savings.

**Ash** — [Historical/Coined] 1. The tree (Fraxinus). Preferred wood for spear shafts and tool handles -- strong, flexible, straight-grained. 2. Call-name for the band's galdr-worker. 3. What remains after a fire. In the Rimevegr, where fire is life, "ash" carries triple weight.

**Ash-born** — [Coined] Call-name for Thorne. Refers to the hall-fire that scarred him. In broader usage: anyone born out of a catastrophe. Applied to settlements rebuilt after burning. "An ash-born village" means it has been destroyed and remade.

**Ashfall** — [Coined] A named barrow near Frostfjord Hollow. Cleared by the band. Also used as a common descriptor for the grey particulate that falls with heavy rime-fog -- not true ash, but it looks and tastes like it.

**Axe-debt** — [Slang] What a man owes when someone else's axe saved his life. Informal, unrecorded, but remembered. An axe- debt between Named Men is serious. Between commons, it is a bond that elevates both.

**Axe-hand** — [Historical] The dominant hand. Also: a general term for a competent fighter. "He has an axe-hand" means the man can swing and hit what he aims at. The minimum qualification for band membership.

**Axe-haft** — [Historical] The wooden handle of an axe. Checked daily for looseness. A loose haft kills the wielder. In prose, Voss's hand resting on his axe-haft is his centering gesture -- the way other men press palms together.

**Ale-sick** — [Slang] Hungover. Useless until midday. "He is ale-sick" is said without sympathy. The band has no patience for men who drank what the band could not afford.

**Ale-wife** — [Historical] A woman who brews and sells ale. In many settlements, the only woman conducting independent trade. Ale-wives know the price of grain before anyone else.

**Alm** — [Historical] Elm. The wood for bows and shield- planks. Alm-wood is resilient -- it bends before it breaks. "He has alm in him" means a man absorbs punishment.

**Altar-stone** — [Historical] A flat rock, usually glacial, used for blóts and oath-swearing. Stained dark with old blood. Permanent fixtures at older settlements.

**Amber** — [Historical] Fossilized tree resin. Trade good. Rare in the Rimevegr since the Cracking. A piece of amber is worth more than its weight in silver, primarily to southern traders who no longer come.

**Andvari's curse** — [Slang] Gold or silver that brings bad luck. Named for the old saga. "That loot has Andvari's curse" means spending it will cost more than it yields.

**Andaþrumur** — [Coined] Breath-thunder. The roaring inward noise heard when the Veil presses too close to the living body: pulse, blood, breath, and bone all sounding too loud inside the skull. Common among outlaws and overworked seiðr-practitioners.

**Andavörðr** — [Coined] Spirit-ward. An object saturated by long use, oath, grief, or repeated touch until it behaves as if it remembers its owners. Not fully alive, not safely inert.

**Aptrgangr skugga** — [Coined/Historical] Returning-shadow. The weakest kind of dead presence: not a full revenant, but a repeating impression of motion, fear, or habit left in a place.

**Angle-iron** — [Coined] An L-shaped iron bracket. Used for structural joints in wooden construction. A settlement with angle-iron available has a good smith.

**Anvil** — [Historical] The smith's primary tool. A heavy iron block with a flat face, a horn, and a heel. The sound of hammer on anvil carries across any settlement. Silence from the smithy means trouble.

**Arm-brace** — [Historical] A leather guard worn on the bow- arm to prevent string-slap. Simple and necessary. Bruised forearms mark a man who forgot his arm-brace.

**Arrow-bag** — [Coined] A quiver. Leather tube with a flap. Carried across the back or at the hip. A full arrow-bag holds twenty shafts. After a fight, arrows are recovered and returned to the bag -- shafts are too costly to leave in the ground.

**Ash-pit** — [Historical] The depression beneath a hearth where ash accumulates. Cleaned weekly in a settlement, never on the march. Ash-pit ash is used for making lye soap.

**Ash-tree** — [Historical] Yggdrasil, the World-Tree, was an ash. In the Rimevegr, ash trees are practical: they provide the best spear-shafts and axe-handles. The mythological connection makes ash-wood feel more important than it has a right to feel.

**Ashen Reach** — [Coined] A village of 142 souls. Ruled by the Pale Widow. Grain economy. Significant political player.

**Auger** — [Historical] A T-handled boring tool. Drills round holes in wood. Used for barrel-making, boat repair, and construction. Every settlement smithy makes them.

**Awl** — [Historical] A pointed tool for punching holes in leather. Essential for field repairs to belts, boots, and harness. Carried by Gest alongside the ledger kit.

**Auðsýn** — [Coined] First-sight. The initial völva break in ordinary seeing, when patterns begin to glimmer at the edge of the visible world. Feared because it usually arrives unasked.

**Axle** — [Historical] The shaft connecting two wheels. In the Rimevegr, axles are rare because carts are rare. The word appears in settlement contexts and as machinery metaphor: "the axle of the operation" means the part that keeps things turning.

## B

**Back-trail** — [Slang] The path the band has already walked. Checking the back-trail means looking for followers. "Our back- trail is clean" means no one is tracking us.

**Bait-fire** — [Slang] A fire left burning at an abandoned campsite to delay pursuit. The enemy investigates; the band gains distance. Also: a fire set as a trap -- enemies approach the light and find arrows instead of warmth.

**Bale-fire** — [Historical] A large outdoor fire, typically for burning the dead or their possessions. A bale-fire for a Named Man uses good wood. For a common, whatever is at hand. A bale-fire seen at distance tells you someone died and was important enough to burn.

**Band-break** — [Coined] The moment a mercenary band disintegrates. Not a battle -- a collapse. Pay is too late, the captain is too weak, the grievances pile too high. Men walk away in the night. "We are three bad days from a band-break" is the arithmetic of failure.

**Beinasöngr** — [Coined] Bone-song. The low male mourning chant sung at the barrow while the dead are settled into earth or stone. Rhythmic, whispered, and more felt in the ribs than heard in the ear.

**Blóðminni** — [Coined] Blood-memory. The residue left in a weapon, garment, or place by intense pain, oath, or killing. Some seiðr-workers and völvas can read it like heat in metal.

**Bank-fire** — [Historical] A fire covered in its own ash to preservecoals overnight. The morning ritual: scrape ash, expose coals, blow gently, add tinder. Flame in two minutes. Letting the bank-fire die is a punishable failure on watch.

**Barley-water** — [Historical] Grain soaked for hours and the runoff drunk. Mildly nutritious. The drink of sick men and those whose teeth have given out. Not complimentary.

**Barn-post** — [Historical] The structural upright of a byre. Carved with runes in older settlements -- livestock is watched over, even if no one admits by whom.

**Barrow** — [Historical] A burial mound. In the Rimevegr, barrows are dangerous -- the dead inside may not be quiet. A sealed barrow is a problem for later. An open barrow is a problem for now.

**Barrow-breath** — [Coined] The cold, stale air that pours out of an opened barrow. Smells of old earth, old bone, and something sweet-rotten that has no name. Men who have cleared barrows can identify the smell from twenty paces.

**Barrow-black** — [Coined] Total darkness inside an unopened barrow. Torchlight reaches only a few paces. Beyond is nothing. The darkness has weight.

**Barrow-price** — [Coined] The fee for clearing a barrow. Higher than any other contract because the work is terrible and men do not always come back. Paid in advance -- half before entry, half on completion.

**Barrowrise** — [Coined] A named month in the Rimevegr calendar. The Veil is thinnest, the dead most restless. Travel is avoided. Settlements bolt their doors. Mercenary bands demand double rates.

**Barrow-wight** — [Historical] An older term for a restless dead within a barrow. Essentially synonymous with draugr but more literary. Skalds use "barrow-wight"; fighting men say "the dead thing."

**Bark-bread** — [Historical] Ground inner bark (pine, birch) mixed with whatever flour is available and baked into flat loaves. Famine food. The taste is resinous and bitter. Keeps a man alive when grain has failed.

**Barter-weight** — [Coined] The agreed exchange value of goods in a non-coin transaction. "What is the barter-weight of a good axe?" Answer: two months' grain for one family. Gest maintains barter-weights in the ledger.

**Bast** — [Historical] Inner bark of lime or linden trees. Twisted into rope, woven into rough cloth, or used raw as bandage material. The cheapest cordage available. "Bast-rope" means improvised and temporary.

**Baton** — [Historical] A thick stick used to drive a blade through wood when splitting kindling. The knife is placed on the wood; the baton strikes the knife spine. Every man in the band knows this technique.

**Bed-roll** — [Historical] A sleeping arrangement. Wool blanket or hide, ground cloth, rolled tight for march. The man who loses his bed-roll sleeps in his cloak on bare ground. "He has no bed-roll" is shorthand for destitute.

**Bed-rock** — [Slang] A heated stone wrapped in cloth, placed in the sleeping area for warmth. Heated by the fire, carried to the bed-roll. Holds warmth for two to three hours. The difference between sleeping and shivering.

**Berserker** — [Historical] A warrior who enters killing fury. In the Rimevegr, not supernatural -- probably. The eyes change, the pain stops, the reason leaves. What remains is a weapon that does not know when to stop swinging. Feared by both sides. "He went berserker" is not a compliment.

**Biface** — [Historical] A stone tool knapped on both sides. Paleolithic technology, but in settlements where iron is scarce, flint bifaces still serve for scraping hides and cutting leather.

**Bilge** — [Historical] The lowest interior of a ship's hull, where foul water collects. By metaphor: the worst position in any group. "He is in the bilge" means he is at the bottom.

**Bile-water** — [Slang] Brackish or contaminated water. Standing pools, runoff near latrines, anything that smells wrong. Drinking bile-water brings gut-sickness within hours. Dalla's first rule: clear water or boiled water, never bile-water.

**Biting-wind** — [Coined] Wind cold enough to cause frostbite on exposed skin within minutes. The first stage of a rime storm. When the biting-wind starts, shelter becomes urgent.

**Black-iron** — [Coined] Unfinished or poorly forged iron. Brittle, pitted, prone to cracking. Common in weapons looted from dead men or bought cheap in failing settlements. "He carries black-iron" means a man's equipment will fail him when he needs it most.

**Black Pine** — [Coined] The dominant tree of the Outvegr -- tall, close-growing, dark-needled conifers. Their resin is useful. Their shade kills undergrowth. Their density muffles sound. Walking through the Black Pine Wilds is walking through an absence.

**Black-tongue** — [Slang] 1. A disease: ulcers on the gums and tongue from vitamin deficiency. Common in late Long Dark when foraging yields nothing green. 2. A gossip or slanderer.

**Blade-edge** — [Historical] The sharpened cutting face of a weapon. Maintaining a blade-edge in the field is daily work. "He has lost his blade-edge" describes a man past his prime.

**Blade-light** — [Slang] The faint gleam of a well-maintained weapon in firelight. A fighting man's blade should show blade- light -- it means the steel is clean, oiled, and sharp. "No blade-light" is an insult: the man does not care for his tools.

**Blót** — [Historical] A sacrifice. Animal blood poured on an altar stone or the roots of a sacred tree. In the Rimevegr, blóts continue even though the gods do not answer. The ritual is the point. The silence afterward is everyone's private problem.

**Blood-eagle** — [Historical] A ritual execution of disputed historicity. In the Rimevegr, referenced as a threat but never performed on-page. The name alone carries enough weight. "He deserves the blood-eagle" is the worst thing one man can say about another.

**Blood-galdr** — [Coined] Galdr performed using another person's blood as material component. Universally forbidden. Automatic outlawry if discovered. The blood must be unwilling -- a galdr-worker who uses his own blood is merely paying the standard cost. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.3 (blood-galdr exception).

**Blood-mark** — [Coined] The stain left on the ground where a man died. In frozen ground, blood-marks persist for weeks -- dark patches in the frost. The band walks past blood-marks from previous fights. Sometimes their own.

**Blood-oath** — [Historical] An oath sealed by exchanging blood on iron. Binds individuals, not institutions. Stronger than a verbal oath, weaker than an Odynn-sworn contract. Breaking a blood-oath does not end a career, but it ends a reputation. Men who break blood-oaths find fewer men willing to bleed with them.

**Blood-price** — [Historical] See _weregild_. The silver value of a human life. "What is the blood-price for a farmer?" is a question that gets asked at contract negotiations.

**Blood Sun** — [Coined] An extremely rare weather event. The sun appears blood-red and radiates no warmth. Animals freeze in place. Wise folk stay indoors. No one has a satisfactory explanation.

**Bloom** — [Historical] A mass of spongy iron produced in a bloomery furnace. Must be hammered repeatedly to drive out slag before it becomes usable iron. The starting point for all Rimevegr iron-work.

**Bloomery** — [Historical] A small furnace for smelting iron ore. Clay-walled, charcoal-fired, bellows-driven. Produces a bloom of crude iron. A working bloomery is a settlement's most strategically important installation after the grain store.

**Blowpipe** — [Historical] A hollow tube for directing breath onto an ember or coal. Precise and effective. Faster than bellows for starting fires. Part of the fire-pouch kit for some men.

**Boar's-bristle** — [Historical] Stiff pig hair. Used for brush-making, thread-threading (the stiff point acts as a needle), and wound probes.

**Board-game** — [Historical] Settlement entertainment. Mainly hnefatafl and variants. Carved from whatever wood is on hand. The pieces are sometimes bone, sometimes pebbles.

**Bodkin** — [Historical] A thick, blunt needle for threading cord through eyelets. Field repair tool. Also: a type of narrow arrowhead designed to punch through mail.

**Bog-cotton** — [Historical] Eriophorum. White-tufted marsh grass. The tufts are collected for lamp-wicks, wound padding, and emergency tinder. One of the few useful plants found in moorland bogs.

**Bog-iron** — [Historical] Iron extracted from peat bogs. Low quality but available where ore is not. Smelted in a clay furnace. The resulting metal is spongy and must be heavily worked. Bog-iron weapons are better than no weapons.

**Bone-dice** — [Historical] Gambling dice carved from knuckle- bones (astragali). Four faces: broad flat, narrow flat, convex, concave. The primary camp game. The game matters less than the social dynamics around it.

**Bone-meal** — [Historical] Ground bones added to stew for calcium and thickening. Extracted from marrow-cracked long bones after the marrow has been eaten. Nothing is wasted.

**Bone-needle** — [Historical] A sewing needle carved from animal bone. Less effective than iron, but iron needles are expensive and bone is free. Most clothing repairs in the field are done with bone-needles.

**Bone-Walker** — [Coined] Epithet for Skathi, goddess of winter and the hunt. Also applied to anyone who walks the High Rime-Moors alone, because only the mad or the divine would.

**Bondi** — [Historical] A free farmer. The backbone of settlement economy. A bondi owns land, pays no lord's rent (there are no lords, only jarls with swords), and votes at the thing. The bondi's opinion matters because the bondi grows the grain.

**Brand** — [Historical] A sword. More literary than common speech -- skalds use "brand" in stories. Working men say "sword" or "blade." When a character says "brand," he is quoting a saga or trying to sound important.

**Brazier** — [Historical] A metal container for holding burning coals. Portable heat. Rare and expensive in the Rimevegr. Jarls have them. The band does not.

**Breach** — [Historical] 1. An opening in a wall or palisade. 2. A violation of oath. Both meanings are equally dangerous. "He made a breach" requires context to know which.

**Break-ice** — [Coined] The man sent first onto frozen water. Lightest in the band. He walks, stops, stomps. If the ice holds, the column follows. Break-ice duty is assigned by weight, not by cowardice.

**Breast-plate** — [Historical] A shaped piece of iron covering the chest. Rarer than brynja in the Rimevegr. A breast-plate protects less but weighs less. Infantry prefer brynja; scouts prefer leather.

**Brine** — [Historical] Salt water. Used for preserving meat and fish. A brine barrel is part of the band's essential supply train. Brine also cleans wounds (painfully).

**Brine-barrel** — [Coined] A barrel of salt water used for preserving fish or meat on the march. Heavy, awkward, vital. Gest's accounting treats the brine-barrel as a strategic asset: its weight is a cost; its contents are survival.

**Brynja** — [Historical] A mail shirt. Chainmail. The Norse term. Weight: 20-30 pounds. Sound: constant jingling. A man wearing a brynja cannot sneak, but he can survive a sword stroke that would open an unarmored man from shoulder to hip. In the Rimevegr, a brynja is wealth you wear.

**Brúðarboð** — [Coined] The bride-offer. A song performed by the groom's party at the threshold of the bride's hall, listing his lineage, property, and deeds. A legal document set to melody. The door stays closed until the song is finished.

**Brúðargrátr** — [Coined] The bride-grief. The women's mourning song for the bride who is leaving her family. Minor key, descending melody. Acknowledges the loss the wedding represents. The bride herself is silent.

**Brúðarlög** — [Coined] Wedding-songs. The full body of marriage verse: threshold songs, bride-grief, ribald feast verses, and wedding-night songs that turn household transfer into communal memory.

**Burn rate** — [Coined] The daily cost of keeping the band alive. Approximately one silver per day for food, replacement gear, and minor operating costs. Gest tracks this daily. The burn rate is the band's heartbeat. When it exceeds income, the band is dying.

**Byre** — [Historical] An animal shelter or barn. In settlements, the byre adjoins the longhouse. The animals' body heat warms the building through the shared wall. Being quartered in the byre means the settlement tolerates the band but does not trust it.

**Byrnie** — [Historical] A short mail shirt. Lighter than a full brynja. Covers the torso, not the thighs. A compromise between protection and mobility.

## C

**Cairn** — [Historical] A pile of stones marking a trail, a grave, or a boundary. In the Rimevegr, older cairns were built by people no one remembers, for purposes no one can explain. Newer cairns mark paths through the Outvegr.

**Calf-skin** — [Historical] The softest available leather. Used for writing surfaces (vellum), glove linings, and fine bags. Expensive because every calf-skin means one less calf grown to a cow.

**Call-name** — [Coined] An earned name that replaces the birth name in daily use. Never self-chosen. Given by the band when a man has done something memorable or displays an inescapable trait. "Cold-Eye" (Voss), "Hook" (Kell), "Ledger" (Gest). Receiving a call-name is informal promotion -- the band has noticed you.

**Camp-follower** — [Historical] Anyone who travels with a military force without being a fighter. Cooks, healers, servants, partners. In the Rimevegr, Dalla is technically a camp-follower. She would disagree with the term.

**Candle-mark** — [Historical] A unit of time. One candle-mark is approximately one hour -- measured by how far a candle burns between scored lines. A settlement technique. On the march, time is sun position and gut feeling.

**Cauldron** — [Historical] A large iron or bronze cooking pot. The communal cauldron is the band's second most important possession after the strongbox. Dalla's instruments. It hangs from a tripod over the fire.

**Cave-camp** — [Coined] A natural cave used for shelter. Better than open ground but dangerous -- caves collect smoke, harbor animals, and sometimes connect to barrows. Enter with torch and weapon.

**Caulk** — [Historical] Material used to seal gaps -- in ships, in buildings, in barrels. Pine pitch, moss, animal hair mixed with tar. The smell of caulk is the smell of construction.

**Cape-weather** — [Slang] Wind and rain bad enough to pull the cloak over the head and hunch forward. Not shelter-worthy, just miserable. "It is cape-weather" means keep marching, but it will cost morale.

**Captain's share** — [Coined] The portion of loot or payment that goes to the band's captain. In a standard svarthird: 40%. In a tyrant hird: 60%. The percentage is set when the band forms. Changing it mid-contract is a band-break trigger.

**Char-cloth** — [Historical] Linen scorched in a sealed tin until carbon-black. The best fire-starting tinder. Catches a spark instantly. Carried in the fire-pouch. Making char-cloth is a camp task -- everyone contributes old cloth scraps.

**Charcoal** — [Historical] Wood burned in a sealed kiln until only carbon remains. Burns hotter than wood. Essential for smithing. Charcoal kilns are strategic assets -- a settlement without charcoal cannot forge.

**Charcoal-ledger** — [Coined] The band's financial record. Kept by the sergeant (Gest) in charcoal notation on scraped hide or rough parchment. Numbers, names, dates, debts. The ledger is the band's institutional memory. It outlasts men.

**Chest-beat** — [Historical] The physical invocation of Thurr. A closed fist struck against the sternum, hard enough to bruise. Done before a fight. Not prayer -- more like priming a weapon.

**Chin-rime** — [Coined] Frost that forms on beards and eyebrows in deep cold. Every exhaled breath freezes on facial hair. After an hour of marching in deep Long Dark, every man's face is white-crusted. The chin-rime is shaken loose with a hand-swipe that becomes unconscious habit.

**Cloak-weight** — [Slang] How heavy your cloak is from absorbed moisture. A dry wool cloak weighs five pounds. A soaked one weighs fifteen. "Full cloak-weight" means the weather has been bad for days and nothing has dried.

**Close-quarter** — [Coined] Fighting in confined space -- inside a longhouse, a barrow corridor, between trees. Shields are useless. Seax and short-axe prevail. The tall man has no advantage. The vicious man does.

**Coal-bed** — [Historical] The bed of glowing coals beneath a mature fire. The coal-bed, not the flame, provides heat. A good fire-keeper maintains a deep coal-bed and keeps the flames low. Flame is waste; coal is warmth.

**Cod-stack** — [Coined] Dried cod stacked on outdoor racks in the wind. The primary preserved food of coastal settlements. Hard as wood, keeps for months. Must be soaked before cooking. The smell of a cod-stack carries on the wind -- the first sign of a coastal village.

**Cold-camp** — [Slang] A camp without fire. Made when concealment outweighs warmth -- enemy nearby, or fuel unavailable. Cold camp in deep winter is survival risk. The men eat cold food, sleep in piles for body heat, and do not speak above a whisper.

**Cold-Eye** — [Coined] Voss's call-name. The clouded left eye from an old axe wound, and the temperament behind it.

**Cold-iron** — [Historical] Iron without carbon content -- wrought iron, not steel. Softer, bends rather than breaks. Used for nails, hinges, chains. In superstition, cold-iron repels the hidden folk. Whether this is true in the Rimevegr is deliberately ambiguous.

**Column** — [Coined] The marching order of the band. The column has a head (lead scout), a body (main group with mule), and a tail (rear guard). Column discipline means maintaining spacing and silence. Column breaks happen at fords, rough terrain, and ambush sites.

**Comb** — [Historical] Bone or antler, double-sided. Surprisingly common personal item. Not vanity -- lice management. A man who does not comb attracts vermin. Vermin spread disease. Disease kills bands.

**Commons** — [Coined] The lowest rank in a mercenary band. New or unproven fighters. They carry the most weight, do the worst jobs, eat last, and die first. Advancement from common to veteran requires surviving long enough to be worth trusting.

**Contract-stone** — [Coined] A flat stone used as a surface for signing contracts. The rope-and-eye mark (Odynn's sign) is scratched into the stone beside the terms. After the contract is fulfilled, the stone is scored through -- marking the debt paid. Some settlements have permanent contract-stones near the gate.

**Copper-count** — [Slang] A small amount. "That is worth a copper-count" means it is nearly worthless but not quite nothing. Also: the exact tally of copper coins in the band's treasury, which Gest knows to the piece.

**Cotter** — [Historical] A tenant farmer who works another man's land for a share of the harvest. Not a thrall -- free to leave, but leaving means starvation. The functional difference between a cotter and a thrall is that the cotter can choose to starve.

**Cow-hide** — [Historical] Untanned leather. Stiff, thick. Used for shield-facing, crude bags, and temporary repairs. Must be worked (softened, oiled, scraped) before fine use.

**Crack-Handed** — [Coined] Epithet for Thurr, god of thunder. His hands are split knuckles and broken fingers healed wrong.

**Cracking, the** — [Coined] The event -- or process -- that damaged the Veil between the living world and whatever lies beyond. Whether it was a single catastrophe or a slow degradation is disputed. What matters: the Veil is thinner now, the dead walk, and no one knows if it will get worse. See `08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §1.

**Cradle** — [Historical] 1. An infant bed. 2. The framework that holds a boat during construction or repair. 3. In mining: a simple rocking device for washing alluvial gold from river gravel. Meaning depends on context.

**Craw** — [Historical] The crop of a bird. By extension: the throat or gullet. "That sticks in my craw" means it is unacceptable. A man with something stuck in his craw will not let it go.

**Cresset** — [Historical] An iron basket on a pole, filled with burning pitch or fat, used as a beacon or warning signal. Settlement defenses light cressets along the palisade when attack is expected.

**Croft** — [Historical] A small farm. Smaller than a steading. One man's work. A croft produces enough for one family with nothing left over. The smallest economic unit that can sustain itself.

**Cross-grain** — [Historical] Wood cut perpendicular to the grain. Weak, splits easily. A cross-grain axe-haft will snap at the first hard swing. By extension: anything poorly planned. "That plan is cross-grain" means it will fail under stress.

**Cruck** — [Historical] A curved timber pair forming the main structural arch of a building. Crucks are set in the ground and joined at the top. Simple, strong, achievable with primitive tools.

**Cud** — [Historical] Rechewed food. Also: something chewed over mentally. "He is chewing his cud" means the man is thinking hard and not sharing his conclusions.

**Curfew** — [Historical] The practice of banking or covering the fire at night. In settlements with wooden buildings, an open fire at night is a risk to the entire village. The curfew bell signals fire-banking.

**Curry** — [Historical] To groom an animal with a curry-comb: a toothed metal scraper. Currying removes mud, loose hair, and parasites. A curried horse performs better than a neglected one.

## D

**Dagger** — [Historical] A short stabbing weapon distinct from the seax (which is a utility knife that fights). A dagger is a weapon that happens to cut rope sometimes.

**Dagmál** — [Coined] Day-speech. The public or daylight face of a rune: the meaning used in lawful inscription, healing, oath, and ordinary warding.

**Djúpsýn** — [Coined] Deep-sight. The second and more controlled level of völva seeing, where patterns no longer flash -- they hold.

**Dýrríða** — [Coined] The Riding. The second soulbane, in which something from beyond seems to sit inside a living body and use it as borrowed meat.

**Dagfinn** — [Coined] A common man in the band who signed his contract with an X. This is not unusual. Most commons are illiterate.

**Dall** — [Historical] A shallow dish or bowl, usually carved from wood or shaped from clay. The standard eating vessel. Men eat from dalls around the fire. A man with his own dall has a small claim to civilization.

**Danegeld** — [Historical] A tribute paid to avoid violence. In the Rimevegr, the word is not used (no Danes) but the practice is identical. Settlements pay svarthird bands to leave, and both sides pretend it is a contract for services.

**Dark-count** — [Coined] The number of Long Darks a man has survived on the march. "He has six dark-counts" means six winters in the field. More than ten and the man is either very capable or very lucky. Very few reach fifteen.

**Deadfall** — [Historical] 1. Fallen timber. The primary fuel source on the march. Standing dead wood is preferred (drier than ground-contact wood). 2. A trap: a heavy log propped on a trigger stick, designed to crush whatever disturbs it.

**Dead-man's boots** — [Slang] Equipment inherited from a killed comrade. "He is wearing dead-man's boots" is literal. Boot inheritance is one of the band's recurring material realities. Haldr's boots on Lump.

**Dead-name** — [Coined] The name of a man who has died. Not spoken for a time after death -- then someone tests it at the fire. Speaking a dead-name too soon is felt as wrong but not articulated as a rule.

**Dead-reckoning** — [Historical] Navigation by estimated distance and direction from a known point. The primary method on the march when landmarks are hidden by rime-fog. Relies on pace-count and compass sense. Accumulates error with every step.

**Death-seiðr** — [Coined] Seiðr that compels the dead rather than asking them. Spirit-lore rank 4+ effects -- binding spirits, commanding lesser dead. Classified as forbidden by all jurisdictions in the Rimevegr. The mechanical line: rank 1-3 is counsel, rank 4+ is coercion. See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.8.4.

**Deepholm** — [Coined] Largest settlement in the region (population 520). Jarl Sigrun's seat. Stone fortress, iron gate, masterwork smithy, barrow archive, galdr workshop.

**Degradation** — [Coined] The cumulative physical decline of magical practitioners from years of practice. Galdr-workers lose fine motor control. Seiðr-speakers take longer to recover from trance. Wyrd-readers lose signal clarity. Tracked mechanically in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §11.9. Every practitioner knows it is coming. None of them stop.

**Distaff** — [Historical] A wooden rod that holds raw fiber for spinning. The distaff is women's technology, but in a mercenary band anyone who can spin thread spins thread. Thread is the medium of repair.

**Ditch** — [Historical] A defensive excavation. Even a shallow ditch (knee-deep) slows a charge and breaks formation. The simplest fortification. A camp with a ditch and a hurd-fence sleeps better.

**Dog-watch** — [Slang] The worst watch shift -- late night, before dawn. Cold, dark, lonely. The man on dog-watch fights sleep. "He drew dog-watch" means bad luck or punishment.

**Door, the** — [Coined] Kell's earliest call-name, earned for holding a barrow entrance single-handed.

**Draugr** — [Historical] The restless dead. In the Rimevegr, not mindless -- they remember old grudges. Physical. Can be fought, blocked, burned. Their nature is never confirmed as supernatural. They might be the abandoned dead. They might be Hael's subjects who refused to sit down.

**Draugr-stink** — [Slang] The smell of the restless dead. Not rot exactly -- something drier, sweeter, wrong. Men who have fought draugr can smell it on the wind. It follows you home in your clothes.

**Draw-knife** — [Historical] A two-handled blade pulled toward the user to shave wood. Used for making arrow shafts, peeling bark, shaping hafts. A skilled man with a draw-knife and suitable wood can produce serviceable tool handles in an afternoon.

**Dread-mark** — [Coined] A carved or painted rune left as a warning. Not magical in itself -- but superstitious travellers avoid marked paths. Galdr-workers sometimes leave dread-marks to claim territory or deter trespassers. The marks work because people believe they work. Costs nothing and requires no skill.

**Drift-ice** — [Coined] Ice that has broken free from shore and moves with current or wind. Navigating drift-ice on foot is lethal -- the ice shifts underfoot with no warning. Crossing a frozen fjord during thaw means reading drift-ice patterns.

**Drit** — [Historical] Excrement. The blunt Norse word. Used as a general-purpose profanity: "Drit!" is the equivalent of a short sharp obscenity. Also: "He is drit-lucky" means the man's survival defies explanation.

**Drop-edge** — [Coined] The rim of a cliff, ravine, or steep slope. The kill-zone in ambush terrain. A man pushed to the drop-edge has no retreat.

**Dross** — [Historical] Waste material from metal-working. Slag that separates during smelting. In slang: anything worthless. "That offer is dross" means the deal is not worth the breath it took to speak it.

**Dry-camp** — [Slang] A camp without water nearby. An acceptable one-night risk in cool weather. In Long Summer heat, a dry-camp becomes dangerous by midday. Water-skins must be full before making dry-camp.

**Dub** — [Historical] A pool of standing water. Dark, still, often peat-stained. The Outvegr is full of dubs. They look shallow; some are not.

**Dust-iron** — [Coined] Iron filings. By-product of grinding and filing. Saved by smiths for use in charcoal-ledger ink (mixed with vinegar to make a dark permanent stain).

**Dwale** — [Historical] A sedative drink made from henbane, lettuce, bryony, and opium poppy (when available). Used by healers to dull pain during wound treatment. Too much kills. The dosage is guesswork.

**Dyke** — [Historical] A drainage ditch or earthen wall. Settlement technology for water management. A well-dyked field survives spring melt without flooding.

## E

**Eagle** — [Historical] A large raptor. In Norse culture, eagles circle battlefields alongside ravens. An eagle over a camp means open ground and high thermals. An eagle over a battle means carrion.

**Ear-mark** — [Historical] A notch cut into a livestock animal's ear to mark ownership. Extended to mean any identifying sign. "He has the ear-mark of a deserter" means the signs are visible.

**Ear-rime** — [Coined] Ice that forms inside the ear canal during extreme cold. Painful and disorienting. A cloth cap worn under a helm prevents it.

**Eld** — [Historical] Fire. The Norse root. "Eld-light" is firelight. "Eld-keeper" is whoever tends the flames. The word carries more weight than the English "fire" -- eld is survival, community, light against the dark, the center of everything that is not wilderness.

**Eld-keeper** — [Coined] The designated fire-tender, especially on watch. Letting the eld die in deep cold is a punishable offense. The eld-keeper feeds the fire, banks it for the night, and resurrects it at dawn.

**Elkhide** — [Historical] Tanned elk skin. Thicker than deer- hide. Used for heavy-duty leather goods: belts, scabbards, pack-straps. An elkhide shield-cover absorbs sword blows better than cow leather.

**Elm-bow** — [Historical] A bow made of elm wood. Less powerful than yew, but elm is available in the Rimevegr where yew is not. Effective for hunting at moderate range. Not a battlefield weapon -- in the close terrain of the Outvegr, a thrown axe reaches farther than an arrow flies.

**Ember-pot** — [Coined] A clay or iron vessel for transporting live coals during a march. Packed with ash and carried carefully. A good ember-pot means not striking fire from cold at every camp.

**Entrails** — [Historical] The viscera of a slaughtered animal or a dead man. Wyrd-readers study entrails for omens. Practical men study them for parasites. Both find what they are looking for.

**Escort-fee** — [Coined] Payment for accompanying a merchant or traveler through dangerous territory. The band's most common contract type. Escort-fees are calculated per day, per man, per risk level. "Double escort" means the route is actively hostile.

**Ewe** — [Historical] A female sheep. Ewes produce wool, milk, and lambs. A settlement's ewe-count is an economic indicator -- more ewes, more wool, more trade.

**Ergi** — [Historical] A term of deep insult implying unmanliness or sexual passivity. In historical Norse culture, the worst accusation. In the Rimevegr, the taboo survives in weakened form, applied primarily to male seidr-practitioners. Thorne avoids the word by calling his rune-work "reading" -- a deliberate linguistic dodge.

**Eye-debt** — [Coined] What is owed when you have seen something you should not have -- a theft, a secret meeting, a moment of weakness in a Named Man. Eye-debts are never spoken. They are banked. They surface when leverage is needed.

**Eye-guard** — [Coined] A strip of leather or bark with thin slits cut for vision. An improvised defense against frost- blindness. Ugly, effective, essential on bright snow.

**Eyelet** — [Historical] A small hole reinforced with stitching or a metal ring, used for threading laces through leather or cloth. Boot eyelets, tent-flap eyelets, armor lacing eyelets. The work is tedious. The result is critical.

**Eyre** — [Historical] A circuit court. A traveling judge who moves between settlements dispensing justice. In the Rimevegr, no formal eyre exists -- the closest equivalent is a well-respected jarl who arbitrates disputes between settlements.

## F

**Fallow** — [Historical] Agricultural land left unplanted for a season to restore fertility. In the Rimevegr's short Long Summer, fallowing means losing half a settlement's food production. The decision to fallow is agonizing.

**Farrier** — [Historical] A specialist in horse and mule hoof care. The band does not have one. Someone learned the minimum. A farrier's rasp and hoof-knife are part of the mule's gear.

**Fat-lamp** — See _tallow-lamp_.

**Feather-bed** — [Historical] A luxury mattress stuffed with goose or duck down. Exists in jarls' halls. The band has never slept in one. The word is used sarcastically: "This rock is a proper feather-bed" means the ground is terrible.

**Fehu** — [Historical] The rune ᚠ, meaning cattle/wealth. Carved on strongboxes and purse-clasps as a ward against theft. Whether the ward works is another of those questions the novel does not answer.

**Fen** — [Historical] Low-lying marshy ground. Softer than a bog, wetter than a moor. Walking through fen country exhausts the legs and damages boots. The mule sinks to the fetlock. Progress halves.

**Fen-fire** — [Historical] A ghostly light seen over marshes at night. Methane ignition from decaying matter. Or the dead. In the Rimevegr, the scientific explanation and the supernatural one coexist, and neither is confirmed.

**Fenrir's teeth** — [Slang] A line of jagged rocks, ice formations, or any natural barrier that looks like it was designed to kill. "We have Fenrir's teeth ahead" means bad terrain.

**Fire-circle** — [Coined] The nightly campfire arrangement around which the band eats, talks, gambles, and establishes hierarchy. Seating is social positioning. Changes in who sits where communicate power shifts without words.

**Fire-pouch** — [Historical] A waterproof leather bag containing fire-starting materials: fire-steel, flint, char- cloth, amadou, birch bark. Sealed with beeswax or fat. Losing the fire-pouch in bad weather is a survival emergency.

**Fire-ring** — [Coined] A circle of stones containing the campfire. Defines the cooking area, the social center, the warmth boundary. Beyond the fire-ring is darkness.

**Fire-steel** — [Historical] A C-shaped striker of high-carbon steel. Struck against flint, it sheds tiny curls of hot metal that ignite tinder. The steel sparks, not the flint -- this is the physical fact that separates men who can light fires from men who cannot.

**Fjord** — [Historical] A deep, narrow coastal inlet carved by glacial action. The primary geography of the Inner Fjords region. Where most people live. Fog clings to fjord water even at midday.

**Fjord-born** — [Coined] Raised in the Inner Fjords. Implies familiarity with boats, fish, salt wind, and the particular claustrophobia of living between vertical rock walls and cold water.

**Fjord-side** — [Coined] The strip of usable land between the water and the cliff. Where fjord-born people build, plant, and die. Never wide enough.

**Fjörbaugsgarðr** — [Legal] Lesser outlawry. Three-year exile from one jurisdiction with loss of property. The outlaw may be killed within the exile territory but is protected elsewhere. Weregild payment can end the exile early. See `data/culture/outlawry_system.yaml`.

**Flake** — [Historical] A wooden drying rack for fish. Cod split and salted is draped over flakes in the wind. The smell announces a fishing settlement before the rooftops appear.

**Flat-bread** — [Historical] Unleavened bread cooked on a hot stone. Dense, gritty, filling. Goes stale in two days and hard as wood in four. Hard flat-bread is soaked in stew or ale. Teeth have been broken on four-day flat-bread.

**Fley** — [Historical] To frighten, to scare away. "The barrow fleyed them" means the men were scared off. Not a mark of cowardice in the Rimevegr -- some things should fley you.

**Flint-knapping** — [Historical] The art of shaping flint by striking off flakes. Ancient technology that survives because flint is freely available and iron is not. Arrowheads, scrapers, and fire-making flints are all knapped.

**Flint** — [Historical] The striking surface for fire-making. The sharp edge against which the fire-steel is scraped. Flint does not spark -- the steel does. This distinction matters.

**Fold** — [Historical] An enclosure for livestock. A sheep- fold, a goat-fold. By extension: any enclosed space. "Back to the fold" means return to safety, but in mercenary usage it carries irony -- the fold is wherever the band is camped, which is rarely safe.

**Folk magic** — [Historical/Coined] The household layer of protective practice -- herb-bundles above doorways, salt lines on thresholds, carved threshold runes. Does not produce mechanical magical effects but affects morale: men who see familiar rituals feel safer. See `08_MAGIC_OF_RIMEVEGR.md` §3 and `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §6 (folk magic morale modifiers).

**Forager** — [Coined] A band member assigned to find food. Foraging is survival, not supplement. Terrain determines what is available. A forager returning empty-handed means the band goes hungry.

**Fordable** — [Historical] A river crossing shallow enough to walk. Whether a river is fordable changes with season, weather, and recent rain. A ford that was knee-deep last week may be neck-deep after three days of thaw.

**Forecastle** — [Historical] The forward platform of a ship. By extension: the leading position of any group. "He stands at the forecastle" means he takes point.

**Forge** — [Historical] A controlled fire hot enough to work metal. The forge is the heart of a smithy. A portable forge is an anvil and a bellows on the mule's back -- crude but functional for emergency repairs.

**Fork-beard** — [Slang] A man who splits his beard into two points. A common descriptive call-name for men who lack a more interesting one.

**Foster** — [Historical] To raise another's child. Fostering is a political act -- it creates bonds between families. A foster-son owes loyalty to both birth and foster parents. The resulting network of obligation is the closest thing the Rimevegr has to diplomacy.

**Fraeja** — [Coined] The Grief-Bride. Goddess of the grieving, barren fields, and women who outlive everyone. She taught women seidr -- her cruelest gift. Her tears became the Veil-fog. Invoked mostly by women, mostly in silence.

**Freedman** — [Historical] A former thrall who has earned or purchased freedom. A freedman can become a full member of the band, but the thrall-mark (see _thrall-mark_) on the story of his past follows him.

**Frost-blind** — [Coined] Temporary blindness caused by light reflecting off ice and snow. The Rimevegr's equivalent of snow blindness. Treated by sitting in dark shelter for a day. On the march, a frost-blind man is led by the arm and contributes nothing.

**Frost-heave** — [Historical] Ground that has risen from ice expansion beneath the surface. Creates uneven terrain that breaks ankles and wheels. Frozen roads in the Rimevegr are not flat -- they are frost-heaved ridges and hollows.

**Frostfjord Hollow** — [Coined] A village of 87 people. Jarl Hrothgar's seat. Dried-cod economy. The band's first named settlement interaction.

**Frost-nail** — See _ice-nail_.

**Frost-sore** — [Coined] A weeping skin wound caused by repeated freezing and thawing. Common on hands, ears, and feet. Painful, slow to heal, and prone to infection.

**Fuel-budget** — [Coined] How much wood or peat the band needs per night versus what is available. Gest tracks fuel-budget as ruthlessly as food. A forest camp has unlimited fuel. A moorland camp may have none.

**Fulling** — [Historical] The process of cleaning and thickening woven wool by pounding it in water mixed with urine or clay. Fulled wool is denser, more water-resistant, and warmer. The smell during fulling is terrible. The result is worth it.

**Fury-drunk** — [Slang] Berserker state. Also applied loosely to any man who has lost control in a fight. "He went fury- drunk" means the man stopped thinking and started destroying. Usually the man does not remember clearly afterward.

**Furrow** — [Historical] A long, narrow trench cut by a plough. The basic unit of agricultural work. In the Rimevegr's thin topsoil and short growing season, furrows are shallow and closely spaced.

**Futhark** — [Historical] The runic alphabet. Named for its first six characters: F, U, Th, A, R, K. The writing system of Norse culture. In the Rimevegr, futhark literacy is rare. Most men cannot read. Rune-carvers guard the knowledge.

## G

**Gait** — [Coined] The pace the band sets on the march. Three gaits: road-pace (standard), hard-march (forced), and crawl (bad terrain or injury). The gait determines daily distance and daily exhaustion.

**Galdr** — [Historical] Rune-scribing magic. The art of carving runes and speaking words of power. Practical effects: wards, blade-strengthening, trail-marking. Always costs blood and willpower. Miscarved runes twist their intent. The most accepted magical tradition -- even jarls keep a galdr-worker. But even they are watched.

**Galdr-worker** — [Historical] A practitioner of galdr. Gender- neutral in the Rimevegr (Ash is female). Negotiates services like a mercenary: terms, conditions, price. Keeps separate fire. Sets own terms.

**Galdramenn** — [Historical] Plural of galdr-worker. Those who practice runic inscription. Feared and needed in equal measure.

**Gjafaþrál** — [Coined] Gift-thralldom. The binding created by repeated gifts, shelter, or favor until refusal becomes socially impossible. A soft chain no less real for being soft.

**Gambeson** — [Historical] A padded cloth jacket worn as armor or under mail. Linen or wool stuffed with scraps and quilted. Surprisingly effective -- a thick gambeson stops a glancing cut. The most common body armor among commons. Cheap, bulky, warm.

**Garth** — [Historical] An enclosed yard or farmstead. "Behind the garth-fence" means within the domestic compound.

**Gate-ask** — [Coined] The conversation that happens before a mercenary band enters a settlement. Who are you. What do you want. How long. At whose cost. The gate-ask determines whether weapons are set down or drawn.

**Gate-Blind** — [Coined] Epithet for Heimr, god of thresholds. He sees everything and recognizes nothing.

**Gilt** — [Historical] Gold-coated. By extension: anything that looks better than it is. "Gilt promises" means the contract sounds generous but the terms will not hold.

**Gimbal** — [Historical] A pivot-mounted holder for a lamp or candle on a ship. The light stays level while the ship moves. Uncommon inland but known in coastal settlements.

**Girdle** — [Historical] A belt. Not ornamental -- functional. The girdle holds the seax, the purse, the fire-pouch, and any small tools. A broad girdle distributes the weight of a brynja at the hips instead of the shoulders.

**Glaive** — [Historical] A pole weapon with a single-edged blade at the head. Uncommon in the Rimevegr -- the terrain is too close for long weapons. Sometimes seen at settlement gates because they look intimidating.

**Gnaw-bone** — [Slang] A bone given to chew for the last traces of marrow and gristle. "He is on the gnaw-bone" means supplies are critical.

**Goat-path** — [Coined] A trail too narrow and steep for anything but goats and desperate men. The Outvegr has goat- paths that connect valleys no road reaches.

**Going dark** — [Slang] Breaking camp and leaving before dawn without fire or noise. Escape protocol. "We go dark at second watch" means the band is in trouble and needs to disappear.

**Goose-grease** — [Historical] Rendered goose fat. Used for waterproofing leather, treating chapped skin, lubricating metal, and cooking. More available in settlements than on the march. A small pot of goose-grease is a luxury item.

**Grátlög** — [Coined] Weeping-songs. The mourning keening performed by women around the dead. A sustained rising wail that layers as more women join, producing a chord that shifts and never resolves. The children's grátlög uses the cradle-song melody sung backward. Men have the _beinasöngr_ (bone-song) at the barrow.

**Greyling** — [Slang] A man whose hair is going grey. Not a term of respect or mockery -- just identification. "The greyling with the limp" is how commons reference unnamed veterans. In a band, grey hair means survival, which means competence.

**Grief-gift** — [Coined] An object left with the dead. Personal items placed on the bale-fire or in the burial. The grief-gift is what the living can spare, which is usually nothing of value.

**Grief-Bride** — [Coined] Epithet for Fraeja. She outlived every love. Her dowry was grief.

**Grievance** — [Coined] A formal resentment tracked in the charcoal ledger. Late pay, dead Named Men, broken promises -- all recorded as grievances. When grievances accumulate past a threshold, the band fractures.

**Grim-gods** — See _Aesrgrim_.

**Grimholt** — [Coined] A large village on the High Rime-Moors. Population 118. Warchief Ordovast's stronghold. Iron mining. Strong defenses.

**Grip-leather** — [Historical] Leather wrapped around a sword hilt or axe haft for purchase. Applied wet (rawhide shrinks as it dries, tightening the grip). Roughened with a knife-point or rubbed with pine resin when it wears smooth.

**Grist** — [Historical] Grain destined for the quern. By extension: raw material for any process. "That is grist for the ledger" means the information goes into Gest's records.

**Ground-cloth** — [Coined] A piece of oiled leather or hide laid beneath the sleeping roll to keep moisture from wicking upward. Without a ground-cloth, a man wakes wet. Wet in the Long Dark means cold. Cold means danger.

**Ground-frost** — [Historical] Ice that forms in the top layer of soil. Ground-frost makes digging impossible. Latrine trenches must be dug before the ground freezes. Missing that window has consequences no one wants to describe.

**Gruel** — [Historical] Thin porridge. The least amount of grain that can be suspended in the most water. What the band eats when supplies are critically low. "We are on gruel" means three days from starvation.

**Gut-rot** — [Slang] Dysentery or severe food poisoning. The single most dangerous non-combat condition a band faces. Weakens men, slows the march, spreads through the camp. "Two men have gut-rot" means four will have it tomorrow.

**Guðníðing** — [Coined] God-scorned. One who has deliberately placed himself outside sacred order through defilement, blasphemous practice, or appetites that even outlaws fear.

**Guðslag** — [Coined] God-stroke. A catastrophic rupture in the body -- seizure, burst vessel, or collapse -- brought on by overreaching in galdr or forcing too much power through voice.

**Gutter-fire** — [Slang] A fire that has been badly made and burns unevenly. Spits, smokes, gives no heat. Usually made by a man who does not know to split his wet wood for dry interior.

**Gyrfalcon** — [Historical] A large arctic hunting bird. The most valued bird for falconry. In the Rimevegr, wild gyrfalcons are admired at distance and eaten when caught.

**Gyre** — [Historical] A circular or spiral motion. Water gyres in a whirlpool. Wind gyres in mountain passes. A gyre of ravens above a battlefield is the first sign visible at distance.

**Gust-wall** — [Coined] A sudden blast of wind in mountain passes that hits like a physical barrier. Gust-walls knock men off their feet and scatter loose gear. The band learns to rope together in exposed terrain after losing a supply pack to a gust-wall.

## H

**Hack-silver** — [Historical] Silver cut into weighed pieces for transactions. A arm-ring hacked into thirds is three payments. The pieces are weighed on a balance scale, not counted. Size is irrelevant; weight is everything.

**Hael** — [Coined] The Half-Rotted. Goddess of the dead, the barrows, the space between dying and being forgotten. Not evil -- indifferent. Draugr are Hael's subjects who refused to sit down.

**Hael's chair** — [Slang] Death. "He took Hael's chair" means he died.

**Hael's half** — [Slang] The share of supplies reserved for wastage, spoilage, and loss. Gest builds Hael's half into every calculation. If ten days of food remain, Gest counts eight.

**Haugvættr** — [Coined/Historical] Barrow-wight. A mound-bound presence made from dead flesh, grave-goods, old oath, and place- memory compacted together until the burial itself seems to wake.

**Holdboð** — [Coined] Flesh-command. A galdr effect where the body obeys before the mind has agreed: knees buckle, hands open, voice stops, or the gut turns.

**Haft** — [Historical] The handle of any striking tool -- axe, hammer, mattock. Always wood. The haft transmits the shock of impact to the hands. A bad haft breaks at the worst moment.

**Hair-rope** — [Coined] Rope twisted from horsehair or cattle- tail hair. Rough, strong, resistant to moisture. Used for tying loads and repairing tack. Not comfortable against skin.

**Half-Rotted** — [Coined] Epithet for Hael. Her left side is living flesh; her right side is green-grey bone.

**Hall** — [Historical] A longhouse large enough for communal gathering, feasting, and governance. A jarl's hall is his seat of power. Being quartered in the hall means honored guest. Being quartered outside the hall means tolerated.

**Hall-burned** — [Coined] Descriptor for someone who lost their home to fire or attack. A hall-burned man has nothing. Some become mercenaries. Some become thralls. Some become both.

**Halter** — [Historical] A rope headpiece for leading a horse or mule. The mule wears a halter. The man who holds the halter walks at mule-pace, which is the band's actual speed regardless of what the captain orders.

**Hammer-scale** — [Historical] Tiny flakes of iron oxide that fly from heated metal when struck. The black grit that covers a smithy floor. Crunches underfoot. The sound and smell of a working smithy.

**Hand-axe** — [Historical] A light single-handed axe. The primary weapon and tool of commons. Cuts wood, splits skulls, hammers tent pegs. The most versatile object a poor man owns.

**Hand-quern** — [Historical] A simple grain mill. Upper stone rotated on lower stone, grain fed through a center hole. The flour is coarse. The work is tedious. Grinding enough grain for twelve mouths takes an hour. This is why grain is usually traded as flour, not whole.

**Hard-bread** — [Historical] See _flat-bread_. Specifically the stale version -- four days old, dense as wood, carried as trail food. Requires soaking to be edible.

**Hard-march** — [Slang] An accelerated march without rest stops. Covers distance at the cost of exhaustion. After three days of hard-march, men check for desertion each morning.

**Hare-snare** — [Coined] A simple wire or cord loop set on a game trail. The primary protein trap in the Outvegr. Setting snares is evening work; checking them is dawn work. When the snares are empty three days running, the terrain is hunted out.

**Harness** — [Historical] The complete set of straps, buckles, and fittings for a pack animal. The mule's harness is checked every morning. Chafing causes sores; sores cause infection; infection loses the mule. Lose the mule, carry everything.

**Hauberk** — [Historical] A knee-length mail shirt. Rarer and heavier than the short haubergeon. In the Rimevegr, owning a full hauberk is wealth and status. Most men fight in leather and hope.

**Haubergeon** — [Historical] A short-sleeve, thigh-length mail shirt. The standard brynja. 20-30 pounds. Sound: constant jingling.

**Headman** — [Historical] The leader of a village. Not elected, not formally hereditary. The headman is whoever the village trusts to deal with armed strangers. Usually the oldest competent landowner.

**Hearth-stone** — [Historical] The flat stone at the center of a longhouse fire pit. The symbolic center of domestic life. A man who loses his hearth-stone has lost his home. Metaphorical use: "He has no hearth-stone" means a man belongs nowhere.

**Hearth-right** — [Coined] The claim to shelter and fire that a settled person has and a landless wanderer does not. A bondi has hearth-right. A svarthird does not. This is the fundamental social division.

**Hemp** — [Historical] The plant Cannabis sativa, grown for fiber rope and coarse cloth. Not cultivated for intoxicants in the Rimevegr -- labor is too scarce to grow plants that do nothing structural. Hemp rope is the standard cordage.

**Hen-house** — [Historical] A small wooden structure for chickens. One of the few sources of fresh protein (eggs) available year-round. Raiding a settlement's hen-house is war.

**Hel-road** — [Historical] The path to the realm of the dead. "Walking the Hel-road" means dying. In the Rimevegr, the Hel- road is metaphorical for most people -- but the barrows are literal, and what is inside them suggests the road is shorter than anyone likes.

**Helm** — [Historical] An iron helmet. In the Rimevegr, simple iron caps with nose-guards -- no horns, no wings, no decoration. Practical headgear. A sharp axe blow that hits a bare head splits the skull. The same blow on a helm dents the metal but the man lives.

**Hervísur** — [Coined] Battle-songs. The vocal progression that accompanies a shield-wall advance: chest-drone, rhythmic syllables, name-chant, roar. Synchronizes breathing, drowns fear, and terrifies the enemy. A band that has lost its hervísur has lost its cohesion.

**Herd-law** — [Coined] The unwritten rules of livestock management that every rural settlement follows. How many animals the pasture can support. Whose goat eats what. When culling happens. A herd without herd-law starves itself.

**Hide** — [Historical] 1. Animal skin, raw. 2. A unit of land measurement (approximately what one family can work). Both meanings are in common use. "He owns three hides" means land. "He is scraping a hide" means leather-work.

**Highseat** — [Historical] The jarl's chair in the hall. Flanked by carved posts. The physical manifestation of authority. When a jarl sits in the highseat, he is ruling. When the highseat is empty, the hall is just a room.

**Hilt** — [Historical] The handle assembly of a sword. Crossguard, grip, pommel. The hilt balances the blade. A good hilt is leather-wrapped iron. A poor hilt is wrapped in cord that will slip with blood.

**Hird** — [Historical] A household guard or retinue. The personal fighting force of a jarl or warchief. A hird eats well, drills regularly, and dies for its employer. The mercenary svarthird is a landless hird -- loyalty for silver instead of hearth-right.

**Hnefatafl** — [Historical] A Norse board game. One side defends a king piece (the hnefi) against an attacking force. Strategic, asymmetric. Played by Named Men in settlements. Too bulky and too delicate for the march.

**Hollow** — [Historical/Coined] A depression in terrain. Also: the suffix of place-names (Frostfjord Hollow). A hollow is sheltered from wind but collects cold air. Camp in a hollow for wind-break; pay with ground-chill.

**Hook** — [Coined] Kell's earlier call-name. Origin unstated in the text. A hook is a tool, not a weapon -- which says something about what Kell was before the band.

**Hornblast** — [Historical] A signal blown on an animal horn. One blast: attention. Two: alarm. Three: retreat. The sound carries half a mile in clear air. In rime-fog, much less. The horn is the band's only long-range communication tool.

**Horn-spoon** — [Historical] A spoon carved from cattle horn. Personal eating utensil. Most men carry one. Losing a horn- spoon means eating with fingers or whittling a replacement.

**Horse-price** — [Coined] An extravagant cost. "That is a horse-price" means the amount demanded is 20-30 silver -- the cost of a decent horse, which is to say, a luxury the band cannot afford.

**Hrime** — [Historical] Rime. Frost crystals. The white crust that covers every surface during the Long Dark. The Rimevegr is named for it. Hrime thickens on surfaces overnight and must be scraped from weapons, gear, and faces each morning.

**Huh** — [Slang] A universal grunt of acknowledgment. Not agreement. Not refusal. Just: message received. "Huh" is the most common sound in camp.

**Hull** — [Historical] The body of a ship. By extension: any large container. A settlement's grain hull is its storage building.

**Hurd-fence** — [Historical] A woven-stick fence. Quick to build, light, temporary. Used for livestock enclosures, camp perimeters, and wind-breaks. Two men can build twenty feet of hurd-fence in an hour.

**Hush, the** — [Coined] A supernatural phenomenon. In the deep woods, all sound dies completely for minutes at a time. Every bird stops singing at once. Something is listening. The Hush is more common during the Long Dark. Old woodsmen argue the name covers more than one event: a place-fixed hush, a wandering hush, and the hush that lingers after killing grounds go quiet. No one has an explanation that satisfies.

**Husk** — [Historical] The outer shell of a grain kernel. Removed during milling. Husk is fed to animals or, in desperation, mixed into flour to extend supply. Husk-bread is gritty and tastes of nothing.

**Huscarl** — [Historical] A professional warrior in a jarl's household guard. The closest historical equivalent to a permanent soldier. Huscarls drill, eat well, are paid regularly, and owe loyalty to a specific lord. A band composed of former huscarls is called "Old Huscarls" and holds itself to professional standards.

**Hut-circle** — [Historical] The stone foundation of a demolished or abandoned roundhouse. Common in the Outvegr. Visible as a ring of moss-covered stones. Useful for shelter -- the wind-break still works even without walls.

**Hymn** — [Historical] A sacred song. In the Rimevegr, hymns to the gods are rare -- the gods went silent. Old hymns survive as work-songs with the religious words changed to profanity.

## I

**Ice-axe** — [Coined] A short-hafted axe used for cutting steps in ice slopes and breaking trail through packed snow. Doubles as a weapon but was not designed for it.

**Ice-bound** — [Coined] Frozen in place. A settlement that is ice-bound cannot be reached by road until thaw. A river that is ice-bound is crossable on foot. The condition is both trap and opportunity.

**Ice-bridge** — [Coined] A frozen river or fjord crossing usable in deep Long Dark. Tested by the lightest man, alone. If he crosses, the column follows. Ice-bridges fail unpredictably during thaw season. Losing a man through thin ice is the fifth most common non-combat death.

**Ice-nail** — [Coined] Short iron spikes driven into boot soles for grip on ice. Improvised from any available iron -- nails, pins, broken fittings. Walking indoors with ice-nails destroys flooring, which is why boots come off at settlement thresholds.

**Ice-quake** — [Coined] A sudden crack and boom as a frozen lake or river adjusts under temperature change. Can be heard for miles. Startling. Sometimes the prelude to an ice-break that swallows anyone standing on the surface.

**Ice-Reach** — [Coined] The Northern Ice-Reach. The edge of inhabitable land. The Veil is thickest. Almost no one goes there. Some say the gods retreated there.

**Icicle-beard** — [Slang] See _chin-rime_. The more extreme version: actual icicles hanging from a man's beard after a day of hard marching in deep cold.

**Ingle** — [Historical] A fire or fireplace. More domestic than eld. "Sit by the ingle" means take warmth and be welcome. Settlement word. The band does not have an ingle -- they have a fire-circle on bare ground.

**Ingot** — [Historical] A bar of metal, usually iron or silver. Trade goods rather than currency. An iron ingot is raw material for a smith. A silver ingot is wealth in an untransacted form.

**Ink** — [Historical] A liquid for writing. Made from iron gall (crushed oak-galls mixed with iron vitriol and gum arabic). The Rimevegr has limited ink production -- most writing is carved (runes) or scratched (charcoal). Ink is a settlement luxury.

**Insole** — [Historical] A shaped piece of leather or wool placed inside a boot for insulation and comfort. Insoles wear out faster than the boot itself. Replacement insoles are a field necessity.

**Iron-tongue** — [Slang] A smith. Someone who works iron. "Find me the iron-tongue" means find the settlement smith. Iron-tongues are the most protected craftsmen in any settlement.

**Ironmoon** — [Coined] A named month in the Rimevegr calendar. Hard cold, deep Long Dark. The name describes what the month does to people, not what people feel about it.

**Iron-rot** — [Coined] Rust so severe the metal is structurally compromised. An iron-rot weapon looks functional but will snap under impact. Gest checks the band's equipment for iron-rot after every wet stretch.

**Iron-shod** — [Coined] Fitted with iron reinforcement. An iron-shod boot has a metal plate on the toe. An iron-shod door resists axes. The term indicates serious investment.

## J

**Jagged-ice** — [Coined] Broken, refrozen ice along river banks and lake shores. Unstable, sharp, impossible to walk on without ice-nails. The zone between solid ice and solid ground where falls happen.

**Jarl** — [Historical] The highest secular authority in the Rimevegr. Rules a town and the territory the town can project force over. A jarl's domain extends exactly as far as his warriors can march in a day and return. No feudal chain -- jarls deal with each other as independent powers.

**Jarl-seat** — [Coined] The settlement where a jarl holds court. Also: the physical chair (see _highseat_).

**Jaw-bone** — [Slang] A man who talks too much. "Shut your jaw-bone" is camp language for be quiet.

**Jerkin** — [Historical] A sleeveless leather or wool jacket. Worn over a tunic for extra warmth or minimal protection. A fur-lined jerkin is good camp clothing. A hardened leather jerkin is poor-man's armor.

**Jotun** — [Historical] A giant of old saga. In the Rimevegr, jotun have not been seen since the Cracking. Referenced in place-names (Jotun-pass, Jotun's Step) and idiom. Whether they ever existed is a question no one asks aloud.

**Jotun-cold** — [Slang] Cold so extreme it feels intentional. Named for the giants of old saga. "Jotun-cold tonight" means no one sleeps well and frostbite is a real risk.

**Juniper** — [Historical] An evergreen shrub whose berries are used for flavoring mead and preserving meat. Juniper smoke repels insects. Burning juniper branches inside a longhouse clears the air -- or at least masks the smell. A bundle of juniper tied to a pack keeps moths from eating wool.

**Jury-rig** — [Historical] A temporary repair made with whatever is available. A jury-rigged axe-haft is a branch lashed to a head with wet-hide strips. It will work for one fight. The term has no connection to courts -- it is a nautical word meaning "improvised mast."

## K

**Keel** — [Historical] The backbone timber of a ship. The strongest single piece of wood in a vessel. By extension: the most important structural element of anything. "Cut the keel" means destroy the foundation. "Sound in the keel" means structurally solid.

**Karl** — [Historical] A free man. The base social unit. A karl has rights: he can own land, speak at the thing, bear weapons. Below the karl is the thrall. Above him is the jarl. Most of the band's commons are karls with nothing left to lose.

**Keelson** — [Historical] The internal spine of a ship's hull. By extension: any structural backbone. "The keelson of the band" is Gest -- take him out and the structure collapses.

**Keep** — [Historical] A fortified structure. In the Rimevegr, stone keeps are rare -- most defenses are wooden palisades. A settlement with a stone keep is wealthy and afraid.

**Kenning** — [Historical] A compressed metaphor in skaldic poetry. "Whale-road" for sea. "Wound-dew" for blood. In the Rimevegr, kennings survive in everyday speech as fossils -- people use them without thinking of them as literary devices. "The wound-dew on his hands" is not poetry. It is how a man says "blood" without saying "blood."

**Kern** — [Historical] A light-armed foot soldier. Irish term but applicable. In the Rimevegr, the word is not used, but the concept maps to the band's commons -- poorly armored men with hand-axes and determination.

**Keel-haul** — [Historical] A naval punishment. Dragged under the ship's hull. Referenced in coastal settlements. Applied metaphorically: "He got keel-hauled" means punished severely for incompetence.

**Kettle-hat** — [Historical] A wide-brimmed iron helmet shaped like an upside-down cooking pot. Protects the head and shoulders from overhead blows. Practical, ugly, effective.

**Kyrrseta** — [Coined] Sitting-still. The core völva discipline of holding the body quiet long enough for deeper sight to rise without panic or self-loss.

**Kvikindi-barn** — [Coined/Insult] Beast-child. A fear-term for a child believed marked by animality, corruption, or a broken line of human inheritance.

**Kiln** — [Historical] A furnace for firing pottery, burning lime, or producing charcoal. Settlement infrastructure. Kiln- smoke is a navigation landmark -- you can smell a settlement's kiln before you see its palisade.

**Kin-price** — [Coined] The blood-money paid specifically to a victim's family (kin) rather than to a court. Distinct from weregild in that kin-price is personal and negotiated face-to- face with grieving relatives.

**Kirtle** — [Historical] A long garment, essentially a tunic reaching to the knees or ankles. Worn by both men and women. A woman's kirtle is her primary garment. A man's working kirtle is shorter, belted, and worn under an outer coat.

**Kill-money** — [Coined] The informal bonus for a confirmed enemy kill during a contract engagement. Not formalized -- the captain decides. Kill-money is how commons earn extra. It encourages aggression, which is a double-edged policy.

**Kindling** — [Historical] Small, dry material used to build a fire from tinder to fuel. Thin sticks, bark strips, dry grass. The stage between spark and flame. A man who cannot produce kindling in wet conditions will die.

**King-post** — [Historical] The central vertical timber in a roof truss. The building's spine. "Taking away the king-post" means removing the one thing holding the structure together.

**Kin-bond** — [Historical] Blood relation or shield-oath equivalent. The strongest social bond in the Rimevegr. A kin-bonded man will fight for his bonded kin when he would not fight for silver.

**Kin-clan** — [Coined] A mercenary band composed of blood relatives or shield-oath siblings. See "Blood-Shield" archetype. Morale checks easier when defending kin.

**Kit** — [Slang] A man's total personal equipment: weapon, armor, clothing, tools, fire-pouch, water-skin, bedroll. "His kit is poor" means the man is inadequately equipped. "Good kit" means he can march and fight without needing resupply. Kit quality determines survival.

**Knee-timber** — [Historical] Naturally curved wood used for structural joints in ship-building and construction. Cut from branch junctions. Strong because the grain follows the curve.

**Knife-rest** — [Slang] The moment at a negotiation when both sides stop reaching for weapons. "We are past the knife-rest" means the danger has passed and talking can begin. Not always true.

**Knorr** — [Historical] A broad, deep-hulled Norse cargo ship. Designed for ocean crossings. In coastal Rimevegr settlements, knorr-hulks sit beached and rotting -- relics of the trade networks that died with the Cracking.

**Knot** — [Historical] A fastening made by intertwining rope. A man who cannot tie three knots (bowline, clove-hitch, sheet-bend) is useless on a march. Knots hold shelters together, secure loads to the mule, and bind wounds.

**Krona** — [Coined] A silver coin. The standard currency in larger settlements. Stamped with a jarl's mark. Smaller transactions use hack-silver. A krona buys a day's room and board in a settlement, or a week's bread on the road.

## L

**Lackey** — [Slang] A derogatory term for a man who follows another without independent will. Not the same as a loyal follower -- a lackey does as told because he has no capacity for independent action.

**Ladle** — [Historical] A long-handled spoon for serving stew from the communal pot. Dalla controls the ladle. The ladle is power -- who gets served first, who gets the thick portion, who gets broth.

**Landless** — [Coined] The Rimevegr's underclass. Free men who own nothing -- no property, no kin network, no prospects. They drift between settlements seeking seasonal work. In good times, they labor. In bad times, they steal or join mercenary bands. The band's commons are recruited from the landless.

**Landmark** — [Coined] A recognizable feature used for navigation. A distinctive rock, a dead tree, a curve in the river. The Rimevegr has no maps. Landmarks are traded between bands like currency. "There is a split pine two days north" is navigation in the Rimevegr.

**Lanolin** — [Historical] The natural oil in sheep's wool. Makes wool partially water-resistant. Raw wool retains lanolin; washed wool loses it. Do not over-wash wool garments. They should smell somewhat of sheep. The lanolin is doing its job.

**Lífinnanlíf** — [Coined] Life-inside-life. The terrifying experience of standing inside another person's memory so fully that their fear, motion, and last decisions feel like your own.

**Ljóðveiðr** — [Coined] Verse-hunting. The galdr-worker's practice of deliberately initiating a söngœði by planting a seed-line, then recording the truths the collective singing produces. The galdr-worker triggers the song but does not control it -- he sets the trap and writes down the catch.

**Löglag** — [Coined] Law-songs. The Rimevegr's legal code encoded in verse for memorization. Performed by the Lawspeaker at the Thing in a chanting monotone. A forgotten verse is a forgotten law. A sung law cannot be burned.

**Last-fire** — [Coined] The final fire of a camp before moving. The last-fire is used to burn anything not worth carrying and to signal departure to anyone watching. A camp left without a last-fire looks abandoned in panic.

**Latch** — [Historical] A simple bar-and-slot door fastener. The difference between a latched door and an unlatched door is measured in seconds of warning when someone forces entry.

**Lath** — [Historical] A thin strip of wood. Used for wall-construction, roof-support, and temporary splints. Readily split from any straight-grained log.

**Lean-to** — [Historical] A simple shelter: poles leaned against a tree or wall, covered with branches or hides. The fastest camp shelter. One man can raise a lean-to in minutes. It blocks wind from one direction only.

**Leather-jack** — [Historical] A leather drinking vessel, often waxed inside to hold liquid. More durable than clay, less precious than horn. A hardened leather-jack can stop a weak knife thrust -- not by design, just by accident of thickness.

**Leather** — [Historical] Tanned animal hide. The universal material. Boots, belts, scabbards, shield-covers, pouches, armor, rope, water-skins. A man with leather-working skill never starves -- the demand is constant.

**Ledger, the** — [Coined] The charcoal record kept by Gest. Pay rituals, foraging quotas, supply counts, grievances, debts. The ledger is the band's institutional memory. When it is full, the information is condensed onto a new sheet and the old one is burned. The burn is witnessed to prevent tampering.

**Ledger-day** — [Coined] The daily cycle of the band, as structured by the ledger's rhythms: morning count, midday march, evening fire, night watch.

**Lie-price** — [Coined] The cost of discovering a broken promise. When a jarl's terms do not match his actions, the lie- price is what the band demands to correct the shortfall. Usually double the original amount. "The lie-price is steep" is a warning that the band knows it was cheated.

**Lime-bast** — [Historical] See _bast_. Specifically from linden (lime) trees. The strongest plant-fiber cordage locally available.

**Line-fighter** — [Coined] A man whose job in combat is to hold a position and fight in formation (such as it is). Kell is a line-fighter. Not skilled enough for skirmishing, not smart enough for scouting. But he stands where you put him and he does not move.

**Lodestone** — [Historical] Naturally magnetic iron ore. Used (rarely) for compass-like navigation in Norse culture. In the Rimevegr, lodestones are superstitiously valued -- they "point to the truth," which is to say, they point north.

**Loft** — [Historical] An upper floor or sleeping platform inside a longhouse. Usually reached by ladder. Warmer than ground-level because heat rises. The loft is where children sleep and grain is stored.

**Long Dark** — [Coined] The 300-day winter season of the Rimevegr. Cold, snow, diminishing light, The Veil at its thickest. Everything costs more energy. Fire is the center of life. The Long Dark is not a season but a state of existence.

**Long Summer** — [Coined] The 60-day warm season. Near-constant daylight. Aggressive growth. Mud replaces ice. Insects are vicious. Not summer as the Norse knew it -- compressed, violent, exhausting in its own way.

**Longhouse** — [Historical] The standard Norse dwelling. Long, narrow, turf-roofed. Central fire pit. Animals at one end, people at the other. In the Rimevegr, longhouses cluster on fjord slopes. The band does not own one. They shelter in abandoned ones when lucky.

**Loot-share** — [Coined] The contractual division of plunder. Percentages set at band formation: captain's share, Named Men's share, veterans' share, commons' share. Changing the loot-share mid-contract is a band-break trigger.

**Loom** — [Historical] A frame for weaving cloth. Settlement technology. The vertical warp-weighted loom is the Rimevegr standard. A woman at a loom produces wool cloth at about one yard per day. The sound of a working loom is the sound of a functioning settlement.

**Lopt** — [Coined] The Scar-Mouth. God of lies that are true, truths no one wants to hear, and fire that burns the house so the family escapes. His lips are sewn shut. His eyes laugh. Invoking Lopt by name requires spitting afterward.

**Lopt's fingers** — [Slang] Spies, poisoners, quartermasters who cook the books. Anyone who serves through deception. Not a compliment. An acknowledgment.

**Lopt's mouth** — [Slang] A liar. Someone hiding something. "That is Lopt's mouth talking" means the speaker is not to be trusted.
