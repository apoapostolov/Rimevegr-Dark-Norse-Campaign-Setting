# Vignettes and Scenes

**Project:** Iron Ledger — Mercenaries of the Rimevegr
**Purpose:** Voice-standard fiction for the Iron Ledger novel and text-adventure. Each vignette stands alone. Prose follows the Cook-Abercrombie composite voice: logistical baseline, emotion through cost, character through gesture, silence as structure, subtext unexplained.

## 1. The Ford Count

Voss counted heads at the ford and came up one short. The river had not taken anyone — the river was knee-deep and slow, the kind of crossing that killed no one. He turned in the saddle, scanning the column, and Gest met his eye from the rear guard and held up five fingers. Five where there should have been six.

Ketil was gone. He had been behind Kell at the last count. Before that, behind Orm. Before that, nobody could say with certainty, because nobody watched Ketil the way Ketil watched everyone else.

Voss did not send men back. The road ahead was paid for. The road behind was not. Gest noted the name in the ledger with a stub of charcoal and asked no questions aloud. He had been doing this longer than questions helped.

The column moved on. One lighter. The arithmetic was the same as always: feed the ones who stayed, pay the ones who fought, bury the ones who fell, and forget the ones who walked off in the dark. That was the work.

---

## 2. The Ledger Stone

Every seventh day, Gest sat on a rock or a fallen trunk or whatever was nearest to flat, and the men came past one at a time.

The ritual never changed. Gest had the purse on his left side and the ledger on his right and a small knife stuck in the wood beside him, because once — years ago, before Voss's time — a man had grabbed the purse and run. The knife was not for the purse. It was for the hand.

Each man received his retainer in copper, counted it in front of Gest, and walked away. Some counted slowly. Some did not count at all, because they trusted Gest, which was a kind of compliment the sergeant did not acknowledge.

Kell never counted. He put the coin in his belt without looking at it and walked back to whatever he had been doing. If someone had asked him the exact amount, he could have named it. He simply did not need to look.

Lump came last. Big hands, thick fingers. Gest dropped two silver and a handful of copper into his palm. The amount was correct. It was always correct. Gest closed the ledger with a soft thud. No one spoke. The arithmetic had been satisfied for another week.

## 3. The Black Pine

The column moved single file through the black pine. No one spoke. The only sound was boots on frozen needles and the soft clink of iron.

Then the Hush fell.

All sound died at once. Birds stopped mid-call. Wind vanished. The men froze. Voss's hand went to his axe. Gest's eyes narrowed.

For ten heartbeats the world was perfectly silent.

Then a raven croaked somewhere far off. The wind returned. The column started moving again without a word.

Kell had his hand on his rune-amulet the whole time. Lump saw it. Nobody mentioned it. Mentioning it would make it real.

---

## 4. Orm's Hiring

The new man's name was Orm, and he had the look of someone who had been eating regularly until about three weeks ago. Good shoulders. Clean teeth, which was unusual. A scar across the back of his left hand that said he had blocked a blade once and learned from it.

"What can you do?" Voss asked.

"I can hold a line. I can dig. I can walk thirty miles if someone feeds me at the end."

"Can you keep your mouth shut when the job isn't what you were told?"

Orm looked at him. Then at Gest. Then at the eight men behind them who were not looking at him, because nobody cared about new men until they had survived something.

"I've done that before," he said.

Voss held out a coin. Half-silver, clipped at the edge — the advance. Orm took it. That was the whole ceremony. No oath, no contract, no handshake. Voss paid him, which meant Orm was his, which meant that if he ran with the advance Voss would put his name on a board somewhere and somebody down the road would collect. Orm understood this without being told. The men who needed it explained were the men who did not last.

Gest wrote him into the roster that evening. Common. No rank. Shares last.

---

## 5. The Barrow Mouth

The barrow entrance was a black mouth in the hillside. Runes carved around it had bled in the rime — thin red lines tracing the grooves like veins in old stone.

Voss went first, axe ready. Lump held the rear and the torch. The flame flickered as if something was breathing on it from inside.

The air was colder than the outside. Walls lined with old bones. Halfway down the passage one of the skeletons shifted. Not fully — just enough to turn its skull toward the light.

Voss killed it with one efficient blow. The bones clattered across the flagstone and settled.

Thorne, three paces back, did not blink. He touched his rune-amulet once, a gesture he had not made at the entrance. He made it now, where the dead had moved.

The torch Lump held threw shadows that did not match the shapes casting them. He said nothing. A spooked torchbearer was worse than a careless one.

---

## 6. Payment in Kind

The village elder was thin and grey. He had no silver left. Instead he offered dried cod, two thralls, and the use of his remaining winter stores.

Voss looked at the elder a long time.

"Take the cod and the thralls," he said. "Keep enough stores to last your people a fortnight. We are not here to watch your children starve."

The elder's shoulders dropped an inch. Not relief — the end of one calculation and the beginning of another. Gest wrote the numbers in the ledger, face neutral.

Later, Thorne caught Lump by the woodpile. "Remember that," he said, voice low. "Voss spent future silver to buy the old man's gratitude. If the next contract pays short, he will remember who ate the food that mercy cost."

In the Rimevegr, even kindness had a ledger entry.

---

## 7. The Ledger Argument

Gest and Voss were arguing quietly by the fire. The ledger was open. Numbers did not add up. Someone had been shorting the common men's rations.

Voss did not look at anyone in particular. Gest did not need to.

"Next time it happens," Gest said, voice low, "we take it out of the last man's share."

The silence that followed was the kind where every man in the column recalculated who was last in every line. Pay line. Ration line. March order.

No one looked at Lump directly. But everyone knew who the last man was.

---

## 8. Campfire Silence

The fire crackled. Ten men, ten cloaks, one shared silence. Someone passed a skin of weak ale. Voss took a pull, handed it on. When it reached Lump the veterans watched to see if he drank too much or too little.

He took one careful swallow and passed it on.

For a moment the only sound was the fire and the wind in the pines.

Then Voss spoke, so quiet it was almost not speech:

"Tomorrow we clear the barrow. Anyone who slows us down gets left behind."

Nobody looked at Lump. The words settled on his shoulders like fresh rime.

---

## 9. The Rune-Stone

Thorne found it at the edge of camp. A rune-stone, half-buried, carvings crusted with rime. He called Gest over.

Gest crouched, blew the frost from the runes, and read in a low mutter. The stone did not whisper back — not at first. Then a faint hum, almost below hearing, rose from the carved lines.

"Fehu reversed," Gest said. "Loss of wealth. Or a warning against greed."

Thorne spat. "Every stone in this cursed land says the same thing."

But Gest kept staring at the rune. For a moment his eyes went distant, as if he was seeing something the rest of them could not.

"We march east," he said. "And we do not come back this way."

No one asked why. In the Rimevegr, the men learned when not to ask.

---

## 10. The Bridge

The bridge was half-rotted and the fjord water below was black with cold. The band needed to cross with full packs. No one trusted the timber.

Voss pointed at Lump. "You cross first. If it holds your weight, it holds ours."

The wood groaned under his boots. Every step sounded like the last. Halfway across, a plank cracked — but held. He made it to the other side, legs shaking beneath the pack weight.

Voss nodded. Just once. The others crossed behind him, quick and light.

No one thanked him. But for the rest of the day the flat contempt in their eyes had softened just a fraction. The weak link was still the weak link. But today the weak link had been useful.

---

## 11. The Village Tribute

The village was smaller than Frostfjord. Nine houses, a ditch, and a palisade that leaned in three places. The elder was a woman with forearms like a smith's and a face that suggested she had heard speeches before.

Voss did not give a speech.

"Your village needs a patrol," he said. "The contract at Bleakwater is finished. We can stay through the season. One meal per man per day, drawn from your stores. Shelter in the barn. No looting. In exchange, we walk the road and answer whatever comes from the north."

The elder looked at Gest. Then at Kell, who was leaning on his spear twenty paces back and looking at nothing, the way Kell looked at nothing when he wanted someone to think about what he could be looking at.

"We cannot feed ten for a season," the elder said.

"Eight, then. I'll rotate. Eight mouths for eight weeks."

"Six."

"Eight, for ten weeks, and my foragers hunt your tree line — none of it touches your stores."

The elder's jaw moved. She was calculating: not whether the offer was fair, but whether refusing it was more expensive. An unprotected village on that road had lost two goats the previous season and a man the season before.

"Eight," the elder said. "But your men stay out of the houses."

"Done."

Neither of them shook hands. Neither needed to.

## 12. Kell's Wound

Kell sat on the log and held his arm out straight. The cut ran from elbow to wrist, shallow but long, crusted with dried blood and barrow dirt.

Thorne heated the needle over the fire. No one offered ale to dull the pain. Ale cost copper.

Kell did not flinch. Not once. Fourteen stitches, each placed with a veteran's precision. When it was done, Thorne wrapped the arm in a strip of linen and tied it off.

"Two weeks," Thorne said. "No axe work."

Kell's jaw was clenched so tight the muscles stood out like rope.

Two weeks of one arm meant someone else carried his pack. Someone else took his watch shift. Everyone's load got heavier because one man caught a draugr's claw. That was the arithmetic of wounds.

---

## 13. The Rime Fog March

The fog was so thick Lump could not see the man ahead. Only the clink of gear and the wet sound of boots told him Orm was still there.

Voss ordered a rope line. Ten men tied wrist to wrist, stumbling through white nothing. The column moved at half speed.

Twice someone fell. The rope caught them. Once it was Snorri, whose knees were going bad. Once it was Lump.

No one said a word either time.

By midday the fog lifted enough to see pine trees on both sides of the path. The column had drifted a full league south. Petra checked the sun angle and corrected.

Gest marked the distance lost in the ledger. Every wasted hour cost food.

---

## 14. The Driving Snow

The snow came sideways, hard as thrown gravel. Visibility dropped to arm's length. The column huddled against a rock outcrop and waited.

One hour. Two. The wind did not relent.

Voss sent Kell and Bjorn to scout for shelter. They returned with frostbite on their fingers and news of a shallow cave two hundred paces north.

The band moved. In the cave the men stripped wet cloaks and pressed together for warmth. Lump ended up between Orm and Rolf, his bulk for once an advantage — a windbreak for smaller men.

No one thanked him. But no one moved away either.

---

## 15. The Barrow Payment

The contract was simple: clear the barrow, bring proof, collect eighty silver. The proof was a rune-carved skull from the deepest chamber.

Voss laid the skull on the jarl's table. It stank of old earth and something else — something metallic and old, like the air after a lightning strike.

The jarl's steward counted out the silver slowly: eight stacks of ten, each coin tested with teeth.

Gest recorded every coin in the ledger. The advance of twenty-five had already been entered. Net payment: fifty-five silver. After retainers, supplies, and the healer's fee for Kell's arm, the band cleared eleven silver profit.

Eleven silver for three men wounded and two nights in the dark.

Voss took his captain's share without expression. The arithmetic of the Rimevegr was always exact, even when the margins were thin.

---

## 16. Night Watch

Third shift. The dead hours between midnight and dawn. The fire had burned low. The others were shapes under cloaks.

The forest was perfectly still. Too still. No wind. No owls. No rustling.

Then: a sound. Not a branch breaking. Not an animal. Something like wood flexing under weight, a slow, deliberate creak, as if something very large was adjusting its position among the trees.

Lump gripped his spear. His palms were wet.

The sound did not repeat.

When dawn came he said nothing. A spooked sentry was worse than a careless one. But for the rest of the day he could not shake the thing — something in the pines had been watching. Patient. Unhurried. Utterly indifferent to whether he noticed.

---

## 17. The Stripped Village

The village at the fork of the Fen River had already been hit when they arrived.

One house burned, still smoking. Grain stores torn open. Three dead — two men and a woman, cut clean, which meant swords, which meant it had not been animals. The surviving families stood near the well, watching the column approach with the empty faces of people who had already been through the worst part and were waiting to find out if the next part was worse.

Gest went through the village to count what was left. He came back in an hour with a tally. The stores were gutted. The livestock pen was empty. The grain that had not been taken had been scattered in the mud — deliberate waste, the mark of a band that wanted to punish the village for not having more.

"Raiders?" Voss asked.

"Mercs. Three-season men, probably out of Vargheim. They left a camp twelve miles south — cold fire, track for ten to twelve feet, moving east." Gest spat. "They took what they wanted and salted the rest."

Kell stood among the dead and looked at the cuts. He did not say anything. After a while he walked back to the column and sat down and began sharpening his sword, which did not need sharpening. Some men prayed for the dead. Kell sharpened things.

There was nothing to take. No tribute to negotiate. No stores to draw from. Voss left six days of the band's own food with the elder — more than he could afford, less than the village needed — and moved on. Some men looked back. Most did not.

## 18. Frostfjord Market

The market at Frostfjord Hollow was three wooden stalls and a woman selling dried fish from a handcart. Prices scratched on slate.

Gest bought carefully: food for the week, ten copper. A replacement axe-haft, three copper. Linen for bandages, two copper. He logged each purchase in the ledger, cross-referenced against the treasury balance.

The feud with the local jarl meant every price was marked up. The fish-seller watched the band with flat suspicion. Voss kept his men in line — no stealing, no intimidation, no raised voices.

"We buy fair and leave clean," he told the column. "One theft and the gates close. Then we eat bark."

The fish-seller's prices fell slightly after the band passed through without incident. Reputation had its own currency.

---

## 19. Petra's Hunt

Petra returned at dusk with a winter hare and four small ptarmigan. She carried them strung on a cord over one shoulder, bow across the other.

The men watched her gut the hare with quick, efficient cuts. No wasted motion. The offal went into the stew pot — nothing was discarded. The ptarmigan she plucked by the fire, feathers drifting into the dark.

"Fourteen mouths," Gest said, checking the ledger. "That gives us one more day before deficit."

Petra nodded. She did not need praise. The hunt was its own proof.

Lump volunteered to stir the pot. It was the one task he could not ruin. Even the weak link kept stew from burning.

---

## 20. The Thrall Question

The village offered two thralls as partial payment. A man and a woman, both thin, both silent.

Voss studied them. "Can you march?"

The man nodded. The woman said nothing.

"Can you carry?"

Another nod.

Gest marked them in the ledger: two thralls, value twelve silver at market, current keep cost three copper per week each. Net value depreciated if they ran or died of exposure.

Lump carried their bundles to the column because he was the one most used to being told what to lift. The woman caught his eye once. Her expression was the same flat resignation he saw in still water when he washed his face.

The Rimevegr made thralls of everyone. Some wore the collar on the outside.

---

## 21. The Blood Sun

The sun rose red. Not dawn-red — blood-red, a deep arterial crimson that stained the snow and turned the fjord to rust.

The old hands knew what it meant. Thorne touched his rune-amulet. Even Voss paused, one hand on his axe, staring at the horizon.

"We do not march today," he said.

No one argued. The band made camp early, weapons within reach. The blood sun hung for two hours, then faded to the usual grey.

Nothing happened. But the tension stayed in the men's shoulders all day, like a held breath that was never released.

Gest wrote "Blood Sun — camp day" in the ledger. One day of food consumed for zero distance covered. The arithmetic of superstition.

---

## 22. Desertion at Dawn

Ketil's bedroll was empty when the column woke. His weapons were gone. So was a sack of dried fish — four days' food for one man.

Voss said nothing for a long time.

Gest opened the ledger. "Down to nine. Retainer savings: twelve copper per week. Food loss: half a day's supply."

"He went south," Petra said, reading the tracks. "Fast. He planned this."

Voss turned to the column. "Anyone else want to walk? Say it now."

Silence. The men looked at their boots.

"Then we march."

The column moved out one body lighter. The gap where Ketil had marched — between Orm and Rolf — stayed empty all morning, as if the column was reluctant to close rank around a deserter's ghost.

---

## 23. The Veil-Thinning

It came without warning. One moment the world was solid. The next, everything had a faint second image, like double vision without the headache.

The trees had shadows facing the wrong direction. The stream ran uphill for a stretch, then corrected itself. A raven spoke a word that sounded almost like a name.

Thorne knelt beside the path, eyes closed, one hand on the frozen earth. His lips moved but no sound came out.

"Do not look at the shadows," Gest said quietly. "And do not answer if something speaks your name."

Lump felt something brush the back of his neck. Cold fingers, maybe. Or maybe just the wind. He did not turn around.

The thinning passed after twenty minutes. The world snapped back to its proper shape. The men moved on without discussion.

---

## 24. Loot Division

Voss spread the take on a flat stone. Three silver arm-rings, a bronze brooch, sixteen coins of mixed silver and copper, a good iron knife.

"Standard split," he said.

Gest calculated. Captain's share: forty per cent. Named Men split twenty-five. Veterans took twenty. Commons divided fifteen.

Lump's share came to three silver. Three silver for two days of cold and a barrow that whispered something he would not repeat.

He pocketed it without complaint. Three silver meant three more days of not being the man who cost more than he earned.

---

## 25. Thorne's Warning

Thorne caught Lump alone at the stream, filling water skins.

"You heard something in the barrow," he said. Not a question.

Lump looked at the water. "I did not —"

"Do not lie to a man with rune-lore." Flat, not cruel. "The dead know the weak. They call to those whose will is thin."

Silence. The stream ran cold over his hands.

"Build your will," Thorne said. "Pray if you know how. And if the whispers come again, do not answer. To answer is to invite."

He walked away. Lump filled the skins with hands that would not stay still.

That night Thorne moved his bedroll. Not close enough to be obvious. Just close enough to intervene.

---

## 26. The Oath Breaker's Skull

At the crossroads there was a skull on a stake. Weathered, eyeless, jaw wired shut with iron. A carved rune below it read: _Oath-Breaker._

Voss did not slow the column. But every man looked.

"What did he do?" Lump asked Orm.

Orm shrugged. "Broke his word to a jarl. Stole from his captain. Bedded the wrong man's wife. Does it matter?"

It mattered to Lump. He wondered what the threshold was. How many broken promises, how many failed duties, before someone's skull ended up at a crossroads with its jaw wired shut.

Gest saw him staring. "Keep moving, Lump. The road is long and the skull is not going anywhere."

---

## 27. The Jarl's Hall

The jarl's hall smelled of smoke and old mead. Tapestries lined the walls — scenes of whale hunts and shield walls, faded but proud.

Voss stood before the jarl. Nine armed men behind him, arranged by rank. Named Men at the front. Commons at the rear. Lump stood behind Orm, taking up more space than he wanted to.

"Sixty silver for the season," the jarl said. "Guard the north road. Kill whatever comes down it."

Voss did not blink. "Sixty covers my commons. What about my Named Men?"

The jarl's steward whispered. The number went to eighty.

Voss accepted with a handshake. No parchment — parchment was for merchants. In the Rimevegr, a captain's handshake was either binding or fatal.

---

## 28. Dalla's Kitchen

Dalla managed the cook-fire with the same precision Gest brought to the ledger. She knew exactly how much water stretched the stew without thinning it past the point of nutrition.

Lump chopped firewood. An easy task, one of the few where his size was an asset. The axe rose and fell in a rhythm that was almost calming.

"Smaller pieces," Dalla said without looking up. "Small burns hot and fast. Big burns slow and smoky. We need hot tonight."

He chopped smaller. The pile grew. When the stew was ready, Dalla served in strict order: Voss first, then Named Men, then veterans, then commons.

The stew was thin. But it was hot, and Dalla had gotten every calorie possible from the ingredients. In the Rimevegr, a good cook was worth as much as a good fighter.

---

## 29. Snorri's Story

The old man started talking after the second cup of weak ale. No one asked him to. No one stopped him.

"I marched with the Iron Tide," Snorri said. "Twenty years. Six captains. Three of them I buried. Two deserted. One I killed myself when he tried to sell us to a warchief."

The fire popped. The men listened with the studied indifference of men who had heard old soldiers talk before.

"You know what kept me alive?" Snorri looked around the fire. His eyes stopped on Lump. "Being useful. Not brave. Not strong. Useful. Find the one thing you can do that no one else wants to, and do it every day until they cannot imagine the column without you."

He took another sip. The moment passed.

The next morning, when no one volunteered to dig the latrine pit, Lump picked up the shovel first.

---

## 30. The Three Wolves

They saw them from the ridge: a column of twenty, moving fast along the valley floor. Their banner was a triple wolf-head on grey cloth.

Voss watched through the trees. His face gave nothing away.

"Hadric's band," Petra said softly. "They burned Feldwick last month. The jarl there owes them passage rights now."

"How many?"

"Twenty, maybe twenty-two. Three Named Men at least. The big one is Hadric — Red Jaw, they call him."

Voss was quiet for a long time. Then: "We go east. Different path. No reason to cross their line today."

The column turned east. No one argued. In the Rimevegr, discretion and cowardice looked the same from the outside, but one kept men alive.

---

## 31. The Broken Contract

The steward at Ashen Reach shook his head. "The contract is void. The Widow has changed her terms."

Voss's hand tightened on his axe-haft. Gest put one steadying hand on the ledger, a gesture that was half-calculation, half-restraint.

"We marched eight days," Voss said. "My men ate. My men bled. That costs silver."

The steward looked uncomfortable. "I can offer half the agreed sum. Fifteen silver."

Gest opened the ledger. "Our expenses for the march: twenty-two silver in food, retainer, and one man's healing. You are asking us to eat the difference."

The steward swallowed. The number went to twenty.

Voss took it. Barely. As the column left, Gest wrote two entries: twenty silver received, and seven silver noted as debt owed by Ashen Reach, collectible at the Widow's displeasure.

The ledger remembered what the steward would prefer to forget.

---

## 32. The Sorcerer at the Ford

The woman at the ford had the look of someone who had not eaten well in a long time and did not care. Thin through the shoulders, sharp at the jaw, hands that moved like someone used to working with things smaller than a sword. She smelled of birch bark and something metallic, like old blood or fresh lightning.

"I hear you need a caster," she said.

Voss looked at her the way he looked at a bridge before crossing — testing what would hold. "What can you do?"

"Galdr. Wound-closing, enough to save a man who would otherwise bleed out. Rune-reading at a distance of two days. Ward-laying — nothing complex, but enough to make a camp unwelcome to things that walk the barrows."

"What do you cost?"

"Full share. My own fire. No man touches my kit without asking."

Gest wrote nothing down yet. He watched.

"If you're worth the share, you'll get it," Voss said. "If not, you walk."

"I've walked before," she said. No smile. No pitch. She stated facts the way Gest stated accounts.

She came with nothing but a leather satchel and the smell. The first time someone bled in the field, she pressed her palms flat on the wound and something happened that nobody wanted to describe afterwards. Kell stopped bleeding. That was the fact that mattered. How it happened stayed between the galdr-worker and whatever she drew on, and the men did not ask because asking was the beginning of knowing, and knowing was the beginning of being afraid of the person who shared their fire.

They called her Ash, after the tree. Not after anything she burned. Though she did that too, once, and nobody spoke about it.

---

## 33. Haldr's Kit

Haldr's boots went to Lump, because Lump's were rotting and Haldr's were the right size. His belt knife went to Orm, who had been using a borrowed blade since he signed. His cloak went to stores. His shield — a good one, banded iron, two seasons of service on it — went to the company inventory, where Gest marked it and did not assign it until the next man was hired, because a dead man's shield on a new man's arm on the same day was bad for morale in ways that did not show immediately but showed eventually.

What Haldr had in personal effects: a copper ring, a scrap of letter from someone whose handwriting nobody recognized, two silver coins hidden in his boot lining, and a lock of straw-fair hair tied with leather cord. These went into a pouch. The pouch went to Voss.

If they passed through Vargheim in the spring, the pouch would go to the sister Kell had mentioned. If they did not pass through Vargheim, the pouch would stay in the strongbox until it did not matter anymore, and eventually the ring would be spent, the letter would rot, and the lock of hair would be one more thing that outlived the person it was meant for.

The space in the column where Haldr had marched filled in within a day. The space around the fire where he had sat took longer. Lump sat there on the fourth night and nobody said anything about it, and after that it was Lump's spot, and the dead man's space belonged to the band the way everything eventually did.

## 34. The Door

The thing Kell did at the Ashfall barrow was not complicated. The contract was going wrong — two men down, the kill ground inside the ruin compromised, and the thing they had been hired to clear between them and the exit. The air had turned wrong an hour ago — hot and close, the way stone gets above a forge after the bellows have run all day, and the walls wept moisture that was not rain.

Kell was on the right side near a gap in the wall, and when the thing came through he did not wait to be told. He heard it before he saw it — a heavy dragging over flagstone, wet underneath, and the heat hit his face before the shape did. He put himself in the gap — not heroically, the way men talked about it later. He stepped into it the way a man steps into a queue, because nobody else was there and somebody needed to be. He braced his shield and the impact drove him back a foot on the stone floor. The shield held. Whatever scraped across its face left a gouge he could fit two fingers into afterwards. He held long enough that Voss could close the passage and Gest could turn the column into something that functioned again.

He did not drive the thing back. He held long enough that the men who could do those things had room to do them.

After, when it was done and two men were bleeding and Ash was kneeling beside the worst with her hands pressed flat on the wound, Voss looked at Kell.

"That was right," he said. Two words. From Voss, it was a speech.

That evening at the fire Orm sat down next to Kell instead of across from him, which he had never done before. Within a week, three men were calling him Kell the Door — not a title, not a rank, but a story compressed to a single word.

Kell did not use it himself. He did not need to.

## 35. The Shadow

Lump was washing his face in the stream when he saw it. His reflection rippled, but his shadow on the bank did not move in sync. For half a heartbeat it stayed still while he leaned forward.

Then it caught up.

He looked around. No one watching. The pines were quiet. The water was clear and cold.

He leaned forward again, slowly. His shadow followed perfectly. Normal. Ordinary.

But he was certain — certain the way he was certain of cold or hunger — that for one moment his shadow had been its own thing. Separate. Watching.

He dried his face and rejoined the column. He did not mention it. In Voss's band, men who talked about shadows did not last.

Thorne, walking three paces behind, narrowed his eyes at the back of Lump's head. He saw nothing. But he felt something. Thorne trusted what he felt more than what he saw.

---

## 36. The Blood Oath

Voss knelt down and drew his knife across his palm. Not deep. Deep enough. The blood welled and he held the hand over the bare earth and let it fall.

"I will leave nothing in that hole," he said. "No man of mine stays behind. We go in, we come out, and whatever the barrow has, we take. I am binding this with blood because words do not hold in the dark, and the dark is where we are going."

The men watched. Some gripped their weapons. Some looked at the ground. None of them spoke, because speaking during a blood-oath was the kind of thing that got remembered in ways that benefited no one.

The blood soaked into the earth. It did not steam. It did not glow. It was blood on cold ground, red turning dark, and the weight of the moment was not in what it meant but in the fact that every man there had agreed, separately and silently, that the oath was binding.

## 37. Dagfinn

The man standing at the tavern door was thin as a fence post. Farmer's hands, no calluses from weapons. He kept glancing at Voss's axe.

"I can dig," the man said. "I can carry. I eat less than most."

Gest flipped open the ledger without looking up. "Name."

"Dagfinn."

"Background."

"Barley. Pigs. My lord's steward burned the grain stores for spite when the tax came short."

Gest wrote it down. Two silver signing cost, twenty copper a week retainer. Dagfinn signed with an X. His hand shook.

Later, as the column moved out, Lump caught Dagfinn watching the veterans. The same flat look Lump remembered from his own first march. The look of a man who understood exactly what he had traded his freedom for.

---

## 38. The Kidnapping

The job came through a middleman — a trader named Sorli who wore good boots and bad teeth and who named a price in the corner of the Frostfjord inn without ever saying the word "kidnap."

"Retrieval," was the word Sorli used. "A merchant's son who has run up debts with the wrong family. The father wants him back before the wrong family decides to collect what the son cannot pay in coin."

"Where is he?"

"Three days east. A village called Ashfall. He's living with a woman there. He thinks he's hidden."

Voss took the job for ninety silver — half on acceptance, half on delivery.

The grab went clean. Gest and Kell and four men walked into Ashfall at first light. The merchant's son was asleep. Kell put a hand over his mouth and Gest tied his wrists, and by the time the woman woke up and started screaming the column was already two hundred paces out of the village. The woman followed for half a mile, barefoot, calling names that nobody in the column answered.

The merchant's son ate what they gave him and slept where they told him and twice tried to negotiate his own release. The second time, Kell told him to shut up, and there was something in Kell's voice — not anger, not threat, something quieter — that made the merchant's son understand that the conversation was finished.

They delivered him in four days, standing and unharmed. The father paid. Voss put the silver in the strongbox and did not discuss what the father intended to do with the son, because that part was not in the contract and the contract was the only part that mattered.

---

## 39. The Coin Runs Out

They were three days from the Stillmire contract when Voss opened the strongbox and counted what was left.

He did not say anything to Gest. He did not have to. The sergeant had been watching the box the way a farmer watches the sky in a dry season — not looking directly at it, but never quite looking away.

"Two days," Voss said.

"I know."

"The men will know by tomorrow."

"They already know." Gest rolled his shoulders. "Orm has been talking to Ketil. Ketil has been talking to everyone. They are not stupid."

Voss closed the box. Two days of retainer for ten men was twenty copper he did not have. The contract ahead was worth a hundred and ten silver if they finished the job. Between here and there, the men would eat, the men would march, and the men would do the arithmetic, and by the second morning without pay someone would ask the question that could not be answered with promises.

"I'll talk to them tonight," Voss said.

"Keep it short," Gest said. "They don't need a speech. They need to know you know."

---

## 40. The Hidden Hoard

Voss told Gest and no one else. He walked an hour east of camp at dusk, carrying twenty silver and a good knife wrapped in oilcloth, and dug a hole at the base of a boulder where the stream split around a stand of birch. The boulder was distinctive — split down the middle, pale granite against the dark earth, visible from the path only if you knew to look left at the fork.

He buried the bundle, covered it, scattered needles and leaf-rot over the disturbed soil, and walked back. He did not mark it. He wrote the location in the ledger in a cipher Gest had taught him, using the names of rivers for compass directions and the names of the dead for distances.

If he died, Gest could read the cipher, because Gest had taught it to him. If they both died, the money stayed in the ground, and whoever found it in ten or fifty years would wonder who had buried a good knife beside twenty silver at the base of a split rock in a forest that did not have a name.

---

## 41. End of the Week

The seventh day. Gest sat on the flat stone with the ledger open and the purse beside him. Same ritual. Same silence.

This week the numbers were bad. Two days of fog lost, one day of blood-sun camp, food stores critical, no contract pay pending. The treasury was forty-one silver and falling.

Voss took his share. Named Men took theirs. Veterans. Commons.

When it reached Lump, his pay was correct. Two silver, copper on top. Some captains would short the weak link first. Voss did not.

Gest closed the ledger. "Three weeks at current burn," he said, voice neutral. "After that we are choosing between paying the men and feeding them."

Voss stared at the fire. The arithmetic had spoken. In the Rimevegr, the arithmetic always got the last word.
