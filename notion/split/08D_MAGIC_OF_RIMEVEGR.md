## The Practitioner's Lifecycle

Every practitioner has the same story. The details change — runes or spirits or bone lots, Skaldhaven or Icebreak or some unnamed settlement on the ice — but the arc does not. Something wakes in childhood. Someone notices. Training begins, and with it the cost. The working years burn through body and mind at a rate that only becomes visible in retrospect. Decline arrives earlier than anyone expected. Death comes in forms specific to the tradition but universal in their inevitability.

The mechanical framework is in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`. The stat blocks are in `22_MEMBER_STATBLOCKS.md`. This section covers what neither of those documents can hold: the lived shape of a practitioner's life from the first sign through the last breath. What it looks like from the inside. What it costs in years, in relationships, in the slow accumulation of damage that no healer can reverse because the damage is the practice.

Magic in the Rimevegr is not a gift. It is a condition. And like all conditions, it has a progression.

### Manifestation — How Magic Finds Its Practitioners

Nobody chooses to be a practitioner. The capacity arrives uninvited, manifests without permission, and cannot be returned. It shows up in childhood, during the years when the body is still forming and the mind has not yet learned to filter out what it should not perceive. The traditions differ in how the talent presents, but they share one truth: it always looks wrong before it looks right.

**Galdr manifestation** typically surfaces between ages eight and twelve. The child hears something. Not voices — galdr-workers are careful to distinguish this from what seiðr-wives experience. The runes hum. A piece of carved wood on a doorframe makes a sound that nobody else hears, and the child cannot stop listening. They trace shapes on surfaces — the dirt floor of a longhouse, the bark of a birch, the flat of their own thigh — without knowing the shapes have names. To their parents, it looks like restlessness. A child who cannot sit still, who scratches and scrapes at everything, who stares at carved posts the way other children stare at fire.

Not all strange children become galdr-workers. Many children in the Rimevegr are strange — the Long Dark does that, the isolation does that, the cold and the hunger and the dark do that. But every galdr-worker was a strange child. Ash was scratching rune-shapes into his sleeping bench at age nine. He did not know what they were. His mother thought he was carving his name. He was not carving his name. He was carving Isa, the ice-rune, over and over, because he could hear it in the wall and the sound would not stop until the shape was right.

Some families recognize this. In settlements with a working galdr-worker — someone who carves wards and charges for the service — a parent might bring the child forward. "My boy scratches at things. Is it the talent or is he bored?" The galdr-worker takes one look at the scratches and knows. The shapes are too precise for boredom. A bored child carves lines. A galdr-child carves runes they have never been taught.

In settlements without a galdr-worker — and there are more of these every decade — the talent goes unrecognized. The child grows up, the humming fades to background noise, the urge to carve becomes a habit that nobody connects to anything larger. Another potential practitioner lost. Not to death, not to violence, but to the simple fact that nobody was there to say: "That sound you hear is your life's work."

**Seiðr manifestation** arrives earlier, between ages five and ten, and it arrives louder. The child does not hear shapes. The child sees things. Talks to things. Stares at empty corners and describes what is there to adults who see nothing.

In a settlement that respects the seiðr tradition — one with a working seiðr-wife, one where the women who keep the old knowledge have not been driven out or frightened into silence — this is recognized. "The girl talks to the air." The seiðr-wife visits. Watches the child. Asks questions. "What does the air look like when it talks back?" If the answers are right — if the child describes shapes and colors and temperatures that match what the seiðr-wife herself perceives in trance — the talent is confirmed. The child is not mad. The child is seeing through the Veil without the training that is supposed to come first.

In a settlement that fears seiðr — and by the late Rimevegr period, that is most settlements — the child is punished. Stop staring at nothing. Stop talking to the wall. Stop frightening your sister. The punishment works in the sense that the behavior becomes less visible. The child learns not to speak about what it sees. It does not learn not to see it. The talent does not stop because someone hit the child. It hides. It goes underground. It surfaces at night, in sleep, in moments of crisis when the conscious mind loses its grip on the filters it has built.

A punished seiðr-child grows into an adult who sees the dead and tells nobody. This is more common than anyone in the Rimevegr would admit.

**Wyrd-reading manifestation** is the most common and the least dramatic. It does not announce itself. It seeps in. The child has a sense for weather before it turns. They know who is coming to the door before the knock. They pick up the bone lots at a festival and read them correctly on the first throw — not perfectly, but close enough that the old woman running the lot-game gives them a second look.

This is the talent that passes most easily as mundane wisdom. A child who reads weather well is a useful child, not a strange one. A child who guesses visitors is observant, not magical. Only the bone lots raise questions, because the lots are supposed to require training, and when a ten-year-old reads them correctly three times running, the adults in the room start exchanging glances.

Petra did not know she was a wyrd-reader until she was fourteen. She thought she was good at guessing. The shepherd who taught her — a man named Torben who read weather and fate from the same set of signs — was the one who told her the guessing had a name. "You are not guessing," he said. "You are reading. The world is telling you things and you are listening without knowing it." He gave her a set of carved elk-bone lots and told her to practice until the reading was deliberate instead of accidental. She practiced. The reading became deliberate. The guessing stopped being guessing.

**How many never manifest at all.** This is the number nobody knows and everybody should. For every Ash who was recognized, there are five children whose galdr-talent was beaten out of them by exhaustion, labor, suspicion, or the simple absence of anyone who could name what was happening. For every Dalla who found a teacher, there are ten seiðr-children who grew up believing they were broken rather than gifted. The Long Dark kills the weak. It also kills the talented, because talent that is not trained is talent that eats its host.

The Rimevegr's magic is not scarce because the world ran out. It is scarce because the world keeps killing the supply before it matures. Every settlement that punishes a seiðr-child, every family that ignores a galdr-child's scratching, every community that lets its wyrd-reader die without passing on the craft — they are all burning seed corn and calling it discipline.

_The cruelest part is that the children who manifest are already paying the cost. The humming that Ash heard at nine was not free. The shapes the unnamed seiðr-children see through the Veil are not free. The talent is a door that opens from the other side, and the draft comes through whether or not anyone teaches the child how to close it. An untrained galdr-child hears the runes until the hearing becomes tinnitus. An untrained seiðr-child sees the dead until seeing them becomes indistinguishable from madness. The training does not create the cost. The training teaches the practitioner to survive it._

### Training — The Apprentice Years

The gap between manifestation and practice is filled with years of work that look nothing like the magic they produce. Training in the Rimevegr is not a school. It is not a curriculum. It is one person breaking another person's habits and replacing them with discipline, and the breaking hurts.

**Galdr training** is the most formal of the three traditions. A galdr-apprentice is bound to a working galdr-worker for a minimum of two years, often three. During that time, the apprentice does not carve runes. The apprentice learns to hear them.

The first year is listening. The master carves. The student watches. Not the hands — the experienced masters insist on this — but the wood. Watch the wood. Listen to the grain. A rune carved against the grain splits the mark and splits the power. A rune carved into a knot concentrates force where force should not concentrate. The apprentice sits for hours beside a carving bench, watching chisel meet wood, learning to read the material the way a butcher reads a carcass — along the grain, counting the flaws, knowing where the resistance lives.

The first mark comes at the end of the first year, or early in the second. The apprentice picks up the chisel. Their hands are shaking. Not from cold — from the humming, which has grown louder during the training because the training has opened what little filter the child had built. The first mark is Isa. Always Isa. The simplest rune, the straightest line, the vibration that a first-year apprentice can hold without the hold breaking them.

The chisel cuts into the wood. Blood follows. Always blood, from the first mark onward. Not ritual blood. Incidental blood. The chisel slips because the rune pulls, because the vibration in the hand interacts with the vibration in the wood, and the body pays for the interference. White scar-lines appear on the apprentice's hands within the first month of carving practice. Thin lines, like thread laid under the skin. By rank 1 — which comes at the end of the second or third year — the hands are mapped with them. Ash's hands are "scarred with white lines no blade made" (see `22_MEMBER_STATBLOCKS.md`). Those scars began before he was twelve.

The physical cost starts immediately and never stops accumulating. A rank-1 galdr-worker has scarred hands. A rank-2 worker has scarred hands and forearms, and the scars have begun to branch, following the paths of the nerves. By rank 3, the scarring extends to the shoulders, and the hands have a faint, persistent tremor at rest that calms only when holding a chisel. The body learns to associate the tool with the work, and the tremor is the body shaking off the excess vibration that builds when the rune-channel is open but not in use. It is not a disease. It is a side effect of being a conduit. See MAGIC_TRUTH_003 in the encoded truth system for the full cost accounting.

Higher ranks take longer and cost more. Rank 2 requires an additional two to three years of practice beyond rank 1. Rank 3 requires three to five years beyond rank 2, and by this point the apprenticeship is over — the practitioner is working independently, earning their keep, and the learning is self-directed. Ash reached rank 3 in his mid-twenties. He is thirty-two now and has not progressed. The cost curve steepens with each rank, and rank 4 demands a physical investment that rank 3 has already begun to exhaust.

Regional training centers shape the tradition. Skaldhaven maintains the largest rune archive in the Rimevegr — scrolls and carved tablets recording galdr techniques across generations. A Skaldhaven-trained galdr-worker learns from records as well as from a master. They tend to be more theoretical, more precise, and more fragile — because theory without sufficient physical conditioning produces brittle practitioners. Deepholm takes the opposite approach: a formal workshop model where apprentices carve repetitively under supervision, building hand-strength and endurance before attempting complex marks. Deepholm-trained workers are physically harder but sometimes lack the theoretical background to innovate. Most working galdr-workers were trained by neither center. Most were trained by a single practitioner in a settlement somewhere, with whatever materials and methods that practitioner had inherited.

**Seiðr training** is the opposite of galdr in almost every structural respect. There is no formal program. There is no workshop. There is a woman — almost always a woman, though the tradition does not formally exclude men — who recognizes the talent in a child and says: "Come. I will teach you what I know."

The relationship between a seiðr-wife and her student is closer to a family bond than a professional apprenticeship. The student moves into the teacher's home, or the teacher moves into the student's. They live together. Eat together. The teaching happens in the gaps between daily work — while preparing herbs, while cleaning, while walking between settlements. It is oral, personal, and unreproducible. When a seiðr-wife dies without a student, everything she knew dies with her. There is no archive. There is no scroll. There is the woman, and then there is the silence.

The training itself is trance-work first, everything else second. The student learns to reach through the Veil — not far, not deep, but enough to feel what is on the other side. The first sessions are terrifying. The student closes their eyes, the teacher chants, the herbs burn, and the student's awareness tilts sideways into a space that does not correspond to anything the waking mind can process. Shapes that are not shapes. Voices that are not voices. The dead are there, and they are interested in the newcomer, the way wolves are interested in a lamb that has wandered into the tree line.

The teacher is the anchor. The student reaches and the teacher holds the line and pulls back if the reaching goes too far. Many students break during the first year. "Break" is a precise word. The trance opens something in the mind that was not open before, and some minds are not built to hold the opening. The student comes back from the trance and cannot stop crying. Or cannot sleep. Or cannot stop talking to things that are not there — not in the controlled, deliberate way the training intends, but in the raw, unfiltered way of a mind that has seen too much and cannot close the curtain.

The students who break are not abandoned. The teacher pulls them out, grounds them, helps them close what was opened. Sometimes it works. Sometimes it does not, and the student lives the rest of their life half-in-trance, seeing the dead at the edges of their vision, never quite present, never quite gone. This is one of the Rimevegr's open secrets: in every large settlement, there is someone like this. A woman who stares at walls. A man who flinches at nothing. They were seiðr students who broke, and nobody tells their story because the story is not useful.

The students who do not break continue. They learn herb-craft — which plants dull pain, which plants open the trance, which plants close it. They learn spirit-communion protocols — how to address the dead, how to set boundaries, how to dismiss a spirit that will not leave. They learn the geography of the Veil: where it is thin (old battlefields, barrow-fields, places where many people died), where it is thick (running water, new-growth forest, places of active human habitation), and how the thickness changes with the seasons. The Long Dark thins the Veil. Midsummer thickens it.

At Icebreak, the hermit Ragnhild teaches a stripped-down version of seiðr that emphasizes survival over skill. Ragnhild's students learn to reach quickly, gather information, and pull back before the trance does damage. They are not elegant practitioners. They are fast, practical ones. Ragnhild herself is the example: she has been practicing for forty years and she is still alive, which in seiðr terms counts as an extraordinary achievement.

Emotional resilience is the foundation. Everything else — the herb-craft, the trance technique, the spirit protocols — rests on the student's ability to experience something horrifying and continue to function. The teacher builds this the only way it can be built: by exposure. Small horrors first, then larger ones. The student sees a dead child in the trance and the teacher says: "Yes. Now what will you do?" The student who can answer that question, session after session, year after year, becomes the next seiðr-wife. The student who cannot answer it was never going to survive the practice.

**Wyrd-reading training** is the least structured and the most self-directed. There is no formal apprenticeship. There are no regional centers. There is a person who reads the signs — bone lots, cloud patterns, bird-flight, the configuration of spilled ash — and a person who wants to learn how. The teaching happens wherever the teacher is, and it looks like conversation.

"What do you see?" The teacher throws the lots. The student reads them. The teacher corrects. Not the interpretation — the observation. "You saw the elk-bone crossing the wolf-bone. You read that as conflict. Look again. The elk-bone is above the wolf-bone. That is not conflict. That is survival. Conflict is side by side. Survival is one above the other."

The training is practice. Throw the lots. Interpret. Check against reality. Adjust. Throw again. Over months and years, the student builds a grammar of signs that corresponds to a grammar of outcomes. The grammar is personal — no two wyrd-readers use exactly the same system — but the foundations overlap. Certain configurations recur. Certain patterns mean death regardless of the reader's personal lexicon.

Petra learned from Torben, the shepherd. He read weather and fate from the same set of signs, because to a shepherd on the ice, they are the same thing. A storm that arrives when the flock is two days from shelter is not a weather event. It is fate. Torben taught Petra to read the sky and the lots together, to cross-reference wind against bone, to see the pattern where others saw noise. He did not frame this as magic. He framed it as paying attention. "The world tells you everything," he said. "Most people cannot be bothered to listen."

The cost of wyrd-reading training is not physical. It is psychological. The student who learns to read the lots also learns that the lots do not lie. When the bones say a man will die this winter, the man dies this winter. The student must learn to read that outcome and continue functioning — to throw the lots for the next person, knowing that the previous person's fate is already sealed. The emotional numbness that veteran wyrd-readers develop is not a personality flaw. It is an adaptation. A wyrd-reader who feels every reading will stop reading within five years. The practice selects for detachment.

_Training in every tradition is the same transaction dressed in different clothes. The student gives the teacher their time, their labor, their trust, and their intact body and mind. The teacher gives the student a skill that will eat them alive. The kindest teachers are honest about this. Ragnhild at Icebreak tells her students on the first day: "This will kill you eventually. The question is whether you will do enough good before it does." Most teachers are not that kind. Most teachers were not told either._

### Active Practice — The Working Years

The working years are the years that count. Everything before — manifestation, training, the slow acquisition of skill — exists to produce a practitioner who can do the work. The work is seasonal and relentless and it grinds through human bodies the way winter grinds through thatch.

**The galdr-worker's round** follows the year the way a farmer follows the year, but the crop is not grain. The crop is marks on wood and stone and iron, and the harvest is measured in wards that hold and bindings that do not break.

Spring is maintenance. The Long Dark is hard on ward-work. Cold contracts wood. Moisture seeps into carved marks and freezes, splitting delicate rune-channels. A galdr-worker walks their settlement's perimeter in spring the way a herdsman counts stock after winter — expecting losses, cataloguing damage, assessing what can be repaired and what must be recarved from scratch. In a well-kept settlement, ten to fifteen percent of the ward markers need recarving after winter. In a poorly kept one — where the ward-worker is overextended or underequipped — the number is higher. Thirty percent damage is the point where the ward network has functional gaps. Things come through gaps.

Summer is new construction. Building season means new longhouses, new storehouses, new animal pens, and every structure needs ward markers. A skilled settlement galdr-worker carves forty to sixty new ward markers in a summer. Each one takes half a day to a full day depending on complexity. The work is physically brutal — hours of focused carving in a state of partial rune-contact that drains willpower and steadiness. By the end of a full summer season, the galdr-worker has given eight to ten weeks of concentrated rune-work, and the body shows it. Weight loss. Exhaustion. Tremor increase in the rest hand. Two weeks of recovery before autumn, and autumn is not restful.

Autumn is emergency work and preparation. The Veil begins thinning as the days shorten. The dead become more active. Ward networks that survived spring and summer in adequate condition begin to strain under increased pressure. The galdr-worker patches, reinforces, and prays — an ironic act for a tradition that accepts the gods are gone, but the word persists even when the faith does not. Last preparations before the Long Dark: stockpiling carving materials, pre-cutting wood to the right grain, setting aside the reserve supply of iron chisel-heads because a chisel that breaks during the Long Dark cannot be replaced until spring.

Winter is the vigil. The Long Dark presses on the wards, and the galdr-worker maintains them through months of dark and cold and the relentless hum of dead things pressing against marks that are slowly weakening. Sleep becomes difficult. The runes are louder in winter — the same thinning of the Veil that empowers the dead also amplifies the rune-channels, and the galdr-worker hears them through the walls at three in the morning when everyone else is asleep. Ash describes winter as "the season when the work does not stop and the body does not recover." He sleeps in fragments. He eats when reminded. He carves, and checks, and carves again, and the winter is dark and the wards are bright and neither of those facts makes the other easier.

In a mercenary band, the cycle compresses. Ash does not have the luxury of a settled galdr-worker's year. The band moves. Every relocation means new camp wards — a full day's work to raise a temporary perimeter that will last weeks, not seasons. Trail marking for scouts requires carved path-wards that degrade within days. Contract requirements — the specific magical services a jarl is paying for — demand concentrated, high-quality work on a client's schedule, not the practitioner's. Ash's annual output is lower than a settlement galdr-worker's, but his work is more varied and more dangerous because he has no backup, no institutional support, and no rest season. See `22_MEMBER_STATBLOCKS.md` for Ash's current capacity and its limitations.

**The seiðr-wife's round** is less seasonal and more responsive. Healing happens year-round — broken bones do not wait for spring, fever does not respect the calendar. A seiðr-wife in a settlement handles three to five significant consultations per week in the active months, and the number increases during the Long Dark because illness and injury increase when the cold deepens.

Spirit consultations are seasonal by nature. The Veil thins in the Long Dark, which makes reaching easier — the spirits are closer, the trance is shallower, the information flows faster. A seiðr-wife who attempts a deep consultation in midsummer may spend hours in trance trying to reach a spirit that would come in minutes during winter. Smart practitioners schedule their spirit work accordingly. Clients who need to speak to the dead learn to wait for winter, or to pay the premium that a summer consultation costs in extra recovery time.

Ting season brings testimony work. A seiðr-wife's word carries evidentiary weight — she can speak to the dead, confirm alibis, verify inheritance claims through spirit consultation. This is not comfortable work. The spirits she contacts for ting purposes are not always cooperative, and the questions she asks are not always questions the spirits want to answer. A murder victim's spirit may want justice. It may also want revenge, and distinguishing the two inside a trance while a lawspeaker waits for an answer is one of the hardest things a seiðr-wife does.

Dalla's practice in the band is a compressed version of the settlement round. She heals injuries after combat — not with magic alone, but with herb-craft supplemented by seiðr insight. She reads the wounded body through the Veil, seeing where the damage lies beneath the skin the way a sighted person sees what a blind person cannot. The information is valuable. The trance-cost of extracting it is not trivial. Dalla looks worse after healing three wounded men than the wounded men look themselves. She does not talk about this. The men do not ask.

**The wyrd-reader's round** is daily rather than seasonal. A competent wyrd-reader casts the lots every morning. Not for herself — for the group. "What does the day hold? What should we avoid? Which direction is safer?" In a settlement, this is a public service, performed in the meeting hall or on the commons, available to anyone who brings a question and a small payment — food, usually, or labor, or the occasional copper piece.

Pre-battle readings are the highest-stakes work a wyrd-reader does. The band is about to fight. Voss wants to know the outcome. Petra throws the lots and reads what is there, and what is there is not always what Voss wants to hear. A wyrd-reader who softens the reading for the commander's comfort is a wyrd-reader who gets people killed. A wyrd-reader who delivers the reading raw — "The lots say three of your twelve will die and two more will take wounds that do not heal" — is a wyrd-reader who gets blamed for the deaths. Petra learned early to frame readings in choices rather than outcomes: "The lots say the west approach is death. The north is blood but survival." This is not dishonesty. It is translation. The lots do not care about morale. Petra does.

Ting-season divination fills the rest of her professional calendar. Legal disputes, inheritance questions, commercial decisions — all benefit from a reading, and a well-known wyrd-reader can earn enough in ting season to carry her through the quiet months. Petra does not yet have that reputation. She is young and her readings lack the weight of decades. This will change. Whether she wants it to is another question.

**The physical toll during active years** accelerates with practice. This is the mechanism that `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` models abstractly and this section makes concrete.

Galdr scarring accelerates through the working years. The white scar-lines deepen from threads to cords. The branching pattern extends from hands and forearms to shoulders and, in advanced cases, the upper back and neck. The scarring follows the nerve pathways — it is not random damage but a mapping of the channels that rune-force uses when it passes through the body. By age thirty-five, a rank-3 galdr-worker's hands look sixty. By forty, they look older than that. Ash is thirty-two and his hands are the oldest part of him.

Seiðr practitioners show premature aging that presents differently. Hair loss begins in the late twenties for active practitioners — patchy at first, then general. Dalla is twenty-eight and already wearing her hair shorter than she used to, because the thinning is visible and she does not want people counting the cost in her appearance. Joint problems follow — the hips and knees first, then the spine. Trance-work involves physical stillness for extended periods, and the body that holds the trance also holds the position, and the damage accumulates. Sleep becomes uneven. The seiðr-wife sleeps when the dead are quiet, which means she sleeps at unpredictable hours and wakes when no one else does. The body's rhythms follow the practice, not the sun.

Wyrd-readers develop obsessive patterns and anxiety. The lots speak constantly — not literally, but the reader cannot stop reading. Every arrangement of objects becomes a potential sign. Spilled grain. Dropped tools. The way a flock of birds changes direction. A veteran wyrd-reader processes the world as a continuous stream of potential information, and the processing does not stop. Petra is not yet deeply into this phase, but she already catches herself reading the arrangement of pebbles on a riverbank and has to force herself to stop. The anxiety comes from the gap between what the signs suggest and what the reader can do about it. Knowing something bad is coming and being unable to prevent it is the working condition of every wyrd-reader who has practiced for more than a decade.

**Social position** during the working years is the same across all three traditions: respected and isolated. The practitioner eats with the group but is never fully of the group. Dalla heals the men and they thank her and sit somewhere else. Ash wards the camp and they sleep soundly and forget who made that possible. Petra reads the lots and they follow her advice and attribute the outcome to their own good judgment.

This is not ingratitude. It is proximity aversion. The practitioners handle forces that the non-practitioners cannot see, cannot measure, and cannot verify except by the results. A man who watches Ash carve a ward sees a person cutting marks into wood. He does not see what the marks do, does not feel the vibration, does not hear the hum. He knows the ward works because the dead stay outside, but the connection between the carving and the protection is invisible to him. Invisible work is easy to forget. Easy to undervalue. Easy to sit away from, because something about the person who does it makes the non-practitioner's skin crawl, and the non-practitioner does not know why.

The exception is ting season, when practitioners are publicly visible and publicly useful. For a few weeks, the galdr-worker is the person who wards the Allthing stones. The seiðr-wife is the person who provides spirit testimony. The wyrd-reader is the person who predicts the harvest. They are central to communal life, and the community treats them accordingly — with respect that lasts until ting season ends and the practitioners return to their normal position on the edge of things.

_The working years are the years the practitioner lives for and dies from. The work is the purpose, the identity, the thing that justifies the cost. A galdr-worker who is not carving is restless. A seiðr-wife who is not reaching is incomplete. A wyrd-reader who is not reading is anxious. The practice is the practitioner, and the practitioner is the practice, and the distinction between self and skill dissolves somewhere around the fifth year of active work and never re-forms. This is not metaphor. This is the mechanism by which the cost accumulates. The body keeps the score, and the score is the life._

### Decline — When the Body Stops Keeping Up

Nobody announces the beginning of decline. It arrives the way winter arrives — not on the day the first snow falls, but on the day you realize the last snow never fully melted. By the time a practitioner recognizes the decline, the decline has been underway for years.

**Galdr decline** starts in the hands and works inward. The tremor that was a background hum during the working years becomes a foreground problem. A galdr-worker who cannot hold a steady chisel cannot carve a clean rune. An unclean rune is a rune that fails under stress. A rune that fails under stress is a ward that breaks when the dead push against it, and that is not an abstract problem — that is a hole in the perimeter through which something comes.

The progression is measurable. Tremor intensity increases at a rate of roughly five to eight percent per year after age forty for a rank-3 practitioner. This means that the quality gap between a forty-year-old and a forty-five-year-old is significant. A forty-year-old rank-3 galdr-worker produces wards at rank-3 quality with moderate effort. A forty-five-year-old produces the same wards at rank-2-plus quality with extreme effort, and the effort itself accelerates the tremor, creating a feedback loop that cannot be broken without stopping work entirely.

Most galdr-workers lose a full rank of effective output by age fifty. Not a formal rank — the knowledge does not leave. A fifty-year-old galdr-worker with a lifetime of rank-3 practice knows everything a rank-3 worker should know. But the hands cannot execute what the mind holds. The chisel wanders. The mark is imprecise. The rune activates at rank-2 potency because the carving carries rank-2 precision, and precision is physical and physical precision declines with age and accumulated damage.

The exceptional ones hold longer. Audun at Deepholm remains an effective rank-4 worker at fifty-three, but Audun is the exception that galdr-workers point to for comfort and that honest observers point to for scale. If the best galdr-worker in the Rimevegr starts declining at fifty-three, the ordinary worker starts at forty-five. "Longer" means fifty-five, not seventy. The idea of a galdr-worker practicing at full rank in old age is a story from before the Cracking, when divine power supplemented the physical cost, and telling that story now is cruelty dressed as hope.

Ash is thirty-two. He is already showing rank-3 strain — not decline, not yet, but the leading indicators. The tremor at rest is slightly worse this year than last year. Recovery time after a major carving session is half a day longer than it was two years ago. These are not problems. They are previews of problems. Ash will not be a rank-3 worker at forty-five unless something changes. Nothing will change. The gods are not coming back. The rune-channels are not refilling. The body is not going to reverse what three centuries of orphaned magic have set in motion. Ash's best years are the years he is living right now, and neither he nor Voss has the honesty to say it aloud.

**Seiðr decline** starts in the mind and stays there. The hands remain steady — seiðr does not require the physical precision that galdr demands. What frays is the boundary between the practitioner and the Veil, and the fraying is not a disorder. It is a consequence. Push through a membrane often enough and the membrane thins.

The dead become harder to dismiss after the trance ends. At first, it is a lingering presence — the sense that the spirit contacted during the session is still nearby, still watching, for minutes or hours after the trance closes. Over years, minutes become hours, hours become days, and the practitioner lives in a state of permanent partial contact with the dead. Voices linger. Not auditory hallucinations — no seiðr-wife calls them that, because they are not hallucinations. The dead are speaking. The fact that the trance is over does not mean the dead have stopped.

Boundaries between self and spirit blur. The oldest seiðr-wives become the strangest people in any settlement. They talk to the air, answering questions nobody in the room has asked — because the questions came from someone not in the room, someone not in the living world at all. They sleep at odd hours because the dead are quieter at midday and louder at midnight, and the seiðr-wife has learned to sleep when the noise drops and wake when it rises. They forget names — living names. They know the dead by name but call their own grandchildren "the girl" because the living have become less vivid than the dead, and the dead do not change.

This is not madness. The distinction matters. Madness is the mind failing. Seiðr decline is the mind succeeding in an environment it was never meant to sustain. The seiðr-wife sees accurately. She perceives correctly. She processes what others cannot process, and the processing wears grooves in her attention that the living cannot fill. She is not broken. She is eroded. The river is real. The fact that the river carved a canyon does not make the canyon a flaw in the rock.

Ragnhild at Icebreak, at sixty-two, is the Rimevegr's best case study in controlled seiðr decline. She maintains boundaries through brutal discipline — fixed trance schedules, strict duration limits, herb protocols that close the Veil contact as completely as herbs can close it. She still hears the dead between sessions. She still talks to them when she thinks no one is listening. But she functions. She teaches. She heals. She is the proof that managed decline is possible and the proof that managed decline is not the same as no decline.

**Wyrd-reading decline** is the quietest and the cruelest. It does not damage the body, does not fray the mind. It damages something else — the part of a person that believes outcomes are worth caring about.

Fatalism settles over a wyrd-reader the way sediment settles over a riverbed. Slowly. Invisibly. Each reading adds a grain. The lots say a child will die, and the child dies. The lots say a storm will destroy the roof, and the storm destroys the roof. The lots say the battle will be lost, and the battle is lost. Each correct reading confirms that the world moves in patterns the reader can perceive and cannot change, and the accumulation of that confirmation produces something that is not quite despair and not quite acceptance but shares features with both.

Wyrd-readers who practice for decades develop a resigned certainty about outcomes. They stop being surprised. Then they stop being invested. Then they stop reading, because the lots tell them things they do not want to know, and knowing without the ability to change is a punishment that the practice imposes and the practice does not relieve.

Petra is years from this. She is young, her readings are still fresh, and she still cares about what the lots say. She flinches when the reading is bad. She celebrates internally when the reading is good. But the trajectory is visible in every old bone-caster the Rimevegr has produced — the flat eyes, the level voice, the way they deliver a death reading with the same tone they use to announce clear weather. That tone is not strength. It is the absence of something that used to be there.

**What decline means for a band.** A mercenary company whose galdr-worker is declining is a company whose contract value is dropping. The wards weaken. The binding-marks hold for shorter periods. The clients notice before the band does, because the clients are paying for results, not for effort, and the results are measurable. A ward that lasted a year now lasts eight months. A binding-mark rated for a barrow-hold now fails at the six-month check. The jarl who hired the band last season hires a different band this season, and the reason is not personal. The reason is rank-2 work at rank-3 prices.

Voss does not understand this. The analysis from the previous section — the wasting-asset framework from Section 7 — applies here in its most concentrated form. Voss sees Ash carving. The carving looks the same. But the output quality is declining, and the decline is invisible to anyone who does not measure it, and Voss does not measure it because Voss does not count magic as a quantifiable asset. By the time the decline produces a contract failure — a ward that breaks, a binding that does not hold, a client who walks away — the decline will have been underway for years. The correction will arrive all at once, and it will look like a sudden catastrophe rather than what it is: the end of a gradual process that nobody tracked because nobody knew how.

_Decline is not the end of practice. It is the end of effective practice, which is worse, because the practitioner is still there. The knowledge is still there. The desire is still there. What is missing is the capacity, and the gap between what the practitioner knows and what the practitioner can do is a gap that fills with bitterness or resignation or, in the best cases, teaching. The galdr-worker who cannot carve can still teach. The seiðr-wife who cannot close the Veil can still counsel. The wyrd-reader who cannot care can still throw the lots. But the work they do in decline is not the work they were — it is the shadow of the work, and they know the difference, and the knowing is its own cost._

### Death — How Practitioners Die

Practitioners die the way everyone in the Rimevegr dies — badly, and earlier than they should. The difference is specificity. A sword-carrier dies of steel. A farmer dies of cold. A practitioner dies of the practice, and the practice kills in ways that are particular to each tradition and unmistakable to anyone who has seen the pattern before.

**Galdr-workers' deaths** follow two tracks. The first is sudden: accidental galdr-backlash. A rank-4 or rank-5 effect that fails in cascade — the rune-channel opens, the mark does not hold, the force that should have been directed into the wood or stone or iron redirects into the body. The result depends on the rank. A rank-4 backlash can shatter the carving hand. A rank-5 backlash can stop the heart. The failure rates at rank 4 are documented in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, and those rates apply every time a rank-4 effect is attempted, with no allowance for experience. A galdr-worker who has cast a thousand rank-4 effects is no safer than one who has cast ten. The rune does not care who holds the chisel.

The second track is slow: accumulated physical toll. The scarring that begins in the hands and arms in the working years continues to spread through the body in later life. The nerve damage that produces tremor in the hands extends to the arms, the shoulders, the spine. White-haired at forty. Hands shaking at forty-five. The body ages faster than the years — a fifty-year-old galdr-worker has the body of a sixty-five-year-old, and the discrepancy widens each year. Most galdr-workers die before sixty. Heart failure is common — the rune-channels stress the cardiovascular system in ways that no healer fully understands. Stroke is common. Simple collapse after a major working is common.

Stopping practice is not an escape. A galdr-worker who stops carving is a galdr-worker who has removed the purpose from a body that has been shaped by that purpose since childhood. The rune-hunger does not end when the practice ends. The hands shake not because they are carving but because the channels are open and there is nothing flowing through them. The body was built to conduct rune-force, and without that conduction, it shakes itself apart. Stopping is its own kind of death — slower, quieter, and arguably crueler, because the galdr-worker is conscious for all of it. A galdr-worker who stops is nobody. The name stays. The skill stays as knowledge. The identity leaves, because the identity was the carving, and without the carving there is a person-shaped absence where a practitioner used to be.

**Seiðr-wives' deaths** are stranger and harder to count. Many die during trance. The reaching goes out and does not come back. The spirit — the seiðr-wife's awareness, her self, whatever word fits the thing that goes through the Veil during trance — goes out and stays out. The body continues. This is the detail that makes seiðr-death different from any other kind: the body does not stop immediately. It continues breathing. It remains warm. The heart beats. For hours, sometimes days, the body maintains itself the way a fire maintains itself after the person who built it has left. And then it stops. No spasm, no struggle. The breath spaces out and ceases. The heart slows and stops. The body cools. The seiðr-wife has been dead since the moment the spirit did not return; the body simply took time to notice.

Others die of accumulated spirit-damage — the gradual erosion of the boundary between self and not-self that Section 8's decline subsection describes in its early stages. In its late stages, the erosion is total. The seiðr-wife becomes the dead. Not a specific dead person — the dead in general. She walks among the living and does not recognize them. She speaks, but the words are answers to questions the living did not ask. She eats if food is placed before her. She sleeps if led to a bed. But the person who inhabited the body has thinned to a membrane, and the membrane has torn, and what comes through is not any identifiable spirit but the aggregate presence of everything on the other side. She walks until she forgets to walk. She breathes until she forgets to breathe. It takes weeks. It is the worst way a practitioner can die, because nobody can tell when the dying started, and the woman who is dying does not know she is dying, because the woman is no longer there to know anything.

**Wyrd-readers' deaths** are the most mundane. They die of the same things everyone in the Rimevegr dies of — cold, steel, sickness, age. The practice does not kill them directly. The bone lots do not scar the hands. The readings do not thin the mind. A wyrd-reader's body is no more damaged at fifty than a non-practitioner's body.

But they see it coming. A wyrd-reader who has practiced for thirty years reads their own death in the lots. Not the exact day, not the specific mechanism — but the season. The direction. The shape. They throw the lots one morning and the configuration says winter, and they know this is the last autumn, and they have weeks to prepare. Whether preparation helps depends on whether the death allows preparation. A death by sickness can be prepared for — affairs settled, final words spoken, the body positioned for proper rites. A death by steel comes too fast for preparation to matter. A death by cold means the preparation failed, because preparation for cold death is survival, and survival means not dying.

Most deaths do not allow preparation. The wyrd-reader knows this. The lots told them that too.

Torben — Petra's teacher, the shepherd who read weather and fate from the same set of signs — died in a late-spring blizzard when Petra was nineteen. He had read the blizzard two days before it arrived. He told Petra to take the flock south. He did not go with them. She asked why, and he said the things an old man says when the lots have told him what they have told him. She took the flock. He stayed. They found his body when the snow melted. He was sitting upright against a rock, facing north, with his bone lots arranged in front of him in a pattern Petra has never been able to read. She kept the lots. She has never thrown that particular configuration. She is not sure she wants to learn what it says.

**Practitioner funerals** are more elaborate than ordinary death-rites, and the reason is practical, not ceremonial. The Rimevegr's standard death-rites — see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` for the rune-marking protocols — are designed to prevent the dead from returning. For ordinary dead, the rites work reliably. For practitioners, they are insufficient.

A galdr-worker's body carries residual rune-force. The channels that conducted power during life do not close cleanly at death. The body hums for days after death, producing faint vibrations that any trained galdr-worker can detect and that some untrained people can feel as a wrongness in the air near the corpse. If the standard death-rites are applied, the residual force interferes with the binding marks, weakening them at exactly the points where they need to be strongest. A galdr-worker buried with standard rites has a significantly higher return rate than an ordinary corpse — estimated at three to five times higher in the Deepholm records, though the data is sparse.

A seiðr-wife's spirit is already half through the Veil at the time of death. The trance-work of a lifetime has thinned the barrier to the point where death — which is itself a passage through the Veil — is barely a transition. The spirit does not settle on the far side. It lingers. It drifts. It returns to the places it knew in life, not out of malice but out of habit, the way a living person returns to a familiar chair. If the death-rites are insufficient, the seiðr-wife's spirit returns not as a draugr — not as a mindless revenant — but as something closer to a living presence. Aware. Purposeful. Capable of the work she did in life, including reaching through the Veil and pulling others through.

A returned practitioner is worse than a returned warrior for one specific reason: the returned practitioner still remembers how to work. A returned warrior has strength and rage and the residue of combat skill. A returned galdr-worker has rune-knowledge, and residual rune-force, and the ability to carve, and the marks a dead galdr-worker carves are not wards. They are invitations. A returned seiðr-wife has the ability to reach, and a dead seiðr-wife who reaches does not pull information from the other side of the Veil. She pulls people.

The enhanced death-rites for practitioners involve triple the standard rune-marking — every binding mark carved and then cross-bound with a secondary set of marks that specifically target the residual force. The body is treated with salt and specific herbs before the rites are applied. The burial site is warded independently of the settlement ward network, creating a contained zone that any future galdr-worker can check without approaching the body. These rites take a full day to perform. They require a galdr-worker — which means that a settlement whose only galdr-worker has died is a settlement that cannot properly bury its own practitioner. The implications of this are left to the reader to calculate.

_Practitioners die the way they lived: specifically, painfully, and in ways that make the non-practitioners around them uncomfortable. The deaths are not tragic in the narrative sense — the Rimevegr does not have room for tragedy, which requires the expectation of something better. They are factual. The body kept the score, and the score came due, and the books are closed. What remains is the work the practitioner did while they could still do it. The wards Ash carves this season will outlast him. The healings Dalla performs today will keep men alive who will forget her name. The readings Petra gives this morning will inform decisions whose consequences will unfold without her. The practitioner's lifecycle is the lifecycle of all useful things: made, used, worn out. The Rimevegr does not mourn its tools. It replaces them. And when it cannot replace them, it does without, and the gap where the tool was becomes a hole that the cold comes through._

### The Unnamed Problem — What Happens When There Are Not Enough

The numbers are not written down because nobody in the Rimevegr counts practitioners the way they count livestock or silver. There is no census. There is no registry. A settlement either has a galdr-worker or it does not, and the jarl who runs the settlement knows which it is, and that is the extent of the data collection.

But the pattern is visible to anyone who looks. And a few people have looked.

**The decline is real.** The Rimevegr's practitioner population is shrinking. Not dramatically — not a collapse, not yet — but a slow, generation-over-generation contraction that has been underway since the Cracking and has accelerated in the last century.

The galdr tradition keeps the best records because Deepholm keeps records. The archive scrolls show that three centuries ago, before the Cracking, approximately one in four hundred people in the Rimevegr had some galdr talent. One in eight hundred was a trained, practicing galdr-worker. Today, the best estimate — and it is an estimate, compiled from the observations of galdr-workers who travel and compare notes — is one in six hundred for talent and one in fifteen hundred for trained practitioners. The trained population has halved. The talent pool has contracted by a third.

For seiðr, the data is worse because the data barely exists. Seiðr-wives do not maintain archives. They maintain relationships, and when the relationships break — when a teacher dies without a student, when a community drives out its seiðr-wife, when the knowledge that would have been passed on is not passed on — the loss is total and invisible. Nobody writes it down because nobody who remains knows what was lost. The best estimate is that the number of practicing seiðr-wives in the Rimevegr has dropped by more than half since the Cracking. Some regions have none. The Icebreak coast, which once supported a seiðr tradition strong enough to sustain three or four active practitioners per major settlement, now has Ragnhild. When Ragnhild dies, the Icebreak seiðr tradition dies with her unless one of her current students survives the training and stays in the region. The odds of both of those conditions being met are not favorable.

Wyrd-reading has contracted less sharply because the training cost is lower and the manifestation rate is higher. But even wyrd-reading is declining, because the pipeline that produces wyrd-readers depends on older readers recognizing talent in younger people and teaching them, and the number of older readers is dropping for the same reason everything else is dropping: the Cracking broke the infrastructure that used to support magical practice, and nothing has replaced it.

**The reasons are structural.** Three forces are killing the supply.

First: the physical cost is too high. Before the Cracking, divine power supplemented the personal cost of magical practice. A galdr-worker channeled rune-force through channels that the gods maintained. The channels are still there, but the maintenance stopped three centuries ago, and the deterioration is cumulative. Every year, the channels carry less and the body pays more. A rank-3 galdr effect that cost a moderate physical toll in the age before the Cracking now costs a significant one. The cost increase discourages new practitioners. A parent who watches the village galdr-worker age twenty years in ten is not eager to send their talented child into the same trade. The talent goes untrained. The supply drops.

Second: the social position is too dangerous. Practitioners occupy a space in Rimevegr society that is necessary and feared, useful and suspected, paid and despised. The galdr-worker wards the settlement and the settlement cannot do without the wards, but the galdr-worker is still the person who handles forces that nobody else can see. The seiðr-wife is the healer and the spirit speaker, and she is also the woman who talks to the dead, and the dead are not popular. The wyrd-reader tells the future, and nobody wants to know the future when the future is likely to be worse than the present. A child who manifests in a supportive community may be trained. A child who manifests in a suspicious community will be suppressed. Most communities are suspicious. The pipeline loses candidates at the intake.

Third: the Cracking broke the pipeline itself. Before the gods went silent, magical training was supported by institutions — the temple complexes, the formal rune-schools, the seiðr networks that linked practitioners across the Rimevegr. Those institutions depended on divine patronage for legitimacy and divine power for resources. When the patronage ended and the power diminished, the institutions collapsed. What remains are fragments: Deepholm's workshop, Skaldhaven's archive, isolated teachers like Ragnhild. These fragments cannot produce practitioners at the rate the old institutions did. They produce a few per generation, where the old system produced dozens.

**What happens when a settlement loses its last practitioner.** This is not a hypothetical. It happened to three settlements in the Uppmark region within the last fifteen years. The pattern is consistent.

The galdr-worker dies or leaves. The wards are not maintained. Within two to three years, the perimeter weakens to the point where the dead begin testing it. Small incursions first — a shade at the edge of the settlement, a draugr at the outlying barrow that was supposed to stay sealed. The settlement cannot address the incursions because addressing them requires a galdr-worker, and there is no galdr-worker. They hire a traveling one, if they can find one and if they can afford one. The traveling galdr-worker patches the worst gaps and leaves, because they have a home settlement that is also losing wards. The patches hold for a season. Then they fail. The settlement either abandons or contracts — pulling the inhabited area inward, leaving the outer buildings to the dark, accepting that the perimeter is smaller and the margins are tighter and the next loss will be harder to absorb.

The same pattern applies to unions and regional governance. An Allthing requires warded stones — the assembly site must be protected from spiritual interference because legal proceedings conducted in the presence of the unquiet dead are not valid. A union whose Allthing stones are not properly warded is a union whose legal system is degrading. This has already happened in two minor unions in the eastern Rimevegr. The lawspeakers do not speak about it publicly. They continue holding ting, but they know the wards are thin, and they know that the next time a difficult case draws the attention of the dead, the protections may not hold.

**The timeline** is the part nobody has calculated, because nobody wants to know the answer.

At current rates of attrition — practitioner deaths exceeding new practitioner training by roughly three to two — and current rates of recruitment — one new galdr-worker for every two who die, one new seiðr-wife for every three, wyrd-readers roughly sustaining but not growing — the Rimevegr has perhaps two generations before the magical infrastructure fails irreversibly.

Two generations. Forty to sixty years. That is the window.

After that window closes, the ward networks will not be maintainable. Not because the wards have failed — the physical marks will still be there — but because there will not be enough practitioners to maintain them. A settlement whose wards decay and cannot be restored is a settlement open to the dead. A region whose settlements are open to the dead is a region that is no longer habitable. The dead do not push in all at once. They seep. They accumulate. They build in the spaces where the wards used to be, the way water builds behind a dam that is cracking one fissure at a time.

The data exists to prove this. The Deepholm archive contains three centuries of records — practitioner counts, ward-failure rates, recruitment figures. Skaldhaven's sealed records hold the theoretical framework — the mathematical models that the early post-Cracking galdr scholars built to predict the decay curve. Nobody has compiled the data from Deepholm and the models from Skaldhaven. Nobody has run the calculation. The archives are a thousand miles apart and neither institution shares willingly.

But the calculation does not require both archives. Anyone with access to either set of records, and the mathematical competence to project the curves, would arrive at the same answer within a margin of a few years. Two generations. Three at the outside, if recruitment improves. One and a half, if a major event — a war, a plague, a particularly bad Long Dark — kills a significant fraction of the current practitioner population in a single blow.

Nobody has done this calculation because nobody wants to know the answer. The jarls do not want to know because the answer means their grandchildren will inherit indefensible settlements. The practitioners do not want to know because the answer means their work is a rearguard action against an outcome that is already determined. The lawspeakers do not want to know because the answer means the legal infrastructure they maintain is temporary.

The people who are closest to understanding the problem are the practitioners themselves — the Ashes and Dallas who feel the cost increasing year by year, who sense something draining that cannot be refilled, who do the work and watch the work diminish and do not need an archive scroll to tell them what the body already knows. But practitioners are not analysts. They are workers. They solve the problem in front of them — the next ward, the next healing, the next reading — and they do not step back to see the pattern because the pattern is too large and too frightening and because seeing the pattern does not change the pattern.

---

The encoded mysteries track this in acute form. MAGIC_TRUTH_003 documents the accelerating cost. The Cracking broke the world three centuries ago. The practitioner shortage is the world noticing — reacting, adjusting, failing — three centuries late. The acute crisis, if it comes, will not look like the Cracking. It will look like a long winter during which the wards do not hold and there is nobody left to repair them, and the dead walk through every door, and the living discover that the barrier between their world and the next was always maintained by a handful of strange, damaged people who heard things and saw things and read things that nobody else could, and those people are gone.

_The practitioner's lifecycle is the Rimevegr's lifecycle, compressed and accelerated. Birth, growth, the working years, decline, death. The Rimevegr went through these stages. The gods were born into the world. The runes grew. The working years produced the civilization that exists now — the wards, the unions, the laws, the trade routes, the settlements that survive in a land that should not support survival. The decline began with the Cracking and has continued for three hundred years. The death has not arrived yet. But the lots have been thrown, and the configuration is readable, and the reading is not ambiguous. The Rimevegr's magic is dying the way its practitioners die: slowly, specifically, in ways that are visible to anyone willing to look and invisible to everyone who is not. And the ones who are willing to look are the same ones who are dying. And they are not being replaced._
