## The Rune-Tongue of Rimevegr

The runes are older than the Cracking. They were cut into stone before the gods went silent, and they still work — diminished, unreliable, hungry — in the three centuries since. This section catalogues the runes as the galdr-workers of the Rimevegr know them: not as a tidy alphabet of power, but as a dying language spoken by people who learned it from people who learned it from people who could still hear the gods correcting their pronunciation.

Every rune has two faces. The galdr-workers call them _dagmál_ (day-speech) and _náttmál_ (night-speech). The day-speech is what the rune means when the world is working as intended: when the wards hold, when the fire burns orange, when the dead stay down. The night-speech is what the rune means when the Veil is close, when the air tastes of iron, when the carver's hands move in patterns they did not choose.

The distinction is not metaphorical. A rune carved during daylight hours with clear intent and steady hands produces the dagmál effect. The same rune carved in the Long Dark, near a Veil-thinning, by a galdr-worker whose fingers have gone numb and whose breath clouds blue — that rune reaches for the náttmál. The carver does not always choose which face answers. At rank 1 and 2, the carver controls the voice. At rank 3 and above, the rune begins to have opinions.

This is the part that the casual practitioners miss and the masters understand too well: the runes are not tools. Tools do not care how you hold them. The runes care. They respond to context — the season, the Veil's proximity, the carver's state of mind, the blood on the chisel. A rune carved in grief cuts differently than a rune carved in anger, and both cut differently than a rune carved in the flat, affectless state that the Veil-touched carry like a second skin.

### Rune Scribing — The Physical Act

Galdr is not abstract. It begins with material: a chisel, a surface, a steady hand, and something to say. The galdr-worker selects the medium — stone for permanence, bone for intimacy with death, wood for temporary work, iron for war. The medium matters because the rune reads the material the way a singer reads a hall: the acoustics change the song.

The carving stroke follows the rune's grain. Every galdr-worker describes this identically — the sensation that the correct line already exists inside the material and the chisel's job is to release it. A wrong stroke resists. The wrist aches. The chisel catches. The rune buzzes with a low-frequency irritation that travels through the hand and lodges behind the eyes. A correct stroke offers no sensation at all. Absence of resistance. The rune finishes itself.

At rank 3 and above, the carver's hands are not entirely their own. The rune demands a shape, and the shape may not be what the carver intended. This is where the failure cascade begins (see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`). Fizzle means the rune gave up on the carver. Backlash means the carver fought the shape and the shape won. Inversion means the rune decided what it wanted to say and said it regardless of what the carver had planned.

The physical marks accumulate. Thin white lines score the palms — not cuts, not scars, but frost-patterns that emerge over months of practice. The fingertips go numb, then cold, then permanently frostbitten at the tips even in summer. A master galdr-worker's hands look like birch bark: white-lined, stiff, cracked at the joints. The runes write themselves on the carver while the carver writes them on the world.

### The Canonical Rune-Set

Twenty-four runes survive in active use across the Rimevegr. A further ten are known from saga-fragments, barrow inscriptions, and the sealed records at Skaldhaven and Deepholm. The twenty-four are listed here with their dagmál and náttmál meanings. The ten expansion runes follow.

The rune-names are the pre-Cracking pronunciations preserved in oral tradition. Some galdr-workers dispute the vowels. Nobody disputes the shapes — the physical forms are carved into standing stones across the Rimevegr and have not changed in five centuries.

### Fé

**Dagmál (day-speech):** Wealth. Cattle. Stored value. Property. The honest accumulation of a household that has survived another season. Fé in its daylight face means the herd is fed, the storehouse is full, and the jarl's silver-arm weighs what it should.

**Náttmál (night-speech):** Hoarded power. Inheritance seized before the body is cold. Wealth that poisons the hand that holds it. Fé in the dark means avarice — the sickness of a man who counts his grain while his neighbor's children starve, and the grain counts him back. The rune whispers: _what you own owns you._ A night-carved Fé on a rival's door curses his wealth to rot from within — the cattle sicken, the silver tarnishes, the stored grain fills with weevils that have too many legs.

### Úr

**Dagmál:** The aurochs. Strength. Raw endurance. The body's animal refusal to stop breathing. Úr is the rune carved on shield-rims and doorframes, the brute promise that this thing will not break today.

**Náttmál:** Beast-will. The strength that outlasts the mind controlling it. Úr in the dark is the aurochs that does not know when to stop charging — muscle and bone moving after the hugr has gone quiet. Night-carved Úr on a weapon makes the wielder stronger than he should be, and the strength stays after the fighting stops, and the man cannot put the weapon down, and his hands remember the killing grip for days. Berserker-smiths carve Úr by firelight. They do not carve it twice on the same blade.

---

### Þurs

**Dagmál:** Thorn. Active defense. Giant-force. The dangerous protection of a threshold that will hurt whatever crosses it. Ward-posts carved with Þurs are the standard perimeter defense of every settlement in the Rimevegr.

**Náttmál:** Hostile intrusion. Violent rejection. The thorn that grows inward. Night-carved Þurs does not merely defend — it attacks whatever approaches, including the people it was meant to protect. A ward-post with inverted Þurs has been found at three abandoned settlements in the eastern waste. In each case, the inhabitants were found dead inside the perimeter, killed by their own defenses. The wards held perfectly. Nothing got in. Nothing got out.

---

### Kaun

**Dagmál:** Fire. The forge. Transformation through controlled burning. Kaun is the smith's rune — the understanding that destruction and creation are the same act performed at different temperatures.

**Náttmál:** Punitive suffering. Cleansing pain. Fire that chooses what to burn. Night-carved Kaun on a man's threshold does not set the house ablaze — it burns what the man loves most and leaves the rest standing. A galdr-worker in Skaldhaven used Kaun náttmál to burn every written record of a merchant's debts while the merchant's hall stood untouched. The merchant was ruined. The galdr-worker's hands smelled of smoke for a year.

### Óss

**Dagmál:** Speech. Authority. Wisdom. The voice that carries weight because the speaker has earned the right to be heard. Óss is carved on law-stones and speaker's staffs and the posts of every ting in the Rimevegr.

**Náttmál:** Command language. The voice that compels because the listener cannot resist. Omen-speech — words that become true because they were spoken aloud. Night-carved Óss on a speaker's staff makes every word from that podium land like a verdict, regardless of truth. The Lawspeaker at Icebreak ordered every Óss night-staff in the settlement destroyed after a dispute was settled by a man who was wrong but whose words tasted right in every listener's mouth. Three of the five were found. The other two are still missing.

### Reið

**Dagmál:** Riding. Motion. The journey that has a destination. Safe passage. Reið on a traveler's staff smooths the road and shortens the distance and keeps the wolves at bay.

**Náttmál:** Forced movement. Pursuit that cannot be escaped. Route-breaking — the destruction of a path so that it cannot be traveled again. Night-carved Reið on a boundary stone means anyone who crosses that line walks in circles until they collapse or until the rune is broken. Outlaw bands have used Reið náttmál to trap pursuit parties in loops of forest that should take an hour but take three days. The pursuit party's horses refuse to eat afterward.

### Gjöf

**Dagmál:** Gift. Trust. Reciprocity. The exchange that binds two people into obligation. Gjöf is the rune of hospitality, carved on guest-cups and marriage beds and the thresholds of halls where the host's word is sacred.

**Náttmál:** Binding favor. Debt that cannot be repaid. The gift that becomes a chain. Night-carved Gjöf on a gift-object means the recipient owes something and does not know what and cannot learn what and the debt grows heavier each day until the debtor does whatever the giver demands just to make the weight stop. The seiðr-wives call this _gjafaþrál_ — gift-thralldom — and they consider it worse than physical slavery because the thrall at least knows who holds the chain.

### Vend

**Dagmál:** Bliss. Joy. The warmth of kinship and the comfort of a hall where the fire burns steady and the ale is passed and nobody is watching the door. Vend is carved on hearthstones and cradles and the posts of feasting halls.

**Náttmál:** Enforced contentment. Joy that masks the rot beneath. Night-carved Vend on a hall's central post makes everyone inside feel safe and warm and loved, and nobody notices that the wards have failed, and nobody checks the perimeter, and the dead that have been gathering at the tree-line for three nights walk through the unwatched doors. Dalla would call this the most dangerous rune in the set, and she would be right, and nobody would listen because they feel so warm.

### Hagall

**Dagmál:** Hail. Disruption. The storm that clears the air and leaves the ground changed. Hagall is transformation through violence — the understanding that some things must be broken before they can be rebuilt.

**Náttmál:** Disaster. Ruin. The storm that does not stop. Night-carved Hagall on a settlement's ward-post invites catastrophe — not a specific disaster, but the _inclination_ toward disaster. Accidents multiply. Joints fail. Livestock stumble into ravines that were not there yesterday. A year of Hagall náttmál does not destroy a settlement. It exhausts one. It makes every day slightly worse than it should be, until the people inside begin to believe that the land itself has turned against them. They are not wrong.

### Nauð

**Dagmál:** Need. Hardship. Endurance under pressure. The grinding survival of the Long Dark, when the food is low and the fire is small and the only virtue left is the stubborn refusal to die. Nauð is carved on ration-posts and survival caches.

**Náttmál:** Compulsion. Need so deep it overrides the will. Night-carved Nauð on a person's belongings makes them need something — anything — with a desperation that has no object. The man does not know what he needs. He only knows the absence is killing him. He will do terrible things to fill it. The outlaws in Chapter 14 who walk toward the Veil are feeling Nauð náttmál whether they know it or not — the need for something the Veil promises and the Veil does not name and the Veil does not deliver.

### Íss

**Dagmál:** Ice. Stillness. The calming of what was turbulent. Íss is the rune of mediation and cooling, carved on fever-stones and placed on wounds and scratched into the posts of halls where tempers run hot.

**Náttmál:** Emotional freezing. Arrested life. The stillness that is not peace but paralysis. Night-carved Íss on a man's hearth-post stops the household — not through violence but through the absence of all motion. The family stops speaking. The children stop playing. The fire burns but gives no warmth. Everyone sits and stares and waits for something that does not come. Settlements near Veil-thinnings report this effect without any rune being carved — the Veil itself is Íss náttmál, the cosmic stillness that the ice-rune merely echoes.

### Ár

**Dagmál:** Year. Harvest. The long cycle that rewards patience. Ár is the rune of seasons and timing, carved on field-stones and granary posts and the boundary-markers of land that has been tended for generations.

**Náttmál:** The cycle that will not break. The harvest that comes due whether you planted or not. Night-carved Ár on an enemy's land means the land remembers every debt, every trespass, every drop of blood, and the year's turning brings the reckoning. An Ár náttmál carved into a battle-site means the battle repeats — not literally, but in the dreams of everyone who sleeps within a day's walk, every year on the anniversary, until someone pays the price the land is owed.

---

### Dagur

**Dagmál:** Day. Brightness. Clarity. The light that reveals what the dark was hiding. Dagur is carved on truth-stones at the ting and on the lintels of halls where honest dealing is expected.

**Náttmál:** Exposure. Scrutiny without mercy. The light that burns away every shadow a man was hiding behind. Night-carved Dagur on a liar's threshold means his secrets walk out of his mouth while he sleeps — he speaks his crimes aloud in the dark, and anyone listening hears the truth he cannot suppress. Three Lawspeakers have used Dagur náttmál in the last century. All three condemned the practice afterward. All three used the evidence.

---

### Yr

**Dagmál:** Yew. The bow. Patience at the edge of mortality. Yr is the rune of the dead — not the restless dead, but the properly dead, the quiet ones, the ancestors who stay where they were put.

**Náttmál:** Death-mark. The arrow that has already been loosed. Yr náttmál carved on a living person's possession means the death is coming and the death knows where to find them. Not murder — the rune does not kill. It removes the small accidents of luck that keep a person alive in the Rimevegr: the foot that lands on solid ground instead of rotten ice, the instinct that says _duck_ a heartbeat before the branch falls. Yr náttmál strips the target of the casual grace that separates the living from the nearly dead. After that, the Rimevegr does the rest.

### Peorð

**Dagmál:** The dice-cup. Chance. Hidden outcome. The uncertainty that makes every decision a wager and every wager a prayer.

**Náttmál:** The loaded die. Fate-shaking — the manipulation of outcome by a hand that should not be in the cup. Night-carved Peorð on a set of gaming lots ensures that the lots always favor the carver, but the favor comes from somewhere, and somewhere is keeping a ledger. Every rigged game borrows luck from the carver's future. A season of cheating at dice costs a year of misfortune — injuries that should not have happened, illnesses that should not have caught, falls on clear ground. The luck-debt compounds. The interest is physical.

---

### Óðal

**Dagmál:** Inheritance. Estate. Homeland. The land that knows your name because your father's father bled into it. Óðal is the deepest rune — the one that ties a family to a place and a place to a family across generations.

**Náttmál:** Ancestral judgment. The land that remembers what the family did. Night-carved Óðal on a family's boundary-stone wakes the inheritance — not the silver and the cattle, but the debts and the crimes and the oaths broken three generations ago that nobody living remembers but the land never forgot. Óðal náttmál is the rune used in the most devastating legal curses in the Rimevegr: the curse that does not punish the man but punishes his line, reaching backward through the dead and forward through the unborn, because the land does not distinguish between the grandfather who sinned and the grandson who inherited.

### Elgur

**Dagmál:** Elk. Protection. The antlered boundary that says _this space is defended_. Elgur is the ward-rune — passive, patient, enduring. Every settlement's inner perimeter carries Elgur marks.

**Náttmál:** Defensive authority that will not let go. The sanctuary that becomes a prison. Night-carved Elgur on a shelter's posts means nothing harmful can enter — and nothing inside can leave. The distinction between protection and imprisonment is a line drawn by intent, and intent is the first thing the Veil erodes. Three cave-shelters in the eastern waste carry Elgur náttmál carved by outlaws who wanted safety. The outlaws are still inside. The rune held. They starved.

---

### Sól

**Dagmál:** Sun. Wholeness. Vitality. The revelation that comes with light and the health that comes with warmth. Sól is carved on healing-stones and recovery beds and the east-facing posts of every longhouse.

**Náttmál:** Total exposure. Life-force that burns what it touches. Sól náttmál is the light that has no shadow — the scrutiny that does not reveal truth but obliterates privacy. A person under Sól náttmál cannot hide, cannot rest, cannot close their eyes without the brightness finding them. Seiðr-wives use Sól náttmál to force a spirit to manifest — the dead cannot hide in the dark when the dark is burning — but the light damages everything it touches, living and dead alike. There is a reason the sun sets. Sól náttmál forgets that reason.

---

### Týr

**Dagmál:** Law. Sacrifice. Justice. The warrior's honor that says _I will pay the price of what is right_. Týr is carved on oath-stones and weapon-hilts and the pillars of every ting in the Rimevegr.

**Náttmál:** Binding judgment. The law that has no mercy. Týr náttmál carved on an oath-breaker's weapon means the weapon turns — not physically, not visibly, but at the moment of crisis, the blade finds the wrong target, the shield drops a heartbeat too soon, the arm does not answer. Warriors who carry Týr-cursed weapons do not die in battle. They die in the pause after battle, when the adrenaline fades and they discover the wound they did not feel and the wound is from their own blade.

---

### Bjarkan

**Dagmál:** Birch. Growth. Recovery. The green returning after the Long Dark. Bjarkan is carved on cradles and sick-beds and the posts of midwifery halls.

**Náttmál:** Growth that does not know when to stop. Recovery that overshoots. Night-carved Bjarkan on a wound makes the flesh close faster than it should, and the flesh that grows is not quite right — too thick, too pale, too warm to the touch. The seiðr-wives call Bjarkan náttmál healing _lífsvǫxtr_ (life- overgrowth) and they warn against it: the body heals, but the body changes, and the change is not reversible. A man healed three times by Bjarkan náttmál has skin that moves independently of the muscle beneath it, as though the skin has developed its own intentions.

---

### Maður

**Dagmál:** Mankind. Community. The social order that separates the human from the beast. Maður is carved on assembly-posts and treaty-stones and the doors of halls where disputes are settled by words instead of iron.

**Náttmál:** The crowd that turns. Public standing as a weapon. Night-carved Maður on a man's property makes the community forget him — not his name, not his face, but the instinct to include him. He speaks and no one responds. He enters a room and no one looks up. He calls for help and the sound arrives but the urgency does not. Social erasure. The outlaws call it _dreaming alive_ — being present in a room full of people who cannot perceive that you are present. It is worse than exile because exile is a decision. This is an oversight that never corrects.

---

### Eykur

**Dagmál:** Horse. Loyal motion. The partnership between rider and mount that makes distance manageable. Eykur is carved on saddles and harnesses and the gate-posts of steadings.

**Náttmál:** Momentum that cannot be stopped. The horse that will not halt. Night-carved Eykur on a mount means the horse runs and the rider cannot slow it and the destination is chosen by the rune, not the rider. Three riders in the Frostmark disappeared on Eykur-cursed horses. One was found two weeks later, ninety miles north of his intended path, the horse dead under him and his hands fused to the reins by dried blood and cold. He could not explain where he had been. He could not explain why the horse's eyes were open.

---

### Lögur

**Dagmál:** Water. Flow. Memory. The depth that holds what the surface has forgotten. Lögur is carved on wells and boat-keels and the stones placed at river-crossings.

**Náttmál:** Drowning memory. The current that pulls the mind under. Night-carved Lögur on a drinking vessel makes the drinker remember — not what they choose to remember, but what the water chooses. The worst thing. The thing they buried. The face they cannot look at. The sound of the ice breaking under someone they loved. Lögur náttmál is used by seiðr-wives during interrogation: a cup of water, a carved rune, and the prisoner drinks and weeps and tells you everything because the memory will not stop until the truth comes out.

---

### Ing

**Dagmál:** Fertility. Peace. Health. Safety. The blessing of a household that is growing as it should. Ing is carved on marriage-beds and birthing-stools and the hearth-stones of halls where children are expected.

**Náttmál:** Safeguarded growth that serves the wrong master. Night-carved Ing on a pregnant woman's threshold ensures the child survives — ensures it with a ferocity that overrides everything else. The mother may not survive. The household may not survive. The child _will_, because the rune has decided, and the rune's priority is the new life, and everything that is not the new life is fuel. The midwives of the Rimevegr refuse to carve Ing without daylight and steady hands. The stories of what happens when Ing náttmál takes hold during a difficult birth are not told in public.

### The Expansion Runes

Ten additional runes survive in fragments — barrow inscriptions, sealed Skaldhaven records, the oral traditions of remote settlements. They are not universally known. A galdr-worker may know three or four of them. Nobody living knows all ten. Their inclusion here is a reconstruction, assembled from multiple sources, and some of the náttmál meanings are speculative.

---

### Nið

**Dagmál:** Shame. Disgrace. The public naming of what a man has done wrong. Nið is the rune of social accountability — the carved declaration that says _this person has failed and the community knows_.

**Náttmál:** Social death. The shame that eats the name. Nið náttmál carved on a man's door does not merely humiliate — it unmakes his social existence. His name loses weight. His oaths lose binding force. His word at the ting becomes noise. The guðníðing described in Chapter 14 carve Nið into their own flesh, deliberately, as the first step of the god-scorned path. They are writing their own erasure.

---

### Hrafn

**Dagmál:** Raven. Omen. Battlefield intelligence. The bird that sees the dead before the living know they have fallen. Hrafn is carved on scout-staffs and message-poles.

**Náttmál:** Carrion-vision. The sight that cannot stop seeing the dead. Night-carved Hrafn on a warrior's helm gives him the raven's perspective — he sees the battlefield as a field of meat, and the living are merely meat that has not stopped moving yet. The sight persists after the battle ends. The warrior looks at his companions and sees future corpses. He looks at children and sees small corpses. The raven's gift is truth. The truth is that everything alive is a body that does not yet know it is a body. The sight fades in days. The knowledge does not.

---

### Blóð

**Dagmál:** Blood. Kinship. Sacrifice. The price paid in the only currency the gods ever respected. Blóð is carved on oath-rings and sacrifice-altars and the stones where blood- brotherhood is sworn.

**Náttmál:** Blood-debt. The price that never finishes being paid. Night-carved Blóð on a compact between two parties means the betrayer bleeds — not metaphorically, not symbolically. The wounds open without a blade. Small at first: nosebleeds, bleeding gums, cuts that reopen. Then larger. A woman in the Uppmark broke a Blóð-sealed trade agreement and bled from her eyes for three days. She honored the agreement. The bleeding stopped. She never trusted a galdr-worker again.

---

### Móðr

**Dagmál:** Fury. Courage. Battle-temper. The red heat that makes a man stand when his body wants to fall. Móðr is the warrior's rune, carved on weapon-hilts and shield-bosses.

**Náttmál:** Wrath without direction. The fury that does not know friend from enemy. Night-carved Móðr on a warrior's weapon makes him impossible to cow and impossible to control. The berserker state — Úr for the body, Móðr for the spirit — is the most feared rune-combination in the Rimevegr. The warriors who carry both do not come back. Not dead. They come back _wrong_. The fury does not leave when the battle ends. It lives in the muscle memory. It lives in the dreams. It lives in the way the man's hand tightens on the haft when someone raises their voice in the hall.

---

### Mórk

**Dagmál:** Boundary. Marked land. Threshold. The line between this and that, here and there, ours and theirs. Mórk is the foundation of property law in the Rimevegr — carved on every legal boundary-stone.

**Náttmál:** The boundary that seals. The edge that cuts. Night- carved Mórk on a settlement's perimeter means the perimeter becomes absolute — not a ward but a wall, not a defense but a severance. Everything inside the boundary is one world. Everything outside is another. The two do not communicate. Messages do not cross. Sound does not carry. The settlement exists in a pocket of silence, and the people inside do not know the outside world still exists, and the outside world forgets the settlement was ever there.

---

### Steinn

**Dagmál:** Stone. Permanence. The mound. What lasts when everything else has rotted. Steinn is carved on grave-markers and foundation-stones and the pillars of halls meant to stand for generations.

**Náttmál:** The immovable curse. The weight that holds the target in place. Night-carved Steinn on a man's boots means his feet will not carry him from the spot where he stands. Not paralysis — the muscles work, the joints bend — but the ground will not release him. He pulls and the earth holds. Steinn náttmál is used to anchor barrow-wards: the dead who are Steinn-bound cannot rise because the stone remembers them as stone and the stone will not let go. When Steinn-wards fail after centuries, the draugr that emerge have skin like granite and joints that crack like breaking rock.

---

### Salt

**Dagmál:** Preservation. Cleansing. Anti-corruption. Salt is the rune of purity — the carving that keeps the rot at bay and the boundary between clean and unclean intact.

**Náttmál:** Preservation without mercy. The refusal to let the dead be dead. Night-carved Salt on a corpse keeps the body fresh indefinitely — skin supple, eyes clear, no bloating, no stink. The body looks asleep. It is not asleep. It is dead, perfectly dead, and the perfection is obscene. Families have kept Salt- preserved bodies for years, unable to grieve because the evidence of death is absent. The rope-readers insist on burning any body found with Salt náttmál carved into the burial wrappings. The families resist. The argument never ends well.

---

### Mjǫðr

**Dagmál:** Mead. Poetry. Inspiration. The word-gift that transforms speech into something that matters. Mjǫðr is the skald's rune, carved on drinking-horns and speaker's posts.

**Náttmál:** Intoxicating insight. The truth that poisons the speaker. Night-carved Mjǫðr gives the speaker eloquence that comes from somewhere deeper than training — the words arrive fully formed, devastating in their precision, impossible to argue against. The speaker does not compose the speech. The speech composes itself, using the speaker as a vessel. Afterward, the speaker cannot remember what they said. Others can. Others always remember. The speech changes them. It changes the speaker too, but the speaker does not know how, because the speech has erased the memory of its own delivery. Skaldhaven's archive contains fourteen transcribed Mjǫðr-speeches. Reading them is restricted. The restriction is not political. It is medical.

---

### Skuggi

**Dagmál:** Shadow. Concealment. The ambiguity of things half- seen. Skuggi is carved on thief-tools and spy-staffs and the cloaks of men who need to be elsewhere.

**Náttmál:** Hidden identity. The doubled self. Night-carved Skuggi on a man's cloak does not make him invisible — it makes him _someone else_. Witnesses see a person. They describe a person. The description is consistent and detailed and completely wrong. The man they describe does not exist. Skuggi náttmál is the forgery of presence — a false person layered over the real one like frost over stone. The false person persists in memory even after the rune is broken. Witnesses swear they saw someone who was never born.

---

### Hlið

**Dagmál:** Gate. Passage. Threshold crossing. Hlið is the rune of entry and exit — carved on door-frames and bridge-posts and the entrance-stones of halls where guests are expected.

**Náttmál:** The sealed crossing. The door that opens one way. Night-carved Hlið on a doorway means anyone may enter but no one may leave — or anyone may leave but no one may enter, depending on which face of the frame the rune is carved. Outlaws have used Hlið náttmál to trap pursuit parties inside buildings: the door opens inward, the pursuit enters, the door does not open outward. The galdr-worker walks away. The building becomes a box.

---

### Mál

**Dagmál:** Speech. Measure. Legal speech. Agreement. Mál is the rune of the lawspeaker — the spoken word that carries the weight of decision.

**Náttmál:** Verdict without trial. The word that becomes law because it was spoken in the right voice at the right time with the right rune on the right post. Night-carved Mál on a ting- stone means the first person to speak from that stone speaks binding truth — not their opinion, not their argument, but the underlying reality of the dispute, dragged into the open whether the parties want it or not. Both sides leave destroyed. Justice is served. Nobody is happy. This is what justice looks like in the Rimevegr: a verdict that punishes everyone and satisfies no one and is, nonetheless, correct.

---

### Vetr

**Dagmál:** Winter. Endurance. Survival under the weight of cold. Vetr is carved on storehouse doors and winter-camp markers and the walking-staves of people who travel the Long Dark.

**Náttmál:** The season that does not end. Patient ruin. Night- carved Vetr on a settlement's well means winter touches the water — not ice, but cold that persists into the Long Summer. The crops falter. The ground stays hard. The livestock huddle. Vetr náttmál is the Veil's favorite rune, and the galdr-workers know this, and they carve it last and erase it first and they handle it the way a smith handles a crucible of molten iron: quickly, carefully, and with deep respect for what happens when it spills.

## Galdr Vocalization — The Throat-Singing

The rune carved into material is half the working. The other half is the voice.

Galdr is not spoken. It is not sung in the way a skald sings or a mother hums a lullaby. Galdr is _throat-voiced_ — a guttural, resonant, compressed vibration that originates in the chest and exits the throat as something between speech and growl. The galdr-worker does not choose the pitch. The pitch chooses itself, rising from the gut the way the rune's shape rises from the material. The correct tone is the one that makes the carved line glow. The incorrect tone is the one that makes the carver's nose bleed.

The old galdr manuals — fragments survive at Skaldhaven — describe the voice as _rúnamunnr_ (rune-mouth): the physical apparatus that translates carved symbol into activated power. The carving is the letter. The voice is the breath that makes the letter a word. Without the voice, the rune is dormant. With the wrong voice, the rune inverts. With the right voice, the rune remembers what it was meant to do and does it, and the world around the carver shifts to accommodate the demand.

### What the Singing Does to the Singer

The physical effects accumulate. Short-term, a galdr-working leaves the singer's throat raw, the voice hoarse, and the chest aching as though the ribs have been compressed. The taste of copper persists for hours. The breath clouds even in warm air, as though the body is exhaling something colder than the surrounding temperature.

Long-term, the throat changes. A galdr-worker of ten years has a speaking voice that carries a permanent undertone — not a pitch, but a texture, as though the vocal cords have developed a second resonance that runs beneath the normal voice. People hear it without identifying it. It makes them uneasy. Children cry. Dogs lower their ears and back away. The galdr-worker sounds _inhabited_ — as though the voice is not entirely one voice.

At rank 4 and above, the throat-singing produces involuntary effects on listeners. The galdr is not aimed at them — the rune is the target, the carving is the focus — but the vibration spills. The overflow manifests as:

**Dark emotional resonance.** Listeners within earshot experience a sudden, sourceless shift in mood. Not fear exactly — deeper. A memory of grief surfaces without trigger. An old anger flares without cause. The feeling is indistinguishable from genuine emotion, and that is the danger: the listeners believe the grief is theirs, the anger is theirs, and they act on it. A hall full of armed men hearing galdr at rank 4 may erupt into violence not because the galdr targeted them but because the sound woke something in each man that was already there, waiting for permission.

**Compulsive joining.** The most disturbing effect. At rank 4 and above, some listeners begin to sing along. Not consciously. The throat vibrates. The chest resonates. A low hum emerges from the listener's body before the listener's mind has agreed to produce it. The hum harmonizes with the galdr — not perfectly, but enough. In a room of twelve people, three or four will begin humming. They cannot stop until the galdr-worker stops. They do not know they are doing it until someone tells them, and when told, they cannot explain why their body obeyed a sound their mind did not authorize.

**Physical arousal.** The galdr's resonance moves through the body. At certain frequencies — the náttmál register, the low growl that activates the dark-face runes — the vibration triggers involuntary physical responses. Pulse accelerates. Skin flushes. Sexual arousal manifests without stimulus, without desire, without the listener's consent or participation. It is a body responding to a frequency the way a tuning fork responds to its note — mechanically, impersonally, and with no regard for the person inside the body. This effect is most pronounced during extended workings (thirty minutes or longer) and most dangerous during group-galdr, when multiple voices produce harmonic interference that amplifies the base frequency. The old manuals describe this as _holdboð_ — flesh-command — and the galdr-workers who still know the term do not use it casually.

**Numbness and pressure.** Listeners report tingling in the extremities — fingers, toes, lips — followed by numbness that climbs. At thirty minutes of exposure, the hands are insensate. At an hour, the arms. The galdr-worker himself feels this too — the singing numbs the body from the outside in, as though the vibration is replacing nerve-signal with rune-signal. A galdr- worker who has been singing for two hours cannot feel his own hands on the chisel. He carves by muscle memory and by the rune's guidance, and if the rune decides his hand should slip, his hand slips, and he does not feel the blade enter the palm.

**Blood pressure and the divine stroke.** The most severe physical consequence. Extended galdr — rank 4 or 5 workings that last hours — produces symptoms identical to acute hypertension: pounding headache, visual disturbance, nausea, chest pressure, ringing ears. The galdr-workers call this _guðslag_ (god-stroke). It is not metaphorical. Galdr-workers die of it. The throat-singing forces blood pressure upward through a mechanism that the seiðr-wives say is spiritual (the body trying to contain divine energy) and the galdr- workers say is physical (the vibration constricting blood vessels in the throat and chest). Both are probably right.

Guðslag at its worst produces hemorrhagic stroke. The galdr- worker collapses mid-chant. One pupil dilates. The face goes slack on one side. The rune on the stone glows brighter than it should — brighter than any rune the witnesses have seen — and then it goes dark, and the galdr-worker is dead or wishes they were. Survivors of guðslag lose function on one side of the body and lose access to the higher ranks of galdr permanently. Their hands still know the shapes. Their throat still knows the tones. The runes no longer answer. The body paid and the runes took and the runes did not give back.

Three galdr-workers in the last century have died of guðslag during rank 5 workings. Two more survived with permanent disability. The Deepholm archive records each case with clinical precision and does not editorialize, because the Deepholm archivists understand that the data speaks for itself: the most powerful magic in the Rimevegr kills the people who practice it, and the mechanism is indistinguishable from natural disease, and this is either coincidence or design, and neither answer is comforting.

### Poetic Form — The Galdr Chant Structure

Every galdr working should have a voiced form: the _kvæði_ (verse-chant) that the galdr-worker speaks or growls while carving. The kvæði is not decorative. It is the targeting system. The rune carved into stone says _what_. The kvæði says _where_, _when_, _how hard_, and _to whom_.

A well-formed kvæði has four elements:

1. **Name the rune.** Speak the rune's name aloud. This is the invocation — the act that tells the rune it is being called.
2. **State the image.** Give the rune a concrete anchor in the physical world: a place, a person, a thing. The more specific the image, the more precise the effect.
3. **State the change.** Say what the rune should do. Not in abstract terms — in physical, observable, sensory terms.
4. **Leave room.** The kvæði should not close every door. A line of ambiguity — a space for the rune to interpret — is the difference between galdr that works and galdr that works exactly as intended and nothing more.

The tone is guttural, compressed, rhythmic. The lines should feel like they cost something to speak. The Rimevegr's kvæðir sound like commands issued through clenched teeth — not because the carver is angry, but because the voice is carrying weight that vocal cords were not designed to carry.

## Rune-Script Construction — The Grammar of Galdr

Rune magic is a language. Each rune is a word. Combining runes creates a sentence, and the sentence is the spell.

### Script Size and Power

The number of runes in a script determines its scope:

- **3 runes:** A single, clean effect. One sentence. One demand. Most galdr in the Rimevegr is three-rune work.
- **5 runes:** A layered effect. Two sentences that reinforce each other. Rank 3+ galdr-workers use five-rune scripts for complex wards and bindings.
- **7 runes:** Major scene control. The galdr-worker is not asking the world to change — they are telling it. Rank 4 minimum. The kvæði for a seven-rune script takes twenty minutes to chant.
- **9 runes:** Mythic-level working. The old sagas describe nine-rune scripts that sealed entire valleys, raised storms that lasted seasons, and bound the dead to obedience for generations. No living galdr-worker has successfully completed a nine-rune script. The last verified attempt was in year 247. The galdr-worker's name was Eiríkr. The script was meant to seal a barrow-field permanently. The galdr succeeded. Eiríkr died of guðslag before the final line was cut. His apprentice finished the chant. The barrow-field is still sealed. The apprentice cannot speak above a whisper and has not been able to since.

### Script Construction Pattern

Every script follows a grammar:

**Pattern 1 — Noun + Verb + Consequence:**

- `Fé + Óss + Gjöf` — Cattle obey the voice and return home through trust.
- `Kaun + Hagall + Nið` — Fire falls like a storm on a house of shame.
- `Yr + Mál + Hrafn` — The grave speaks through omen and carrion-sign.

**Pattern 2 — Object + Force + Outcome:**

- `Steinn + Nauð + Elgur` — Stone endures and becomes sanctuary.
- `Skuggi + Reið + Hlið` — Shadow travels and the gate opens one way.
- `Blóð + Týr + Nið` — Blood demands justice and the betrayer is shamed.

**Pattern 3 — Social + Natural:**

- `Gjöf + Ár + Vend` — Gift and harvest build household bliss.
- `Mál + Vetr + Nauð` — Law endures through winter's hardship.
- `Nið + Kaun + Hagall` — Shame burns and the storm takes what's left.

### Dark Script Examples — Rimevegr Workings

These are the galdr-scripts that galdr-workers whisper about and Lawspeakers pretend do not exist.

#### 1. Seal the Dead Who Will Not Stay

Runes: Yr + Steinn + Elgur

Kvæði:

> "Yr in the bone, Steinn on the lid, Elgur at the mouth.
> What sleeps here stays sleeping. The stone remembers the
> weight."

Effect: The barrow-ward holds the dead in place. Standard defensive galdr. Every settlement has at least one of these. The dark part is not the script — the dark part is that they need refreshing every twenty years now, and twenty years ago it was thirty, and thirty years before that it was fifty.

#### 2. Burn the Oath-Breaker's Hall

Runes: Kaun + Nið + Óðal

Kvæði:

> "Kaun in the rafter, Nið on the name, Óðal in the ash.
> Let the fire know what the line has done. Let the land
> forget the house."

Effect: The hall burns. The fire targets the structure, not the people — but the people are inside the structure, and the fire does not distinguish between the two after the first minute. The land where the hall stood will not support a building for a generation. The family name becomes a cautionary tale.

#### 3. Curse the Betrayer's Blood

Runes: Blóð + Týr + Nið + Ár + Óðal

Kvæði:

> Ár in the debt, Óðal in the line. The blood remembers. The

Effect: A five-rune curse-script. The betrayer bleeds for the broken oath. The shame follows the family. The land records the transgression. The year brings the reckoning. This is rank 4 galdr — expensive, dangerous, and devastating. The galdr- worker who carves this pays 3 Willpower and 3 HP minimum, and the script must be carved in the betrayer's blood (obtained legally — a nick during a witnessed ting, a wound from an honor-duel, a deliberate cut offered by the betrayer themselves before they knew what the blood would be used for).

Runes: Hlið + Þurs + Mórk

Effect: The building becomes a box. The door opens inward. People can enter. No one exits until the rune is broken. Used historically in three recorded cases: twice against outlaws who occupied a settlement building, once against a jarl's household during a coup. The third case is not discussed in public because the jarl's household included fourteen children, and the building was sealed for three days before anyone broke the rune.

Runes: Lögur + Nauð + Hrafn

Two men subjected to Lögur-Nauð-Hrafn scripts killed themselves before the effect ended. The galdr-worker who carves this is legally niding if caught, and no ting in the Rimevegr will hear a defense. ### Bind-Runes — Permanent Workings

A bind-rune is a compound rune: two or more rune-forms overlaid into a single carving. Where a script is a sentence, a bind-rune is a single word made from multiple roots. The binding compresses the effect but makes it permanent — or as permanent as anything is in the Rimevegr, which means it lasts until the material fails or the Veil eats the power.

**Ward bind-runes** — Protection and defense.

- `Þurs + Elgur + Úr` — Active defense, warding, bodily endurance. Kvæði: "Þurs at the flank, Elgur on the ridge, Úr in the ribs. Stand and do not break."
- `Mórk + Steinn + Salt` — Boundary sealed, permanent, purified. The standard barrow-ward. Kvæði: "Mórk at the edge, Steinn in the deep, Salt on the seal. The line holds. The dead sleep."

**Witness bind-runes** — Memory and truth.

- `Dagur + Mál + Óss` — Clarity, speech, authority. Carved on ting-stones. Kvæði: "Dagur sees, Mál measures, Óss speaks. The truth has no shade."
- `Hrafn + Yr + Steinn` — Omen, death, permanence. Carved on grave-markers to record how the dead died. Kvæði: "The raven saw. The yew remembers. The stone keeps."

**Burden bind-runes** — Curses and weight.

- `Nið + Blóð + Óðal` — Disgrace, blood-debt, lineage. The generational curse in compact form. Kvæði: "Nið on the house, Blóð on the name, Óðal remembers what the line has done."
- `Nauð + Vetr + Íss` — Need, winter, stillness. The binding of despair. Kvæði: "Need in the dark, winter in the bone, ice on the will. Wait. Wait. Wait."

**Battle bind-runes** — War and force.

- `Týr + Móðr + Úr` — Justice, fury, strength. The warrior's compound. Kvæði: "Týr in the hand, Móðr in the blood, Úr in the bone. Strike true and stand."
- `Kaun + Hagall + Þurs` — Fire, storm, thorn. The siege-breaker. Kvæði: "Kaun opens, Hagall breaks, Þurs punishes. Nothing holds."

## Rune-Blight — The Corruption of Galdr

The runes are not safe. They were never safe. The pre-Cracking galdr-workers had divine patronage — the gods corrected the pronunciation, steadied the hand, absorbed the overflow. Post- Cracking galdr-workers have none of that. They carve with orphaned tools and channel power through a system designed for divine supervision that has been running unsupervised for three centuries. The runes still work. They work wrong, sometimes. They work _more than intended_, sometimes. And the wrongness accumulates in the carver's body the way the cold accumulates in the Rimevegr: slowly, specifically, and without mercy.

The galdr-workers call it _rúnavíti_ — rune-blight.

### What It Is

Rune-blight is the physical and mental corruption that results from prolonged, excessive, or improper rune use. It is not the same as the normal costs of galdr (the white hand-lines, the numb fingertips, the permanent vocal undertone). Those costs are the expected price. Rune-blight is what happens when the price is not enough and the runes take more.

The distinction is cumulative exposure versus acute damage. A galdr-worker who carves within their rank, rests between workings, and respects the material's grain will pay the standard costs and live to old age with strange hands and a stranger voice. A galdr-worker who overreaches — carving above rank, forcing runes into wrong materials, chanting náttmál without understanding what the night-face wants — accumulates blight.

### The Stages of Rune-Blight

**Stage 1 — The Tremor.** The galdr-worker's hands develop a fine tremor that appears only during carving. The chisel jumps. Lines waver. Simple runes that should be second nature require concentration. The galdr-worker compensates by gripping harder, which makes the tremor worse. At this stage, galdr checks take a -5 penalty and the failure cascade shifts upward by 10 on the table (see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`).

**Stage 2 — The Echo.** The runes begin to speak back. Not audibly — the galdr-worker hears the rune's resonance in peripheral attention, the way you hear a sound in a dream. The echo is the rune's night-face offering opinions. A galdr-worker carving Fé for a simple livestock blessing hears, beneath the dagmál chant, the náttmál whisper: _what you own owns you_. The echo cannot be silenced. It can be ignored, but ignoring it requires Will checks, and each failure means the rune tilts toward its dark face regardless of the carver's intent.

**Stage 3 — The Mark.** The blight becomes visible. The white hand-lines that every galdr-worker develops begin to spread — up the wrists, along the forearms. They are no longer thin. They are raised, textured, and they glow faintly in the dark with the same blue-white flicker as active rune-lines. The galdr-worker's hands look like ward-stones. The galdr-worker _is_ a ward-stone — the runes have decided that the carver's body is a valid medium, and they are writing on it the same way the carver writes on wood and bone and iron.

At this stage, the galdr-worker's presence begins to affect the environment. Fires burn blue within arm's reach. Small metal objects drift toward the hands. The air around the worker smells of old iron permanently, not just during carving. Animals refuse proximity. Other galdr-workers can feel the blight at a distance — a pressure, a wrongness, the sensation of standing too close to an active rune-stone.

**Stage 4 — The Speaking.** The galdr-worker's voice splits. The permanent undertone that all experienced galdr-workers develop separates into a distinct second voice — audible, recognizable, and not the carver's. The second voice speaks in rune-fragments: partial kvæðir, rune-names, the beginning of chants the carver did not start and cannot stop. The speaking happens during sleep, during conversation, during silence. The galdr-worker is chanting constantly, beneath the threshold of conscious control, and the chant is building toward a working that the carver has not chosen and cannot identify.

Other people hear it. The effect is the same as galdr overflow — dark emotional resonance, compulsive humming, the physical effects described above — but constant, low-level, unending. Living with a Stage 4 galdr-worker is living inside a permanent low-grade rune-working. The household adapts. The household changes. The household stops noticing things that visitors notice immediately: the blue fire-tips, the drifting objects, the children who hum in their sleep.

**Stage 5 — The Rune Becomes the Person.** Terminal blight. The galdr-worker's body completes its transformation into a medium. The white lines cover the entire body — face, torso, legs. The eyes develop the blue-white flicker. The voice is no longer human in any recognizable sense; it is rune-speech, constant, a low guttural chant that does not stop even when the galdr-worker is unconscious. The person inside the body is still present — this is not possession — but the person is now living inside a rune-stone, and the rune-stone is active, and the rune-stone is running a working that no one authorized.

No Stage 5 galdr-worker has been documented in the last century. The Deepholm archive records three cases from the first century post-Cracking, when galdr-workers pushed harder because the failure of the old systems demanded more power than human bodies could safely channel. All three were killed by their settlements — not out of malice, but out of necessity. A Stage 5 galdr- worker warps the environment in a radius of approximately fifty paces. Within that radius, wards fail, the Veil thins, the dead wake, and the living experience continuous galdr overflow: dark emotions, compulsive humming, physical arousal, numbness, headache. The settlement cannot function. The galdr- worker cannot stop. The only recorded resolution is death, and even then the body must be burned completely and the ashes scattered in running water, because Stage 5 corpses have been known to continue chanting for hours after the heart stops.

### Connection to the God-Scorned Path

The guðníðing described in Chapter 14 sometimes pursue rune- blight deliberately. Where a normal galdr-worker accumulates blight through overwork and overreach, the god-scorned carve inverted runes into their own flesh, chant náttmál without target or intent, and force the blight's progression as a form of spiritual self-destruction.

The overlap between Stage 3 rune-blight (the Mark) and the guðníðing's inverted ward-marks is not coincidental. Both involve the runes writing on the carver's body. The difference is direction: the blighted galdr-worker's marks are the runes' initiative, carved from outside in. The guðníðing's marks are self-inflicted, carved from inside out. The result is similar but not identical. The blighted worker is being consumed by the magic. The guðníðing is consuming themselves with the magic.

The three soulbanes described in Chapter 14 — mannát, dýrríða, villimennska — interact with rune-blight in specific ways:

- **Mannát (the Eating)** kills the hugr. A galdr-worker whose hugr is dead cannot hear the rune's dagmál face. All galdr defaults to náttmál. The rune-blight accelerates because the brake is gone.
- **Dýrríða (the Riding)** kills the hamr. A galdr-worker whose hamr is dead has no barrier between the rune's will and the body. The speaking (Stage 4) arrives immediately. The runes write freely.
- **Villimennska (the Wilding)** kills the fylgja. A galdr- worker whose fylgja is dead has no divine inheritance, no residual connection to whatever the gods left behind. The galdr stops working entirely — not because the runes reject the carver, but because the carver has nothing left for the runes to channel through. A villimaðr cannot carve. The hands still move. The chisel still cuts. The runes are shapes on stone. They do not glow. They do not answer. The magic is gone.

This creates a bitter irony: the god-scorned who pursue rune- blight as self-destruction find that the path of corruption leads not to greater dark power but to the complete loss of power. The runes are not tools of rebellion against the divine order. They are fragments of the divine order, and destroying the divine inheritance within oneself destroys access to the fragments. The wolfshead who expected to become a dark galdr- master becomes, instead, a man with scars on his hands and silence where the runes used to speak.

_This is the Rimevegr's cruelest joke, and it is a joke nobody tells because nobody finds it funny: the magic that is killing the world is also the only thing keeping the world alive, and the people who try to weaponize the killing find that the weapon does not work without the thing it is destroying._

## The Phenomenology of Silence

Galdr is sung. The rune-carver opens his throat and the vibration passes through the body into the wood. The sound is the channel. The voice is the instrument. The body pays in blood and tremor, and the work is audible — anyone standing near a galdr-worker at full throat-song can hear the power moving through the air the way they can hear wind through a crack in a wall. Galdr announces itself. Galdr is loud.

Seiðr is the opposite.

When a seiðr-wife works, the world goes quiet. Not silent in the way that a room is silent when no one speaks. Silent in the way that a room is silent when something has eaten the sound. The fire stops crackling. The timber stops settling. The wind outside dies as if it has lost interest. The men in the room stop hearing their own breathing, and it takes them several seconds to realize that they have stopped — not that the breathing has stopped, but that the sound of it has been swallowed. The seiðr-wife opens her mouth and what comes out is not song. It is speech — flat, barren, stripped of every quality that makes a voice recognizable as belonging to a living person. The words are plain. The pace is dull. The tone carries no warmth, no urgency, no music. And the voice is not hers.

This is the defining phenomenology of seiðr: the practitioner speaks, and the voice that emerges rides her lips like a traveller riding a borrowed horse. The words are hers in the sense that her mouth shapes them. They are not hers in any other sense. The pitch is wrong. The cadence belongs to someone who breathed differently, who formed vowels in a throat shaped by different years of use. Sometimes the voice is recognizable — the client clutches the edge of the bench because the dead man's wife just heard her husband's pronunciation of a word he always said wrong, and the seiðr-wife has never met the dead man, and the voice came through anyway. Sometimes the voice belongs to no one present or remembered. It is old, or foreign, or simply other, and the room cannot place it, and the room is afraid.

The silence is not a side effect. The silence is the mechanism.

### Drawing the Veil

A seiðr-wife works by pulling the Veil toward herself. Not through it, not against it — toward. The Veil is the boundary between the living and the dead, and it has properties that galdr-workers measure and seiðr-wives feel: it swallows sound, muffles firelight, bends distance, drops the temperature until breath fogs and fingertips go numb. These are the Veil's ambient effects — the things it does when it is merely near.

When the seiðr-wife reaches, she draws those effects inward. She becomes the centre of a localized Veil-thinning. The boundary between the living and the dead does not weaken everywhere — it weakens here, where she sits, in a radius measured in arm's-lengths rather than miles. And as the Veil gathers around her, it withdraws from the space around those who are watching.

This is why the silence feels wrong to the witnesses. The Veil is being pulled away from them. Their world — the world of crackling fire and settling timber and wind through cracks — becomes more itself, not less. The sounds should be sharper. The fire should be brighter. But the seiðr-wife is closer, and the Veil she has drawn around herself radiates the silence outward like heat radiating from a forge. The listeners are caught between two truths: the world is louder because the Veil has retreated from them, and the world is quieter because the person three paces away is wrapped in a silence so complete that it leaks.

The leak is the seiðr-wife's working. She draws the Veil close so that the dead can speak through the thinned boundary. The dead do not shout. They do not need to. The Veil around the practitioner is so thin that a whisper on the other side arrives as clear speech on this one. And the silence is the cost — the Veil swallows the living sound around the practitioner to make room for the dead sound that comes through. It is not that the seiðr-wife creates silence. It is that the Veil, gathered tight, does what the Veil always does. It eats the noise of the living world, and in the space it makes, the dead have room to speak.

A galdr-worker hearing the hum of rune-channels is hearing the residue of divine infrastructure. A seiðr-wife hearing the dead is hearing what the Veil carries on its other face. These are not the same phenomenon. They share a source — the Cracking broke both — but the channels are different. The runes were built. The Veil was always there. The runes are failing. The Veil is not.

This is why seiðr has not weakened since the Cracking. The dead are easier to reach now, not harder. The Veil thins a little more each decade. The seiðr-wives' theory — the Devouring — holds that something is eating the barrier from the other side, thinning it, making the dead more accessible and the living world more permeable. If they are right, then the seiðr-wife who draws the Veil toward herself is not performing a difficult act. She is performing an easy one, made easier by three centuries of progressive decay. She is stepping into a river that is already flowing downhill.

### The Silence in the Room

The silence has stages. Witnesses who have attended multiple seiðr-workings describe a consistent progression, and the progression is important because it maps the depth of the practitioner's reach.

**Stage one: the dampening.** The fire quiets. The timber stops its settling-sounds. Conversation becomes effortful — not because speaking is harder, but because the acoustic space has changed. A voice that carried across the room a moment ago now seems to stop a pace from the speaker's mouth. People lean forward. They repeat themselves. They notice, without being able to articulate it, that the room has become smaller in a way that has nothing to do with the walls.

**Stage two: the smothering.** Body sounds disappear. The listener cannot hear their own breathing. They cannot hear their heartbeat — not the heartbeat itself, which was never loud, but the subliminal pulse that living bodies are accustomed to and do not notice until it is gone. The absence is deeply unsettling. A person who has never heard true silence — and no person living has, because the body always makes noise — discovers what true silence feels like, and it feels like death. The instinctive response is to make noise: cough, shift weight, scrape a boot on the floor. The noise is swallowed. The boot scrapes, but the scrape arrives muffled, distant, as if it happened in another room. Panic begins at stage two. Not the loud panic of a fight. The quiet panic of a person who has realized that they are not entirely present in their own body.

**Stage three: the arrest.** This is the stage that separates casual observation from genuine seiðr-exposure. The witnesses stop blinking. Not a decision — a physiological response. The eyes fix on the seiðr-wife, dry and staring, and the witness cannot look away. The visual field narrows. Peripheral objects lose detail. The seiðr-wife's face, hands, and mouth become the only things in focus, and the focus is so absolute that the witness's eyes begin to ache from not blinking and they do not blink anyway. The eyelids have stopped responding to voluntary command.

Simultaneously, the sensation of time collapses. Five minutes feels like thirty seconds or three hours, and the witness cannot tell which. The fire has not moved — has it? The shadows have not shifted — have they? The room exists in a single, extended present tense that has no past and no future, only the seiðr-wife's mouth and the words that come from it in a voice that does not belong to her.

**Stage four: the stillness.** The witness's body goes quiet. Not relaxed — arrested. The muscles hold their last position. The hands do not move. The jaw does not clench or unclench. The chest stops its visible rise and fall because the breathing has become so shallow that the diaphragm barely moves. In some cases — the well-documented cases, the ones the seiðr-wives themselves talk about in careful, clinical terms — the witness stops breathing entirely. Not a held breath. A cessation. The autonomous nervous system, which has maintained respiratory rhythm since birth without requiring conscious direction, simply pauses. The body forgets to breathe.

This is not magic acting on the witness. This is the Veil's proximity effect, amplified by the seiðr-wife's drawing. The Veil does not distinguish between the practitioner and the audience. It swallows what is near, and what is near includes the living processes of everyone in the room. The dampening that took the crackle from the fire takes the rhythm from the lungs. The smothering that ate the heartbeat's subliminal pulse eats the autonomic drive to inhale. The body does not resist because the body does not know it should — the silence has already removed the sensory cues that would trigger alarm.

The result is lightheadedness. Then dizziness. Then, if the seiðr-working continues at depth, loss of consciousness. A witness who faints during a seiðr-working has not fainted from fear. They have fainted from hypoxia — oxygen deprivation caused by the cessation of breathing that the Veil's silence induced. The seiðr-wife did not do this to them. The seiðr-wife was focused on the dead. The Veil did this to them because the Veil is not selective and the seiðr-wife cannot control what the Veil does to the people around her any more than a forge-worker can control where the heat goes.

This is why seiðr-workings are attended by watchers — not guards, but monitors whose job is to observe the audience and intervene when someone stops breathing. The protocol is simple: shake the person. Slap them if shaking fails. Drag them out of the room if slapping fails. The fresh air and distance from the Veil's radius restarts the breathing within seconds. The watcher does not need to know why it works. They need to know that a person who has stopped breathing in the seiðr-wife's presence will start again if removed from the seiðr-wife's presence.

Dalla does not have watchers. The band does not know they are needed. When Dalla works in camp — rarely, reluctantly, at Voss's insistence when the contract requires it — the men sit around the fire and watch, and they feel the silence press in, and they attribute the dizziness to nerves and the lightheadedness to the cold and the strange fixation on Dalla's mouth to something they do not name. Nobody has fainted yet. Dalla has not yet worked at a depth that would push stage four to its conclusion. But the depth of her reach is increasing — seiðr strengthens with practice, unlike galdr, which weakens — and the day will come when a standard working pulls enough Veil into camp to stop someone's breathing, and neither Dalla nor Voss will understand what happened, because nobody told them this was coming.

### The Voice That Is Not Hers

The voice is the signature of seiðr-working, and it is the detail that no witness forgets.

The seiðr-wife opens her mouth. The words that emerge are spoken in a tone and register that do not match the woman who is speaking them. The pitch may be higher or lower. The accent may be regional, archaic, or unplaceable. The cadence is wrong — the pauses fall in places where a living speaker would not pause, and the emphasis lands on syllables that carry meaning in the dead person's speech pattern, not in the seiðr-wife's.

The content is plain. No poetry, no drama, no elevation. The dead speak the way they spoke in life, which means they speak the way ordinary people speak: in fragments, in half-finished thoughts, in the particular verbal habits they carried from birth to death and apparently carry still. A farmer's dead husband talks about the grain the way he always talked about the grain. A dead warrior reports on his death with the flat, unelaborated directness of a man who never learned to tell a story properly. The dead do not become eloquent. They become more themselves — stripped of the social filters that the living maintain, speaking without the awareness that someone is listening and judging.

The seiðr-wife is not translating. She is not interpreting. She is a surface — a membrane stretched thin enough that the dead can press through and leave the imprint of their voice on the living air. Her own awareness during the working varies by practitioner and by depth. At shallow working — rank 1 and 2 — the seiðr-wife is present. She hears what the dead say. She can intervene, redirect, even refuse a spirit that is pushing too hard or saying something the client should not hear. At deeper working — rank 3 and above — the seiðr-wife recedes. Her awareness narrows to a point and then extinguishes, and the dead have the mouth, and the dead say what the dead want to say, and the seiðr-wife comes back afterward with a headache and a gap in her memory and asks the watcher what happened.

Dalla works at rank 3. She is aware during most of her workings, but the awareness is thin — a candle flame in a wind that could extinguish it at any time. She has come back from sessions with no memory of the last third of the conversation. She has come back and found the men staring at her with expressions she cannot read — not fear exactly, but something adjacent to fear, something that lives in the space between respect and revulsion. She does not ask what she said. She has learned that the answer is never comfortable.

The worst cases — the cases that seiðr-wives discuss among themselves in quiet voices and never share with clients — are the sessions where the voice does not leave. The seiðr-wife closes the working. She pulls back from the Veil. She opens her eyes and speaks, and the first words out of her mouth are in the dead person's voice. Not because the spirit is still present. Because the mouth has been shaped by the dead person's speaking for an hour, and the muscles and the breath and the tongue have learned a pattern that is not the seiðr-wife's, and the pattern does not release immediately. It takes minutes. Sometimes hours. The seiðr-wife speaks to her student and the student flinches because the voice is the dead man's voice, and the seiðr-wife clears her throat and tries again, and the voice is still wrong, and the wrongness sits in the room like a cold spot that will not warm.

Ragnhild at Icebreak has a protocol for this. After every working, she hums. Not a song — a hum, a single tone, held for as long as her breath allows. She says the humming resets the throat. "The dead borrow the mouth," she tells her students. "The hum takes it back. If you do not take it back, the mouth forgets who owns it." Her students hum after every session. The sound is thin and unremarkable and it is the most important thing they do.

## The Cost of Silence

Galdr kills with fire. The rune-song builds in the throat until the blood vessels strain and the heart labours and the stroke comes like a fist behind the eyes. A galdr-worker who pushes too far blows his heart singing. It is violent, immediate, and terminal. The body burns from the inside. The man who dies of galdr backlash dies loudly.

Seiðr does not kill like that. Seiðr does not kill loudly at all.

### The Slow Suffocation

The cost of seiðr is measured in oxygen. Not metaphorically — the Veil's silence effect suppresses the autonomous breathing reflex, and the suppression is cumulative. A single working of moderate depth leaves the seiðr-wife with a headache and the feeling of having held her breath too long. Two workings in a day — a pace that contract demands sometimes require — leave her grey-faced and unsteady, with the specific dizziness that comes from blood that has not carried enough air. Three workings in a day has happened twice in documented seiðr history. Both practitioners collapsed. One recovered. One did not.

The mechanism is the Veil's ambient effect on the practitioner herself. She draws the Veil close. The Veil suppresses sound, and sound includes the feedback loops that the body uses to regulate breathing. The seiðr-wife in trance breathes at a rate that would concern a healer if the healer were monitoring — four or five breaths per minute instead of twelve to fifteen. The breaths are shallow. The chest barely moves. The body is in a state of controlled oxygen deprivation that the trance makes tolerable the way cold makes frostbite tolerable — by numbing the signals that would otherwise force a correction.

Over years, the accumulated hypoxic episodes leave marks. Seiðr- wives who have practiced for two decades show cognitive slowing — not dramatic, not disabling, but measurable in the pauses between question and answer, in the moments when the word they want is on the other side of a gap that was not there five years ago. Their sleep is poor, not because of nightmares (though the nightmares come too) but because the respiratory drive has been trained by years of suppression to operate at a lower baseline. They breathe less deeply asleep than other people. They wake more often, gasping, because the body dropped below the oxygen threshold and the survival reflex overrode the trained suppression.

This is the slow cost. Not the dramatic cost — not the stroke that kills the galdr-worker mid-song, not the spectacle of a man dying with rune-fire in his throat. The slow cost is a woman who breathes a little less each year, who thinks a little slower each decade, who sleeps a little worse each winter, who will die at fifty-five of something the healer calls pneumonia or heart weakness but which is actually the sum total of ten thousand trance-sessions in which the Veil ate the air from her lungs and she did not fight it because fighting it would have broken the working.

### Physical Markers

The galdr-worker's cost is written in white scars. The seiðr-wife's cost is written in what is missing.

**Complexion.** Active seiðr-wives develop a pallor that does not respond to sun or diet. The skin takes on a blue-grey undertone — not dramatic enough to alarm a casual observer, but visible to anyone who knew the woman before she began serious practice. The pallor is circulatory: years of low-oxygen trance-states produce chronic low-grade anemia and reduced peripheral perfusion. The body learns to route blood to the core, protecting the organs at the expense of the skin. Dalla is twenty-eight. She has been pale since she was twenty-three. The men in the band attribute it to the cold. It is not the cold.

**Temperature sensitivity.** The seiðr-wife runs cold. Not the temporary chill of Veil-proximity — a permanent, baseline shift in body temperature that seiðr-wives notice in themselves and their students notice in their teachers. Hands that are always cool. Feet that do not warm in bedding. A preference for sitting closest to the fire that is not social but physiological. The Veil's cold follows the practitioner out of the trance the way the galdr-worker's tremor follows the carver away from the bench.

**Voice changes.** A seiðr-wife's natural voice changes over the course of her practice. Not from the dead — from the Veil itself. The repeated suppression of normal vocal patterns during trance-work, combined with the months and years of speaking in voices that are not her own, produces a voice that becomes increasingly flat. The music leaves. The inflection narrows. The woman who laughed with a bright, carrying sound at twenty laughs with a low, level sound at thirty-five, and by forty she does not laugh at all — not from joylessness, but because the vocal apparatus has been so thoroughly colonized by dead voices and Veil-silence that the spontaneous sounds of living expression no longer come naturally. She must choose to inflect. She must decide to vary her tone. The effort is small but constant, and when she is tired, she stops making the effort, and the voice that comes out is the voice of someone who is not entirely here.

**Nail and lip color.** The chronic low oxygen shows in the extremities. Fingernails and toenails develop a persistent blue-grey tinge. Lips are darker than they should be — not blue, not obviously cyanotic, but a shade deeper than healthy tissue. These are the details that a trained healer would notice and a layperson would not, and they are the details that other seiðr-wives read instantly when meeting a colleague. The color of the nails tells the story of the practice: how deep, how often, how long.

### Seiðr Is Not Terminal the Way Galdr Is Terminal

The critical distinction: galdr can kill you in a single session. The guðslag — the god-stroke — is a catastrophic event. The rune-song builds until the heart cannot sustain it, and the practitioner receives what amounts to a massive stroke as the price of pushing the throat-song past what the body can channel. The galdr-worker who "blows his heart singing" dies on the floor with rune-fire in his veins and the last carved mark unfinished on the wood. It is violent. It is immediate. It is the temptation that every galdr-worker carries: to push the song one rank higher, to feel the full resonance one more time, to die in the work rather than fade from it.

Seiðr offers no such temptation. There is no single session that kills. There is no dramatic peak, no moment of transcendent risk, no glorious self-destruction. Seiðr kills the way water kills stone — by persistence, by repetition, by the slow accumulation of damage that is too small to notice in any single instance and too large to survive across a lifetime. A seiðr-wife does not blow her heart. She empties her lungs, one trance at a time, one breath at a time, until the lungs have been emptied so many times that they forget how to fill.

This means that seiðr practitioners live longer than galdr-workers, on average. They are not killed by their art in their thirties or forties the way the most active galdr-workers are. They die in their fifties and sixties, of conditions that look natural and are natural — pneumonia, heart failure, the simple failure of a body that has run on reduced oxygen for decades. The deaths are quiet. The deaths are unremarkable. The settlement buries the old seiðr-wife and says she died of age, and the healer writes nothing unusual in the record, and nobody counts the ten thousand trances that took ten thousand breaths that took ten years from the back end of a life that was never going to be long enough.

The seiðr-wives know. They count. Not formally — there is no ledger, no archive. They count in the way that people count who have watched their teachers die: Ragnhild watched her teacher die at fifty-one. Ragnhild is sixty-two. She knows what the math means. She does not discuss the math with her students because the math is the same for all of them and discussing it changes nothing. The practice kills. The practice is necessary. The dead need to be spoken with and the Veil needs to be navigated and the silence is the price and the price is breath and breath is life measured in litres of air that the lungs did not take when the Veil said not to.
