The gods went silent when the sky cracked. No one knows whether they died, fled, or simply stopped caring. People still sacrifice. Still carve their names on rune-stones. Still curse by them. The temples are cold and the statues stare at nothing.

What follows is what the people of the Rimevegr believe. Whether any of it is true is a question the novel never answers directly.

## Conversion and Faith Transmission

### How Children Learn Religion

Children in the Rimevegr do not learn religion. They absorb it, the way they absorb language — through repetition, context, and the fear of getting it wrong. No one sits a child down and explains the pantheon. No one teaches the Cracking as a historical event with causes and consequences. The theology enters through the routines of daily life: knock before you enter (Heimr), do not lie or Lopt feeds (Lopt), leave porridge at the crow-pole (Odynn), do not speak the dead's name after the Letting Go (Hael), break something for Thurr when you are afraid. The child learns the gods' names the way they learn the names of the settlement's dogs — through repeated hearing, casual invocation, and the occasional sharp correction when they get it wrong.

The corrections are the real education. A boy who carves the Heimr-mark wrong — the line without the circle — is corrected by his mother with a slap to the hand and the words: "That is not a door. That is a crack." The boy remembers the slap and carves the mark correctly for the rest of his life. A girl who speaks a dead uncle's name after the Letting Go is corrected by the barrow-keeper with a cold stare and the words: "He is in the ground. Keep him there." The girl does not understand the theology. She understands the stare. The effect is the same.

### The Naming-Rite

A child's first religious act is being named. The naming happens at the picking-up — when the father lifts the newborn from the ground and gives it a name, it enters the divine order. Before the name, the child is a body. After the name, the child is a person — a node in the oath-network, a future entry in the contract-stones, a name that a skald might carry and a barrow- keeper might carve. The name is the first rune written on the child's life, and like all runes in the Rimevegr, it works whether or not the gods are listening.

### The First Oath

A child's first oath is usually sworn between the ages of ten and twelve — a small oath, to a parent or a foster-parent, promising a season's labor or a winter's obedience. The oath is sworn with a hand on the nearest contract-stone (or, in settlements without one, on the doorpost's Heimr-mark). The child does not understand the theology of oaths. They understand that their father's expression is grave, that the stone is cold, and that failing to keep the promise will result in consequences they cannot predict but are certain will be unpleasant. This is sufficient. The oath-network does not require understanding. It requires participation.

### The First Barrow-Visit

A child's first visit to the barrow-field is a carefully managed encounter with mortality. The barrow-keeper leads. The parent follows. The child is brought to a barrow — usually a grandparent's — and told to place a hand on the barrow-stone and speak. "Tell them your name," the parent says. The child speaks. The stone is cold. The child feels nothing, because children do not yet have the accumulated loss that makes the barrows resonate. But the act establishes the relationship: you are the living, they are the dead, and the stone between you is the contract. Maintain it.

### Outsider Absorption

Outsiders — traders, prisoners, absorbed populations from conquered settlements — are folded into the faith through the same mechanism that teaches children: repetition and social pressure. A trader who stays long enough in a settlement begins to knock before entering, not because they believe in Heimr but because everyone else knocks and the social cost of not knocking is higher than the effort of compliance. A prisoner who is absorbed into the settlement's thrall-population learns the Þrælasiðr from other thralls, not from any formal instruction. A conquered population that is folded into a jarl's domain learns the jarl's gods because the jarl's gods are the framework within which the jarl's laws operate, and operating outside the framework means operating outside the law.

There is no conversion rite. There is no baptism, no formal declaration of faith, no moment of acceptance. The Rimevegr's religion does not require belief. It requires behavior. Knock when you enter. Leave porridge at the crow-pole. Do not speak the dead's name. Carry your oaths. Visit the barrow. Do these things and you are within the faith, whether or not you believe a single word of it. Faith, in the Rimevegr, is a habit, and habits are acquired through proximity, not conviction. This is the religion's greatest strength and its most fundamental vulnerability: a faith that does not require belief can survive anything except the question of whether it should.
