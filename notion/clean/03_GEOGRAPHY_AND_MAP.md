# Geography and Map of the Rimevegr

**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr
**Purpose:** Prose geographic reference for GM narration and world navigation.

---

## Overview

The Rimevegr stretches roughly two hundred kilometres north to south and a hundred and fifty east to west -- a cold northern territory with no unified government, named after the old trade road that runs its length. The shape of the land is simple to draw and hard to cross. A granite mountain range -- the Black Pine Range -- runs north-south like a crooked spine, dividing the territory into a fjord-cut western coast and a forested eastern interior. Below the mountains, moors fill the centre: windswept peat heath where nothing grows taller than heather and the horizon is a flat grey line in every direction. The northern edge is permafrost and ice shelf. The southern edge is coast -- salt cliffs, shingle, tidal flats. Underground, the Deepholm mining complex connects the mountain and moor regions through tunnels older than human settlement.

Climate controls everything. The Rimevegr has two seasons: sixty days of compressed, mud-soaked Long Summer and three hundred days of cold, dim Long Dark. Summers bring near-constant daylight, aggressive growth, biting insects, and flooding. Winters bring short days, deep snow, frozen rivers, and the constant arithmetic of firewood versus freezing. The Veil -- the rime-mist that rolled south after the Cracking -- never fully lifts, thinning in summer to a grey haze and thickening in winter to a fog that swallows landmarks and muffles sound. Travel distances are measured in days, not kilometres, because terrain type and season change the conversion constantly.

For a mercenary band, geography is money, time, and risk. Every contract begins with a route decision: which path, through whose territory, at what season. A forty-kilometre crossing of open moor in summer is two hard days with poor foraging and no shelter. The same crossing in winter is four days of hypothermia risk, and the band arrives hungry, cold, and combat-ineffective. Mountain passes close entirely from autumn through spring. Tidal access gates half the coastal locations behind six-hour windows. The fjords are highways in calm weather and coffins in storms. The band that knows the forty-two named routes -- their terrain segments, shelter options, seasonal access, and foraging quality -- survives. The band that guesses does not.

## The Fjords (Frostfjord Region)

The fjords cut inland from the western coast like axe-wounds in the rock. Deep salt-water channels, barely wide enough for two boats abreast in the narrowest arms, walled on both sides by sheer cliff faces forty feet high, dark with water-stain and streaked white with bird droppings. The water is black, still, and deep -- deep enough that the bottom is never visible. Pine-clad ridges rise above the cliffs, wind-hammered and exposed, the soil so thin that tree roots grip bare rock. The air smells of brine, pine resin, and the low-tide stink of rotting bladderwrack.

Three settlements cling to this rock. Frostfjord Hollow (population 87) sits in the main fjord channel, Jarl Hrothgar's seat, where the economy runs on dried cod and pine tar. Ashen Reach (population 142) controls the southern arm, the Pale Widow's territory, trading pine tar, iron, and a skilled blacksmith's output. Skaldhaven (population 110) perches on a sea-cliff where the fjord meets the open coast, its Hall of Sagas and sealed rune archive making it the closest thing to a centre of learning the Rimevegr has. The rivalry between Frostfjord and Ashen Reach defines the region -- they control different fjord-arms and have feuded over the Beinvegr corridor for three generations. Blood has been spilled on that ridge more than once.

Travel here is vertical and narrow. The Beinvegr (Bone Road) follows the exposed ridge between Frostfjord and Ashen Reach -- forty-five kilometres, two days in summer, four in winter. Whale-bone navigational markers line the first ridge, old posts driven into rock by hands no one remembers. Wind hammers from the west. The only shelter is a ridge overhang that fits eight men, poorly. The Fjörðskuggaleið (Fjord-Shadow Path) cuts along the cliff face above the fjord toward Skaldhaven -- a path so narrow that one stumble means a long fall into black water. Rope-holds driven into rock at the steepest points. Sea fog rolls in without warning. The fjord shadow falls across the path for most of the day. The Hreggbraut (Storm Passage) is shorter but worse: a headland scramble with no proper trail, hand-holds worn smooth, and wind that funnels through the gap hard enough to flatten a person.

The fjord is both highway and trap. In calm weather, a longship covers the distance between settlements in hours. In a storm, the narrow channels become funnels that amplify wave action, and the cliffs offer no landing. The Kveldsarm -- a side-inlet barely two boats wide -- is so enclosed that sound bounces between the walls and returns as a whisper half a minute later. At its inner end, a pebble beach backed by rockfall blocks further progress. The standing stones here are older than the settlements: Hrothgar's Mark on the ridge, carved with ancestor-names and humming at dusk since the spring of Y312; the Seidr-Nail above Skaldhaven, warm to the touch in deep winter, the pines leaning away from it in a rough circle. Something is waking in the rock, and the fjord concentrates it.

## The Rime-Moors (Grimholt Region)

The rime-moor announces itself through the ground. Walking north-east from Thornwall along the Hrímheiðarvegr (Rime-Moor Road), the transition is gradual but unmistakable. The soil darkens. The vegetation thins to lichen and moss. Iron-stained water seeps from the ground, staining every stream bed rust-orange and every stone black with oxidation. The cairns marking the route are blackened too, as if the land is being pickled in its own minerals. The air changes taste -- a metallic tang that coats the tongue and gets into food. Rime coats every surface on autumn mornings, ice crystals on every blade and stone, and the silence deepens until the only sounds are boots on frozen ground and the wind. The wind never stops.

Grimholt (population 118) squats at the heart of this territory: Warchief Ordovast's stronghold, fortified to level four, fed by iron mining and defended by iron-guard patrols. The settlement's approach is marked by slag deposits and old mine tailings, the ground a compacted industrial waste that crunches underfoot. Ordovast controls the region through force and infrastructure. The Járnvegr (Iron Road) between Grimholt and Deepholm is the best-maintained road surface in the Rimevegr -- compacted slag and crushed rock, wagon-width, with iron-guard checkpoints at both ends and lanterns on iron posts lighting the final descent. Toll stations extract payment from every wagon. The busiest road in the territory, and the only one that resembles civilisation.

Standing stones here serve as patrol markers for Ordovast's outriders. The land is too flat and too open for ambush, but that works both ways -- a band approaching Grimholt is visible for miles, and the iron-guard rides to meet anything that moves. The Rime-Moor Road stretches sixty kilometres across open ground with nothing to break the wind. Hypothermia kills more travellers than bandits on this route. Shelters are sparse: a stone shepherd's hut at twenty-two kilometres, a cairn-hollow at forty-two, and nothing between. Foraging is poor in every season. The iron-poisoned streams are not safe to drink without boiling.

The contrast is what makes the region. Grimholt itself is fortified strength -- walls, garrison, smithing, the infrastructure of a settlement that controls its surroundings through discipline. Beyond the walls, the moor is emptiness. Flat, treeless, the wind scything across rime-crusted ground that swallows colour. At night, travellers report pale flickering lights that move against the wind -- the locals call them moor-lanterns and avoid travel after dark. The Ordovast's Shame barrow, two hours out from the settlement, was deliberately collapsed on Ordovast's orders. Nobody will say what got out before the rubble fell. The rubble has been settling for three years. The iron in the ground stains everything it touches, and the taste of it stays in the mouth for hours after leaving the region.

## The Black Pine (Forest Region)

Enter the Black Pine and the world closes. The canopy is so thick that noon looks like dusk. The trail is a foot-beaten line of packed needles threading between trunks the girth of three men, and the light that filters through is grey-green and thin. Sound travels strangely here -- voices carry, but direction is lost. A shout comes from everywhere and nowhere. The crunch of needles underfoot is the loudest thing in the forest, and when it stops -- when everything stops, the birds and the insects and the wind in the upper branches all at once -- that is what locals call the Hush. It lasts ten seconds or ten minutes. Then the forest starts again, and nobody speaks of it.

Vargheim (population 165) is the forest's settlement: Jarl Ulf Vargson's territory, deep in the pines, sustained by wolf-warden kennels and charcoal kilns. The charcoal economy defines the Black Pine -- controlled burns, kiln-camps, charcoal hauling along trails marked by axe-blazes on birch trunks, some refreshed each spring, some grown over and useless. The smell of pine resin, charcoal smoke, and wet earth fills the air year-round. Ashmark (population 82), on the forest's southern border, processes the charcoal and smelts iron ore from Deepholm. The smoke from Ashmark's smelters is visible before the settlement is, a grey smear above the treeline that tastes of hot metal.

Kveldulf's wolf-packs are the Black Pine's border enforcement. The Svartfuruvegr (Black Pine Way) from Vargheim to Feldwick runs fifty-five kilometres through the heart of the forest -- three days in summer, five in winter, with a twenty-five per cent chance of wolf encounter. The wolves are named: the Scar Pack, the Grey Bitch's litter. They follow travellers but rarely attack groups larger than three. In winter, the calculus shifts. The Úlfavegr (Wolf Road) between Vargheim and Ashmark is older than either settlement -- Vargheim's founders followed wolf trails to find their clearing. The wolves were here first. Vargheim's hunters know every trail, every den, every ambush point. They let travellers pass. They watch.

Two groves matter. The first is the sacred grove near Vargheim -- old trees, undisturbed, where offerings are left and axes are not swung. The second is wrong. The Ashmark Root-Grave sits two hours from the settlement in forest so dark the canopy blocks all light. Tree roots have grown through the barrow beneath, and some of those root systems are bone. The forest floor above the grave is dead: no moss, no undergrowth, bare black earth in a rough oval that the woodcutters refuse to cross. Navigation in the deep pine is by blazed trees and game trails. Visibility is ten paces. Getting lost takes minutes. Getting found takes hours, if the wolves do not find you first. Foraging is good in summer -- mushrooms, game, berries -- and fair even in winter, which makes the forest the most forgiving terrain in the Rimevegr for a band that knows where to look. The Black Pine is both shelter and prison: the canopy keeps the rain off and the wind out, but it also kills your sightlines and swallows your voice.

## The Coast

The coast is salt and wind. Salt-scoured cliffs of dark volcanic rock, basalt headlands streaked with orange rust where iron-bearing seams weep in the rain, shingle beaches littered with whale bone and driftwood and things less identifiable. The tidal rhythm governs everything. Half the coastal locations are accessible only at low tide -- the Tide-Teeth standing stones, Skerjaholm island with its shingle causeway, The Maw sea-cave that floods in minutes when the water turns. Misjudge the tide and you swim, or you drown, or you stand waist-deep in near-freezing water waiting six hours for the sea to let you go.

Kolvik (population 95) is the only real port -- Harbour-Master Eadric's domain, with the only shipwright between Frostfjord and Grimholt. The curved basalt breakwater is older than the settlement, built by hands no one claims, barnacle-crusted below the high-water mark and stinking of bladderwrack in summer. Four longships moor in its lee. Eadric charges a half-mark per hull per week and posts a lighthouse keeper at the Kolvik Light -- a squat iron fire-basket on a soot-blackened stone plinth, the keeper chained to the cliff against the wind. Stonebay (population 23) clings to the top of the Whalebone Cliff, where the pale limestone face is layered with darker bands that look from the sea like the ribcage of something vast and buried. Twenty-three souls whose economy is whale blubber, bone, and rendered oil, accessed by a cliff-walk narrow enough that visitors are rare and welcome is scarce.

Sea fog is the great equaliser. It rolls in without warning, drops visibility to five paces, and makes the cliff-edge indistinguishable from safe ground. The Salt Haar -- thick, salt-tasting, smelling of rotting kelp -- costs bands their mules, their supplies, and their bearings. Storm surge raises the sea two arm-lengths in half an hour, hammering spray across cliff paths and dragging everything loose seaward. The Beinastrand (Bone Beach) south of Stonebay is where the current deposits everything the sea takes: whale bones, dead birds, lost cargo, and fragments of human bone ground smooth by the waves. The smell carries half a mile inland.

The economic lifeline runs on fish, salt, coastal trade, and whatever washes ashore. The Hamargata (Cliff Walk) between Kolvik and Stonebay is twenty kilometres of cliff-top path -- one side heather, the other a sheer drop to rocks and surf. The Hafnarvegr (Harbour Road) to Skaldhaven crosses a rocky headland where storms from the open sea deliver sleet and horizontal rain. The Suðrstrandleið (South Shore Path) from Bleakwater to Kolvik is the only overland connection between the ice-border settlements and the south, walked twice a year by Bleakwater's eighteen inhabitants to trade seal-hides for grain. Everything on the coast is tidal, temporary, and one bad storm from destruction.

## The Moors (Central Lowlands)

The moor begins where the trees stop and the peat starts. South of Thornwall, walking the Mosavegr (Moss Way) toward Moor's End, the ground goes soft. Open heather, spongy underfoot, the suck and release of boots in saturated peat. The horizon is the same flat grey line in every direction. No trees, no rocks, no features except the standing stones -- and those are sinking. The Niðsteinar Circle, two hours from Thornwall, is nine stones set in a rough ring on a slight rise. Three have sunk to knee-height in living memory. Fog pools inside the ring like a bowl, and the stones vanish in a white silence that swallows sound. The charcoal stains at the centre say someone lights fires here. No one in Thornwall admits to it.

Thornwall (population 180) is the moor fortress: Jarl Helga Thornwall's seat, protected by thorn-hedge walls and a stone gatehouse. Sheep market, wool and mutton trade. The settlement exists because the moor needs a centre, and Helga's walls are the only thing standing between the sheep economy and the raiders who would take it. Moor's End (population 31) is the dying hamlet that proves the moor wins. Thirty-one souls scratching a living from sheep and peat, and the road to reach them -- the Mosavegr -- passes through deep peat country where head-high banks of cut peat create a disorienting maze in fog. Every turning looks the same. A laden mule was lost in a sinkhole here in spring. Moor's End is barely worth the journey. It exists because Thornwall needs peat for fuel.

The standing-stone circles predate human memory. The Heiðr-Steinn, one hour south of Thornwall, is a lone granite pillar where shepherds touch the stone for luck before crossing the moor. Lose sight of it in fog and you are already dead. The Drowned Pillars near Moor's End are five stones sinking into peat, the tallest showing a hand's breadth above black water. The Ghost Moor Cairns, ten hours from any shelter, are seven sealed cairns arranged in a pattern that means nothing to anyone alive. Between them, compasses spin and the stars look wrong -- a Veil-field that distorts navigation. None of the seven has been opened.

The moor does things to morale. The flatness, the wind, the endless sucking of boots in peat, the fog that reduces the world to arm's-length -- it grinds men down. The drovers' roads and shepherd tracks are the only paths, beaten flat by generations of flocks. Off the track, the peat swallows. Bog-bodies surface in the cuttings, tanned to leather by the acid water, slow and terribly hard to damage. The Feldwick Peat Grave, three hours from Feldwick, is where peat-cutters exposed a burial pit. The bodies still move. Nobody claims the deep moor because the moor claims everything put into it.

## The Deep (Deepholm Region)

The surface scars tell you what is underneath. Around Ashmark, slag fields and sinkholes pock the forest floor. Burned woodland stretches in every direction from the smelter works. The ground is brittle -- thin soil over worked stone and old mine tailings. A man in armour can feel the hollow beneath his feet. Then the road descends. The Járnvegr switchbacks cut into the hillside, lanterns on iron posts marking the way, and the daylight fades not because the sun is setting but because you are going below it. The tunnel mouth breathes -- a slow exhalation of mineral-damp air that smells of wet rock, copper, and the sour undertone of stale sweat from a thousand years of work underground.

Deepholm (population 520) is the largest settlement in the Rimevegr and the economic heart of the region. A stone fortress with an iron gate and a garrison of forty, built into and through the mountain itself. The Hoard-Pillar -- a squared column of greenstone left standing when dwarven miners cut the hall around it -- serves as the central landmark. Carved tally-marks cover its polished face: weight-records of iron, copper, and tin in dwarven base-twelve notation that Magnus Iron-Counter has been quietly trying to decode. The main trade-hall extends beyond the pillar into torchlit darkness, stalls and workshops hewn from living rock. The air underground never changes -- cool, damp, carrying the constant sound of hammering and the distant groan of settling stone.

The Council of Three governs Deepholm: Magnus Iron-Counter (trade), Sif Halvsdóttir (politics), and Old Brokk (craft). Their rivalry is carved into the stone itself -- Sif's Finger, a natural stalactite-stalagmite column in a side-passage, became her council chamber when the other two refused her a seat in the main hall. The politics run on iron, bronze, silver, and leverage. Everyone in the Rimevegr needs Deepholm's output. No one controls Deepholm. The settlement's defences are depth -- layers of tunnels, choke-points, and the simple fact that an invading force fights underground in the dark against people who have lived there for generations.

The Námuvegr (Mine Road) connects Deepholm to Ashmark through a ravine lined with grey tailings. The Himinstígr (Sky Climb) ascends through upper mining levels to Raven's Perch -- eight kilometres of tunnel giving way to twenty-two kilometres of exposed mountain switchback. The Leynibraut (Smuggler's Way) runs sixty kilometres through a hidden secondary tunnel, out a ravine, and through unmarked deep forest to Vargheim -- knowledge of the entrance is a trade secret worth killing for. The old dwarven construction throughout Deepholm is stone-work no living mason can replicate: fitted blocks, self-draining channels, load-bearing arches that have held for centuries without repair. Deepholm is proof that something was here before the humans, something that built to last, and then left. The humans filled the spaces. They have not improved them.

## The Mountains (Black Pine Range)

The granite spine of the Rimevegr divides the world. West of the range: fjords, coast, salt wind. East: forest, moor, peat. The range itself is bare rock above the treeline -- wind-scoured granite, scree slopes, and air thin enough to make a healthy man dizzy after ten minutes of exertion. The Tindrsteininn, a wind-carved granite obelisk on a bare summit north of Raven's Perch, is visible on clear days from every pass in the range. Frost crystals grow on it year-round. Lightning strikes it regularly; the surrounding rock is scarred with glass-lined channels. Eagle feathers collect in the crack at its base, driven there by updraft.

Raven's Perch (population 74) is the sentinel: Thane Egil Raven-Eye's lookout, stone walls on a narrow approach, defensibility four. The settlement sits above the weather. On clear days, half the Rimevegr is visible below. On foul days, the garrison is cut off entirely, surviving on stored food and stubbornness. The mountain cats -- solitary, silent -- hunt the switchbacks where prey is trapped between scree walls with nowhere to run. The thin air, the constant wind, the vertigo of exposed ridges where the path narrows to boot-width -- the mountains strip away everything except the immediate physical reality of not falling.

Three passes control movement between east and west. Járnskarð is the main trade route, flanked at the summit by the Skarðvarðar -- twin boundary stones placed close enough that a loaded pack-horse cannot pass between them, wind funnelling through the gap hard enough to snatch breath. Kveldsskarð is the emergency winter alternative, less maintained, higher, and used only when Járnskarð is blocked by snow that can bury a man standing. Hrafnaskarð provides access to Raven's Perch itself. Whoever holds these passes controls the traffic between the coastal settlements and the interior -- iron going west, fish and timber going east. In winter, the passes close. The mountain communities survive in isolation or they do not survive.

The Himinstígr (Sky Climb) and the Hrafnaniðrstígr (Raven's Descent) are the two routes to and from Raven's Perch. The climb takes three days in summer and is impassable in winter. The descent is faster: bare rock ridge to scree gully to rime-moor. Mountain weather is unpredictable -- clear skies at the Perch can mean blizzard conditions in the gully. The Eggjasteininn, a flat slab of dark schist driven vertically into a knife-edge ridge, sits at the point where the ridge narrows to a boot-width. Passing it means pressing flat against the stone with empty air on both sides. The Spine Ridge Ossuary, fourteen hours from Grimholt above the treeline, is an ossuary carved into the rock face where dishonoured warriors' bones are stacked in alcoves. Wind through the chambers sounds like screaming. The mountains are the oldest thing in the Rimevegr, and at altitude, the supernatural feels closer. Summit cairns hold ancient offerings that no one removes. Things are seen from summits that cannot be seen from below. The Barrow-King at Raven's Perch sits on his throne three centuries dead, and the map stone in his chamber shows the connections between every barrow in the eastern region.

## The Ice (Northern Extreme)

The world ends here. Not metaphorically -- the land simply stops being land. North of Bleakwater, the last hamlet with a name and a population worth counting, the ground hardens to permafrost and the horizon becomes a white line indistinguishable from the sky. The Svartvatnssteinn stands on the gravel bank where Bleakwater's black glacial runoff meets the frozen shore: a basalt standing stone canted south as if something pushed it from the north, one side glazed with permanent rime that no summer sun melts. The rune-work on the south face is legible -- boundary markers with the repeating glyph for "turn back." The glyph faces south, warning those coming from the deep ice, not those going in. This detail unsettles anyone who thinks about it.

Bleakwater Landing (population 18) clings to the edge of inhabitable land. Icebreak (population 12) is beyond it -- twelve people in dugout shelters at the edge of the permanent ice shelf, invisible until you are almost standing on them, the only marker a thin smear of seal-blubber smoke. Hermit Ragnhild, Icebreak's only permanent resident with any authority, is a seidr practitioner of extraordinary power who offers divination for a blood price and says the Veil is thinning permanently here. The hot spring at Helbrannr, eighteen kilometres south on the Suðrstrandleið, is the only warmth between the coast and the ice shelf -- warm water in a frozen land, and the most valuable camp site north of Kolvik.

The Ísvegr (Ice Road) between Bleakwater and Icebreak is the most dangerous route in the Rimevegr: seventy kilometres of frozen shore, ice shelf, and tundra. In summer the shelf surface is a maze of melt-pools and crevasses hidden by thin snow crusts. In winter it is a featureless white expanse where direction is guesswork without stars. The ice sings -- a deep resonant tone from beneath the shelf, not wind, a sound that travellers who listen too long follow until they lose direction and wander. Blizzards last for days. White-out conditions. Wind chill kills exposed flesh in minutes. Polar bears range the shelf, curious and faster than a running man. The foraging is poor in every season. The shelter options amount to a single ice-cairn camp at twenty-five kilometres, rated poor.

Why anyone would come here: the Icebreak Complex, one hour from the settlement, is a network of interconnected burial chambers linked by ice tunnels older than any human settlement in the Rimevegr, with rune-stones that glow blue when the living approach. The First Barrow, six hours further, is the source of the death-energy network that links every barrow in the territory -- a hill that breathes, that shifts its corridors, that has been collecting the dead since before memory extends. The power at the Veil's edge draws seidr practitioners, desperate scholars, and bands willing to risk everything for artifacts frozen in ice or knowledge sealed in chambers that predate human occupation. The aurora light this far north casts wrong shadows -- multiple light sources from impossible angles, colours that do not exist in the natural sky. The standing stones up here are not markers or memorials. They are anchors. Ragnhild says they hold reality together. Given what the rune-work says, she may be understating it.

## The Barrow Downs (Cross-Regional)

The barrows are not a single region but a condition -- a disease the land carries. Twenty-six known barrows are scattered across every region of the Rimevegr, from the minor Kolvik Barrow on a coastal hill to the First Barrow beneath the northern ice. They are burial sites, most ancient, some pre-dating human settlement, and since the Veil-Thinning began in Y312 they have been waking. The dead are getting out.

Barrow-proximity changes the landscape. Within an hour's walk of an active barrow, the temperature drops. Grass dies in patches. Animals refuse to graze. Corpse-lights drift at dusk -- pale, cold, visible from a distance. The ground around the Ashmark Root-Grave is bare black earth where nothing grows. The Standing Stone Barrow near Moor's End hums with a chanting sound heard from too far away to make out words. The Iron Barrow south of Grimholt opened in autumn and the draugr inside walk in patrol patterns that suggest a military burial -- death has not erased the discipline. The barrows are connected. A death-energy network links them underground, flowing like water through root systems, with the First Barrow as the source. As the source strengthens, the connected barrows stir. Kolvik was first -- dead cattle. The Whispering Barrow followed. The Iron Barrow opened. The pattern moves outward from the ice toward the coast.

The economy of barrow-robbing sustains half the mercenary bands in the Rimevegr. Skaldhaven pays silver for rune-stone rubbings and relics. Deepholm's Archive Barrow contains records that scholars access periodically. The sealed barrows -- the Ghost Moor Cairns, the War-Grave, Salt Cairn, Thornwall Passage -- are the greatest unknowns and the greatest threat. When the network fully activates, every sealed barrow opens simultaneously. The barrows are both the Rimevegr's greatest treasure and its greatest existential risk. Every band that enters one weighs the reward against the cost: silver and artifacts versus broken men, cursed wounds, and the knowledge that the dead remember your face.

## Travel Realities

Travel in the Rimevegr is the core strategic decision a mercenary band makes, repeated with every contract. In Long Summer, a band on maintained road covers twenty to twenty-five kilometres per day. On open moor, fifteen to twenty. In deep forest, twelve to fifteen. Mountain passes, ten. The Ice Road, eight if the weather holds. In Long Dark, halve all numbers and double all risks. Winter travel on the Rime-Moor Road means six days for sixty kilometres -- six days of wind, exposure, and poor-to-zero foraging with two marginal shelters. A band that arrives exhausted is a band that cannot fight.

Food consumption runs at roughly one copper per man per day in stable areas, but foraging quality varies wildly. The Black Pine is the most forgiving terrain -- good foraging in summer, fair in winter, with game, mushrooms, and berries for a band that knows where to look. The moors are poor in every season. The coast is fair in summer, poor in winter, and the mountains and ice are poor year-round. The difference between a three-day crossing with good foraging and a three-day crossing with poor foraging is the difference between arriving fed and arriving hungry enough to accept a bad contract.

The forty-two named routes documented in the route atlas are the band's lifelines. Each route records distance in kilometres, travel days by season, terrain segments with descriptions, seasonal access ratings, hazard types and probabilities, shelter locations with capacity and quality, foraging quality by season, trade traffic levels, and military patrol presence. Choosing a route is a strategic decision with real consequences: the Járnvegr is safe, maintained, and patrolled -- but tolled at both ends. The Leynibraut avoids the tolls but runs through flooded tunnels and wolf territory with no shelter. The Ekkjubraut (Widow's Crossing) crosses open moor where outlaws approach from any direction and vanish into fog. The band that studies its route data before marching spends silver on caution. The band that does not spends blood on ignorance.
