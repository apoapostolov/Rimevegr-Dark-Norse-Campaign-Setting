# Mercenary Lifestyle Database

**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr

---

## 1. Core Concept of Mercenary Life

In the Rimevegr, a Svarthird (mercenary band) is not an army of heroes. It is a moving ledger wrapped in wet wool and iron. Men sell their axes because the alternative is starving in a failing hall or freezing on a barren slope. They follow a captain only as long as the arithmetic works: silver for blood, food for labor, warmth for loyalty. When the coin runs out or the grievances pile too high, the band fractures like ice on a fjord.

Everything is measured:

- The sergeant's charcoal ledger records every copper.
- Foraging quotas decide who eats tonight.
- Grievances (late pay, dead Named Men, broken oaths) are tracked like debts that must be paid in trust or blood.
- The weak link (the slow, clumsy man who still carries weight) is tolerated only while useful.

## 2. Daily Cycle and Rituals (The Ledger Day)

### Morning Quarter (First Light -- Pale Grey)

- Watch change.
- Forager assignment (terrain decides survival).
- Provision count: how many mouths vs. how many scraps.
- Captain decides route and who carries extra weight.

### Midday March

- Silent column through black pine or along frozen fjord paths.
- The weak link slows everyone. Veterans glance but rarely speak until it costs silver or blood.
- Occasional stop to check for barrow signs or whispering runes.

### Evening Fire (When the Pale Sun Fails)

- Men gather under oiled hides or in half-collapsed longhouses.
- Weapon maintenance by firelight.
- Silent chewing of hardtack or whatever the foragers brought.
- Pay ritual when due: Sergeant sits on flat stone or log, purse left, ledger right, small knife stuck in wood. Each man steps forward, receives copper, counts it openly, walks away. Advance-debt men watch their pile come up short and stay silent.

### Night Watch

- Two men minimum.
- Whistling after dark is forbidden (calls the hidden folk).
- The Hush sometimes falls -- all sound dies. Something listens.

## 3. Economy and Scarcity

### Wages (in Silver and Copper)

| Fighter Type            | Retainer (per week) | Mission Pay (per day) |
| ----------------------- | ------------------- | --------------------- |
| Common Axe-Man          | 2 silver            | 5 copper              |
| Veteran                 | 3 silver            | 1 silver              |
| Named Man               | 5 silver            | 1 silver 5 copper     |
| Shield-Maiden (veteran) | 3 silver            | 1 silver              |

### Non-Payment Table (d100, checked when wages 14+ days late)

| Roll   | Consequence                                             |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| 01-15  | D3 unhappy men desert at night. May sell information.   |
| 16-30  | Named Man demands written debt acknowledgment.          |
| 31-50  | Band becomes Shaken for the week (-5 to all).           |
| 51-70  | Sergeant confronts captain. Command check or Morale -1. |
| 71-85  | Quiet muttering. No immediate effect.                   |
| 86-100 | Men accept. Next non-payment rolls twice.               |

### In-Kind Payment

Pelts, grain, thralls, salted meat -- acceptable for up to three weeks. Fourth week requires Persuade or Command check (difficulty rising). Failure = Morale drop.

### Loot Division by Archetype

| Archetype          | Captain | Named Men | Veterans | Commons |
| ------------------ | ------- | --------- | -------- | ------- |
| Tyrant Hird        | 60%     | 20%       | 12%      | 8%      |
| Standard Svarthird | 40%     | 25%       | 20%      | 15%     |
| Fraternal Band     | 25%     | 25%       | 25%      | 25%     |
| Kin-Clan           | 20%     | 20%       | 25%      | 35%     |

### Tribute from Settlements

Compare band strength to settlement fighters (2:1 advantage = easy). Demand with Persuade (negotiation) or Intimidate (fear). Intimidation advances Feud Track faster and damages Standing more.

**Settlement Decay** Repeated tribute or occupation shrinks settlements: Town -> Large Village -> Village -> Hamlet -> Deserted Ruins.

## 5. Band Archetypes

1. **Rural Mob** -- Fishermen, farmers, woodcutters turned axe-men. Strong in wilderness, weak far from home. Trust-held.
2. **Tyrant Hird** -- Ruled by fear and one strong captain. Effective but unstable. Fear-held.
3. **Military Hird** -- Former huscarls or Varangian-style veterans. Professional, higher pay expectations.
4. **Kin-Clan** -- Blood or shield-siblings. Extremely loyal internally, dangerous when blood is spilled.

## 6. Recruitment

- Search takes one quarter-day at a settlement.
- Recruitment pool by settlement size (hamlet d4, village 2d4, etc.).
- Quality roll per recruit (terrain and training grounds modify).

## 7. Contracts and Work

Common types:

- **Escort** -- Protect longship or merchant through contested fjords.
- **Barrow Clearing** -- Enter restless barrow, deal with whatever stirs.
- **Weregild Collection** -- Enforce blood-price on a rival clan.
- **Hall Protection** -- Guard a jarl through the Long Dark.
- **Raid** -- Hit a rival settlement for silver or thralls.
- **Rune Investigation** -- Investigate whispering rune-stone or berserker sightings (rare, high pay, high risk).

Payment always includes advance for mission pay. Breach damages reputation across the region.

---

## 8. Named Men and Internal Politics

Every band has 2-6 Named Men with:

- Earned call-name (Goat, Hook, Coldhand, Shepherd).
- Private Agenda (recover lost kin land, repay blood debt, find a specific rune-stone).
- Trigger (being treated as weak, broken oath, harm to kin).
- Loyalty 1-5 (drops when Trigger ignored).

The captain must balance these agendas or risk quiet betrayal.

---

## 9. The Weak Link

Every band has one -- the slowest, clumsiest, least proven member. Tolerated only while useful. Veterans watch the weak link's clumsiness with flat contempt. The weak link carries extra weight, takes the worst watch shifts, and eats last when food is short. Survival depends on proving value -- volunteering for hard work, foraging successfully, or simply being tough enough to keep up without complaining. The day a common becomes a net drain on the band's survival arithmetic is the day they get left behind.

In Voss's Black Axes, that position belongs to Lump -- a burly, aging ex-thrall who compensates for his clumsiness by carrying double weight and keeping his mouth shut.

## 10. Mercenaries and Horses

Most mercenary bands in the Rimevegr are not cavalry. They are infantry who may own one or two mounts between them, usually for the captain, scouts, or the quartermaster's most time-sensitive work. Horses are too expensive to waste in a shield-wall grind and too vulnerable to treat as free mobility. A good captain thinks in terms of when a horse should be used, when it should be spared, and what pace the band can sustain without destroying the animal before the real fight begins.

### What Horses Are For in a Band

- Getting a captain or scout to the next contract before rivals do
- Carrying spare food, rope, hide, iron, and loot that would otherwise slow the band
- Running messages between settlement, camp, and advance screen
- Pursuing routed foot in open ground
- Rapid withdrawal when the contract goes bad and distance must open now

They are not for vanity rides around camp. A mercenary horse that has no job is an expense, not an asset.

### The Working Horse Types Bands Actually Want

| Band Role        | Preferred Breed                    | Why                                                |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Captain's mount  | Rimefjord pony or Southblood cross | Steady seat, enough presence, enough brain         |
| Scout mount      | Moor-runner                        | Speed and recovery matter more than load           |
| Pack mount       | Pine-cob                           | Carries band wealth without drama                  |
| Mountain courier | Spine pony                         | Survives where a better-looking horse dies         |
| Prestige charger | Southblood warhorse                | Useful only with open ground, feed, and discipline |

The hard truth is that a Pine-cob often makes a poorer band richer than a Southblood warhorse does. Pack capacity and reliable movement keep the ledger stable. Glory does not.

### Camp Horse Discipline

Every competent band develops horse rules fast.

- The same handler feeds and checks the same horse whenever possible.
- Horses are rubbed down before the rider eats if the march was hard.
- Tack comes off immediately after danger passes; nobody leaves a saddle on to save time.
- Hooves are checked every evening on stone or frozen ground routes.
- The horse line is placed downwind of the sleeping line but inside the perimeter if raiders are possible.
- Grain is locked. Hungry men steal food; desperate men steal horse feed.

Mercenary bands learn quickly that horse care is not separate from camp discipline. A loose picket, bad girth, or ignored hoof bruise becomes a combat problem two days later.

### Mounted Roles Inside a Band

The captain's horse is usually political first and tactical second. It lets him move between foragers, rear guard, scouts, and negotiation sites without spending his own legs. The scout's horse is operational gold. It multiplies the band's eyes and shrinks uncertainty. The pack horse is the ugliest and most valuable animal in camp because it carries spare food, tents, arrows, cooking iron, and the loot that turns a contract into profit.

When a band has only two horses, the usual distribution is simple: captain plus scout, or scout plus pack. If the captain insists on the best animal while the scout rides trash, he is prioritizing rank display over survival math and the veterans will notice.

### Horse Loss and Replacement Logic

Mercenary bands are ruthless about replacing mounts because they have to be. A horse that goes permanently lame may be sold cheap, shifted to pack duty, traded to a farmer, or slaughtered if the band cannot carry its keep. Sentiment exists, but winter arithmetic wins most arguments.

Bands track horse value in three layers:

- purchase value: what the animal cost
- working value: what jobs it can still do
- campaign value: what losing it would do to the current contract

A mediocre horse can have high campaign value if it is the only mount in band. A superb horse can have low campaign value if the terrain is all forest, bog, and barrow tunnels.

### Mercenary Horse Lore

Mercenary riders care less about breed purity than civilians do. They breed and buy for function, then invent stories afterward. Still, some opinions are near-universal:

- Never trust a horse that sweats before the work starts.
- A mount that hates one rider may still serve another perfectly.
- A horse that refuses a barrow path gets one second look before anyone laughs.
- The first winter tells the truth about a horse.
- A rider with good hands can borrow speed; he cannot borrow nerve.

### Dog Data to Track

For any recurring mercenary band with mounts, track at minimum:

- breed
- sex
- age
- primary role
- mood baseline
- active conditions
- bond rider
- two or three defining traits
- one or two trained tricks
- whether the horse is band property, personal property, or borrowed

## 11. Mercenaries and Dogs

Dogs fill the gap between what mercenaries can see and what they can notice in time. A good dog cannot replace a scout, but it can make every scout more dangerous and every camp harder to surprise. Poor bands are more likely to own a dog than a horse because the feed burden is lower and the utility begins immediately.

### What Dogs Are For in a Band

- Barking the camp awake before men on watch can finish failing
- Holding trail on fugitives, scouts, or stolen stock
- Finding game, corpses, lost kit, and wounded men in bad ground
- Guarding food stores, baggage, and tether lines from thieves
- Harrying routed foot or isolated archers in open-country fights

Dogs are also morale objects whether mercenaries admit that or not. Men who share nothing share scraps with a camp dog. That matters in long weather.

### Dogs Bands Actually Want

| Band Role           | Preferred Breed             | Why                                               |
| ------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Camp alarm          | Fjord hound or hall mastiff | Voice and suspicion matter more than beauty       |
| Tracker             | Bog tracker                 | Holds sign where men lose it                      |
| War dog             | Black Pine wolf-dog         | Grit, bite, and intimidation                      |
| Hunter-courser      | Moor lurcher                | Fast kill on hare and fast warning on open ground |
| Hall or wagon guard | Hall mastiff                | Stays put and makes theft noisy                   |

### Camp Dog Discipline

- One handler feeds and corrects the dog whenever possible.
- Dogs sleep where they can scent the approach, not where they are warmest.
- A dog that barks all night at nothing is retrained, moved, or culled.
- Dogs are watered before scraps are handed out after a hard march.
- War dogs are kept away from horse lines unless deliberately cross-trained.
- Nobody strikes a proven camp dog in anger and stays popular for long.

Mercenary bands notice quickly whether a dog improves discipline or reveals its failure. A quiet camp with a good dog is safer than a louder camp with two extra men on watch.

### Dog Loss and Replacement Logic

Dogs are cheaper to replace than horses and harder to replace than fools think. Any cur can bark. Very few can track under rain, ignore camp chaos, take commands in blood, and still lie quietly by the fire after. Bands usually replace dogs faster than mounts because working dogs bond fast and age fast, but a veteran tracker or war dog leaves a real capability hole.

Bands track dog value in the same three layers they use for horses:

- purchase value
- working value
- campaign value

A low-price mongrel that always wakes before raiders has higher campaign value than a bloodline kennel dog that cannot settle in a mixed camp.

### Mercenary Dog Lore

- A dog that dislikes one man may be telling the truth.
- A tracker dog with a bad handler is just an expensive pet.
- If the dogs go silent together, wake everyone.
- A war dog that loves every stranger is not a war dog.
- Never buy a "wolf dog" from a man who is smiling too hard.

- whether the dog is band property, a specific member's dog, or a hall loan
