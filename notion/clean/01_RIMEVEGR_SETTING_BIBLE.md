# Rimevegr Setting Bible

**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr
**Tone:** Authentic early-medieval Norse (Viking Age / late Iron Age Scandinavia) + a thin layer of deniable, grounded supernatural dread (the Veil Ceiling).
**Core Feeling:** A world drowning in perpetual twilight, rime-fog, superstition, and the long cold. The gods are silent but their absence is felt in every empty sky. Life is short, measured by the ledger, the axe, and the fire. Glory is a lie the dead tell themselves.

**Purpose:** Everything unique about the Rimevegr that makes it more than a generic Viking setting. Assumes familiarity with historical Norse culture (9th--11th century Scandinavia). Documents only what **differs from, replaces, or extends** real-world baselines. If historical Norse practice applies unchanged, it is not repeated here.

---

## The World at a Glance

**Name:** The Rimevegr (literally "The Rime-Way" or "The Frost Road")
**Era:** Roughly equivalent to 850--1050 CE Norse world, but collapsed and frozen in eternal twilight.
**Magic Level:** Extremely low and terrifying. Three Norse traditions survive: galdr (rune scribing), seidr (spirit talking), and wyrd-reading (fate divination). Magic always costs blood, sanity, or the land itself.
**Technology:** Iron-age / Viking. Longships, iron axes, wooden halls, thrall labor, rune-stones, limited steel. No plate armor, no gunpowder.
**Climate:** The sky cracked centuries ago. A grey-white rime-mist called the Veil rolled south from the endless northern ice and never fully lifted. Summers are short, damp, and grey. Winters are endless. The sun is a pale ghost behind cloud and fog.

The world feels one bad winter away from total collapse. People survive by clan loyalty, blood-oath, and the quiet arithmetic of silver, food, and warmth.

---

## What This Setting Is

- **A mercenary-survival Norse setting.** The core lens is hired people keeping themselves fed, paid, and alive in a world where every settlement needs them and fears them.
- **A post-sacred world.** Faith remains culturally total, but divine silence has hollowed certainty out from inside the old forms.
- **Low magic, high consequence.** The supernatural is real enough to wound bodies, alter choices, and deform memory, but rare enough that most people deny it until it is in the room.
- **An economic pressure-cooker.** Weather, supply, oath-keeping, kinship, hospitality, and debt matter as much as axes.
- **Grounded dread.** Horror appears as pressure, omen, hunger, memory, and cost -- not spectacle.

## What This Setting Is Not

Explicit exclusions to prevent generic-Viking drift:

- **Not a raiding culture.** The band does not raid for plunder. It is hired for specific contracts with defined terms. Raiders exist -- Hadric's Three Wolves burn villages -- but the band's identity is professional, not predatory.
- **Not Tolkienesque.** No elves, dwarves, or ancestral evil. The only non-human entities are the ambiguous dead. Magic is a trade, not a destiny.
- **Not grimdark for its own sake.** The world is harsh because the economics are harsh. People are not gratuitously cruel. Violence serves pragmatic ends. Cruelty exists but is represented as a failure mode, not an aesthetic.
- **Not mythological.** The gods are a cultural framework, not active participants. No prophecies drive the plot. No chosen ones exist. The band's fate is determined by its ledger, its contracts, and its captain's decisions.
- **Not a world with a dark lord.** No singular antagonist threatens the world. The threats are local, economic, and human (with rare supernatural edges). The enemy is winter, debt, and other men who also need to eat.
- **Not a setting where heroes win.** The band survives. Sometimes surviving means winning. Usually it means paying a price that could have been higher.

## The Foundational Divergence

### The Cracking

No one alive remembers the exact year, but the sagas agree: the gods went silent.

First came the Long Winter that never ended. Then the sky split with a sound like iron breaking. From the north poured the Veil -- a living rime-mist that swallowed light, muffled sound, and made the dead restless. Crops failed. Longships vanished between fjords. Barrows cracked open. Runes began to whisper back when read aloud.

The great halls burned or froze. Jarls became warlords. Clans fractured. The old trade routes died. What remained were small, hard settlements clinging to fjords and rocky slopes, protected by wooden palisades, superstition, and the axes of men willing to sell them.

This is the world three centuries later. The Veil is thinner in summer but never gone. The gods are still silent. People still sacrifice, still carve runes, still fear the dark -- because sometimes the dark answers.

**What changed:**

- Temples still stand. Sacrifices still happen. No one answers. The rituals continue because the alternative -- admitting the rituals are empty -- is worse.
- The Veil between the living and whatever lies beyond became unstable. Before the Cracking, the dead stayed dead. Now they sometimes do not.
- Magic (galdr, seidr, rune-work) still functions. This is the cruelest evidence: the tools the gods gave still work, but the gods themselves are gone. The workshop is open. The craftsman has left.
- No apocalypse followed. The world continued. Crops grew. Snow fell. Trade happened. The silence was not an ending but an abandonment. Life goes on, cheaper.

### The Consequence for Society

Historical Norse society was organized around reciprocal obligation -- to kin, to jarl, to gods. The Cracking broke the divine leg of the triangle. What remains:

- Oaths still bind because men enforce them, not gods. The rope-and-eye mark on a contract stone is a social mechanism. If Odynn enforces it, that is bonus.
- **Fatalism deepened.** Historical Norse fatalism (wyrd, the sense that fate is set) became harder and more transactional. The world owes nothing. You pay for what you get. What you do not pay for, you do not keep.
- **Community contracted.** Without divine enforcement of social bonds, loyalty contracted to what is immediate and verifiable: kin, band, contract. The wider society -- the idea of a people with shared divine purpose -- eroded. Jarls rule because they have warriors, not because gods sanctioned them.

## Gods and Divine Practices

### The Silent Ones -- Pantheon of the Rimevegr

The gods went silent when the sky cracked. No one knows whether they died, fled, or simply stopped caring. People still sacrifice. Still carve their names on rune-stones. Still curse by them. The temples are cold and the statues stare at nothing.

What follows is what the people of the Rimevegr believe. Whether any of it is true is a question the novel never answers directly.

### The High Court (Aesrgrim -- The Grim Gods)

#### Odynn the One-Hung

God of gallows, knowledge-through-suffering, and debts that cannot be repaid. Depicted as a man hanging upside-down from a dead ash tree, one eye socket empty and weeping black sap.

**Domain:** Sacrifice, painful wisdom, binding oaths, the price of knowing.

**How people invoke him:** Oaths sworn on pain of death. When a man says "Odynn take my other eye if I lie," he means it. Those who break such oaths are said to hear rope creaking in the dark.

**What silence means:** Odynn stopped answering because the world ran out of truths worth the pain of learning them. Or because someone found one that broke him.

**Svarthird relationship:** Mercenary captains swear contracts in his name. The rope-and-eye mark is carved on contract stones. Breaking a contract sworn on Odynn is career-ending -- not because of divine punishment, but because other bands will never trust you again.

#### Thurr the Crack-Handed

God of thunder that splits stone, of violence that solves nothing but must happen anyway, and of the last stand when running is no longer possible.

**Domain:** Battle-fury, storms, desperate last acts, the sound of iron on bone.

**How people invoke him:** Before fights. The invocation is a closed fist struck against the chest, hard enough to bruise. Berserkers claim Thurr rides them -- most likely they are simply terrified men who have forgotten how to stop swinging.

**Depiction:** A broad man with cracked hands (split knuckles, broken fingers that healed wrong). No weapon -- Thurr fights with what he has. His eyes are closed because he cannot bear to see what his hands have done.

**What silence means:** Thurr was always silent. He was never the god who spoke. He was the god who hit things until they stopped moving. His silence is indistinguishable from his nature.

#### Fraeja the Grief-Bride

Goddess of the grieving, of barren fields, of women who outlive their husbands and sons. Also -- reluctantly -- of fertility, but only the kind that comes after loss. The first crop after a field burns. The child born the winter after a plague.

**Domain:** Grief that becomes endurance, bitter fertility, the seidr tradition (she taught women to speak with the dead, which was her cruelest gift).

**Depiction:** A woman with a dry face (she wept until she could not weep, and the tears became the Veil-fog). She holds a distaff in one hand and a child's skull in the other.

**How people invoke her:** Mostly by women, mostly in silence. A mother who loses a child buries a lock of hair and does not speak for three days. That is a prayer to Fraeja.

**What silence means:** Fraeja is the goddess of silence after loss. She was the first to go quiet. Some say she is the only one still awake -- and that the Veil is her grief made visible.

#### Lopt the Scar-Mouth

God of lies that are true, of truths no one wants to hear, of the friend who betrays you for your own good, and of fire that burns the house so the family escapes into the cold.

**Domain:** Necessary cruelty, uncomfortable truth, arson, the breaking of things that needed breaking.

**Depiction:** A man with his lips sewn shut (he told the other gods something they could not bear to hear, and this was their answer). His eyes are open and laughing.

**How people invoke him:** They do not. Invoking Lopt is admitting you need to destroy something. People who speak his name spit afterward. Runestones to Lopt are carved upside-down.

**What silence means:** Lopt's silence is the worst of all, because Lopt was the one who warned them. Whatever he said before they sewed his mouth -- that was the truth the world needed.

**Svarthird relationship:** Spies, poisoners, and quartermasters who cook the books are called "Lopt's fingers." It is not a compliment. It is an acknowledgment.

#### Hael the Half-Rotted

Goddess of the dead, of the barrows, of the space between dying and being forgotten. Not evil -- indifferent. The dead go to Hael not as punishment but because there is nowhere else.

**Domain:** Death, barrows, the restless dead, the slow decay of memory, the moment you realize no one alive remembers your grandfather's face.

**Depiction:** A woman whose left side is living flesh and whose right side is green-grey bone. She sits on a stone chair in a hall with no fire.

**How people invoke her:** Only to bargain. "Hael, give me one more winter" is a plea spoken by the sick and the old. The price is always unstated. Draugr are Hael's subjects who refused to sit down.

**What silence means:** Hael was never loud. Her domain is patience. She is the only god people are certain still exists, because the dead still walk.

### The Low Court (Vanrgrim -- The Bitter Gods)

#### Tyvr the Stump-Armed

God of law, of justice that costs a hand, of the thing-assembly where grudges are weighed in silver and blood.

**Domain:** Law, weregild, oath-keeping, the terrible math of justice (how much silver is a son worth? Tyvr knows).

**Depiction:** A man with one hand. The other is inside the mouth of a wolf that no longer exists -- the wolf ate the hand and then Tyvr killed the wolf, but the hand stayed gone. Justice costs. Tyvr paid.

**How people invoke him:** At thing-assemblies. Jarls who rule justly are called "Tyvr-handed." Those who do not are called "wolf-fed."

#### Skathi the Bone-Walker

Goddess of winter, of the hunt, of the mountain that does not care whether you summit or die halfway up.

**Domain:** Winter survival, hunting, the indifference of high places, the clarity that comes from being very cold for a very long time.

**Depiction:** A woman made of ice shards. No face -- just the shape of one behind frost. She carries a bow strung with sinew from something that was once a god.

**How people invoke her:** Hunters leave the first kill's liver on a flat stone. If it freezes before dawn, Skathi accepted. If animals eat it, Skathi is indifferent. Both are considered acceptable outcomes.

#### Bragi the Unstrung

God of poetry, of the saga, of the lie that is truer than fact. Mirror-twin to Lopt -- Lopt forces truth into the present, Bragi preserves it in the past. Lopt tells truths that sound like lies. Bragi tells lies (sagas) that sound like truths.

**Domain:** Poetry, memory, the saga tradition, the performance of grief that makes it bearable, the lie a mother tells a child about where father went.

**Depiction:** A man holding a harp with snapped strings, the broken ends coiled sharp as wire. His hands are steady. His mouth works fine. The sound is what died -- the melody that gave words their shape. Without strings there is only the voice, alone and flat. The greatest saga-tellers are called "Bragi's echo" -- they carry the words, but the music is gone.

**How people invoke him:** Skalds pour mead on the ground before performing. They do not ask for inspiration. They ask for accuracy -- to remember the dead as they were, not as we wish they had been.

#### Heimr the Gate-Blind

God of thresholds, of the boundary between inside and out, of the watchman who cannot sleep and cannot see.

**Domain:** Borders, warning, the moment before disaster when someone should have spoken up, the horn that was not blown.

**Depiction:** A man standing at a gate with his hand on a horn he will never lift. His eyes are open but filmed over white -- he sees everything and recognizes nothing.

**How people invoke him:** Door-posts are carved with his mark (a vertical line through a circle). The mark wards nothing. But people sleep better with it there.

**What silence means:** Heimr was supposed to warn the gods of the end. He saw it coming and said nothing. Whether this was failure or mercy depends on the teller.

### How the Pantheon Functions in the Novel

#### What Characters Say

People invoke the gods constantly in speech -- curses, oaths, casual blasphemy, desperate pleas. These are cultural reflexes, not theological statements.

- "Thurr's cracked hands" -- an oath of frustration
- "Fraeja take her" -- spoken at a burial (women and children)
- "Odynn's rope" -- the worst oath; unbreakable
- "Hael's chair" -- death
- "Lopt's mouth" -- someone is lying or hiding something
- "Tyvr weigh it" -- let justice decide
- "Heimr saw nothing" -- no one warned us in time

#### What the Novel Shows

The gods are never confirmed or denied. What the novel shows:

- Rune-stones sometimes speak. This could be galdr residue, not divine action.
- The dead sometimes walk. This could be the Veil, not Hael.
- Berserkers sometimes do impossible things. This could be adrenaline and madness, not Thurr.
- Seidr practitioners sometimes see true futures. This could be pattern recognition, not Fraeja.

The Veil Ceiling is always deniable. The reader should never be certain a god is real. The characters should always act as though they might be.

#### What Thorne Believes

Thorne is the band's rune-reader. He treats the gods with the same flat pragmatism he treats weather. They are conditions, not beings. "The runes say what they say. Odynn has nothing to do with it. Odynn is a story we tell so carving runes makes sense."

This is the novel's philosophical center: the gods may be dead, but the world they left behind still works by their rules.

#### What Dalla Fears

Dalla has seidr. She speaks to the dead -- or something that answers. She is not sure the distinction matters. For Dalla, the question is not whether Fraeja is real. The question is whether the voice that answers when she calls is Fraeja, or something wearing Fraeja's name.

### Forbidden Renderings

1. **Never confirm a god exists.** The novel maintains ambiguity.
2. **Never have a god speak directly.** Rune-stones speak. The dead speak. Dreams speak. The gods do not.
3. **Never use the gods as plot devices.** No divine intervention, no miracles, no deus ex machina.
4. **Never have a character lose faith as a dramatic beat.** In the Rimevegr, faith is not a thing you lose -- it is a habit you perform because the alternative is worse.
5. **Never explain the Cracking.** Why the gods went silent is the unanswered question of the setting. Answering it would collapse the ambiguity that makes everything work.

## Geography and Regions

### The Rimevegr as Place

The Rimevegr is not a country. It is a fractured region -- a cold northern territory with no unified government, held together less by law than by route access, weather windows, and trade need. Named after the Rime Road, the old north-south artery that once connected its settlements, the name has widened into the word for the land itself.

At atlas scale, the territory resolves into a few hard geographic truths. A granite spine divides west from east. To the west lie the fjords and coast -- black-water highways in calm weather and death-traps in storms. To the east lie the Black Pine and the interior working lands, where cover, timber, charcoal, and wolves shape movement. The centre is moor and peat. The far north hardens into permafrost and ice shelf, where the Veil grows thickest. Beneath all of it runs a second geography of mines, tunnels, and cut-stone halls around Deepholm. For a mercenary band, geography is never backdrop. It is contract math: shelter, forage, exposure, tolls, and how much cold can be survived before the silver runs out.

### Terrain

| Type      | Character                                      |
| --------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Coast     | Fjords, rough surf, tidal traps, salt wind     |
| Forest    | Dense, ancient, trackable game, fallen timber  |
| Moors     | Open, exposed, wind-scoured, nearly barren     |
| Mountains | Rock, ice, cliff faces, thin air, crevasses    |
| Bog       | Soft ground, navigation failure, drowning risk |
| Ice       | Frozen rivers, lakes, thin-ice traps           |

No formal roads. Mud tracks between settlements. Paths close during Long Summer overgrowth, freeze into ice-ruts during Long Dark. Travel distances measured in days, not miles -- because terrain and weather change the conversion constantly.

### Named Regions

**The Inner Fjords** Deep, narrow inlets where most people live. Wooden longhouses cluster on rocky slopes. Longships are the only reliable transport. Fog clings to the water even at midday. Villages are small (20--150 people). Jarls rule from smoky halls.

**The Black Pine Wilds (Outvegr)** Endless forests between settlements. The trees are ancient and close. Sound dies quickly. Travelers speak of "the Hush" -- moments when every bird stops singing at once. Mercenary bands (Svarthird) are the only ones who regularly cross these woods.

**The High Rime-Moors** Treeless uplands above the tree line. Wind never stops. Rime coats everything. Only the hardiest herders and the mad live here. Barrows are common and sometimes open.

**The Northern Ice-Reach** Almost no one goes there. The Veil is thickest. Some say the gods retreated there. Berserkers driven mad by battle-fury and worse things are said to walk the ice.

**Ruins of the Old Kingdoms** Scattered stone longhouses, broken ring-forts, and half-sunken halls from before the Cracking. Most are avoided. Some contain old rune-stones that still bleed when a traitor passes.

### Named Locations (Established)

| Name           | Type       | Status           |
| -------------- | ---------- | ---------------- |
| Ashfall barrow | Barrow     | Cleared by band  |
| Feldwick       | Village    | Burned by Hadric |
| Kolvik         | Settlement | Burned in spring |

Additional locations generated during writing. Rule: place names are compound descriptors (what it looks like, what happened there, what grows there). Examples: Ashfall, Feldwick, Frostfjord Hollow. Never ornate. Never mythic.

## Calendar and Time

### The Two Seasons

The Rimevegr has two seasons, not four.

| Season      | Duration  | Character                          |
| ----------- | --------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Long Summer | ~60 days  | Light, heat, mud, insects, growth  |
| Long Dark   | ~300 days | Cold, snow, short light, endurance |

**Long Summer** is not summer as the real Norse experienced. It is an intense, compressed growing season. Near-constant daylight. Midnight sun. The world does not cool at night. Sleep becomes irregular. Tempers shorten. Green things grow aggressively -- paths close in days. Insects are vicious. Mud replaces ice. Rivers swell. The change from Long Dark is violent -- ice breaks, flooding, the first flies.

**Long Dark** is 300 days of diminishing and then returning light. Not unbroken darkness -- short days exist, getting shorter until midwinter, then longer again. The cold is the physical constant. Fire is the center of life. Travel contracts to essential movement. Everything costs more energy to accomplish. Perpetual twilight, heavy rime, deep snow. Travel is by longship or frozen rivers. Hunger is constant.

### The Transition

The shift between seasons takes roughly one week. It is not a date on a calendar but a felt change: first frost that does not melt by midday (into Long Dark), first day where the mud is warm (into Long Summer). The transition is dangerous -- infrastructure built for one season fails in the other. River crossings that worked in winter are impassable in thaw.

### Month Names

Named months exist. Characters use them in speech. Month names carry implied weight -- readers learn meaning through context, not exposition.

| Month      | Implied Character                       |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Ironmoon   | Hard cold, deep Long Dark               |
| Barrowrise | Restless dead, Veil at thinnest, danger |

Additional months are generated as needed during writing. Rule: month names are compound words from physical or supernatural conditions, never abstract concepts. They name what the month does to people, not what people feel about it.

### Year Length

360 days. No leap-year adjustments. The calendar drifts slightly from astronomical reality over centuries. Nobody cares. Precision astronomy died with the god who watched the stars.

## Animals and the Natural World

### Domesticated

- **Horses:** Rare for the band. Expensive (20--30 silver). Jarls ride. Mercenaries walk. A mounted man is either a scout, a commander, or showing off.
- **Pack mules:** The band's logistics backbone. Carry supplies, the cook's gear, the ledger. Losing a mule is a logistics crisis.
- **Dogs:** Settlement animals. Guard dogs, herding dogs. The band does not keep dogs -- another mouth to feed.

### Wild

- **Hares, deer, game birds:** Hunted for food.
- **Wolves:** Territorial threat. The Rimevegr's wolves are larger and bolder than historical Norse wolves -- the Long Dark gives them desperate hunger. A wolf attack on a weakened band is a real threat, not a metaphor.
- **Ravens:** Cultural symbol (associated with Odynn, battlefield scavengers). Seeing ravens after a fight is the only acknowledgment the dead receive from the natural world.

### Supernatural Fauna

- **Something in the barrows** that may or may not be draugr. Size, nature, and behavior are inconsistently reported by survivors.
- **No dragons, no trolls, no elves.** The Rimevegr does not include classical Norse mythological creatures as real entities. The creatures of saga survive only as stories people tell. Whether the stories were ever true is not addressed.

## Technology and Craft

### What the Rimevegr Lacks

Historical Norse culture was technically sophisticated (clinker-built ships, pattern-welded swords, advanced textile production). The Rimevegr preserves the knowledge but **production has declined** since the Cracking:

- **Fewer master smiths.** Good steel is harder to find. Weapons are repaired more than replaced. A well-made sword is generations old.
- **No shipbuilding tradition** in the inland Rimevegr (coastal settlements still build, but the band's world is landlocked travel).
- **Textile production continues** but is local and small-scale. No trade in fine cloth. Wool is functional, not luxurious.

### What the Rimevegr Preserves

- **Iron-working** at the settlement level. Every large village has a smith. Quality varies. Repair work is more common than new production.
- **Woodcraft.** Buildings, palisades, simple tools. The Long Dark demands structural competence -- a poorly built shelter kills.
- **Herb-lore.** Medical and practical knowledge of wild plants. Dalla's skill set. Passed through oral tradition. Not magic, but looks like it to the uneducated.
- **Rune-carving** as both magical practice and the only form of written record beyond the charcoal ledger. Rune-stones mark territory, seal contracts, ward locations.

### Writing and Literacy

Most people are illiterate in the conventional sense. Runes are known but primarily as magical/ritual marks, not as a writing system for daily use. The charcoal ledger (numbers, names, simple notation) represents the practical edge of band-level literacy. Dagfinn signed his contract with an X. This is not unusual.

## Seasonal Rhythm, Weather, and Environmental Pressure

The Rimevegr's weather is not backdrop. It is a strategic actor. Command decisions, settlement economics, and survival margins all change by season, terrain, and visibility.

### Full Month Cycle

The 360-day year is divided into twelve 30-day months:

1. Frostwake (Long Summer)
2. Rimeblood (Long Summer)
3. Veilthin
4. Ashfall
5. Ironmoon
6. Wolfmoot
7. Barrowrise
8. Longnight
9. Hearthstar
10. Bloodtide
11. Skaldsong
12. Yuledeep

In practice, most people track the year in three layers at once:

- Season (Long Summer / Long Dark)
- Month-name (ritual and agricultural cadence)
- Immediate weather reality (what can be done today)

### Operational Weather Types

| Weather       | Narrative Effect                              | Tactical Consequence                       |
| ------------- | --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Clear Grey    | Flat light, long sightlines, exposed movement | Baseline movement and ranged effectiveness |
| Rime-Fog      | Sound dampens, distance collapses             | Navigation errors, ranged penalties        |
| Light Rain    | Mud and damp accumulation                     | Slower pacing, gradual fatigue             |
| Driving Snow  | Whiteout edges, frozen surfaces, low morale   | Severe mobility loss, frostbite pressure   |
| Rime Storm    | Route denial, shelter emergency               | March collapse, high injury risk           |
| The Hush      | Total silence, acute superstition             | Fear spikes, command friction              |
| Veil-Thinning | Air "wrongness", omen density rises           | Wyrd-leaning behavior, ritual caution      |
| Blood Sun     | Psychological dread and animal stillness      | Decision paralysis, terror in weak bands   |

### Environmental Hazard Bands

- **Fjords and coast:** tide windows, cliff exposure, sea-fog, cold water shock, storm surge.
- **Black Pine:** low visibility, route confusion, wolf pressure, root hazards, Hush incidence.
- **Rime-moors:** exposure death, sinkholes, orientation failure, shelter scarcity.
- **Mountains:** pass closures, scree falls, altitude fatigue, choke points vulnerable to ambush.
- **Ice-reach:** whiteout and crevasse risk, extreme cold lethality, very low forage, delayed rescue likelihood.

### Strategic Weather Rule

A band that plans by map alone fails. A band that plans by weather, terrain, and shelter sequence survives. In the Rimevegr, route quality is defined less by distance than by whether the men can arrive fed, warm, and still able to fight.

## Political Structure

### No Kingdoms

The Rimevegr has no kings. Historical Norse kingdoms (Denmark, Norway, Sweden as consolidating entities) do not exist here. Power is local and personal.

**Jarls** rule towns and the territory those towns can project force over. A jarl's domain extends exactly as far as his warriors can march in a day and return. Beyond that, the land belongs to whoever is standing on it.

**Headmen** run villages by consensus and pragmatism. Not elected, not hereditary in a formal sense -- the headman is whoever the village trusts to deal with armed strangers. Usually the oldest competent landowner.

**No feudal chain.** There is no hierarchy of jarl to under-jarl to thane to freeholder. A jarl is a jarl. A headman is a headman. They deal with each other as independent powers, trading favors and threats. Alliances are personal, temporary, and transactional.

### The Thing-Assembly

Historical Norse thing-assemblies survive but weakened. Tyvr's silence removed the divine sanction that gave legal rulings their weight. A thing still meets. Disputes are still weighed. But enforcement is now entirely temporal -- the jarl who hosts the thing enforces its rulings only insofar as it serves his interests.

**Weregild** (blood-price) remains functional. The price of a life can still be calculated, still be paid, still buy peace. But the price is negotiated, not fixed. What a man's life is worth depends on who is asking and who is answering.

### Factions

Factions are shown through observable power: who owns the hall, controls the grain, carries better weapons, has more men, and can still get food moved in winter. Political opinions are never articulated as ideology. A faction wants land, silver, influence, leverage, security, or revenge. Those are the only political positions.

The setting's defined powers fall into a few recognizable flavors:

- **Fjord jarls and hall-powers** such as Hrothgar of Frostfjord: transactional rulers focused on shipping lanes, gate control, winter security, and keeping paid violence pointed outward.
- **Iron-road militarists** such as Ordovast of Grimholt: discipline- first powers who want chokepoints, patrol routes, and expansion made durable through force.
- **Widow-court operators** such as the Pale Widow of Ashen Reach: influence brokers who prefer intelligence, debt, rumor, and pressure applied from the side rather than open war.
- **Trade stabilizers** such as Deepholm's ruling interests: powers that want routes open, metal moving, and conflict managed rather than glorified. Their politics is infrastructure.
- **Border traditionalists** such as Ulf Vargson and Helga of Thornwall: local rulers defined by forest-right, herd protection, walls, fuel, and the stubborn defense of regional autonomy.
- **Rival svarthird bands** such as the Three Wolves, Bone Pack, Silent Oar, Iron Tide Remnant, Grave Wardens, and Red Tide: mobile factions with distinct flavors -- terror, kin-loyalty, professional discipline, veteran hardness, funerary zeal, or predatory raiding.

No one in the Rimevegr calls this ideology. These are habits of rule, survival plans, and preferred instruments of pressure. A faction's "political plan" is the pattern of things it is trying to make permanent: safer roads, wider tribute, tighter control, better trade, ancestral protection, or a rival's slow ruin.

The band is a tool, not a faction. It has a price, not an opinion. When the band takes a contract from a faction, it serves that faction's interests. When the contract ends, so does the alignment. Loyalty is proportional to pay.

## Society and Social Structure

### Classes

The historical Norse free/thrall/jarl hierarchy exists but is simpler and harsher.

| Class     | Rimevegr Version                              |
| --------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Jarl      | Rules a town. Has armed men. Has grain stores |
| Free      | Owns land or a trade. Can leave               |
| Bondi     | Free farmer. Backbone of settlement economy   |
| Thrall    | Unfree. Debt-bound or captured. Can be sold   |
| Landless  | Free but owns nothing. Seasonal labor. Drifts |
| Svarthird | Mercenary. Outside the land-based hierarchy   |

**Thralldom** is more economic than racial. A thrall is someone who cannot pay their debts. Capture in raids creates thralls. Children of thralls are born thrall. Freedom can be purchased -- the price is whatever the owner names. There is no abolitionist sentiment. Thralldom is regarded as bad luck, not injustice.

A thrall who earns freedom through valor can become a freedman and eventually a full member of the band.

**The landless** are the setting's underclass -- free men with no property, no kin network, and no prospects. They drift between settlements looking for seasonal work. In good times, they labor. In bad times, they steal. Some join mercenary bands. The band's commons are recruited from this pool.

### Women

Historical Norse women's relative freedom (property rights, divorce, household authority) persists. In the Rimevegr, the practical pressures of the Long Dark push further:

- Women fight when circumstances require it. Not as a norm, but without the cultural prohibition found in most medieval settings. Petra and Ash serve in the band without special comment -- because the band cannot afford to refuse competence based on gender.
- Seidr is traditionally female. Male seidr-practitioners exist but carry stigma (the historical _ergi_ taboo survives in weakened form). Thorne avoids the stigma by framing his rune-work as a craft, not seidr.
- Widows control property. A widow with land and grain is a political power. The Pale Widow is an employer because she has resources and no husband to spend them.

Strength matters more than gender. A shield-maiden who proves herself is treated as any other warrior.

### Superstition as Law

- Never whistle after dark (calls the hidden folk).
- Never speak the name of a drowned man on a ship.
- Break a blood-oath and the land itself turns against you.
- A rune-stone that bleeds when you pass is a warning.
- The dead do not always stay dead -- especially in barrows near old battlefields.

These are not metaphors. In the Rimevegr, most are old wisdom. The rest are true in ways that will kill you.

## Law and Justice

### Without Divine Enforcement

Historical Norse law derived moral authority from the gods. With the gods silent, law is explicitly human -- enforced by whoever has enough armed men to make rulings stick.

**What survived:**

- The thing-assembly as a forum for disputes.
- Weregild as a mechanism for pricing lives and injuries.
- Oath-swearing as the primary legal instrument.
- Outlawry (expulsion from legal protection) as the ultimate punishment.

**What changed:**

- No lawspeaker claims divine mandate. The law is what the strongest jarl says it is, and it changes when the jarl changes.
- Outlaws are more common. Without divine stigma, outlawry is a practical condition (no settlement will harbor you) rather than a spiritual exile.
- Blood-feuds escalate faster. The thing-assembly can arbitrate, but enforcement is optional. If a family rejects the thing's ruling, the alternative is violence. There is always the alternative of violence.

### Feud Escalation

| Level          | Description                                 |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Cold (0)       | Grievance exists but is contained           |
| Tense (1)      | Prices rise, being followed, cold treatment |
| Hostile (2)    | Gates close, camp outside walls             |
| Blood-feud (3) | Ambush risk, no safe passage                |
| Vengeance (4)  | Open war, kill on sight                     |

Feuds are permanent within the novel timeline. Settlements remember. The band's actions in one village reach nearby villages within weeks. Reputation spreads.

## Economy

### Currency

Silver and copper coins. No standard mint -- coins are weighed, not counted. A silver coin from one jarl weighs roughly the same as one from another, because traders refuse light coin. Copper is small change. Worn thin from use.

For everyday reckoning, the setting uses a stable baseline of **10 copper = 1 silver**. That is the standard rate used in contracts, simulation, and ordinary market pricing when people need a shared mechanical assumption. In practice, the exchange still flexes by village and season -- especially in poor settlements where copper is local and silver is trusted by weight.

Gold exists only at the top end of exchange. The accounting baseline is **1 gold = 10 silver**, but actual gold-breaking rates vary by market. In strong trading settlements the exchange may come close to full value; in thin villages a moneychanger may only offer eight to nine silver, or refuse to break gold at all.

### Barter

Most rural transactions are barter. Silver is for mercenaries, merchants, and jarls. A farmer pays in grain, labor, or goods. A village that cannot produce silver pays for mercenary services in food, quarters, and information.

### Prices (Relative Scale)

| Item                 | Approximate Cost | Context                    |
| -------------------- | ---------------- | -------------------------- |
| Day's food (1 man)   | ~1 copper        | Grain price in stable area |
| Week's food (1 man)  | ~1 silver        | Core setting anchor        |
| Signing a recruit    | 2 silver         | Advance against future pay |
| Week's band salary   | 3 silver + Named | Standard contract rate     |
| A good sword         | 10--15 silver    | Months of a common's pay   |
| A horse (decent)     | 20--30 silver    | Luxury. Most bands walk    |
| Weregild (common)    | 15--25 silver    | Depends on who values them |
| Weregild (Named Man) | 50+ silver       | Functional deterrent       |

Prices serve narrative, not simulation. They appear through in-scene transactions. Never listed as a roster.

### Trade

Trade exists but is not the economic engine it was in historical Norse culture. The Cracking disrupted long-range trade networks. What remains:

- **Local trade:** Between settlements. Surplus grain for iron goods. Fish for timber. Functional and small-scale.
- **Long-range trade:** Exists but dangerous and rare. Caravans need armed escorts (a contract type). Luxury goods (metals, good steel, southern cloth) are expensive because they travel far through bad country.
- **No merchant class** as a distinct social group. Trading is something farmers and smiths do when they have surplus. Dedicated traders exist but are individual operators, not a guild or class.

## Customs and Social Rituals

### Oaths

Oaths are the primary legal technology. In the absence of divine enforcement, oath-breaking carries only social cost -- but social cost in the Rimevegr is devastating.

- **Odynn-sworn oaths** (rope-and-eye mark) are the strongest. Breaking one ends a career.
- **Blood-oaths** (blood exchanged on iron) bind individuals. Personal, not institutional.
- **Verbal oaths** witnessed by Named Men carry weight proportional to the witnesses' reputation.

### Hospitality

Historical Norse hospitality laws survive but are conditional. The host provides fire, shelter, and food. The guest provides restraint and news. The exchange is explicit and temporary.

**Rimevegr addition:** Armed guests are a different category. A mercenary band at the gate is not a guest -- it is a negotiation. Quartering terms must be agreed before weapons are put down. The band's standing with the settlement determines the quality of the offer.

### Naming

**Given names** are Norse-standard. Characters use single names, not patronymics (patronymics exist but are not used in daily speech among the band).

**Call-names** are unique to the setting. Earned through action or habit -- "The Door" (Kell, for holding the barrow entrance), "Hook" (earlier call-name), "Cold-Eye" (Voss, for the clouded eye and the temperament), "Ledger" (Gest, for obvious reasons). Call-names replace given names in daily use for established fighters. Receiving a call-name is informal promotion -- it means the band has noticed you.

### Marriage

Marriage in the Rimevegr is a property transaction. The ceremony is a witnessed contract: terms, bride-price, inheritance rules. Love is irrelevant to the institution (though not to the people in it). Divorce exists. Either party can dissolve the contract by returning the bride-price or equivalent.

**For mercenaries:** Marriage is rare and impractical. Most band members are unmarried. Some have partners in settlements they visit seasonally. These are not relationships the band acknowledges or the narrative romanticizes.

### Entertainment

- **Bone dice.** The primary gambling game. Played nightly at the fire-circle. The game itself matters less than the social dynamics around it -- who wins, who cheats, who loses temper.
- **Stories.** Snorri tells stories from former campaigns. Quality varies. The value is warmth and distraction, not literary merit.
- **Songs.** Rare. Men hum on the march when morale is high. Actual singing is a morale indicator.
- **Skalds.** Exist in settlements. Professional entertainers who pour mead before performing (invocation of Bragi). The band has no skald. Stories are amateur.

## Warfare

### What Differs from Historical Norse Combat

**No shield-wall culture.** Historical Norse warfare centered on the shield-wall -- massed infantry formations. The Rimevegr's political fragmentation means no army is large enough to form proper shield-walls. Fighting is small-unit: 5--30 fighters in open terrain, ambushes, skirmishes, raids. The band-level fight, not the army-level battle.

**Professional mercenaries, not seasonal raiders.** The band fights year-round, not during raiding season. This changes everything -- the men are more skilled but more worn. Equipment is maintained obsessively because replacement is expensive and difficult. Every fight costs something that is hard to recover.

**No warships.** The Rimevegr's action is inland. Ships exist along the coast, but the band's story is on foot. The march, not the voyage. Mud tracks, not trade routes.

**Mixed weapons.** No weapon uniformity. Each man carries what he owns, inherited, or looted. The band's equipment is a patchwork -- axes, swords, spears, seaxes, knives. Armor is rarer. Chainmail is wealth. Most men fight in leather and hope.

### Killing Economics

Every fight costs the band something irreplaceable:

- Wounded men march slower, eat the same, cannot fight.
- Dead men leave gaps in the roster. Recruitment is slow.
- Equipment damaged in combat costs silver to replace.
- Morale drops with casualties even when the band wins.

The best captains avoid fights. The second-best captains win fights quickly and cheaply. The worst captains fight often. The ledger records the difference.

## Daily Life

### The Fire-Circle

Where historical Norse life centered on the longhouse, the mercenary band's life centers on the fire-circle -- the nightly campfire around which the band eats, talks, gambles, and establishes hierarchy. Seating at the fire is social positioning. Changes in who sits where are significant events. The fire-circle is the band's longhouse, carried in the arrangement of bodies.

### Serving Order

Food is served in strict hierarchical order: captain first, Named Men, veterans, commons. Serving order is never deviated from. It is how the band makes invisible hierarchy visible twice a day. When serving order changes, the power structure has changed.

### The Stew

The band's primary meal is a communal stew managed by the cook (Dalla). The stew's thickness is a readable supply indicator -- thin stew means short rations. The stew is also a social institution. The men gather around it. Stories are told over it. Disputes happen near it. The stew-pot is the closest thing the band has to a hearth.

### Kit Inheritance

When a man dies, his useful equipment is immediately redistributed. Boots, weapons, armor, coins -- everything transfers. The dead man's name is not spoken for a while. Then someone tests it at the fire. His material existence is absorbed by the band before his social existence is acknowledged as ended.

### Food and Drink

Where it differs from historical Norse diet:

- **Foraging is survival, not supplement.** Historical Norse communities had stable agriculture. The band is mobile and eats what it can find, buy, or hunt. Foraging is life-or-death, not variety.
- **Terrain determines diet.** Coast: fish, tide-pool harvest, nets. Forest: tracked game, mushrooms (possibly edible). Moors: almost nothing. Ice: nothing at all.
- **Ale is weak and rare.** Historical Norse mead-culture does not apply. The band drinks weak ale when it can find it, water when it cannot. Drunkenness is a luxury the band rarely affords.
- **Dried provisions.** Salt fish, hard bread, dried strips of whatever was available. Flavor is not a consideration. Weight-to-calorie ratio is.

### Shelter

- **No longhouses on the march.** The band sleeps rough: tents, lean-tos, caves, abandoned buildings, open air with cloaks. Shelter quality degrades with morale and weather. Good shelter is a morale event.
- **Settlement quartering.** When the band stays in a settlement, quartering arrangements reveal the band's standing: the hall (honored guests), the barn (tolerated), the yard (unwelcome), outside the walls (hostile).

### Clothing

Wool and leather, as historical. The Rimevegr adds:

- **Clothing is armor.** Layers for warmth are also impact padding. A thick wool tunic over a leather jerkin is both winter gear and combat protection. The distinction between civilian and military dress barely exists.
- **Repair over replacement.** Everything is patched, restitched, waterproofed with fat, dried by the fire. A man's clothing tells his history -- each patch is a journey, each repair is a winter survived.
- **No fashion.** No decorative elements. No jewelry except functional (brooches that pin cloaks, arm-rings that store portable wealth). Aesthetics is a settlement luxury.

### Hygiene

Historical Norse peoples were notably clean by medieval European standards (weekly bathing, combs, hygiene tools). In the Rimevegr:

- **Cleanliness is aspirational.** The band cannot maintain historical Norse hygiene standards on the march. Washing happens when water is available and warm enough. Most of the time, men are dirty.
- **Lice and parasites** are a constant. Delousing is communal and unstigmatized -- a camp activity like sharpening weapons.
- **The sick are the cook's problem.** Dalla manages illness with herbs, heat, and practical knowledge. Serious illness on the march is a logistics crisis, not a medical one. A sick man who cannot walk is a decision about whether to wait.

### Daily Realities on the March

- Marching in rime-fog with wet wool that never dries.
- Sleeping in half-collapsed longhouses or under oiled hides.
- Counting every scrap of food.
- Watching the man next to you for signs he will break when the barrow opens.
- The quiet knowledge that tomorrow's meal depends on today's axe.

## Mercenary Life in the Rimevegr (The Svarthird)

Mercenary bands are the only real power that moves between settlements. They are called Svarthird ("Black Hird") because they wear dark wool and iron and leave black reputations behind.

### Band Formation and Structure

A captain gathers men who can swing an axe and keep a promise.

**Historical divergence:** Real Norse society lacked a distinct mercenary class (raiders/warriors served jarls directly). The Rimevegr's political fragmentation created the economic niche. Jarls need military force they cannot permanently afford. Bands fill the gap.

**Structure:**

- **Captain:** Commands, negotiates contracts, keeps final say. Authority is competence-dependent -- one disastrous contract and the men recalculate.
- **Named Men:** Specialists and officers. Extra pay, voice in decisions, established reputations. Veterans with earned call-names (Goat, Hook, Coldhand, Shepherd). Each has a private agenda and a Trigger that can shatter loyalty.
- **Veterans:** Proven fighters with time in the band. Reliable enough to not need watching.
- **Commons:** New or unproven. Do the worst jobs; carry the most weight. Die first.

### The Weak Link

Every band has one -- the slow, clumsy, or unskilled man who still carries weight. The veterans tolerate him only while he is useful. Contempt is constant but rarely spoken. In Voss's Black Axes, that position belongs to Lump.

### The Ledger

Every band has a sergeant who keeps the charcoal ledger. Pay rituals are sacred. Foraging quotas are life or death. Grievances (late pay, dead Named Men, broken oaths) are tracked and must be addressed or the band will break.

### Band Economics

A band costs approximately 1 silver per day in basic operating expenses (food, replacement gear, minor bribes). A band that cannot cover its burn rate takes worse contracts, which destroys it faster. The ledger -- the financial record -- is the band's heartbeat.

### Reputation

Bands live and die by reputation. Contract oaths sworn on Odynn (rope-and-eye mark on contract stones) carry weight because breaking one means no other band will deal with you. Reputation is not abstract honor -- it is an economic asset that determines what contracts a band can bid on and what price it can demand.

**Band names:** Most bands are known by their captain ("Voss's band") or by a descriptor that stuck ("The Three Wolves"). Band names are casual, not heraldic. They change when captains change.

### Contracts

- Protecting a jarl's hall through winter.
- Clearing a restless barrow.
- Escorting a longship through contested fjords.
- Collecting weregild from a rival clan.
- Raiding for thralls or silver when a jarl cannot pay in coin.
- Investigating rune-stones or berserker sightings (rare, high risk).

Pay is silver, thralls, salted meat, or a share of plunder.

### The Code

No glory. Only the ledger, the oath, and the fire. Break the oath and the land remembers. Fail to pay the men and they remember.

## The Supernatural Layer

### The Veil

The boundary between the living world and whatever lies beyond. It was stable before the Cracking. Now it thins unpredictably. Some old galdr-workers insist it feels less like weather and more like failed craft -- as if the Veil had once been built to hold and now is coming apart at the seams.

**What thinning looks like:** Reality becomes unreliable. Shadows at wrong angles. Sounds that should not be there. Distances that shift. Temperature drops without weather cause. Fire burns blue at the edges. The air tastes of old metal.

**Veil intensity varies with:**

- Season (thinnest in Long Dark, especially midwinter).
- Location (barrows, old temples, places where blood was spilled in quantity).
- Unknown factors that the characters cannot predict -- sometimes the Veil thins for no discernible reason.

### Veil Ceiling Phenomena

These are the rare, grounded supernatural elements of the setting:

- **Whispering Runes (Galdr Echoes):** Some carved rune-stones speak when read aloud -- usually in riddles, sometimes in lies. These are remnants of ancient galdr, still resonating centuries later.
- **Restless Barrows:** A barrow may open if disturbed. The draugr inside is not mindless; it remembers old grudges.
- **Berserker Fury:** Rare warriors who enter a killing frenzy that strips away fear and pain but costs them their reason. Not supernatural -- but those who have seen one swear the man's eyes change.
- **The Hush:** In the deep woods, sound can die completely for minutes at a time. Some hushings end in breaths, some linger for hours, and old foresters swear a few places go mute every season in nearly the same way. Something is listening.
- **Veil-Touched:** Some people are born with pale eyes and an affinity for wyrd-reading -- they sense the threads of fate. They are feared as much as respected. Volvas (seeresses) and rune-carvers fall into this category.
- **The Pale Sun:** On certain days the sun appears blood-red and does not warm. Animals refuse to move. Wise folk stay indoors.

All Veil Ceiling phenomena are rare, subtle, and grounded in fear and survival. No dragons. No wizards in towers. Only the cold, the fog, and the sense that something older than the gods is still watching.

### Magic as Trade

Historical Norse magic (seidr, galdr, rune-work) exists but is framed as skilled labor, not mysticism.

| Tradition | Practitioner | Character                       |
| --------- | ------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Galdr     | Galdr-worker | Wound-closing, wards, rune-work |
| Seidr     | Seidr-wife   | Speaking to dead, divination    |
| Rune-work | Rune-reader  | Interpretation, barrow-reading  |

**Key divergence from historical Norse magic:**

- Magic has a **bodily cost.** Galdr-workers lose sleep for days. Seidr-speakers bleed from the nose. Something is subtracted each time. The cost is cumulative and invisible until it is not. Old practitioners in hard districts often look years older than their age, with pale scar-lines in the hands where no blade touched.
- Magic **still works** after the Cracking. This is unnerving. The tools function without the toolmaker. Does this mean the gods are not needed? Were they ever?
- Practitioners negotiate their services like mercenaries. Terms, conditions, price. A galdr-worker joining a band is a contractor, not a follower. They keep separate fire, set their own terms, take full share.
- **The ergi taboo** (historical stigma against men practicing seidr) survives in weakened form. Male practitioners avoid the word "seidr." Thorne calls his work "reading" -- a deliberate linguistic dodge.

### The Restless Dead

Draugr are not monsters. They are dead people who did not stay dead. Hael's subjects who refused to sit down.

- Draugr appear near barrows and places of violent death.
- They are physical -- they can be fought, blocked, burned.
- Their presence correlates with Veil-thinning but is not exclusively supernatural. Some may be explained by mundane desperation (madmen, the lost, the feral).
- The novel never confirms what they are. Characters have theories. None are validated.

### The Veil Ceiling (Prose Rule)

Supernatural content stays rare -- the exception, never the norm. It is always rendered as ambiguous -- deniable, explainable, possibly mundane. The effect is dread without certainty. When a rune-stone hums, it might be the stone cooling in cold air. When a dead man walks, it might be a survivor who crawled out. The reader decides. The novel does not.

## Norse Magic -- The Three Traditions

Magic in the Rimevegr is rare, feared, and always costs something.

### Galdr (Rune Scribing)

The art of carving runes and speaking words of power. Practitioners (galdramenn) must know the old rune-forms and speak the activation chants. Effects are practical: wards against intrusion, strengthening a blade, marking a trail that only the carver can follow. The cost is always blood and willpower. Miscarved runes can twist their intent or draw attention from things best left undisturbed.

Galdr is the most accepted magical tradition -- every jarl has at least one rune-carver. But even they are watched carefully.

### Seidr (Spirit Talking)

Communion with the dead, land-spirits, and hidden presences. Practiced mainly by women (volvas) and deeply stigmatized for men (considered ergi, unmanly). A seidr practitioner enters a trance and speaks with things that should not be spoken to. The spirits answer -- but their help always comes with strings.

Seidr can reveal hidden truths, locate lost things, or commune with the recently dead. The cost is sanity and social standing. Prolonged practice changes a person -- they begin to see things others cannot and hear whispers when alone.

### Wyrd-Reading (Fate Divination)

The most common and least stigmatized tradition. Casting lots (runestones drawn from a bag), reading entrails, interpreting omens in bird flight or weather patterns. Wyrd-reading does not control fate; it reveals the threads already woven. The knowledge gained is always true but never complete, and knowing your fate does not mean you can escape it.

Every band has someone who can cast lots, even if they deny believing in it. In the Rimevegr, no one truly disbelieves.

## Death, Grief, and Memory

### How the Rimevegr Handles Death Differently

Historical Norse culture had elaborate burial practices (ship burials, grave goods, memorial stones, funerary feasts). The Rimevegr's mercenary context strips this:

- **No rituals for commons.** Dead commons are stripped of useful gear and left where they fell, or buried in shallow graves if the band has time. No marker. No words.
- **Named Men get a name spoken.** Someone says the dead man's name once, at the fire. That is the funeral. The band cannot afford more.
- **Objects carry the dead.** The man is gone. His boots are on someone else's feet. His knife is on someone else's belt. The dead persist through their possessions appearing on the living. This is not sentiment -- it is logistics. But it functions as memorial.
- **No mourning scenes.** Grief is shown through absence: the gap in the column, the empty seat at the fire, the moment when someone almost says the dead man's name and stops.

### Memory

In the Rimevegr, memory is fragile and contested.

- **The saga tradition weakened.** Skalds still exist. Stories are still told. But with the gods silent, the frame that gave sagas their authority (divine witness, cosmic stakes) is gone. Sagas now are what they always secretly were: lies that sound like truth, told by men who were not there.
- **The ledger replaces the saga.** What endures is not the story but the record -- Gest's charcoal ledger, with its dates, transactions, names, and numbers. The band's memory is accounting.
- **Individual memory is unreliable.** Characters remember the past differently. No flashback resolves the disagreement. The novel's position: the past is what the present needs it to be.

## Route Network and Choke Points

The Rimevegr's routes are named because they kill enough travelers to require memory and warning.

### Major Strategic Routes

- **Járnvegr (Iron Road):** Deepholm-Grimholt corridor; highest traffic, toll-controlled, militarily contested.
- **Beinvegr (Bone Road):** Frostfjord-Ashen Reach ridge route; exposed, wind-heavy, politically sensitive.
- **Svartfuruvegr (Black Pine Way):** forest crossing through wolf and outlaw territory; navigationally punishing in fog.
- **Mosavegr (Moss Way):** Thornwall-Moor's End axis; sinkhole and exposure risk, poor retreat options.
- **Hafnarvegr (Harbour Road):** coast-fjord commercial artery; storm vulnerability and traffic dependence.
- **Ísvegr (Ice Road):** Bleakwater-Icebreak approach; existential risk corridor where weather can erase a band in a single day.

### Passes that Define Power

- **Járnskarð:** primary mountain crossing; who holds it controls east- west material flow.
- **Kveldsskarð:** hard-winter fallback pass; less stable, more lethal.
- **Hrafnaskarð:** access spine to Raven's Perch; essential for highland surveillance and deterrence.

### Travel Doctrine

Distance is not the planning unit. The planning unit is:

1. shelter interval
2. forage reliability
3. weather failure margin
4. enemy observation risk

Any route that fails two of these is a gamble, not a plan.

## Factions, Rival Bands, and Power Blocs

Power in the Rimevegr is distributed across settlement authorities, mercenary companies, and transient pact structures.

### Notable Rival Svarthird Bands

- **The Three Wolves:** terror-led occupation force, high intimidation, weak internal trust.
- **The Bone Pack:** kin-loyal forest specialists, dangerous at night, politically cautious.
- **The Silent Oar:** disciplined ex-military formation, efficient and professional.
- **The Hollow Hall:** under-resourced contract-takers surviving on adaptability and low burn.
- **Iron Tide Remnant:** reduced but hardened veterans, reputation still stronger than their numbers.
- **The Grave Wardens:** barrow specialists with narrow manpower and deep funerary competence.
- **The Red Tide:** slave-taking raiders; tactically sharp, strategically isolated by universal hostility.

### Major Political Authorities

- **Jarl Hrothgar (Frostfjord):** transactional fjord power, pragmatic and calculating.
- **Warchief Ordovast (Grimholt):** expansionist military organizer, coercion-first governance.
- **The Pale Widow (Ashen Reach):** covert influence broker using intelligence and asymmetry over direct war.
- **Jarl Sigrun (Deepholm):** economic stabilizer leveraging market gravity and infrastructure.
- **Jarl Ulf Vargson (Vargheim):** territorial traditionalist centered on forest authority and wolf doctrine.
- **Jarl Helga (Thornwall):** moor defense strategist balancing levy obligations and settlement survival.

### Three Emerging Regional Blocs

1. **Iron Grip (Ordovast):** military consolidation model.
2. **Fjord Compact (Sigrun-aligned):** economic interdependence model.
3. **Whispering Circle (Pale Widow):** covert manipulation model.

No bloc is stable. Their friction drives contracts, betrayals, border violence, and shifting employer legitimacy.

## Barrow Atlas and Network Dynamics

Barrows in the Rimevegr are a distributed system, not isolated ruins. Their activations exhibit regional patterning and apparent network flow.

### Regional Barrow Pressure Points

- **Frostfjord region:** Whispering Barrow, Draugr-Jarl crypt phenomena, and early-stage wake indicators.
- **Grimholt region:** Iron Barrow and war-grave mass-risk potential.
- **Deepholm region:** archive-linked barrows preserving operationally valuable records.
- **Coastal belt:** tide-exposed burial sites with intermittent access and high attrition risk.
- **Moor region:** standing-stone-linked barrows with unstable Veil behavior and navigation distortion.
- **Forest region:** root-grave complexes and low-light maze effects.
- **Mountain region:** high-grade command barrows including Raven's Perch throne-site patterns.
- **Ice region:** Icebreak complex and deep-source anomalies.

### Network Model (In-World Understanding)

The prevailing model among informed actors is that:

- barrows are linked by a death-energy substrate,
- activations propagate in waves rather than random isolated incidents,
- sealed barrows represent delayed risk rather than dormant safety,
- intelligence from archives, map-stones, and seidr witnesses must be combined to predict large-scale wake events.

### Strategic Consequence

A single barrow contract is never local for long. Clearing, disturbing, or failing a barrow site changes regional risk forecasts.

## Hidden Pressure Patterns (Narrative Reality)

Behind visible politics, the setting is continuously shaped by hidden or delayed pressures that surface through action, not exposition.

### Five Persistent Hidden Categories

1. **Betrayal pressure:** loyalty erosion, desertion plans, factional realignment.
2. **Rival movement pressure:** opportunistic raids, contract races, ambush timing.
3. **Supernatural pressure:** barrow wake cycles, omen chains, Veil-edge events.
4. **Political pressure:** alliance drift, coercive tribute, proxy conflicts.
5. **Environmental pressure:** pass closures, flood routes, severe winter attrition windows.

### Surface Signals that Matter

These pressures appear indirectly through:

- changes in gate protocol,
- unusual patrol density,
- altered market behavior,
- sudden settlement caution,
- recurring omen reports from unrelated locations.

In a healthy reading of the setting, these are not flavor details. They are early warning indicators of structural change.
