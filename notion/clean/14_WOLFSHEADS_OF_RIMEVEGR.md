## The Word

> _The Lawspeaker did not raise his voice. He never did. He
> recited the sentence the way he recited anything -- from
> memory, in the same cadence he used for boundary disputes and
> inheritance claims. The words were old and they carried their
> own weight. They did not need his help._
>
> _Ragnvald stood in the assembly circle and listened to himself
> being unmade. He stood with his shoulders straight because his
> father had taught him to stand that way, and his father was
> dead, and the man who killed his father was standing three
> rows back in the crowd with clean hands and a witness-oath
> that said the killing was fair._
>
> _The Lawspeaker finished. The crowd was quiet. Someone coughed.
> A child asked a question and was hushed._
>
> _Ragnvald had until the assembly closed to leave. After that
> anyone who saw him could kill him like a dog and owe nothing
> for it. He walked out of the assembly ground and the crowd
> parted and no one touched him and no one spoke to him and
> that was worse, somehow, than if they had thrown stones._

The Rimevegr has a word for a man declared outside the law. The Lawspeaker says it at the Thing and the man stops being a man. He becomes _vargr_ -- wolf. Not a figure of speech. A legal reclassification. The man has hair, teeth, a name, debts, a woman somewhere who remembers what he looked like when he smiled. None of that matters. The Lawspeaker said the word and the word is final. Kill him on the road. Kill him in his sleep. Take his boots. Nobody owes wergild for a wolf.

## Two Kinds of Wolf

### The Lesser Outlaw

The _fjörbaugsmaðr_ is exiled for three years. He keeps his property. He has three designated safe places -- his farm, a kinsman's hall, the road between them. Everywhere else he is fair game, but he has a path back. Pay the life-ring -- one silver ounce, the _fjörbaugr_ -- seek passage out of the Rimevegr on three ships each summer, and the law lets you come home when the three years are up.

Miss a passage. Shelter in a _vé_. Fail to pay. Your sentence upgrades. Permanently.

The lesser outlaw lives in a trap made of rules. The rules are precise and the consequences for breaking any single one are the same: full outlawry. The system was designed so that one mistake -- one failure to observe a provision -- upgraded the sentence permanently. A man convicted of lesser outlawry who sheltered in a kinsman's temple because it was the only dry place within a day's walk was discovered there and made a full outlaw. The route from "three years of exile with a path back" to "kill on sight forever" was one procedural violation wide.

### The Full Outlaw

The _skógarmaðr_ -- forest-man -- is dead in the eyes of the law while his body keeps breathing. All property confiscated. A court convenes a bowshot from his home, splits what he owned: half to the plaintiff, half to the local Thing. What remains of his family gets fed from the Thing's share -- if anyone bothers.

No one may shelter him. No one may feed him. No one may trade with him. Anyone who does risks their own outlawry. He is barred from halls, from temples, from the company of people, from "house, cave, and every world except from Hell."

There is no time limit. A man who has been outlaw for nineteen years is no closer to freedom than a man who was sentenced yesterday. The Rimevegr does not forgive. It does not forget. It waits.

### Vargr í Véum -- Wolf in Holy Places

Kill a man at the Thing. Draw a blade in a _vé_ -- any sanctified ground where the holy peace holds. The sentence is immediate, unappealable, and permanent. The man does not get to walk out of the assembly ground. He does not get a bowshot's grace. He is seized right there, or he runs and is hunted, and the hunting never stops because he struck at the place where law itself is made.

A man who commits violence where the _vébǫnd_ -- the holy ropes -- mark the boundary of the assembly ground is not a criminal. He is something beyond criminal. He has attacked the root of everything that keeps the Rimevegr from being wilderness populated by armed men. The sentence reflects that. He is _vargr í véum_ -- wolf in holy places. The worst category.

## How Men Become Wolves

### At the Thing

Most outlawry is litigated. A plaintiff stands at the Thing and says old words in the proper order. The accused answers, or does not. Oath-helpers step forward, or do not. The Lawspeaker recites the applicable provision from memory. If the procedural forms are observed -- the right words, at the right assembly, with the right witnesses -- the court rules.

A case can die on a technicality. Wrong wording. Wrong assembly jurisdiction. Summons delivered improperly. The system is not about justice. It is about procedure and weight -- the weight of the men willing to stand behind your oath.

A rich man with good oath-helpers can deflect a killing that everyone in the valley watched happen. A poor man with no allies can be outlawed for an act that any reasonable person would call self-defense. The thing that matters is not what happened. It is who stands up when your name is called.

### The Captain's Problem

A Svarthird captain can be declared outlaw for what his men do. If your man kills a farmhand under your banner, the dead man's kin can bring the charge against you. You gave the order. You collected the pay. You carry the name. The law does not ask which hand held the blade.

This makes every contract a legal exposure. The captain who burns a granary on a patron's orders is the captain who stands at the Thing when the ashes cool. The patron is not there. The patron's name is never in the charge.

## What Happens Next

### The First Night

He walks away from the assembly ground and the crowd parts and no one touches him and within an hour he is on a road that used to be his road and none of the faces he passes acknowledge him.

He has what he carried to the assembly. The clothes on his back. A knife, maybe. A cloak. Whatever food is in his belt-pouch. That is all he will ever own again because everything else -- the farm, the livestock, the stored grain, the tools, the iron kettle his mother carried from the old country -- belongs to the Thing now.

He walks north, or south, or inland. He does not know where he is going because there is nowhere to go. Every hall in the Rimevegr is closed to him. Every fire he sees through the trees belongs to someone who will earn no penalty for killing him.

### The First Winter

The Long Dark does not negotiate.

A man without shelter, without stored food, without fuel, without anyone willing to share a fire -- that man has two months. Maybe three if he is strong and lucky and finds a cave with a southern exposure.

The cold comes in stages. First the fingers and toes go numb and do not warm back. Then the thinking slows. The man makes decisions that a warm man would not make -- walks the wrong direction, leaves a fire burning when he should hide it, falls asleep in a place where falling asleep means not waking up. Then the shivering stops, which means the body has given up trying to warm itself. Then sleep. Then nothing.

The ones who survive the first winter are the ones who found a fortress, formed a band, or had a woman brave enough to risk her own outlawry by leaving food at a cairn in the dark. The ones who survived two winters are rare enough that men remember their names. The ones who survived a decade are legends precisely because what they did should have been impossible.

### Fire

The outlaw's dilemma: fire means warmth and cooked food, but smoke is visible for miles across the Rimevegr's open ground. A fire at night draws anyone looking for you. A fire by day marks your position for every settlement within sight of the ridge. The man who lights a fire announces himself. The man who does not light a fire freezes.

Most outlaws in the Rimevegr burn fires briefly and in concealed positions -- hollows, deep ravines, cave mouths angled away from the wind. Some survive on raw food for weeks at a time, gnawing frozen meat from a stolen carcass and drinking snowmelt. The ones with island fortresses can burn fires freely because the water protects them. Everyone else gambles every time they strike a spark.

## Outlaw Bands

### How They Form

A fresh outlaw has nowhere to go and no one to trust. Most die alone. The ones who survive long enough to find each other do so by accident or desperation. Two men hiding in the same cave. A lesser outlaw whose sentence escalated, joining a full outlaw who has been living on stolen sheep for a season. A deserter from a Svarthird hird who cannot go home without a rope.

The band grows the way mold grows. No one plans it. No one wants it. It happens because men who are hunted alone are easier to kill than men who are hunted together.

### What They Look Like

An outlaw band is not a Svarthird company. There is no contract. No patron. No banner. No legitimate authority except whatever the strongest man imposes on the rest.

| Feature      | Svarthird Company         | Wolfshead Band                          |
| ------------ | ------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Authority    | Captain, chain of command | Strongest man until someone kills him   |
| Income       | Contracts, tribute, trade | Raiding, extortion, black-market trade  |
| Shelter      | Settlements, strongholds  | Caves, islands, ruins, forest clearings |
| Recruitment  | Hirelings, reputation     | Fugitives, deserters, the desperate     |
| Legal status | Licensed by employer      | Kill on sight                           |
| Discipline   | The ledger, the Articles  | Fear, shared danger                     |
| Lifespan     | Years to decades          | Months to seasons                       |

### The Fortress

The band that survives longest is the one that controls a defensible position. An island with sheer cliffs accessible only by a ladder that can be withdrawn. A collapsed barrow complex with one entrance and no easy approach. A cliff-top ruin above a river bend where the current sweeps anyone who tries to cross at night.

**Requirements for a viable outlaw fortress:**

- Defensible against superior numbers (cliff, water, chokepoint)
- Within raiding range of at least two food sources
- Concealed from casual observation
- Fresh water
- Shelter against the Long Dark

A band with all five can survive a season. A band with four dies when the missing element kills them.

### Discipline

The outlaw band has no mechanism for resolving internal disputes except violence. In a Svarthird company, the Articles govern. The captain judges. Social pressure keeps men in line because the alternative -- expulsion -- is worse than compliance.

In a wolfshead band, there is no expulsion. Where would the man go? He is already outside everything. The outlaw-leader who wants to restrain his men from burning one farm too many has nothing to offer except his own fists and the argument that discipline extends survival.

Some listen. The ones who do not bring the coalition down on everyone. The outlaw-leader's tragedy is specific: he watches the men he is responsible for become what the law already says they are, and he cannot stop it because the structures that make men governable -- fear of exile, loss of standing, the weight of community opinion -- do not exist out here.

### The Silence of the Band

The thing the settlements do not understand about outlaw bands is the quiet.

A settlement sings. Every hour of every day, someone is singing -- a verklag over the grinding-stone, a vǫgguvísur at the cradle, a hervísur in the training ground, a löglag at the Thing. The song never stops. The song is the settlement's heartbeat, and the heartbeat proves the settlement is alive.

An outlaw band does not sing.

Not because the men cannot. Not because the songs are forgotten. Because the songs require a world to mean anything. A cradle- song requires a child. A work-song requires shared labor. A battle-song requires a line of men who trust each other enough to synchronize their breathing. A drinking-song requires a hall and an audience and the social contract that makes mockery permissible instead of lethal. A mourning-song requires women, and the women are not here.

The outlaw has none of this. He has a cave and a fire and men who will kill him for his boots. The songs die in his throat not because the words are lost but because the context that gave the words weight has been severed, and a song without context is just noise, and noise in the waste draws the things that hunt noise.

Occasionally, in the deep Long Dark, a band's fire grows high enough and the stolen ale flows freely enough and the men forget what they are for a moment -- and someone sings. The catch is instant. The men join with a desperation that has nothing to do with music and everything to do with the memory of being human. The song lasts minutes. Then it dies, because the men remember where they are and what they are, and the memory kills the song the way cold kills a flame, and the silence afterward is worse than the silence before, because for a moment the silence was gone and now it is back and the men know it will not leave again.

The settlements fear the söngœði — the sung-madness that seizes halls and produces truths from the collective voice. The outlaws would welcome it. They would welcome anything that broke the quiet. But the söngœði requires a critical mass of voices singing in shared intent, and the outlaw band does not have shared intent. It has shared desperation, which is not the same thing. The song needs a community to catch. The outlaw band is not a community. It is a collection of men who have been cut from every community that matters, and the cutting left them voiceless in the way that matters most: not mute, but unheard.

## The Plunder Economy

### What They Take

An outlaw band produces nothing. It consumes. Every sheep it eats is a sheep some farmer needed to survive the Long Dark. Every grain store it empties is a family's food until the thaw. The economy is purely extractive.

| Source                   | Provides                      | Risk                    |
| ------------------------ | ----------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Livestock raiding        | Food (immediate)              | Farm pursuit            |
| Grain store raids        | Food (storable)               | Settlement mobilization |
| Extortion / "protection" | Food, tools, clothing         | Feud escalation         |
| Ambush of travelers      | Weapons, coin, clothing       | Bounty increase         |
| Stripping the dead       | Weapons, armor, silver, boots | None (immediate)        |
| Night-market trade       | Tools, medicine, rope         | Exposure of contacts    |
| Fishing / foraging       | Supplement only               | Minimal                 |

### Night Markets

Where outlaw silver meets black-market goods. A night market is not a place. It is a transaction -- a corrupt farmer, a traveling tinker, a desperate fisherwoman -- anyone willing to trade with wolves in exchange for coin or stolen goods.

The farmer sells dried meat to the band on the hill and hopes his neighbors never find out. The tinker sells rope and fishhooks and takes payment in a dead man's arm-ring. The fisherwoman trades her catch for the promise that her boat will not be sunk.

Night markets occur at a rate proportional to outlaw pressure. The higher the pressure, the more people deal with wolves because the wolves are dangerous enough that refusing feels worse than complying.

> See `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` §18.14 -- night market chance =
> outlaw_pressure × 15 + season_modifier.

### Why the Economy Always Fails

Every outlaw band that has ever existed in the Rimevegr has failed. The economics guarantee it:

1. **Consumption without production.** You eat the neighbors' livestock. You do not raise your own. When the surrounding farms are stripped, you range farther. Farther means more exposure.
2. **Escalating hostility.** Every raid turns someone into an enemy. The farmer you did not rob will help the farmer you did. The coalition forms. It always forms.
3. **Bounty accumulation.** A wolfshead's price rises with every crime. Below 40 silver, only local men care. Above 150, regional hunters take notice. Above 400, the name spreads. Someone always wants the silver more than they fear the wolf.
4. **Internal collapse.** Men with no legal consequences for misconduct become ungovernable. The outlaw-leader cannot execute a man for insubordination -- the man is already sentenced to die. He cannot exile him -- the man is already exiled. The only discipline tool is violence, and violence breeds more violence.

The arithmetic is merciless. The average band lasts one season. The exceptional ones -- island fortress, strong leader, nearby valley that has not yet organized -- last two or three years. Then they are betrayed, besieged, or starved out.

## Pillaging

> _The village at the fork of the Fen River had already been
> hit when they arrived. One house burned, still smoking. Grain
> stores torn open. Three dead -- two men and a woman, cut
> clean, which meant swords. The surviving families stood in a
> knot near the well, watching the column approach with the
> empty faces of people who had already been through the worst
> part and were now waiting to find out if the next part was
> worse._

When the band takes what it wants without asking -- or forces collection after a refused demand -- the ledger records it as an atrocity. The Standing hit is immediate: -3 minimum at the pillaged settlement, -1 at every settlement within rumor range. The Feud Track advances by 2 steps.

Food and goods taken this way cost nothing in coin. They feed the band. They fill the stores. And they generate every consequence listed under Atrocities.

**Deliberate waste** -- scattering grain in the mud, slaughtering livestock that cannot be carried, burning what cannot be taken -- is the mark of a band that wants to punish a village for not having more. It is also the mark of a captain who has lost control, because deliberate waste produces no material benefit and maximum hostility. The men who scatter the grain are not feeding themselves. They are teaching the next village what happens when a gate stays closed.

### Occupation

The band plants its banner and stays. Not tribute. A power structure imposed by force.

**Requirements:**

- Physical presence (or a detachment left behind)
- 2 fighters per 10 settlement adults, minimum
- MANIPULATION roll difficulty 2 (3 if Standing already -2)

**What occupation produces:**

- 1D6 silver worth of goods per week
- Shelter equivalent for finding hirelings
- Direct access to settlement hunt/forage output

**What occupation costs:**

- Standing at this settlement: -1 per week (floor -5)
- Neighboring settlements: Standing -1 once
- Feud Track: +1 at start

A village of sixty adults produces 3-4 silver per week under occupation. The detachment costs wages and food. The Standing damage spreads. The math works only when the band needs a fixed base more than it needs coin.

**Settlement drain.** An occupied settlement is weakened for a full season after the occupation ends. Foraging yield drops one column. Trade-goods output halves. Recruitable population is tapped. A second occupation within the same season reduces the settlement permanently by one size category.

> The settlement the band ruined is still there. It is just
> less.

## Atrocities

> _The order was given at the granary gate. The elder had
> refused. The contract clock was running and Hadric did not
> ask twice. He looked at Crane and said: "Open it."_
>
> _Crane put his shoulder through the latch. Hadric said:
> "Burn it."_
>
> _Crane carried the torch. He did not hesitate. The thatch
> took in a quarter of an hour and the whole structure was
> gone before dark. The children had been pulled inside by
> their mothers before the first spark._
>
> _Nobody spoke. Not at the evening fire. Not at the morning
> count._
>
> _Hadric checked the ledger. The contract was on schedule.
> The granary was gone. The village would starve by first
> frost. He carried this in no document and in no part of
> himself that anyone could read._

An atrocity is not a battlefield act. Battle is expected. Killing is expected. An atrocity is something that makes the survivors' children remember the band's name for a generation. It is the act that transforms a warband from a known hazard into something that must be destroyed.

### The Catalog

| Act            | What It Means                                                                           |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Massacre       | Killing non-combatants: villagers, the wounded, the surrendered, children, thralls      |
| Burning        | Destroying a settlement's means of survival: granary, livestock pen, boats, seed stores |
| Ransom killing | Executing a captive taken for ransom or who surrendered expecting mercy                 |
| Slave-taking   | Taking free people by force -- converting a free person to property                     |
| Oath-breaking  | Violating a sworn agreement before witnesses                                            |
| Treachery      | Killing an ally, turning on an employer, selling inside knowledge                       |
| Territory      | Marking ground with heads, corpses, shame-poles,                                        |
| marking        | or mutilated remains as warning or claim                                                |

Each act generates consequences that the ledger tracks. The ledger does not judge. The ledger records. The judgment comes later, from men with swords who heard the name.

### Consequences

**Standing crash.** Every settlement within rumor range loses 2 Standing with the band. Settlements with direct survivors drop to -4 or -5. Allied settlements drop to -3. The crash is immediate and does not require a roll.

**Reputation spread.** The act travels as fact, not hearsay. A village burns and people four valleys away know the name within a week. Wolves do not need to introduce themselves twice.

**MORALE check.** Unless the men profited materially, MORALE -1. An ordered massacre with no gain costs morale outright. A Named Man with Loyalty 2 or 3 may announce he will not repeat it. A Named Man with a civilian-harm Trigger must be tested.

**Bounty.** Anyone with standing and motive can post a bounty within a season. Survivors carry the story.

**Plunder and MORALE.** The +1 MORALE from atrocity-with-plunder fires only when MORALE is currently 2 or below. At MORALE 3+, the men do not need blood-money to hold together. This makes atrocity a desperation tool. The captain who orders it when the band is stable pays Standing and Reputation damage with no MORALE payoff.

### Oath-Breaker Status

Repeat the pattern three times and the flag lands.

Two recorded breaches: the GM warns openly. An employer, a gate guard, or a Named Man says it plainly -- one more and the name is finished.

Three recorded breaches: OATH-BREAKER flag. All MANIPULATION rolls for new contracts take +2 difficulty. No settlement elder who knows the flag will hire the band without extraordinary assurance -- collateral, half the pay held in escrow.

**Clearing the flag.** The band must publicly honor a costly obligation -- one where keeping the oath demonstrably hurt the band. Witnessed by at least one settlement elder of Standing 2+. Losing fighters, taking wounds, forgoing profitable work all count. This is the only mechanism. There is no shortcut.

## Territory Marking

### Heads

> _They found the first one a half-day's march from the
> crossing. A man's head on a sharpened pine stake, driven into
> the ground at the edge of the road where a traveler could not
> miss it. The jaw had been broken off. The eyes were gone --
> crows or rats, maybe both. The hair was still braided. Someone
> had taken the time to leave the braids neat, which was worse
> than if they had not._
>
> _Gest counted the teeth. "Three days," he said. "Maybe four."_
>
> _"Whose?"_
>
> _"Does it matter? It matters to whoever left it."_
>
> _Nobody unplanted it. Nobody buried it. That was not their
> business and touching a wolfshead's work is how you become
> the next message on the next pole._

Heads on stakes are the oldest territorial claim in the Rimevegr. They pre-date the Thing. They pre-date law. A skull on a pole says two things: someone died here, and the person who killed them wants you to know.

Outlaw bands use heads to mark the boundaries of their claimed territory. A line of stakes along a ridge tells the farmer below that the men on the other side of the ridge consider this ground theirs. A head planted at a crossroads tells every traveler that the road ahead is taxed.

The display is deliberate. The heads face outward. The stakes are placed where the road bends so you see them before you can turn back. The jaw is often removed -- not cruelty for its own sake but a practical choice. A skull with the mandible broken off is harder to identify, and a head that cannot be identified is a head that could be anyone's. The message is not "we killed this particular man." The message is "we kill men here."

A fresh head lasts two to three weeks before the crows and the weather reduce it past the point of utility. A band that maintains its perimeter replaces them. The replacement is part of the rhythm -- a chore, like checking snares or banking a fire. One of the men walks the line once a week, counts the stakes, notes which heads need replacing, and reports back. The supply comes from whoever the band killed most recently. When the supply runs thin, the line contracts. When the supply is generous, the line expands. The perimeter of staked heads is a territory map that updates itself with every kill.

### The Corpse Road

The worst outlaw bands do not stop at heads. They leave whole bodies.

A corpse hung from a tree at a ford crossing. A body staked out at a road junction, spread-eagled, stripped naked, the chest cavity open and the ribs pulled apart so the lungs are visible -- an invitation for the crows, and for the next man approaching the ford to turn around. A pair of bodies propped upright on either side of a path like a gate, tied to stakes, with their weapons placed in their hands in mockery of the dead men's function as guards.

This is not madness. This is communication. The outlaw band that displays corpses along the approach to its territory is telling the world: do not come here. The preparation takes time and effort and is performed in the open, at road-edges, where the wolves are most exposed. They do it anyway because the ones who do it live longer. A settlement that sees the corpse road does not send its militia. It sends for a Svarthird company, and by the time the company arrives, the wolves have moved.

The smell is part of the function. A decomposing body in warm weather can be detected a quarter-mile downwind. In the Long Dark, the cold preserves the display -- bodies freeze in place, ice forming on the open wounds, the whole arrangement locked in position until the thaw. A traveler who rounds a bend in the road and finds a frozen corpse-gate in January is a traveler who does not continue around the next bend.

### The Shame-Pole

The _níðstöng_ -- a horse's skull on a sharpened pole, runes carved into the wood, the skull turned to face the target's hall.

A curse aimed at both the man and the _landvættir_ -- the land-spirits that protect his ground. The target does not know whether the curse works. That is the point. He does not sleep. His livestock sicken -- or he notices every time they sicken, which is the same thing. His neighbors avoid him because the curse might be contagious. The shame-pole does not kill. It poisons.

The carving matters. The runes must be specific. A níðstöng raised by a man who knows the old forms carries weight that a pole raised by an illiterate wolfshead does not -- but the illiterate wolfshead does not need the runes to work. He needs the farmer to believe they might.

In the Rimevegr, shame-poles are raised against settlements that refused to pay tribute, against jarls who posted bounties, against families who betrayed a wolfshead's hiding place. The pole is planted at night, in view of the target's door, so the first thing the man sees at dawn is a horse skull pointed at his threshold with runes that say his name.

### The Flayed Skins

Rare. Recorded in the Rimevegr during the worst outlaw uprisings -- when a band has been cornered, besieged, starved to the edge, and the men inside have passed through every stage of the wolfshead arc and come out the other side as something that no longer thinks in human categories.

A farmer is captured. He is not killed quickly. He is skinned alive, or partially, and the skin is hung on a frame outside the band's perimeter. The farmer may or may not survive the process -- it does not matter to the display. What matters is that the next armed party approaching the outlaw fortress sees the frame and the skin and understands what it means.

The skin says: the men inside this perimeter have nothing left to lose and nothing left that connects them to the rules you live by. Come in and this is what happens. The arithmetic of siege warfare changes when the defenders have demonstrated willingness to do this. It takes more men, more time, more casualties to take a position defended by men who flay captives than it takes to take the same position defended by men who merely fight. The coalition knows this. Sometimes they withdraw. Sometimes they send for more men. Either way the wolves have bought time, and time is the only currency a dying band has left.

### Gibbets and Iron Cages

Where a wolfshead band has access to a smith -- rare, but it happens when a blacksmith is outlawed or captured and forced to work -- metal cages appear. A captive locked alive inside a cage hung from a tree or a pole at a crossroads. The man inside is not dead. He is dying. The exposure kills him over days -- thirst first, then cold, then the tissue damage from the iron pressing into flesh that has swollen against it.

The cage is the most labor-intensive display. It requires iron, a forge, time, and a captive worth the effort. Wolfshead bands use cages for specific targets -- a bounty hunter who came too close, a militia leader who organized a patrol, a farmer who betrayed the band's location. The specificity is the point. The cage says: we know who you are. We know what you did. This is what we do to people like you.

A man in a cage at a crossroads changes the behavior of everyone who passes. They do not rescue him. Rescuing a man in a wolfshead cage is how you end up in the next cage. They walk past, or they turn back, and the man in the cage watches them go and knows that he is going to die in the iron and nobody is going to stop it.

## What They Do to People

### Livestock Raiding

The most common outlaw predation. A family's entire winter food supply is walking in a pen and the wolfsheads drive it away in the dark. The family does not starve immediately. They eat what stores they have. They ration. They trade what little they can. By first frost the children are thin and the old grandmother stops eating so the children can have her portion. She dies before the thaw. The granddaughter remembers the name of the band that took the sheep for the rest of her life.

The livestock that the wolves take does not last long. A dozen sheep feed ten men for a few weeks. Then the meat is gone and the band needs more and the next farm is farther away and the farms in between have heard the name and the dogs are chained outside and the men sleep with axes by the bed.

### Burning

> _The longhouse burns fast once the thatch takes. A
> quarter-hour and the whole structure is gone._

The people inside have that long to get out. If the doors are barred -- and sometimes the doors are barred -- they do not get out. The smoke kills before the fire does. The sound carries. A settlement two valleys away sees the glow and knows what it means.

Hall-burning is the act that turns an outlaw band from a nuisance into something that must be erased. The immediate destruction is catastrophic -- the building, the stored food, the tools, the bedding, the loom, the iron kettle, the seed grain, everything the family owned is gone in an hour. But the burn's real damage is economic. A longhouse takes a season to rebuild, and the family that lost theirs does not have a season. They have the Long Dark coming and no roof.

A burned family becomes refugees. They move to a kinsman's hall, if they have kinsmen. They beg at the nearest settlement, if they do not. They die on the road if no one takes them in. In the worst cases, the family that was burned joins the band that burned them, because the alternative is death and a man who has lost everything is a man who has nothing left to protect.

### Extortion

The wolfshead stands at the farmstead gate with three men and says: "Feed us or we come back at night."

The farmer feeds them. He feeds them the next week. And the week after. The food he gives the wolves is the food his family does not eat. The children get thinner. The farmer's wife stops speaking to him because she blames him for not fighting and she is right that he cannot win and she is wrong that fighting would help. The extortion continues until the food runs out or someone kills the wolves or the farmer hangs himself in the barn, which happens more often than anyone records.

The farm that pays extortion to wolfsheads is a farm that pays tribute to no one else. The local warchief loses his cut. The settlement elder cannot collect the expected share for the Thing. The wolves have inserted themselves into the economic structure of the valley and they are draining it from the top, and everyone can see it happening and no one can stop it without force that costs more than the tribute is worth.

### What They Do to the Men Who Resist

> _They found Ketil staked out on his own threshing floor. His
> hands had been nailed to the boards with his own door-pins.
> His eyes were gone. His tongue had been pulled forward
> through a slit cut under the jaw -- they call it a blood
> tongue in the valleys north of the Fen, and the name is
> accurate._
>
> _His wife was inside the house. She was alive. She did not
> speak. She did not speak for three weeks. When she finally
> spoke, she said one sentence, and the sentence was the name
> of the man who had done it, and then she stopped speaking
> again and did not start until the following summer._

The outlaw band that meets resistance teaches the next farm what resistance costs.

**The blood tongue.** A slit is cut under the jaw and the tongue pulled through it and laid flat against the chest. The man may survive this. He may not. The message is not the death. The message is the display -- the man's own tongue dragged out through his own throat and arranged where anyone can see it. The next farmer who considers picking up an axe when the wolves come to his gate remembers what Ketil looked like on the threshing floor and puts the axe down.

**Hands and hamstrings.** The practical mutilations. A man whose hands are cut off cannot fight. A man whose hamstrings are cut cannot run. A man with neither cannot do anything except sit where he was left and wait for someone to find him. If someone finds him. These are not killing acts. They are worse than killing acts because the man survives as a burden his family must carry until he dies of infection, despair, or the next winter. A dead man is mourned. A mutilated man is a cost that never stops.

**Blinding.** Eyes put out with a hot iron or a knife point. The blind man is released to walk where he can. He stumbles back to his settlement and the settlement sees him and the settlement understands. The blind man is the message. He carries it in his face for the rest of his life.

**Hanging by the heels.** Slits cut through the skin behind the ankles, a pole run through the slits, the man hung upside down from a tree or a rafter. The weight of his own body tears at the skin and the tendons. The blood pools in his head. He can hear everything. He can see -- inverted, swinging -- whoever is standing below him. The pain is extraordinary and the position is humiliating and the combination breaks men faster than any beating. A man hung by his heels for an hour will agree to anything. A man hung by his heels for a day may not survive to agree.

**Castration.** The extreme insult. In the Rimevegr, where a man's ability to fight, to work, to sire children defines his social existence, castration renders him physically what the law has already declared the outlaw to be: something less than a man. The act is linked to the _argr/ergi_ concept -- the ultimate accusation of unmanliness. A man castrated and released is a man destroyed in every way that matters in a society built on martial reputation and bloodline continuity.

### What They Do to Women

The saga language is flat about what happens to women taken by outlaw bands. The flat language is the worst part. It does not describe. It notes. "She was kept." "She was among them for the winter." "She returned in the spring and did not speak of it."

The modern reader does not need the sagas to spell it out. The women who were kept by wolfshead bands returned -- if they returned -- as different people. They did not discuss it. Their families did not discuss it. The settlement absorbed them back or did not, and the silence around what happened was part of the settlement's own survival mechanism because naming the thing that happened meant acknowledging a wrong that no one had avenged and no one intended to avenge because the cost of vengeance was higher than the cost of silence.

A woman taken by a wolfshead band and held through a winter is a woman who comes back carrying a child, or not. If the child lives, the child is a wolfshead's child, which is not the child's fault but is the child's inheritance. "Wolfson," "Wolfsdaughter" -- the name travels with the child into adulthood. A girl with that name has trouble finding a match. A boy with that name is watched by every man in the settlement for signs of his father's nature.

### What They Do to Children

Children are taken as hostages when the outlaw band needs leverage. A farmer whose daughter is on a hilltop with armed wolves does not organize the militia. He pays what they ask. He pays until his farm cannot sustain the payments and then he pays from his kinsmen's farms and then from his neighbors' farms and the neighbors blame him and the social fabric of the valley tears along the seam of his daughter's captivity.

Children are also taken as replacements. A band that has been in the waste long enough loses its youngest members first -- the boys who joined as teenagers die of exposure, disease, or violence before their twentieth year. The band replenishes from captured settlements. The boys are not recruited. They are absorbed. They learn to fight because the alternative is worse. They learn to raid because it is the only way to eat. Within a season the boy who was taken from his mother's farmstead is the same animal as the men who took him, and his mother would not recognize what comes back if anything ever does.

## The Blood Eagle and the Worst of It

### The Rite

The _blóðörn_. Whether it was ever performed is debated by scholars who study the matter from the safety of libraries. In the Rimevegr the debate is not academic.

A man is held face-down. A blade cuts through the skin of his back along the spine. The ribs are severed from the backbone, one by one. The rib-cage is forced open from behind -- the bones pulled outward and apart until the lung tissue is exposed. The lungs are drawn out through the wound and spread across the opened ribs like wings.

The man does not survive this. The man was never intended to survive this. The blood eagle is not a punishment. It is an offering -- a dedication of the victim to Odin, performed on an enemy of sufficient status to make the sacrifice meaningful. A farmer is not worth the effort. A bounty hunter who killed two of the band's men is worth the effort. A militia captain who organized the coalition is absolutely worth the effort.

In the Rimevegr, the worst outlaw leaders use the blood eagle as a territorial statement. The opened body is displayed on the ridge-line, facing the road. The "wings" dry in the cold air and the arrangement holds its shape through the Long Dark because the frost preserves everything. By spring what remains is a standing skeleton with the rib-cage flared open and the dried lung tissue hanging like parchment and the whole structure bleached white by sun and cold. It lasts longer than a head on a stake. It carries further than a scream.

The band that has performed a blood eagle is not a band that anyone approaches without overwhelming force. The act announces a threshold. The men who did this are not men who can be reasoned with, negotiated with, bribed, or frightened. They have crossed into a space where the normal mechanisms of human interaction no longer apply.

### Other Dedications

The blood eagle is the most extreme. It is not the only corpse display dedicated to the gods in the Rimevegr.

**Hanging.** Odin is the Hanged God. Men hung from trees as offerings -- not always enemies, not always the dead. The outlaw band that hangs a body from the tallest tree visible from a settlement is making a religious claim: we are under the Allfather's protection. The claim is almost certainly false. That does not stop the farmer who sees the swaying body from staying inside his hall and thinking about what kind of men hang corpses in trees and call it worship.

**The stake.** Impalement -- vertical or angled -- as display. A body on a stake is visible for a great distance. It does not decompose quickly in the cold of the Long Dark. A single body on a stake at a crossroads can control traffic on that road for a season because no one wants to walk past it.

**The pit.** Bodies thrown into a bog or a shallow pit, not buried. Left where they can be found. The finder -- a hunter, a woodsman, a child wandering too far from the settlement -- carries the discovery home and the settlement processes the information: there are wolves out there, and this is what they leave behind.

**The message-corpse.** A body returned to the settlement it came from. The militia man who went out to patrol comes back face-down over his own horse, his hands tied to the reins, his head between the horse's ears instead of on his shoulders. The horse knows the way home. The horse walks through the gate. The settlement sees what is on the horse and understands that the patrol is over and the information the patrol gathered is: do not send another.

## Feud

Atrocities and occupation generate feud. Feud escalates through five stages and does not retreat on its own.

| Stage        | Trigger                        | Settlement Response                          |
| ------------ | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| Cold         | Band present, no incidents     | Wary, doors closed                           |
| Resentful    | Tribute forced or Standing -1  | Prices doubled, info withheld                |
| Coordinating | Atrocity or Standing -2        | Messengers sent to neighbors                 |
| Armed        | Second atrocity or Standing -3 | Militia formed, patrols organized            |
| Vengeance    | Occupation or Standing -5      | Coalition attack at full settlement strength |

**Advancing the track:**

- First tribute demand (same year): +1
- Forced tribute after refusal: +2
- Pillage or atrocity: +2
- Occupation established: +1
- Killing a defender: +2

**Retreating the track:**

- One season with no contact: -1 step
- Compensation paid (5× weekly tribute value): -2 steps
- Public oath not to return, witnessed by Named Man: -3 steps
- Genuine service to the settlement (clearing a threat, defending against a third party): -1 to -2 steps (GM)

Feud retreats only through direct restitution. Time alone does not heal feud in the Rimevegr. Men remember.

At Feud Track 3 or 4, the opposing force becomes an active NPC faction. The GM names them, gives them a size tier, and they begin hunting the band. This is the machinery of consequence.

## Bounty Mechanics

### How Bounties Grow

A wolfshead's bounty starts at whatever the plaintiff's kin can scrape together. A dead farmer's family might post 20 silver. A jarl who lost a tax-collector can post 200.

The bounty accumulates. Each new crime adds to it. Each new plaintiff adds their claim.

| Bounty (silver) | Spread                  | Who Hunts                                   |
| --------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Under 40        | Local settlement only   | Farmers, local strong-arms                  |
| 40--150         | Regional (2-3 hexes)    | Experienced hunters, off-duty Svarthird men |
| 150--400        | Wide (a Thing district) | Professional bounty hunters                 |
| Over 400        | Famous                  | Everyone                                    |

### Professional Bounty Hunters

The Rimevegr produces men who make their living hunting wolves. They are not heroes. They are men who have calculated that the silver on a wolfshead's skull is worth the risk of collecting it.

A bounty hunter looks like any other traveling man. He asks questions. He buys drinks. He watches doors. He does not announce himself because announcing yourself to a wolfshead is how you get killed. Patience is his weapon, not courage. He will track a man for a year if the bounty is high enough.

**Proof of elimination.** The posting specifies what proof is required. The most common:

> _"Head. Teeth. Something the council can nail to the gate."_

A head delivered in a sack to the posting elder. Teeth pried out and counted against a known description. A hand with a recognizable scar, a ring still on the finger, a branded mark from a specific crime. The bounty hunter carries the proof and the proof carries the smell and the elder counts the teeth and pays the silver and the head is nailed to the gate as a public notice that the wolf is dead and the settlement is safe and the system worked.

### Clearing Wanted Status

Bounties cool over time. A bounty uncollected for one full year with no renewed pursuit goes cold -- it exists but no longer actively spreads. Spread drops one tier per season the bounty goes uncollected.

Other ways to clear:

- Restitution paid to the grantor
- Kill the grantor (bounty dies with the man who posted it, unless kin re-post)
- MANIPULATION difficulty 3 to negotiate removal
- Outlasting it (cold bounty, no re-posting)

A grantor who re-posts a cold bounty pays double each time. At some point the arithmetic of vengeance exceeds the arithmetic of anger and the bounty stays cold.

## Outlaw Pressure on Settlements

Every settlement tracks an outlaw_pressure stat (0-5) that represents the cumulative effect of wolfshead activity.

| Level | Meaning               | Effects                                 |
| ----- | --------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| 0     | No outlaw presence    | Normal operations                       |
| 1     | Rumors and stragglers | +5% chance of night market              |
| 2     | Active raiding nearby | +15% night market, prices +10%          |
| 3     | Regular predation     | +30% night market, militia forms        |
| 4     | Entrenched band       | +45% night market, road patrols         |
| 5     | Territory control     | +60% night market, coalition mobilizing |

### Settlement Responses

When outlaw_pressure reaches 3+:

- **Hire patrol.** Contract a Svarthird band to clear the area. 10-40 silver per week depending on threat.
- **Organize militia.** All able adults arm themselves. Costs production, costs no silver. Quality is poor.
- **Pay tribute.** Negotiate with the outlaws. Tribute buys temporary peace at the cost of goods the settlement needs. Standing with legitimate authority drops.
- **Negotiate amnesty.** A jarl or Thing-speaker offers conditional amnesty -- submit, serve, and the sentence is commuted. Works only when the outlaw band has something the authority wants.

### Amnesty Contracts

The rarest and most dangerous contract a Svarthird captain can take. Absorbing former wolfsheads into the company's ranks. The patron pays a premium. The former outlaws serve faithfully. The captain is responsible for their behavior.

In practice: the men you absorb have spent months or years outside every structure that makes men governable. They do not respect the Articles. They do not fear expulsion. They fight well because they have been fighting for their lives. They do not take orders because no one has given them orders they could not refuse with a knife. The captain who takes an amnesty contract is gambling that discipline is contagious. Sometimes it is. Usually it is not.

## What Outlawry Does to Men

### The Wolfshead Arc

The law declared them wolves and over time they became wolves. The pattern is consistent across every bleached bone in the Rimevegr waste.

**Phase one: Defiance.** The fresh outlaw hides, scrounges, depends on secret help. He tells himself the sentence was unjust. He is still the man he was before the Lawspeaker spoke. He sleeps with one eye open and does not yet understand that this will break him.

**Phase two: Predation.** The hiding places run out. The secret help dries up because helping a wolfshead is itself a crime. He begins raiding. Each raid makes him more visible. The community's response escalates. He is no longer the man he was. He is the man who stole the widow's sheep.

**Phase three: Entrenchment.** He accepts what he is. The violence that was desperate becomes habitual. The distinction between survival theft and cruelty blurs. He stops trying to justify it. He stops caring. The men around him -- if he has men -- are worse, and he knows it, and he cannot stop them because the structures that restrain men do not exist out here.

**Phase four: Terminal.** He is killed. Usually through treachery -- a man who posed as a friend, a woman who opened a gate, a bribed sentry who looked away. Or exhaustion -- the coalition finds him when he is sick, wounded, sleeping. Or he escapes the Rimevegr entirely, which is the same as dying because no one ever comes back.

### The Psychology

A man declared _vargr_ loses access to every mechanism that keeps a normal man sane in the Rimevegr.

The communal fire is gone. No one will sit with him. The saga-telling that reframes horror as narrative -- the thing that lets warriors carry what they have seen -- does not happen because there is no one to listen.

His _hugr_ (mind-soul) is under unbroken siege. No respite, no ritual, no companionship. His _fylgja_ (guardian spirit) is understood to have turned its face. He is _unlucky_, and bad luck is contagious. Even those who wish to help fear that proximity to a wolfshead draws misfortune to their own hall.

His _hamr_ -- the composure a man presents to the world -- has no audience. There is no community to perform normalcy for. Without the social pressure to hold his mask in place, the damage surfaces unchecked. The dreams come. The darkness becomes a thing with weight. A man who once killed draugar wakes screaming because the shapes in the dark have teeth he can almost see.

The strongest outlaw who ever lived in the Rimevegr ended his nineteenth year afraid of the dark. Not afraid of what was in the dark. Afraid of the dark itself. A man who had killed draugar could not sit alone in a room without a light. Anyone who has spent a winter alone knows what silence does to the mind when there is nothing to fill it except the memory of every bad thing you have ever done.

### The Descent into the Inhuman

> _Something happens to men who live in the waste long enough.
> It is not a thing you can name or a thing you would discuss
> over a fire. You see it in the eyes. The man is still there
> -- the mouth still moves, the hands still grip, the body
> still functions -- but the thing behind the eyes has changed
> its shape. He does not flinch when he should flinch. He does
> not look away when a reasonable man would look away. He does
> things to captured men that a reasonable man would not do and
> he does them without the expression you would expect -- not
> rage, not pleasure, not disgust, just the flat attention of
> a craftsman performing a task he has performed before._
>
> _That is what the waste makes. Not monsters. Craftsmen of
> violence with no remaining reason to stop._

The outlaw who has survived three winters in the Rimevegr is a different creature from the man who walked out of the assembly ground. The environmental pressures -- cold, hunger, isolation, violence, the absolute absence of any human connection that is not founded on mutual need and mutual danger -- have stripped away everything that was not essential to survival. What remains is functional, efficient, and empty.

The flat affect is the tell. A man who burns a farm and shows no expression. A man who mutilates a captive with the focused calm of a man gutting a fish. A man who eats his food next to a body he killed an hour ago and does not move away from it because the body is nothing -- it is meat and bones and clothing and the clothing might be useful and the rest is not worth thinking about.

This is not madness. This is adaptation. The man who retained his capacity for horror in the outlaw waste is the man who froze to death because he could not bring himself to do what survival required. The man who survived is the man who learned to stop feeling what the acts he performed should have made him feel. The learning was not a choice. It was a process of erosion, like water wearing stone, and by the time the stone was smooth the man was gone and the wolf was all that remained.

### Families Left Behind

The outlaw's wife is not divorced by the sentence, but she is destroyed by it. His property is confiscated. She cannot shelter him. The assembly may give her something from the seized goods -- the law says they should -- but the assembly that condemned her husband is not obligated to be generous.

The children bear the stain. "Son of a wolfshead" marks a family for a generation. The sons may inherit from their mother's line. They may not inherit what their father held. They inherit the obligation of vengeance, which in practice means they inherit an enemy too dangerous to fight.

The crime of helping is the cruelest provision. A woman who leaves food for her husband at a prearranged spot risks her own outlawry. A farmer who warns a wolfshead that hunters are coming risks everything he owns. The law isolates the wolf and punishes anyone who breaks the isolation. Even love becomes a legal exposure.

## The Veil-Pull — What the Darkness Wants

### The Gravity of the Waste

There is a direction in the Rimevegr that is not north, south, east, or west. The outlaws call it _inn_ — inward — and they do not mean toward the body's center. They mean toward the Veil. Toward the grey. Toward the place where the air tastes of old iron and the distance between one step and the next becomes unreliable and the fire goes blue at the tips and the sound of your own breathing becomes the loudest thing in the world.

Every outlaw who survives past the third winter feels the pull. It is not a voice. It is not a command. It is a gravitational inclination — the same slow, patient force that pulls water downhill. The outlaw does not decide to walk north. He simply notices, at the end of a day's march, that he has drifted northward again. The camp he chose is fifty paces closer to the treeline than the one he left. The cave he sleeps in faces the ice. The fire he builds is behind him, not in front, as though the warmth is something to be left behind and the cold is something to be walked toward.

The settlements have a term for it: _Veil-pull_. The rope- readers call it the Silence's appetite. The seidr-wives call it the Devouring reaching through the fog for the easiest prey. The common folk call it what it is: the waste eats men, and the men who have been chewed longest are the ones who walk toward the teeth.

### What Happens to the Mind

The outlaw's hugr — the mind-soul, the seat of will and thought and the voice that says _no_ when the body wants to do something terrible — erodes in predictable stages. The existing section describes the functional erosion: defiance to predation to entrenchment to terminal flatness. But the Veil adds a layer that is not psychological. It is environmental. The waste itself is doing something to the men who live in it, and the something is worse than isolation.

**Depression is the first visitor.** Not sadness — sadness requires caring about what was lost. Depression in the waste is the weight of the Veil pressing on the hugr the way fog presses on the lungs. The outlaw wakes and the day has no shape. The fire has no warmth. The food has no taste. The men around him — if he has men — are voices in a fog that might as well be the Veil itself. He functions. He raids. He fights. He does these things the way a man with a broken spine walks: through effort so enormous that the observers cannot see it and the man himself cannot sustain it.

The bone-walkers — Skathi's mountain-keepers — recognize this immediately when they encounter outlaws in the high places. A bone-walker who finds a cave camp during the long_dark can tell which men are Veil-touched by the way they sit: not facing the fire, but facing the cave mouth, staring at the grey nothing beyond the entrance with an expression that is not fear and not fascination but something between the two — the look of a man watching something approach that he cannot name and does not want to name and has stopped trying to name.

**Aggression comes second, and it comes wrong.** A healthy man's anger is directional — aimed at the threat, scaled to the provocation, bounded by the social consequences of excess. The Veil-touched outlaw's anger loses its direction. It becomes ambient. It fills the cave camp the way cold fills a room without a fire. The outlaw does not rage at a particular man for a particular reason. He rages at the air. He rages at the silence. He rages at the dark that is always there and the fire that is never enough and the food that tastes of nothing and the sound of his own breathing that is too loud, always too loud, as though someone is breathing alongside him in the dark.

The violence that follows is not tactical. A raiding party led by a Veil-touched outlaw chief does not calculate targets and risks. It burns. It breaks. It takes what it wants not because the band needs supplies but because the act of destruction is the only thing that drowns the silence for an hour. The settlements that border the waste know the difference between a raid by hungry men and a raid by men who have gone wrong: the hungry men take grain and livestock and leave; the wrong men take nothing useful and stay to break things that serve no purpose — fences, looms, the family's carved door-posts, the children's toys. They break what is beautiful because beauty is a reminder of the world that expelled them, and the Veil has taught them that the world that expelled them does not deserve to have beautiful things.

**Madness is third, and it is the quiet kind.** The outlaw does not rave. He does not hear voices — or if he does, he does not mention them, because admitting you hear voices in a band where every man carries a knife is admitting you are a liability. The madness is structural: the architecture of the mind reorganizes itself around the Veil the way a building leans toward the side with the weakest foundation. The outlaw begins to think in Veil-logic: distances are unreliable. Time is negotiable. Identity is conditional. A man who was called Ragnvald begins to forget why Ragnvald matters. The name is a sound. The body attached to it is a mechanism. The mechanism does things — eats, fights, defecates, breathes — and the man who was Ragnvald watches the mechanism from a growing distance, as though the self has taken a step back from the body and the gap between them is widening, and on the far side of the gap is the grey, and the grey is patient.

**Terminal loneliness is last, and it is the worst.** The outlaw has been expelled from every human structure that gives loneliness meaning — family, settlement, law, saga, god. He is not alone in the way a widow is alone (missing a specific person) or the way a sailor is alone (far from a specific place). He is alone the way the dead are alone: without category, without context, without anyone who is obligated to remember that he existed. The fylgja has turned away. The hugr is under siege. The hamr — the mask of personhood — has no audience. And the Veil, which is always there, which is always pressing, which fills every silence with its own grey frequency, offers the only thing it has ever offered: company. Not good company. Not kind company. But presence, which is what the terminally lonely man craves more than food or warmth or sex or violence.

The outlaws who walk into the Veil are not suicides. That is the part the settlements get wrong. The man who stands up from the fire at midnight and walks north into the grey and does not turn around and does not answer when his bandmates call his name — that man is not seeking death. He is seeking the end of loneliness. The Veil offers something. No one who went in has come back to say what. But the offer is made, and the offer is felt, and the men who feel it strongest are the men who have been alone longest, and the men who have been alone longest are the men who were wolves before the Lawspeaker even spoke.

In the winter of 297, a band of nine outlaws camped in a cave system beneath the Brokentooth ridge. By spring, three remained. Two had been killed in a dispute over a stolen cloak. One had died of infection. Three had walked north. The three who walked did not leave together. They left on consecutive nights — one per night, each standing at the cave mouth and staring at nothing and then walking forward as though answering a summons the others could not hear. The two survivors — a man named Hákon and a boy of fifteen called Burnt — left the cave and went south. They did not discuss what happened. Hákon spoke of it once, years later, to a barrow-keeper who was the only person willing to listen. He said: "They did not look afraid. They looked relieved."

### Andaþrumur — The Breath-Thunder

Before the depression. Before the violence. Before the structural madness and the terminal loneliness and the Walk. Before all of it, there is a symptom that every outlaw reports and that predates the formal documentation of the Veil-Pull by generations:

_The breathing is too loud._

Not metaphor. Not anxiety given physical form. The outlaw, alone in the waste, begins to hear his own breathing with a clarity that is physiologically impossible under normal conditions. The inhale is a roar. The exhale is a gale. The heartbeat is a drum struck by a fist. The blood in the ears is a river. The body — which has been silent background noise for every moment of the outlaw's waking life — becomes the loudest thing in the world.

The Rimevegr has no word for tinnitus. It has _andaþrumur_ — breath-thunder. The outlaws use it. The seiðr-wives recognize it. It is the archaic description of a condition that, in a world with different medical knowledge, would be diagnosed as severe, progressive tinnitus caused by chronic Veil exposure.

The mechanism is the Veil doing what the Veil always does, but in the wrong direction.

A seiðr-wife draws the Veil toward herself during a working. The Veil gathers around her, eating sound, eating the crackle of fire and the settle of timber and the rhythm of the listeners' breathing. The silence she creates is the Veil's ambient property, focused inward, concentrated on the practitioner. The listeners lose their body-sounds because the Veil has retreated from them and wrapped around the woman who invited it. The seiðr-wife's silence radiates outward. The room goes quiet. The people in the room stop hearing their own breathing, and some of them stop breathing entirely, and the seiðr-wife's watchers drag them out before the silence kills them.

The outlaw experiences the inverse. The Veil presses toward him uninvited. He has no training to manage it, no protocol that pulls the boundary away, no seiðr-wife's discipline that turns the approach into a working. The Veil gathers around a person who did not ask for it, and the Veil does not eat his body-sounds. It amplifies them. Because the Veil is a boundary, and when a boundary presses against the living without the living's consent, it does not create silence. It creates resonance. The living body, caught in the Veil's pressure, becomes an instrument — vibrating with the friction between the world of the living and the world of the dead.

The outlaw hears his breathing because the Veil is pressed so tight against his living body that every process of life — respiration, blood flow, the electrical crackle of nerve impulse, the creak of tendon and joint — is amplified by the boundary's proximity. He is hearing himself at a resolution that no living person was meant to experience. He is hearing the sound of being alive, and the sound is deafening, and it does not stop.

The progression is predictable. In the first weeks of exile, the breathing is noticeable — louder than it should be, an oddness that the outlaw attributes to the silence of the waste. By the third month, the breathing is intrusive — audible over conversation, over the crack of a campfire, over the wind. By the first winter, the breathing is dominant. The outlaw cannot sleep because the sound of his own lungs fills the darkness. He cannot think because the thunder of his own heart fills the silence between thoughts. He begins to talk to himself, not from madness but from the desperate need to produce a sound that is louder than the sound of his own body.

By the second winter — if the outlaw survives the second winter — the andaþrumur has become the architecture of his awareness. He processes the world through a filter of body-noise that distorts everything else. Speech reaches him muffled, as if heard through water. The crack of a branch that should signal danger arrives dulled, late, insufficient to trigger the combat reflex that would save his life. He is going deaf, but not from damaged ears — from a signal that has overwhelmed the channel. The Veil has turned his own body into the loudest sound in his environment, and his auditory system, unable to filter it out, has sacrificed everything else.

The connection between andaþrumur and the final Walk is direct. The outlaw who walks into the Veil at the end is not walking toward the grey. He is walking toward silence. The Veil, on its far side, is silent — the dead do not breathe, the dead do not pump blood, the dead do not creak and flex and fire nerve impulses through meat. The man who has heard nothing but his own body for two years walks into the boundary that will stop the sound, and the stopping of the sound is the stopping of the life, and the exchange is one the outlaw has already made in his mind, months before his feet make it in the snow.

The seiðr-wives know this. They know it because they experience the residual version — a seiðr-wife who works without adequate recovery time between sessions begins to hear the andaþrumur between trances. The Veil, drawn too often and too close, does not fully retreat when the working ends. The seiðr-wife hears her own breathing too loudly in the quiet hours before sleep. It always fades. So far. But each seiðr-wife who has practiced long enough knows a colleague for whom it stopped fading — a woman whose breath-thunder became permanent, a whisper-grade version of the roar that kills the outlaws. The mechanism is identical. The degree is different. The seiðr-wife invites the Veil and the Veil mostly leaves when asked. The outlaw receives the Veil uninvited and the Veil does not leave at all.

## Guðníðing — The God-Scorned Path

### What It Is

_Guðníðing_ is the word the rope-readers use, and they speak it the way a man speaks the name of a disease he has seen kill slowly. It translates roughly as "god-shamed" or "god-scorned," but the translation is too clean. _Níðing_ is the worst thing you can call a man in the Rimevegr — a coward, a breaker of oaths, a man who has forfeited every claim to honor and humanity. _Guðníðing_ applies the same total condemnation to the divine relationship: a person who has not merely lost the gods but has turned against them, deliberately, violently, with the specific intention of desecrating every sacred boundary the Rimevegr maintains between man and beast, between the human and the wild, between the ordered world of hearth-fire and oath-stone and the howling nothing beyond the Veil.

This is not god-waste. God-waste is an illness — the Veil bleaching the sacred out of a person the way it bleaches color from the landscape. A man with god-waste forgets the gods. He does not choose to. The greying takes him and he fades.

_Guðníðing_ is a choice. A deliberate, conscious, irreversible act of spiritual self-destruction. The god-scorned man does not forget the gods. He remembers them perfectly. He remembers the oaths he swore and the temples he knelt in and the wards he carved and the porridge he left at the crow-pole every morning of his life. He remembers all of it, and he sets fire to it inside himself, because the gods were silent when he needed them and the law that served the gods expelled him and the society that maintained the law abandoned him and the world that the gods were supposed to govern is a grey, freezing, dying waste where the dead walk and the living wish they didn't, and if the gods built this world then the gods deserve what comes next.

What comes next is the stripping.

### How It Begins

_Guðníðing_ almost always begins in outlawry, because outlawry is the condition that removes every reason to maintain the divine relationship. A man with a hall and a family and a place at the Thing has reasons to keep faith — the oaths hold him, the community watches him, the god-post stares at him from the temple, and the barrow where his father lies requires his visits. Remove all of that. Remove the hall, the family, the law, the oath-network, the community, the barrow-visits. What remains is a man in a cave with a fire that is dying and a body that is cold and a mind that has been under siege for years and a question that the Veil whispers in the silence between heartbeats: _why do you still pray?_

The first act is always the same. The god-scorned man takes whatever sacred object he carries — a carved Heimr-mark on a leather thong, a rune-scratched bone from a dead kinsman, the battered iron Thurr-hammer that his mother hung around his neck when he was six — and he breaks it. Not throws it away. Not buries it. Breaks it, deliberately, with his hands, looking at it as he does so. The act is trivial in material terms — a snapped cord, a cracked bone, a bent piece of iron. In spiritual terms, as the Rimevegr understands them, it is the equivalent of cutting one's own throat: the man has severed the last thread connecting him to the divine order, and the severance is witnessed by no one except the Veil, which has been waiting for exactly this.

The settlements know when a returning outlaw — or an outlaw's body — has walked the god-scorned path because the signs are unmistakable. The absence of every sacred marker. The deliberate inversion of every ward-sign: Heimr-marks scratched into doorposts upside down, Odynn-runes carved backward, Hael's offerings scattered and fouled. The god-scorned man does not merely abandon the gods. He mocks them. He performs the rituals in reverse, the way a man might mock a foreign language — each inverted gesture a deliberate statement that the original gesture was worthless.

### The Process of Unmaking

The god-scorned man does not become an animal overnight. The unmaking is a process, and the process has stages, and the stages are terrifying because they are coherent. The man who walks the god-scorned path is not raving. He is methodical. He is conducting a controlled demolition of his own humanity, and the demolition follows a logic that the rope-readers have reconstructed from the leavings of god-scorned camps:

**First — the sacred identity is destroyed.** The man relinquishes his name. Not formally — there is no ceremony for this, because the god-scorned do not do ceremony. He simply stops answering to it. The bandmates who knew him as Eirik or Gunnar or Björn find that the name produces no response, and after a time the man is called by a description — the Quiet One, Tooth, Bent-Back, Grey — because the name his father gave him at the picking-up was the first rune carved into his identity, and the god-scorned man is erasing every rune.

**Second — the body is marked.** The god-scorned man scars himself. Not randomly — the scars follow the lines of the ward-marks, the galdr-patterns, the rune-forms that are carved into the temple posts and the contract-stones and the barrow- wards. But the scars are wrong. They are the patterns inverted, the marks reversed, the wards cut into flesh in forms that a galdr-worker would recognize as the negative image of protection. Where a ward-mark says _stay_, the scar says _enter_. Where a binding-rune says _hold_, the scar says _release_. The god-scorned man is not decorating his body. He is opening doors in it — carving invitations into his own skin for whatever lives in the silence between the runes.

A barrow-keeper in Grimholt who examined the body of a god- scorned outlaw found the inverted Hael-mark carved into the man's chest over the sternum — the place where the death- breath exits. The inversion meant, in the barrow-keeper's reading: I do not want my breath sealed. I do not want the linen under my chin. When I die, let the mouth stay open. Let whatever comes climb in. The barrow-keeper burned the body rather than bury it, and she was right to do so, because a body carved with inverted wards is a body that has been pre-prepared for habitation.

**Third — the social bonds are severed through transgression.** The god-scorned man commits acts designed to make return impossible. Not the violence of survival — the raiding, the theft, the desperate cruelty of a hungry man — but deliberate transgressions against every taboo that defines the boundary between the human and the not-human. He does not steal because he is hungry. He defiles because defilement is the point. He fouls wells. He scatters barrow-gifts. He pisses on ward-stones and scratches out name-marks on barrow-entrances. Each act is a bridge burned. Each bridge burned is a commitment to the direction he is walking, which is away from the hearth and toward the grey, and the commitment compounds until return is not merely unlikely but structurally impossible — the man has transgressed so thoroughly that no settlement would take him back, no oath would hold him, no barrow would accept him, and no skald would carry his name.

This is the point of no return. The god-scorned man who reaches this stage has dismantled every mechanism that could pull him back toward humanity. The name is gone. The body is marked. The sacred connections are severed. The social bridges are ash. What remains is a creature in the shape of a man, walking north, carrying nothing except the hunger for whatever the Veil has been offering since the first night the silence became louder than the fire.

## The Three Soulbanes — Mannsbani Þrír

### What They Are

The Rimevegr recognizes three acts that kill the soul while the body continues breathing. Not crimes — the Thing adjudicates crimes, and crimes exist within the legal framework. These three acts exist outside the framework entirely. They are the things a man does after the framework has been demolished, after the gods have been scorned, after the wards have been inverted on his skin and the name has been abandoned and the bridges have all burned. They are the acts that complete the transformation from man to something else — something the Rimevegr has no proper word for because proper words belong to the ordered world and these acts belong to the space beyond it.

The rope-readers call them _mannsbani þrír_ — the three man- killers. Not killers of men. Killers of the man inside the man. Each one destroys a different component of the tripartite soul that the Rimevegr's theology assigns to every human being:

- The _hugr_ — the mind-soul, the seat of reason and will
- The _hamr_ — the shape-soul, the human form and composure
- The _fylgja_ — the guardian spirit, the divine inheritance

The three soulbanes are ordered. Each one is worse than the last. Each one requires the previous one to have already happened, because the soul must be dismantled in sequence — you cannot amputate the fylgja while the hugr still functions, because the hugr would scream and the man would stop. The sequence ensures that by the time the final act is committed, there is no part of the man left that is capable of objecting.

### The First Soulbane — The Eating

_Mannát_ — the consumption of human flesh.

This is the act that kills the hugr. Not because the meat itself is poison — the Rimevegr is a practical place, and there are records, kept quietly in the Deepholm archive, of besieged settlements and starving crews who ate the dead and lived and were not considered god-scorned for it, because starvation obliterates categories and the living have always eaten whatever was available when the alternative was joining the available. The difference is intention.

The god-scorned man who eats human flesh does not eat because he is starving. He eats because the eating is the point. The act is a communion — not with the gods, who are silent, but with the thing that lives in the silence. The man sits in a cave or a ruin or a hollow in the frozen ground, and he eats what was once a person, and the eating is slow and deliberate and conducted with the attention that a rope-reader brings to a sacrifice: every bite is a statement. The statement is: I am no longer one of you. Your flesh is my food. The boundary between your body and mine is the same as the boundary between a sheep and a man, which is to say it is a social agreement, and I have withdrawn from the agreement.

The hugr — the reasoning mind — cannot survive this. The hugr is the part of the soul that holds the categories: this is human, this is animal, this is food, this is not food, this is permitted, this is forbidden. The god-scorned man who eats the forbidden has forced his hugr to participate in an act that contradicts its fundamental architecture. The hugr does not break. It reorganizes. The categories dissolve. Human and animal become the same category. Food and not-food become the same category. The distinction between the man's body and the bodies around him — the distinction that is the foundation of individual identity — blurs, and in the blur the hugr stops being a mind and becomes a mechanism, still operating, still processing, but no longer organized around the principle of _I am human and that matters_.

What remains after the hugr reorganizes is a thing that thinks but does not reason. It plans but does not reflect. It calculates the distance to a victim the way a wolf calculates the distance to a deer — with perfect spatial intelligence and no moral dimension. The god-scorned men who have committed mannát and been captured (there are four recorded cases in three hundred years, and each case is kept in the Deepholm archive under a seal that the barrow-keepers do not break for casual inquiry) all exhibited the same pattern: total coherence, total functionality, total emptiness. They answered questions. They understood the questions. They did not understand why the questions were being asked, because the framework within which the questions had meaning — the framework that says eating people is wrong and requires explanation — no longer existed inside them.

In the winter of 288, a man was found in a cave above the Ashford valley. He was alive. He was warm, which was unusual for a cave in deep winter. The warmth came from a fire built inside a ring of human bones — femurs and tibiae arranged upright in the frozen ground like fence-posts, the skulls placed on top, facing inward. The fire was small and well- tended and the man was sitting inside the ring with the expression of a man sitting inside his own hall. He did not resist capture. He did not speak. He was brought to the nearest settlement, where the rope-reader examined him and found the inverted wards carved into his chest and arms and the Hael-mark reversed over his heart. The rope-reader asked his name. The man looked at the rope-reader without comprehension — not the incomprehension of a foreigner who does not speak the language, but the incomprehension of a creature that no longer understands what a name is for.

He was burned. The bones from the cave were collected and given to the barrow-keeper, who identified seven individuals. Three were outlaws whose descriptions matched missing wolfsheads from the Skaldhaven bounty-board. Two were thralls, identified by the iron collar-marks on the vertebrae. Two could not be identified. The barrow-keeper gave them name-stones anyway — "Unknown, died in the dark" — because a body without a name- stone is a body that will wander, and these particular dead had suffered enough wandering.

### The Second Soulbane — The Riding

_Dýrríða_ — being ridden by beasts.

This is the act that kills the hamr — the shape-soul, the human form, the aspect of identity that says _this is what I look like, this is how I move, this is the boundary of my body and everything outside it is not me_. The term is the Rimevegr's own, and it does not mean what the settlements assume it means when they whisper it to frighten children.

The Riding is not a sexual act in the way the settlements gossip about it. It is a spiritual surrender. The god-scorned man who has already eaten the forbidden and killed his hugr has one remaining boundary: the boundary of his own skin. The hamr — the shape — is that boundary. The hamr says: I am contained. I am this body. I begin at my skin and end at my skin and what is outside my skin is the world and what is inside is me. The Riding dissolves that boundary.

How: the god-scorned man goes to the places where the Veil is thickest — the hush-zones where sound dies, the frost-hollows where the temperature drops twenty degrees in ten paces, the barrow-fields where the wards have failed and the dead press against the boundary like animals against a fence. He goes to these places and he opens himself the way the inverted wards on his body have advertised: he becomes permeable. The barrier between what is inside and what is outside dissolves, and whatever lives in the Veil — whatever the seidr-wives feel pressing against the world's membrane, whatever the dying dead are fleeing from, whatever wears the gods' silence like a skin — moves through him.

The Rimevegr calls this "being ridden" because the language of horse-breaking is the closest analogy the culture has. A horse that is ridden has not stopped being a horse. It has not been destroyed. It has been _occupied_ — its body directed by a will that is not its own, its movement governed by something sitting on top of it, its strength channeled toward purposes it did not choose. The god-scorned man who has been ridden is still breathing. Still walking. Still wearing a human face. But the face is wrong in ways that are difficult to articulate and impossible to ignore.

The eyes change. Not the color — the focus. A ridden man's eyes track things that are not in the room. They fix on empty corners and follow movement that no one else can see. In conversation — if the ridden man can still be drawn into conversation, which becomes increasingly difficult — his gaze drifts to the left of the speaker's face, as though something more interesting is standing just behind them. Witnesses describe the sensation of being looked at by a ridden man as "being watched through a window" — the man is there, but the thing looking out is not him.

The body changes. Not dramatically — the god-scorned man does not sprout fur or grow claws. The changes are postural, behavioral, incremental. The way he walks shifts: less upright, more fluid, with a rolling gait that does not belong to a bipedal frame. The way he holds his head changes — tilted slightly, one ear forward, in the attitude of an animal listening for movement. The way he eats changes: faster, lower to the ground, with less use of the hands and more use of the teeth. Each change is small. Each change is noticeable. The accumulation is horrifying — not because the man is becoming an animal, but because the man is becoming an imitation of an animal performed by a human body that was not designed for the role, and the performance is getting better, and the audience cannot look away.

The seidr-wives of Icebreak, who have the most experience with Veil-contact, believe the Riding is literal: something from the other side of the Veil has entered the man through the doors he carved into his own skin, and the something is learning to operate the body the way a child learns to operate a puppet — clumsily at first, then with increasing fluency. The rope- readers of Skaldhaven disagree: they believe the Riding is self-inflicted, the final stage of a mind that has dismantled its own identity and is grasping for any organizing principle to replace the one it destroyed. The beast-behavior is not possession. It is mimicry — the hugr-less, hamr-less man copying the only model of functional existence he can still perceive, which is the animal, which does not need gods or names or oaths to know what it is.

Both explanations are consistent with the evidence. Neither is provable. The Rimevegr does not resolve this question for the same reason it does not resolve any theological question: the gods are silent, the seidr-wives disagree, and the man in front of you is walking on all fours and the academic distinction between "possessed" and "broken" does not change the fact that he needs to be put down before he reaches the settlement.

In the spring of 304, a hunting party from Frostfjord Hollow found tracks in the mud above the snowline. The tracks began as bootprints — heavy, deep, a man carrying weight. Over fifty paces, the stride shortened, the heel-strike shallowed, the toe-print deepened. At the end of the track, the prints were not bootprints. They were handprints and footprints together, four points of contact, moving fast, heading north. The hunters followed for two hours and then stopped, because the tracks entered a hush-zone and the hunters knew better. The tracks continued. Whatever made them did not stop at the zone's edge. It went in, and the silence swallowed it, and the hunters went home and told the gate-watcher, who marked it in the log as "large animal, unidentified, northbound." He did not write what the hunting party told him they suspected. He did not want the words in a document that someone might read.

### The Third Soulbane — The Wilding

_Villimennska_ — the severing of the spirit and the merging with the wild.

This is the last act. The act that kills the fylgja — the guardian spirit, the divine inheritance, the last remnant of whatever the gods placed in human beings to distinguish them from the beasts that the gods did not speak to. The hugr is already dead. The hamr is already broken. The fylgja is the final thread, and when it is cut, what remains is not a man and not an animal but something the Rimevegr calls a _villimaðr_ — a wildling — and the word is spoken in the same tone reserved for the Veil itself: not fear, not contempt, but the flat recognition of something that should not exist and does.

The Wilding is not an act the god-scorned man performs. It is an act that happens to him — the final collapse of a structure that has been systematically demolished. The hugr was killed by the Eating. The hamr was dissolved by the Riding. The fylgja — which has been turning its face away since the man's outlawry began, which withdrew further with each taboo shattered and each ward inverted — makes its final departure. The departure is felt. Not by the man, who no longer has the capacity to feel spiritual events, but by the people near him. Witnesses to a Wilding — and there are witnesses, three recorded, two of whom are sane enough to give testimony — describe the moment as a change in the air: a sudden cold, a pressure drop, the sensation of something leaving the room through a door that does not exist. One witness, a bone-walker named Dalla, said: "It was like watching a fire go out. Not the flame — the heat. The flame was still there. The body was still moving. But the warmth left, and what remained was not alive in any way that matters."

What a villimaðr looks like: a human body moving through the wilderness with the behavior of a predatory animal and none of the animal's innocence. The villimaðr does not speak. Does not use tools. Does not build fires. Does not wear clothing — the clothes have been shed over weeks or months, not in one dramatic gesture but in the slow divestiture of a creature that no longer understands what clothing is for. The villimaðr hunts. Eats. Sleeps in hollows. Moves through the Veil-touched landscape with a facility that no sane human possesses — the Veil does not confuse the villimaðr's navigation the way it confuses a normal traveler's, because the villimaðr is no longer navigating with the faculties that the Veil disrupts. It is navigating with something older, something beneath the human overlay, something that the gods' architecture was built on top of and that the gods' silence has uncovered.

The villimaðr is dangerous not because it is aggressive — a bear is aggressive, and the settlements know how to deal with bears — but because it is unpredictable in the specific way that a human body stripped of human motivation is unpredictable. It does not hunt when hungry and rest when full, the way an animal does. It does things that follow no pattern the hunters can read: kills without eating, hoards bones without building, marks territory with inverted ward-signs that it should not remember how to carve but does, because the body's muscle- memory outlasts the mind, and the rune-patterns scored into the skin are templates that the hands follow without a mind to guide them.

The settlements that border the deep waste know the villimaðr by reputation and by rare, devastating encounter. A villimaðr entered the livestock pen at Ashford in the winter of 309 and killed seven sheep without eating any of them. The kills were efficient — throat-cuts, not mauling. The gate-watcher found the bodies arranged in a circle with the heads facing inward and the blood pooled in the center. He reported it as a wolf attack. The barrow-keeper, who saw the arrangement of the bodies and the precision of the cuts, said nothing. She reinforced the ward-marks on the settlement's perimeter and did not sleep for a week.

### What the Villimaðr Means for Women

The god-scorned path is overwhelmingly male — outlawry skews male, the waste skews male, the violence that precedes the guðníðing skews male. But women walk it. Rarely. The cases that exist are recorded in the Deepholm archive with the same sealed notation as the mannát cases, and the archive-keepers handle them with the same reluctance, because the female god-scorned path includes a horror that the male path does not.

A woman who has completed the Eating and the Riding — whose hugr is dead and whose hamr is dissolved — is a body without a human operating principle but with a functional reproductive system. The Riding — the opening of the body to whatever presses through the Veil — has physical consequences that differ by sex. A man who is ridden carries the intrusion in his posture, his gaze, his movement. A woman who is ridden carries it in her body.

The barrow-keepers and the seidr-wives use the same term: _kvikindi-barn_ — the beast-child. Not a literal half-animal offspring — the Rimevegr's biology does not work that way and the people know it. What the term describes is a child conceived by a woman whose spiritual architecture has been demolished and whose body has been opened to the Veil's influence during the period of maximal permeability. The child that results — in the three recorded cases, and the records are sparse because the mothers did not survive long enough to provide testimony — is born wrong.

Not deformed. Not monstrous. Wrong in the way the ridden man's eyes are wrong: the body is human, the proportions are correct, the features are normal, but the thing behind the eyes has never been human. The child does not cry at birth. The child does not root for the breast. The child lies still and watches the ceiling with the fixed, tracking attention of a predator assessing a room, and the women attending the birth — invariably seidr-wives, because no one else will attend a god- scorned woman's labor — describe the sensation of the child's gaze as looking into the Veil through a keyhole made of flesh.

The mother dies. In all three recorded cases, the mother died within hours of the birth — not from hemorrhage or complications that a Dry-Eyed could treat, but from a cessation that the attending seidr-wife described as "the body simply stopping." As though the purpose the body had been serving — the gestation of the kvikindi-barn — was complete, and without that purpose the mechanism that had kept the body functioning through the Eating and the Riding and the Wilding simply turned off. The body cooled. The child lay beside it, still watching, still silent, still tracking movement with eyes that should not have been capable of focused vision in a newborn.

What happens to the kvikindi-barn is the question the Rimevegr has answered three times in three different ways. In the first case (year 261), the child was burned by the settlement that found it. In the second case (year 289), the child was taken by a seidr-wife who said she could study it. She and the child disappeared into the deep waste and neither was seen again. In the third case (year 311 — last year), the child was found by a bone-walker in a cave above the treeline, beside the cooling body of its mother, and the bone-walker — a woman named Yrsa who had spent thirty years in the mountains and feared nothing that walked on legs — carried it down to Frostfjord Hollow and left it at the gate without a word.

The child is at Frostfjord Hollow now. It is ten months old. It does not cry. It eats when fed but does not request food. It watches the barrow-field through the window of the foster-home where the Dry-Eyed placed it, and the foster-mother — who took the child because the Dry-Eyed asked and the Dry-Eyed do not ask without reason — says it is a quiet baby, an easy baby, a baby that requires nothing and gives nothing and watches everything with an attention that makes the other children leave the room.

The barrow-keeper visits weekly. She says the child's presence does not disturb the wards. She says the barrows have been quiet since the child arrived — quieter than usual, quieter than they have been in years. She does not say what she thinks this means. The settlement does not ask.

### How the God-Scorned Are Handled

A settlement that encounters a guðníðing — at any stage of the process — has one response: kill. There is no trial. There is no thing-assembly deliberation. The god-scorned man is not a legal entity. He is not even a wolfshead — a wolfshead was once a man and retains the shape of manhood. The god-scorned man has abandoned that shape deliberately. He is, in the Rimevegr's theological classification, _útgarðr_ — outside the fence. Outside the ordered world. Outside the gods' domain, by his own choice, which means outside the law's protection, outside the settlement's obligation, outside every social contract that says killing requires justification.

The killing is performed by whoever is nearest and willing. The body is burned, never buried. A god-scorned body placed in a barrow is a contamination — the inverted wards on the skin would interfere with the galdr-wards on the barrow-stone, and the barrow-keeper would need to re-ward the entire field. The ash from the burning is scattered in running water. The name — if anyone remembers it — is never spoken again. Not the quiet death of skald-erasure, which is a social punishment. This is something more complete: the person is removed from the record because including them would acknowledge that the god-scorned path exists as a possibility, and acknowledging the possibility is the first step toward normalizing it, and the Rimevegr cannot afford to normalize a path that leads from man to nothing.

The rope-readers maintain that the god-scorned path proves the gods are real — if the gods were irrelevant, then scorning them would have no effect, and the guðníðing would simply be a dirty, violent outlaw rather than a creature that walks on all fours and kills sheep in circles. The scar-mouths counter that the god-scorned path proves the gods were always a cage — the man who breaks the cage becomes something terrible, yes, but the cage was still a cage, and a cage is not a gift, and a god who builds cages is not a god who loves.

The stump-sworn, who adjudicate everything else, refuse to adjudicate this. The god-scorned are outside the law by definition. The law has nothing to say about a man who has dismantled himself. The stump-sworn press their palms to the judgment-stone and feel nothing — not even the occasional phantom hum that some verdicts produce. The stone is silent. The god-scorned are silent. The gods are silent. The silence agrees with itself, and the living are left to handle the consequences alone, which is, the scar-mouths note, exactly what the living have always done.

## Disease, Starvation, and the Cave Camps

### What the Waste Smells Like

A cave camp after three months of habitation smells like a charnel house. The men do not wash because water is scarce and cold and the skin that freezes wet is skin that dies. The wounds do not heal because the filth is everywhere -- in the bandages, in the food, in the water that drips from the cave ceiling through soil that the band uses as a latrine because going outside to relieve yourself at night is how you get a knife in the kidney from a bounty hunter.

Infection takes more outlaws than violence does. A cut that would heal in a warm hall with clean linen and honey-salve festers in the cave camp and the red lines spread up the arm and the arm swells and the fever comes and the man dies over three days while his bandmates decide whether to drag the body outside -- which means exposure -- or leave it in the cave, which means the smell.

Most leave it in the cave.

### Starvation

A band that has been raiding a valley for two seasons has stripped it. The farms are empty or defended. The livestock is gone. The stored grain was eaten or scattered or burned. The band has two choices: range farther -- which means marching through hostile ground with every settlement watching the road -- or starve.

Starvation in the Rimevegr waste is slow and specific. The body consumes itself. First the fat -- and most outlaws have no fat to spare. Then the muscle. The man who was strong enough to swing an axe becomes the man who cannot lift his arm above his shoulder. The man who could march thirty miles in a day becomes the man who sits against a wall and breathes and that is all he can manage. The mind goes strange. Decisions that should be simple become impossible. The starving man fixates on things -- food he remembers eating, a meal his mother cooked, the taste of butter -- and the fixation crowds out everything else until thinking about bread is all he does.

A band at the edge of starvation is capable of anything. The calculations that normally restrain violence -- the awareness that burning a farm generates feud, that killing a farmer generates a bounty, that visibly predatory behavior draws a coalition -- cease to operate because the men are dying and dying men do not fear consequences. The worst atrocities in the Rimevegr are committed by bands that are already finished. The blood eagle at the crossroads, the flayed skin on the frame, the corpse road -- these are the acts of men who are going to be dead within a season and have decided that the season's remaining days will be spent making sure everyone remembers.

### The Last Winter

When the food is gone and the raiding ground is exhausted and the band is too weak to march, the cave camp becomes a tomb. The men sit in the dark because they have no fuel. They do not speak because speaking costs energy they do not have. The sick ones die first and the dying is quiet -- a cough that stops, a body that does not move when the man next to it shifts position.

What happens in the last days of a starving outlaw band is not recorded because no one survives to record it. The bones tell some of the story. The marks on the bones tell the rest.

## Using This in Play and Fiction

### For the Captain

The wolfshead threat is omnipresent. Every hex the band crosses may contain men with nothing to lose. The captain's job is to read outlaw pressure, avoid unnecessary confrontation with desperate men who fight without restraint, and -- if a patron asks -- clear the wolves for coin that rarely covers the risk.

The wolfshead is also the mirror. The captain who looks at an outlaw camp and thinks "that could never be us" is the captain who has not done the arithmetic. Three contracts fail in a row. The treasury empties. The men have not been paid. The Articles fray. The Named Men argue. The captain who does not pay his men becomes the captain whose men do not follow orders, and the captain whose men do not follow orders is already an outlaw in everything but name.

### For the Ledger-Keeper

Track outlaw_pressure per settlement. Track bounties. Track the band's own Standing relative to atrocity consequences. The ledger does not care whether the burning was justified. The ledger records the Standing drop, the Feud Track advance, the bounty posting. The numbers are the judgment.

### For the Fiction

The atrocity is never consequence-free. The prose does not moralize -- the ledger does not moralize -- but the consequences arrive, patient and mathematical. The village burned in chapter three is the coalition that blocks the road in chapter nine. The sheep stolen in the Long Dark are the children who did not survive until spring. The oath broken at the Thing is the contract that no one will sign three seasons later.

Show the farmer's daughter who remembers the name. Show the blind man sitting in the elder's hall, his empty eyes turned toward the door, waiting for the sound of boots that he knows will come eventually because the men who blinded him are still out there and a blind man is not a closed account -- a blind man is a living witness who cannot be silenced because everyone has already seen his face.

Show the corpse road. Show the men who made it. Show that they were men once -- that they had names and plans and someone who loved them -- and show that the waste stripped all of that away and left something that stacks rocks on a ridgeline and drives stakes into the frozen ground and puts heads on them and faces them toward the road and calls it necessary and means it.

The system does not punish evil. It accounts for cost.
