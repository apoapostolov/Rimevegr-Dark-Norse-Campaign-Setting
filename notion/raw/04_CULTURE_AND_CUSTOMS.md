# Culture and Customs of the Rimevegr

**Project:** Iron Ledger -- Mercenaries of the Rimevegr

**Purpose:** Prose reference for narrating authentic daily life in the
Rimevegr. Each section gives a writer enough texture to describe scenes
naturally -- sensory, physical, grounded. Not a textbook. A toolbox.

---

## Daily Life

A farmer in the Rimevegr wakes before light because there is almost never
light. During the long_dark, first grey comes late and leaves early. He
does not stretch or yawn dramatically. He pulls on a wool tunic that smells
of smoke and old sweat, checks the fire, and eats whatever the pot holds
from last night -- barley porridge gone cold and thick, maybe a strip of
dried cod if the stores are holding. Then he goes out into the cold and
works until the cold sends him back in.

The rhythm is brutal and small. Feed the animals. Break the ice on the
water trough. Mend the fence the wind took. Carry wood. Check the grain
pit. A farmer's hands are cracked and his knuckles swollen by thirty. His
wife works the same hours indoors: spinning, weaving, salting meat, minding
the hearth, watching the children she has not buried yet. In Stonebay
Hamlet, there is a woman who has borne six and raised two. She does not
speak of the others.

A fisher's life is worse. He goes out on black water in a boat that leaks,
hauls nets with hands so numb he cannot feel the rope cutting his palms,
and comes back with whatever the fjord gives -- which is sometimes nothing.
At Bleakwater Landing, a fisherman who brings back an empty net three days
running starts hearing talk about his luck. Bad luck spreads. The
settlement cannot afford it.

A thrall wakes when told and sleeps when allowed. Thralls eat last, sit
farthest from the fire, and do the work no free person wants -- emptying
the latrine trench, hauling water uphill, scrubbing pots with sand in
freezing streams. A thrall's clothing is whatever the master gives, usually
cast-offs worn through at the elbows. In Vargheim, thralls sleep in the
byre with the cattle. The cattle are worth more.

Children work as soon as they can carry. A boy of seven hauls kindling. A
girl of seven cards wool. Play exists -- carved bone pieces for
knucklebones, chasing games in the mud -- but it happens in the margins of
labor, stolen in the brief gaps when no adult is watching. During long_sun,
children are almost feral, running barefoot through the sudden greenery.
During long_dark, they huddle near the fire and listen to the old stories
and learn to be afraid of the right things. Darkness teaches.

Meals are two: morning porridge and evening stew. Bread when the grain
allows it, which is not always. Meat is preserved -- salted, smoked, dried.
Fresh meat means someone slaughtered an animal the settlement could not
afford to keep, or a hunter brought back game. Ale-mist washes it down.
Clean water is a luxury the settlements near Icebreak take for granted and
the moorland hamlets fight over.

## Social Hierarchy

Rank in the Rimevegr is visible from across a room. A jarl sits at the high
seat -- the carved chair at the center of the long-wall bench, facing the
door, fire between him and whoever enters. His cloak is fur- trimmed, his
arm-rings heavy, his cup filled before anyone else's. When a jarl speaks,
the hall goes quiet. Not out of love. Out of the understanding that the
jarl has warriors, and warriors have appetites that someone in the room
will feed.

Karls -- free men, bondi, landholders -- sit along the benches in rough
order of worth. The man closer to the jarl's seat has more land, more
silver, more reputation. The man at the far end, near the door and the
draft, has less. Everyone knows the seating. Changing it is a public
statement. In Grimholt, a bondi named Halvar moved two seats closer to the
jarl's bench without invitation. Three men noticed. One of them was the man
whose seat he took. That dispute went to the thing and cost Halvar a cow.

Thralls do not sit. They stand, serve, and eat what is left. A thrall who
sits unbidden at a free man's bench has committed an insult that can
legally be answered with a beating. In practice, most thralls know the
geometry of the hall better than anyone -- they navigate it carrying
platters, pitchers, and the quiet weight of knowing that everything they
touch belongs to someone else.

Speech follows rank. At a gathering, the jarl speaks first. Then the most
prominent karl. Then others, roughly in order of standing. A younger man
who interrupts an elder is not just rude -- he is declaring that his status
exceeds the elder's. He had better be right. At the allting near
Skaldhaven, speaking out of turn has started more feuds than any dispute
over cattle.

Clothing tells the story without words. Undyed wool means poor. Dyed wool
-- even badly dyed, even faded -- means someone paid. Linen undergarments
mean wealth or trade connections. Silver brooches mean a woman of standing.
Iron brooches mean she married below her birth or times are hard. A man's
arm-rings say everything his mouth does not: each ring is a contract
honored, a battle survived, wealth retained. The man with bare arms has
nothing saved.

Food reinforces it. The jarl eats meat when others eat porridge. His bread
is wheat, not barley. His ale is proper ale, brewed with hops, not the thin
ale-mist the commons drink. At feasts, the best cuts go to the high bench.
The gristle and the bones go to the far end. Nobody complains. Complaining
is for people who have choices.

## Gender Roles

Women in the Rimevegr hold the keys. Literally. The housewife carries the
ring of iron keys at her belt -- to the storeroom, the grain chest, the
silver box. A man may own the land, but his wife controls what comes out of
it day to day. A husband who overrides his wife's household decisions is a
fool twice: once for the bad decision, and again for publicly declaring he
does not trust the person managing his survival.

Property rights are real. A woman can own land, inherit from kin, and hold
wealth independently of her husband. Divorce is available and used. A woman
in Ashen Reach divorced her husband for striking her in front of witnesses.
She kept the farm, the stores, and two of the three thralls. He kept his
reputation, which was ruined. The Pale Widow rules that settlement not
because someone gave her authority but because her husband died and she had
the grain, the silver, and the will to keep both.

Seidr -- spirit-work, veil-reading, the deeper magic -- is women's domain
by tradition. A woman who practices seidr is feared and respected. A man
who practices it carries a heavier stigma, the old ergi shame, the
accusation of unmanliness that clings like woodsmoke. Thorne avoids this by
calling his rune-work a craft, not seidr. The distinction is thin. People
notice and choose not to push it because Thorne is useful.

Shield-maidens exist but are not common. Petra fights in the Black Axes
because she can and because the band needs everyone who can hold a
shield-wall. Nobody writes songs about it. The men do not treat her as a
curiosity or a symbol -- they treat her as one more axe-hand who needs to
pull her weight and does. In Raven's Perch, an old woman remembers a
shield-maiden from her grandmother's time who held a ford for a full day
against a raiding party. The story is told not as a marvel but as a fact,
like weather.

Men's obligations are outward: fight, farm, build, trade, die when
required. A man who will not fight is not merely a coward -- he is a drain
on the settlement's survival. The Long Dark does not tolerate passengers.
In Feldwick, before the Three Wolves burned it, a man who refused the
muster was not punished formally. He was simply not fed from the common
store when it mattered. He left on his own. Nobody recorded where he went.

## Age and Life Stages

Childhood in the Rimevegr is short and conditional. A newborn is laid on
the ground. The father picks it up. That is the acceptance. A child not
picked up is left outside. This happens less than people think and more
than people admit. Deformity, hard winters, too many mouths -- the reasons
are practical and the grief is private.

Naming happens at the picking-up. The name carries weight. A child named
after a dead relative inherits some of that person's luck, or their doom.
In Thornwall, a boy named for his grandfather who drowned was never allowed
on boats. The name chose the fear for him.

Fostering is common among families with alliances to maintain. A jarl's son
sent to live with another jarl's household at seven or eight creates a bond
between the families -- the boy is hostage, student, and future ally in
one. Mercenary bands do not foster in the formal sense, but taking on a boy
of twelve as a camp-follower and teaching him to sharpen blades and haul
water is not far from it. The boy either grows into a fighter or does not
survive long enough to matter.

Coming of age is not a ceremony. It is a moment. A boy picks up a weapon
and someone lets him keep it. He joins a shield-wall and does not run. He
kills a man, or a deer, or hauls a net through a storm, and the older men
stop calling him "boy." In some settlements, a father gives his son a ring
or a blade. In others, the transition goes unspoken. Moor's End marks it
when a boy can carry a full load of peat from the bog to the village
without stopping. That is the test.

Marriage is arrangement. Love may follow, or not. The families negotiate
bride-price and dowry. A woman brings property into the marriage and
retains rights to it. A man brings his name, his arms, and whatever silver
he has saved. The ceremony is an oath before witnesses, an exchange of
swords (his to her family, hers to his), and a feast that the families can
barely afford. In Kolvik, before it burned, weddings happened in long_sun
because nobody can afford a feast in the dark.

Elderhood earns respect while the elder earns it. An old warrior who can
still advise is valued. An old farmer who knows the weather signs is
listened to. An old person who can no longer work and has no kin to feed
them is in trouble. The Rimevegr does not have a system for this. Some
settlements share. Some do not. In Deepholm, there is a stone bench outside
the hall where the old sit in what passes for sunlight and wait for someone
to bring them food. Sometimes, during the worst of long_dark, no one comes.

Death preparation is quiet. A dying man tells his family where his silver
is. He asks that his axe go to his son and his loom to his daughter. He may
ask for a burial mound or a burning. Then he tries to die well, which in
the Rimevegr means dying without begging or weeping, facing whatever comes
with the silence the cold has taught him.

## Hospitality and Guest-Right

Guest-right is sacred in the Rimevegr, not because the gods enforce it --
the gods are silent -- but because the alternative is a world where no
traveler is safe and no settlement can trade. It is a compact of mutual
survival held together by reputation. Break guest-right and the word
travels faster than you do.

A traveler arrives at a settlement gate. He calls out his name and
business. If the gate-warden judges him no threat -- armed bands are
different from lone travelers -- the gate opens. Inside, the host provides
fire, food, and a place to sleep. The traditional offering is bread and
salt, placed in the guest's hand before any other business. In Frostfjord
Hollow, Jarl Hrothgar's household uses a heavy barley loaf and coarse grey
salt from the coast. Taking the bread means accepting the obligation: you
will not harm the host, steal, or dishonor the household. Refusing the
bread is an insult that says "I do not trust your table." It starts fights.

The host's bench -- the seat nearest the fire on the guest-side of the hall
-- belongs to the visitor. A good host offers ale, asks for news, and does
not press for details the guest does not volunteer. Three days of free
hospitality is the custom. On the fourth day, the guest contributes labor
or leaves. An overstaying guest eats stored food that a settlement measured
against the long_dark. Politeness has a caloric limit.

Violating guest-right -- stealing, violence, seduction of the host's family
-- is among the worst crimes in the Rimevegr. Worse than murder in some
respects, because murder can be settled with weregild. Guest- right
violation cannot. A man known for it finds every gate closed. In Ashmark, a
traveler who poisoned his host's dog after a dispute over sleeping
arrangements was driven out in long_dark with nothing but the clothes he
wore. The settlement considered it a lenient judgment. The cold was not.

For mercenary bands, guest-right is complicated. A Svarthird arriving at a
settlement is not a guest -- it is a negotiation. The ale-price is set
before weapons are put aside. The captain and the headman agree on terms:
how long, how much, what is off limits. The band pays or it does not stay.
The formal guest-right applies to the captain and his named men if they
enter the hall. The commons camp outside the walls. Even so, the underlying
principle holds: once bread and salt are shared, violence is off the table.
The Silent Oar is known for honoring this absolutely. The Three Wolves are
not.

## Feasting and Drinking

A feast in the Rimevegr is not leisure. It is politics performed with food
and drink. The hall is cleaned -- rushes replaced, old bones swept out,
torches lit. The fire is built high. The jarl sits at the high seat. The
seating arrangement along the benches is the negotiation that happened
before anyone arrived. A man who finds himself closer to the door than last
time knows what it means without being told.

The food comes in stages. Bread first, then stew, then meat if there is
meat. At Skaldhaven, where the jarl is wealthy by Rimevegr standards, a
harvest feast might include roast mutton, fresh cod, and honey-cakes. At
Moor's End, a feast is a better grade of barley stew with actual chunks of
salt pork. The quality does not matter as much as the act: the jarl is
feeding people, and feeding is power.

Toasts are ritual. The first toast is to the host. The second to absent kin
or the honored dead. The third is open -- and this is where the performance
begins. A man stands, raises his cup, and makes a boast or an oath. He will
kill his enemy by harvest. He will repay his debt before Ironmoon. He will
take his father's land back from the jarl who stole it. The hall listens.
If the boast is credible, men pound the table. If it is empty air, the
silence says everything. A skald in Raven's Perch once said that a man's
third toast is the truest thing he will ever say, because the ale strips
away everything except what he actually wants.

The skald works the room. Between courses, during the natural pauses, the
skald recites -- a saga fragment, a praise poem for the host, a mocking
verse about a rival jarl. The best skalds read the hall like a battlefield
and adjust. If the mood is high, they tell of glory. If tensions are
simmering, they steer toward humor. If a fight is brewing, they either
defuse it with wit or get out of the way.

Fights start after the third round. The ale loosens grievances that
politeness held down. An old insult resurfaces. A debt is brought up at the
wrong moment. A man looks at another man's wife a heartbeat too long. The
weapons are supposed to be left at the door, but there are always knives
and fists. At Grimholt, the jarl's house-karls break up fights before they
draw blood. At lesser settlements, the brawl plays out until someone bleeds
and sense returns.

The morning after a feasting night is grim. Men with splitting heads sit in
the grey light and eat cold porridge. Oaths made the night before are
remembered. Some are honored. Some are quietly forgotten by mutual
agreement. The thralls clean the vomit and the spilled ale and say nothing
because they were not asked.

## The Skald's Place

The skald is the Rimevegr's memory. In a world without books, without
archives, without any record that outlasts the stone it is carved on, the
skald carries the past in his skull and gives it back in verse. A
settlement without a skald forgets itself within two generations. A jarl
without a skald has no reputation beyond the men he can personally
frighten.

Oral tradition means everything is performance. A saga told by a good skald
in firelight is not a story -- it is law, history, entertainment, and
warning wrapped in meter and delivered with the precision of a man who
knows that a misplaced word can start a feud. The skald at Skaldhaven --
the settlement took its name from the tradition -- is a grey-bearded man
named Ornulf who has memorized every significant event in the inner fjords
for forty years. He is not loved. He is necessary. When Ornulf dies, three
decades of disputed inheritance claims will become unresolvable, and
everyone knows it.

Who commissions poems? Anyone with silver or a reputation worth praising. A
jarl pays a skald to compose a praise-poem (drápa) that recounts his
victories, his generosity, his wisdom. The payment is generous because the
alternative -- a skald who feels underpaid -- is a man with the skill to
destroy your name and the motive to use it. Even mercenary captains
commission verse. Voss once paid a wandering skald six copper to compose a
stanza about the Black Axes holding a bridge at Kolvik. It was not a great
poem. But the men heard it and stood taller.

Mockery-verse -- nid -- is the skald's weapon. A nid poem attacks a man's
honor, courage, or manhood in verse so sharp that the insult lives in
public memory long after the poet has moved on. A well-crafted nid can make
a man a laughingstock across the inner fjords. It can cost a jarl
alliances. It can drive a man to violence, which is sometimes the point.
Composing nid without justification is dangerous -- the subject may demand
blood-price, or simply find the poet in a dark passage. But a nid with
truth behind it is almost impossible to answer, because the audience
already suspects the truth and the poet just gave it rhythm.

The power of words in the Rimevegr is not metaphor. Runes whisper. Galdr
chants have physical effect. A curse spoken with the right cadence can --
perhaps -- touch the Veil. The skald stands at the intersection of memory,
art, and something older and less safe. Most skalds are not magic-workers.
But the best ones understand that the line between a poem that moves men
and a galdr that moves the world is thinner than anyone comfortable admits.

## The Rimevegr Sings

The Rimevegr is not a quiet place. The wind howls. The ice cracks. The
timbers settle. And inside every hall, every byre, every boat and camp and
cave where the living gather, someone is singing. Not because the Rimevegr
values beauty. Because the Rimevegr values survival, and song is how
survival is transmitted — the rhythms that teach the body what the mind
cannot hold, the melodies that carry information too important to trust to
prose.

The distinction between song and speech in the Rimevegr is not the
distinction that musicians make. There is no audience. There is no stage.
Song is the texture of communal life — the noise that rises wherever people
work together, mourn together, drink together, kill together. A man who
cannot carry a tune is not mocked for his art. He is watched, because a man
who does not sing is a man who has stopped participating in the group's
shared rhythm, and a man who has stopped participating is a man who is
leaving.

### Cradle-Songs — Vǫgguvísur

A Rimevegr mother does not sing her child to sleep with lullabies about
pleasant things. She sings death-counts.

The _vǫgguvísur_ — cradle-songs — are the first music any person hears, and
they are catalogues of the things that will kill you. The melody is gentle.
The rhythm is the rhythm of rocking — a slow pendulum that soothes the
infant's body while the words lay down the architecture of fear that the
child will need to survive. _The ice will take you if you walk past the
marker. The water will hold you if you fall through the shelf. The men from
the waste will find you if the fire burns too bright. Hael sees you when
you wander. Hael counts your fingers. Hael is patient._

Every settlement has its own variations, but the structure is universal: a
list of dangers, set to a tune the body learns before the mind can parse
the words. By the time the child is old enough to understand what the words
mean, the melody has already written them into the muscles — the flinch
when ice creaks underfoot, the reflexive stillness when a stranger's
silhouette appears on the ridge. The cradle-song is not art. It is
programming. The mother sings because singing is how you write survival
instructions into a creature too young to read.

The darkest vǫgguvísur are the ones about the dead. A mother in Thornwall
sings to her infant about the child she buried last winter, and the dead
child's name is in the melody, and the living child hears the dead child's
name every night before sleep, and by the time the living child can speak,
the dead sibling is as real as the hearth-fire — a presence carried in the
music, never met but always known. The cradle-songs preserve the dead the
way the skalds preserve the famous. The difference is that the skalds
preserve the mighty and the vǫgguvísur preserve the small — the infants who
did not survive, the children taken by fever, the ones the sagas will never
mention because their lives were too brief to earn a verse. The mothers
give them a verse anyway, and the verse lasts as long as a mother sings it,
and mothers always sing.

### Work-Songs — Verklög

Rowing without a song is rowing at half-speed. This is not metaphor. The
oarsmen in a six-bench karvi cannot maintain stroke-timing without an
audible rhythm, and the audible rhythm is a song — a _verklag_ — that the
stroke-caller sets and the crew follows. The words are irrelevant. The beat
is everything. A good stroke-caller can push a crew from twenty strokes a
minute to twenty-six without increasing perceived effort, because the song
carries the body past the point where the mind would quit.

Every repetitive task in the Rimevegr has its verklag. The women at the
grinding-stone sing a song whose rhythm matches the rotation of the quern.
The men splitting firewood sing a song whose downbeat lands with the
axe-fall. The women carding wool sing in a rhythm that matches the draw of
the comb. The smith sings with the hammer — _anvil-song_ is the term the
dictionary carries, and the term understates it, because the smith's
verklag is the most complex of all: a polyrhythm of hammer on hot iron,
bellows-pump, and voice, three lines of percussion woven into a sound that
the apprentice learns before he learns to judge heat by color.

The verklög carry technical information. A net-mending song in Bleakwater
Landing encodes the sequence of knots in its verse-structure — the first
line is a sheet-bend, the second a clove-hitch, the third a bowline, and a
child who has sung the song a hundred times can tie the sequence in the
dark because the fingers follow the melody. A timber-raising song at
Grimholt counts the number of men needed per beam-length in its chorus. A
salting-song at Skaldhaven times the brine-soak in its verses — three
repetitions means the fish has been in the barrel long enough. The woman
who changes the verse-count ruins the batch, and the batch feeds the
settlement through the Long Dark, and the settlement does not forgive a
woman who cannot keep count in a song.

Nobody writes the verklög down. Nobody needs to. They pass from hand to
hand, mother to daughter, master to apprentice, the way diseases pass — by
proximity, by repetition, by the inability to unhear a rhythm that the body
has absorbed. A verklag is never taught. It is caught.

### Battle-Songs — Hervísur

The sound that precedes contact is not a war-cry. It is a hum.

A Svarthird company in formation begins to vocalize before the shield-wall
closes. The sound starts low — a chest-tone, the frequency that vibrates
the sternum and the jaw, felt as much as heard. One man begins. The man
beside him matches the pitch. The third man adds a half-tone. Within thirty
seconds, the entire line is producing a single sustained drone that rises
from thirty throats and carries across the field the way fog carries across
water — not directional, not locatable, a pressure that the enemy feels in
the teeth before they identify it as sound.

The hum serves three purposes. First: it synchronizes breathing. A line of
men breathing together is a line of men whose diaphragms are locked to the
same rhythm, and locked diaphragms mean locked cores, and locked cores mean
a shield-wall that absorbs impact as a unit rather than as thirty
individual men each flinching at their own tempo. Second: it drowns the
internal noise. The man who is humming cannot hear his own heartbeat
hammering, cannot hear the voice inside his skull that says _run_. The song
fills the channel that fear would otherwise occupy. Third: it terrifies.
The enemy hears the hum and understands that the men producing it are not
individuals. They are a mechanism. A mechanism does not flinch. A mechanism
does not break. A mechanism comes forward at the speed of the hum, and the
hum does not stop.

The hervísur — battle-songs — escalate from the hum. When the wall begins
to advance, the drone rises into syllables. Not words. Not yet. Syllables —
rhythmic pulses of vowel-sound that time the step: _ha-ha-ha-ho_, one
syllable per pace, the sound hardening as the distance closes. At twenty
paces, words appear — a name, usually. The enemy leader's name, chanted in
a falling cadence that turns the name into a dirge. At ten paces, the name
breaks into a roar, and the roar is the last thing the front rank of the
enemy hears before the shields meet, and the roar has a function beyond
intimidation: it forces a maximal exhalation that braces the abdominal wall
for impact. The man who roars into the collision has a tighter core than
the man who screams. The roar is not emotion. It is engineering.

After the wall breaks — after contact, after the organized violence becomes
the disorganized kind — the singing stops. The battle-song belongs to the
formation. Once the formation dissolves, the men are individuals again, and
individuals do not sing. They grunt. They gasp. They make the animal sounds
that the hum was designed to replace.

A band that has lost its battle-song has lost its cohesion. Voss knows
this. He listens for the hum before every engagement. If the men are
humming, they will hold. If the men are silent, they are afraid. If the men
are talking — chattering, joking, filling the silence with words instead of
with the drone — they are terrified and pretending otherwise, and the
pretense will collapse at first contact.

### Wedding-Songs — Brúðarlög

The wedding in the Rimevegr is a transaction performed in verse.

The groom's party arrives singing. The song is the _brúðarboð_ — the
bride-offer — and it is a legal document set to melody: who the groom is,
what he brings, what he has done that makes him worthy of the bride's
family's investment. The verses list his lineage, his property, his kills
if he is a warrior, his catches if he is a fisher, his yields if he is a
farmer. The song is composed by the groom's skald — or by the groom
himself, if he is poor enough to lack a skald — and it is performed at the
threshold of the bride's hall, and the bride's family listens from inside,
and the door stays closed until the song is finished.

The bride's party responds with the _brúðargrátr_ — the bride-grief. This
is the song the women sing for the woman who is leaving. The melody is a
minor-key descending line, the notes dropping like water from a roof's
edge, and the words are not congratulations. They are mourning. The woman
is leaving her family. The woman is leaving her mother. The woman is
entering a house where she has no history, no allies, no verklög she
recognizes, no cradle-songs she knows. The bride-grief acknowledges this.
It says: you are being given away, and the giving is a loss, and the loss
is real, and we will not pretend otherwise.

The bride does not sing. She is silent throughout — the only silent person
at a Rimevegr wedding. Her silence is not submission. It is the silence of
the thing being exchanged. A goat does not negotiate its own sale. The
women around her sing the grief she is not permitted to express, and the
singing is a shield, and the shield protects her from the requirement to
perform gratitude for an arrangement she may not have chosen.

After the threshold songs, the hall opens, and the drinking begins, and the
drinking songs take over — the competitive, escalating, increasingly
obscene verses that Rimevegr men produce when ale loosens the part of the
brain that rhymes. The wedding-night songs are inventoried separately in
the settlement's oral tradition, because they are too crude for the
year-saga and too funny to forget, and the skald who performs them at the
wedding is performing a different service than the skald who performs the
year-saga — he is giving the couple a shared joke, a piece of communal
vulgarity that will follow them for decades, so that when the marriage goes
cold, as marriages do, there is at least one memory that makes both of them
laugh.

### Mourning-Songs — Grátlög

The Rimevegr does not weep quietly. It keens.

The _grátlög_ — the weeping-songs — begin at the body. The women of the
settlement gather around the dead, and the closest female relative begins —
a sustained, rising wail that is not music in any formal sense but that
follows a melodic contour as instinctively as a falling body follows
gravity. The wail rises. The other women join. The sound builds in layers —
each woman entering at her own pitch, her own rhythm, the aggregate
producing a chord that shifts and breathes and never resolves, because the
grief that powers it does not resolve.

Men do not keen. This is not a rule. It is an observation. The men stand
outside the circle of women, and they are silent, and their silence is the
complement to the women's wailing the way a shadow is the complement to the
object that casts it. The men will have their own rite later — the
_beinasöngr_, the bone-song, sung at the barrow when the wards are cut and
the body is sealed inside. The bone-song is low, rhythmic, almost
whispered, a male chorus that sounds like earth settling. The words name
the dead and name the dead's deeds and close with a formula that every man
in the Rimevegr knows: _We give you to the stone. The stone does not
forget._

The grátlög can last hours. At Icebreak, where the dead are frequent, a
keening session that runs from dusk to dawn is not unusual. The women
rotate — the lead keener steps back when her voice fails, another steps
forward, the wail continues without interruption. The sound carries across
water. Fishing boats offshore hear the keening and know that someone has
died at the settlement without needing a messenger. The sound is the
message.

The most disturbing grátlög are the ones for children. The melody drops
lower, not higher. The wail becomes a whisper that is somehow louder than
the full-voice keening — a sound that travels through walls and into the
chest cavity and sits there. Men who have heard the children's grátlög
describe it as the worst sound in the Rimevegr. Not the loudest. Not the
most violent. The worst. Because the melody is the vǫgguvísur — the
cradle-song — sung backward. The same notes, the same rhythm, reversed. The
song that put the child to sleep sung as the song that marks the child's
last sleep, and the symmetry is perfect and the symmetry is unbearable.

### Drinking-Songs — Ǫldrlög

The third toast opens the floor, and the floor becomes a competition.

Rimevegr drinking-songs are not composed in advance. They are improvised —
verse by verse, man by man, each singer adding a stanza that must rhyme
with the previous one, must advance the subject, and must be funnier,
cruder, or more inventive than what came before. The subject is set by the
first singer — usually the host's skald or the drunkest man brave enough to
stand — and the subject is always a person in the room. A jarl. A warrior.
A woman. A thrall. The first verse establishes the target. Every subsequent
verse builds the portrait.

The rules are unspoken and absolute. The verses must be clever. Crude is
acceptable. Stupid is not. A man who adds a verse that does not rhyme, does
not scan, or does not advance the portrait is shouted down, and the
shouting is part of the song — the collective rejection folded into the
rhythm as a rest-beat before the next singer stands. A man who adds a verse
that is genuinely brilliant — a rhyme so sharp it cuts, a metaphor so apt
it becomes the subject's permanent nickname — earns a roar from the hall
and a full cup poured by whoever is nearest. The brilliant verse is the
currency of the drinking-song. It buys reputation the way silver buys
goods.

The drinking-songs escalate. By the fourth round, the verses are explicitly
sexual. By the sixth, they are scatological. By the eighth, they are making
claims about the subject's ancestry, hygiene, and livestock that would
start a fight in any other context but that the song's frame renders
permissible — barely. The frame is the protection. Inside the song, a man
can say what would earn him a knife-wound outside the song, because the
song is understood to be a space where truth wears a mask, and the mask
allows truths that the face cannot.

The danger is when the frame breaks. A verse that lands too close to a real
wound — a man's dead son, a woman's barrenness, a debt that everyone knows
about and no one mentions — cracks the song open. The hall goes quiet. The
quiet is the frame collapsing. What follows the quiet depends on the man
who was hit: if he laughs, the song resumes. If he does not laugh, the song
is over, and something else begins.

The greatest drinking-songs survive their nights. A verse-cycle composed at
a feast in Skaldhaven about a jarl's legendary stinginess was still being
recited three valleys away a decade later. The jarl was dead. The song was
not. The song had become a creature independent of its creators — a living
piece of communal composition that no single author could claim and no
authority could suppress, because it existed in fifty memories
simultaneously and each memory held a slightly different version and the
differences were the proof that the song was alive, still mutating, still
being sung.

### Law-Songs — Löglag

The Lawspeaker at the Thing does not read law. He sings it.

The _löglag_ — the law-songs — are the Rimevegr's legal code, encoded in
verse for the same reason the verklög encode technical procedures: because
verse is memorable and prose is not. A law that rhymes is a law that a man
can carry in his head for forty years without error. A law in prose is a
law that shifts every time it passes through a mouth, and shifting law is
no law at all.

The Lawspeaker performs the löglag at every major Thing — standing on the
Law-Rock, facing the assembly, reciting the relevant statutes in a chanting
monotone that is not quite singing and not quite speaking but something
between the two that uses the melody as a mnemonic scaffolding. The melody
is always the same. The words change as the law changes — new provisions
are set to new verses, old provisions are amended by altering the words
while preserving the tune, and the tune is the anchor that holds the whole
structure in memory. A Lawspeaker who forgets a verse has forgotten a law.
A law that is forgotten does not exist.

The löglag are not beautiful. They are not meant to be. They are functional
— dense, rhythmically rigid, packed with legal terminology that has no
synonym because the synonym would break the meter. The Lawspeaker who
performs them is not an artist. He is a filing cabinet with a pulse. The
settlements tolerate his droning because the alternative is a written code,
and the Rimevegr does not write, and even if it did, a written law can be
burned and a sung law cannot.

### Learning-Songs — Námsvisur

Children in the Rimevegr learn to count inside a song about dead men.

The _námsvisur_ — learning-songs — are how the settlement transmits
practical knowledge to children too young to learn by doing. The
counting-song counts bodies: _One man fell at Kolvik ford. Two men fell at
Ashmark gate. Three men died on Brokentooth ridge._ The numbers attach to
places and to deaths because numbers attached to nothing are numbers that
the mind releases. Numbers attached to a dead man at a real place are
numbers the mind keeps, because the mind holds what frightens it, and death
always frightens.

Lineage-songs teach identity. A child of six sings her line backward: her
name, her father's name, her father's father's name, her father's father's
father's name, as far as the family remembers, which in a well-maintained
line is eight to twelve generations. The song's rhythm is the rhythm of the
genealogy — each name occupying one beat, each generation occupying one
measure, the whole structure forming a musical map of the child's place in
time. A child who cannot sing her lineage is a child who does not know who
she is, and a child who does not know who she is cannot be trusted with the
family's obligations, because obligation flows through lineage the way
water flows through a channel, and a child without a channel is a child who
will let the obligations spill.

Season-songs teach survival. A child of eight sings the signs of
approaching storms, the indicators of thin ice, the plants that are food
and the plants that are poison, the animal behaviors that signal predator
presence, the time of year when the fjord current shifts and the fishing
grounds move. The song is the curriculum. The test is whether the child
survives the next Long Dark. The children who remember the songs survive at
a measurably higher rate than the children who do not, and the mothers who
sing the songs know this, and the knowledge is why the singing never stops
— it is not tradition. It is technology. The most effective survival
technology the Rimevegr has produced.

### The Song Never Stops

A settlement without song is a settlement dying.

This is not sentiment. It is diagnosis. The verklög cease when the workers
stop cooperating. The hervísur cease when the warriors lose cohesion. The
vǫgguvísur cease when the mothers stop bearing children, or stop expecting
the children to live. The grátlög cease when there are too few women left
to keen. Each silence is a symptom, and the symptoms compound, and a
settlement that has gone quiet for a full season is a settlement that the
neighboring settlements watch the way you watch a man who has stopped
shivering — not with hope, but with the knowledge that what comes after
silence is not recovery.

The Rimevegr sings because the Rimevegr must. The songs are not decoration
laid over the surface of a hard life. They are the structure of the hard
life itself — the load-bearing walls of a culture that has no written
language, no permanent architecture, no institutions that outlast the
people who inhabit them. Remove the songs and the culture does not become
quieter. It ceases to exist. The laws evaporate. The dead dissolve. The
children grow up without the programming that keeps them alive. The work
loses its rhythm and the rhythm was the thing that made the work possible.
The silence that follows is not peace. It is the sound of a people
forgetting themselves, one unsung verse at a time.

## Söngœði — The Sung-Madness

There is a thing that happens in halls during the Long Dark, and in
war-camps after victory, and at funerals that run too long, and at feasts
where the ale is strong and the fire is high and the bodies are close and
the night outside is very cold and very long. The skalds have a name for
it. The galdr-workers have a different name. The common folk call it what
it feels like: _söngœði_ — song-madness.

It begins with a line.

Someone sings a line. Not a whole song. Not a planned performance. A single
line — a phrase, a couplet, sometimes just a rhythm hummed through closed
teeth. The line is not always good. It is not always original. It is the
right line at the right moment in the right room, and the room catches it
the way dry kindling catches a spark.

A second voice picks it up. Then a third. The third voice changes a word —
not deliberately, not artistically, but because the third singer does not
know the original word and substitutes the word that feels right, and the
word that feels right is a word no one in the room has used before in this
context, and the word is better than the original. A fourth voice hears the
change and extends it — adds a line that follows the new word to its
conclusion, and the conclusion opens a direction that the first singer
never intended, and the direction is true in a way that the first singer's
intent was not.

The song is building itself.

### How It Grows

The söngœði escalates in stages that the galdr-workers have documented with
the same clinical attention they give to rune-blight thresholds.

**First stage: the catch.** A line takes hold. Two to five voices. The
rhythm is simple and the words are familiar — a known phrase, a common
image, a name everyone recognizes. This stage lasts minutes. Most catches
die here. The rhythm falters, the moment passes, the hall returns to its
drinking. Nothing happened.

**Second stage: the pull.** The catch holds. New voices enter without
deciding to. A man who was mid-sentence in a conversation stops talking and
starts singing, and he does not know why, and if you asked him he would say
the song was louder than his thought, and the thought could wait but the
song could not. The words begin to change. The original line mutates —
syllables shift, names appear that were not in the first verse, rhymes
resolve into meanings that nobody planned. The room's attention collapses
inward. Conversations die. Dice games stop. The song is the only sound, and
the song is coming from twelve throats that did not rehearse, and the
twelve throats are producing words that none of the twelve minds composed.

**Third stage: the shaping.** The song is no longer recognizable as the
line that started it. It has a structure now — verses, a chorus that
arrived from nowhere, a narrative that the singers are discovering as they
sing it. The words are not chosen. They arrive. A man opens his mouth to
sing the next syllable and a word he has never spoken fills his throat, and
the word fits the meter and the rhyme and the meaning, and he does not know
where it came from, and he does not care, because the song is moving and he
is inside it and the inside of the song is the warmest place he has been
all winter.

At this stage, the physical symptoms begin. Singers flush. Pulses
synchronize — not gradually, not approximately, but precisely, as though
thirty hearts have been tuned to a single tempo. Breathing aligns. The room
smells different — not the hot-iron of galdr, not the turned-earth of
seiðr, but something older and harder to name: the smell of a crowd of
mammals who have become, temporarily, a single organism. Sweat. Breath. The
chemical signature of shared adrenaline. The women who have witnessed both
seiðr trance and söngœði say the smell is the same. The galdr-workers say
nothing, because the galdr-workers know what the smell means and the
meaning frightens them.

**Fourth stage: the truth.** The song produces knowledge.

Not metaphorical knowledge. Not the vague emotional resonance that a good
poem generates. Specific, verifiable, previously unknown information. A
verse names a man who drowned at a crossing that no one in the room
witnessed. A line describes the location of a barrow that the settlement
has been searching for. A chorus contains a legal precedent that the
Lawspeaker does not recognize but that, when checked against the oldest
löglag, turns out to be a statute that was lost two generations ago. A name
surfaces — a name no one present knows, a name that belongs to someone who
has not arrived yet, and when the person arrives, weeks or months later,
the name is the name they carry and the hall remembers singing it before
the person existed in their world.

The truths are not reliable. Not every söngœði produces them. Not every
truth produced is accurate. But the hit rate is high enough — and the
specificity is disturbing enough — that the Rimevegr cannot dismiss the
phenomenon as collective drunkenness, and the galdr-workers cannot dismiss
it as folk superstition, and the seiðr-wives cannot dismiss it because the
Veil behaves differently during söngœði and they can feel it.

### The Galdr-Worker's Role

The galdr-worker does not sing the söngœði. He starts it.

This is the secret that the galdr tradition guards more carefully than any
rune-combination or carving technique: a skilled galdr-worker can initiate
a söngœði deliberately. Not by singing a rune-chant — the söngœði is
explicitly not galdr, not focused on a rune, not channeled through the
carver's trained infrastructure. The galdr-worker initiates it by singing a
_seed-line_ — a phrase calibrated to catch, designed to be imperfect enough
that the room's collective voice will want to fix it, shaped so that the
fixing draws more voices in, and the drawing is the mechanism, and the
mechanism is older than galdr and older than runes and possibly older than
the gods' silence, and the galdr-workers know this and it is why they treat
the söngœði with the particular reverence that craftsmen reserve for forces
they can trigger but cannot control.

The seed-line is not a rune. It is a name.

A name worth a saga. A loss demanding closure. A question the settlement
has been carrying like a stone in its throat — who really killed Halfdan?
Where did the children of Brennvik go? Why did the ward-stones on the
northern ridge fail in the autumn of 298? The galdr-worker chooses the name
or the question, sets it to a rhythm that the common ear can follow, and
drops it into the room the way a man drops a coal into straw. Then he stops
singing and he listens.

What happens next is not the galdr-worker's to control. The room takes the
seed-line and the room does what the room does — the voices catch, the pull
takes hold, the shaping begins, the truths emerge or they do not. The
galdr-worker's role changes: he becomes the scribe. He sits at the edge of
the song with a wax tablet or a strip of birch-bark, and he writes down
what the room produces — the verses, the names, the knowledge that surfaces
through thirty mouths that are not composing but are being composed
through. He records what the song says because the singers will not
remember. The söngœði, when it passes, leaves the participants with the
physical aftereffects — hoarseness, exhaustion, the pleasant emptiness of a
body that has been wrung out — but not the words. The words belong to the
song. The song keeps them. The galdr-worker's tablet is the only permanent
record, and the permanent record is what the tradition values, because the
tradition believes — with evidence that cannot be dismissed and cannot be
explained — that the truths in the song are real, and real truths should
not be lost to the amnesia that follows the singing.

The galdr-workers call this practice _ljóðveiðr_ — verse-hunting. The
galdr-worker who initiates a söngœði is not performing magic. He is setting
a trap for knowledge that exists somewhere in the collective memory of the
room — or somewhere in the Veil, or somewhere in the ørlög, or somewhere
that no tradition has a name for — and the trap is the song, and the bait
is the seed-line, and the catch is whatever truth the room drags out of the
silence and into the verse.

### What the Song Takes

The söngœði is not safe.

A man who sings in a söngœði for an hour emerges hoarse and happy. A man
who sings for four hours emerges voiceless and confused. A man who sings
for eight hours — and the Long Dark söngœði episodes have been known to run
through an entire night — emerges with a throat that bleeds when he
swallows and a mind that cannot remember the previous day clearly, as
though the song ate not just his voice but his recent memory, consuming the
biographical data to fuel whatever process generates the truths.

The voice damage is temporary. The memory gaps are not always.

Settlements that experience frequent söngœði — Skaldhaven and Thornwall are
the most documented — report a particular kind of communal amnesia: the
hall remembers the song but not the week before the song. The everyday
details — who owed whom, what was said at the Thing, which family's cow was
lame — blur and dissolve, as though the söngœði fed on the mundane to
produce the extraordinary. The truths the song surfaces are paid for in the
small truths that the singers carried, and the trade is not negotiated. The
song takes what it needs.

The worst cases are the singers who cannot stop.

The söngœði ends — the energy subsides, the room exhausts itself, the fire
burns low and the bodies slump and the song fades to silence. But
occasionally, one singer continues. His mouth moves. The melody persists in
his throat, a thin thread of sound that the others have dropped but that he
cannot release. He sings through the dawn. He sings through the morning
meal. He sings at the workbench, at the latrine, at the water-trough, the
song pouring from him in a whisper that does not stop.

The condition lasts hours, usually. Sometimes days. In three recorded cases
in the Rimevegr's recent history, the singer never stopped. One man at
Moor's End sang continuously for eleven days, his voice reduced to a dry
rasp, his body maintained by his wife who fed him broth and water between
verses that never paused long enough for a full swallow. On the twelfth
day, his voice failed entirely. He continued singing in silence — his lips
moving, his throat working, no sound emerging. He died on the fourteenth
day. The cause, as far as the settlement could determine, was exhaustion:
the body ran out of the resources needed to sustain both the singing and
the living, and the singing took priority.

His wife reported that the words he sang in the final days were not words
she recognized. They were not Rimevegr speech. They were not any language
she had heard. But they had rhythm and they had meter and they rhymed, and
the rhymes followed a pattern that the settlement's skald, listening from
the doorway, described as "older than any verse-form I know." The skald did
not enter the room. The skald stood at the threshold and listened and wrote
down what he could hear, and the fragments he recorded were later shown to
a galdr-worker at Icebreak who went very quiet and would not discuss what
the words meant.

### What the Song Is

The Rimevegr has three explanations for the söngœði, and none of them is
satisfying, and all of them are true.

**The Bragi explanation.** The skalds say the söngœði is the string trying
to return. Before the Cracking, Bragi's gift gave the human voice a
resonance beyond its physical capacity — the invisible harp, the harmonic
that made sagas true in a way that went beyond accuracy. The Cracking broke
the string. The string is gone. But the capacity that the string served —
the channel between the collective voice and something larger — did not
close entirely. It leaks. The söngœði is the leak: the channel opening
briefly, chaotically, uncontrollably, producing fragments of the connection
that was once continuous and reliable and is now sporadic and dangerous.
The skalds believe the truths the söngœði produces are Bragi's truths —
fragments of the total saga, the complete record of all things, that the
god once made accessible through the string and that now surfaces in shards
through the broken connection.

**The Veil explanation.** The seiðr-wives say the söngœði is the dead
singing through the living. During a söngœði, the Veil thins in the room —
measurably, perceptibly, in the same way it thins during a seiðr working
but without a practitioner directing the thinning. The dead press close.
They do not speak — the dead have no voices of their own, not in the
seiðr-wife's framework. But they have memory, and memory has weight, and
when thirty living voices create a channel through rhythm and shared
intent, the dead's memories flow into the channel the way water flows into
a ditch. The words that the singers do not recognize are the dead's words —
the knowledge they carried, the things they saw, the truths they died
holding. The living provide the voice. The dead provide the content. The
song is the meeting-point, the joint where the two world-halves press
together and something passes between them.

**The pragmatic explanation.** The common folk say that thirty people in a
room who share a rhythm share a mind, and a shared mind knows more than any
single mind, and the knowing surfaces through the channel that the song
provides. They do not need the gods for this. They do not need the Veil.
They need only the observation that a crowd of people singing together
produces something that none of them could produce alone — a collective
intelligence that draws from every singer's memory, every singer's
experience, every conversation overheard and every fact absorbed and every
thing seen from the corner of an eye and never consciously registered. The
söngœði gathers these fragments and assembles them in the only format that
the unconscious mind can produce at speed: verse. The truths are not
supernatural. They are the sum of a hundred unnoticed observations,
compressed into rhyme by a brain that thinks in patterns, delivered through
a throat that has been liberated from the individual self by the rhythm of
the group.

None of these explanations accounts for the cases where the song produces
knowledge that no one in the room could have possessed. The name of a man
who has not arrived. The location of a barrow that no living person has
visited. The legal statute lost for two generations. The pragmatic
explanation shrugs at these cases. The Bragi explanation smiles. The Veil
explanation nods. And the galdr-worker, sitting at the edge of the room
with his tablet and his birch-bark, writes it all down and does not choose
between the explanations, because the galdr-worker's job is not to explain
the song. It is to catch what the song throws.

### When the Settlement Fears It

Not every settlement welcomes the söngœði.

The larger settlements — Icebreak, Skaldhaven, Grimholt — tolerate it
because they have the infrastructure to manage it: skalds to record,
galdr-workers to read the seed-lines, seiðr-wives to monitor the Veil, and
enough bodies that a night lost to the singing does not cripple the next
day's work. In these settlements, the söngœði is treated the way a storm is
treated — you cannot prevent it, you can prepare for it, and the
preparation makes the difference between a night of useful truths and a
night of damage.

The smaller settlements fear it. A hamlet of forty people cannot afford a
night where every adult voice is consumed by a song that takes memory and
voice and leaves only fragments of knowledge that may or may not be useful.
The hamlet's goats still need milking at dawn. The wood still needs
splitting. The nets still need mending. And the people who should be doing
these things are sitting in the hall with raw throats and blank eyes,
unable to remember what day it is, holding fragments of verse that may
contain a lost law or may contain nothing.

Some settlements ban it. Not by decree — you cannot decree a song into
non-existence — but by practice. The jarl's house-karls watch for the
catch. When a line takes hold and a second voice enters, a house-karl steps
forward and speaks loudly over the singing — breaks the rhythm, collapses
the catch before the pull can take hold. The interruption must be
immediate. Once the pull begins — once the third and fourth voices enter
and the room's attention collapses inward — breaking the söngœði is like
breaking a wave already formed. You cannot. You can only ride it out and
manage the aftermath.

The galdr-workers object to this suppression. They argue — correctly — that
the truths the söngœði produces have material value: lost laws, barrow
locations, knowledge that no other method can surface. They argue that
banning the söngœði is like banning the storm because rain is inconvenient.
The jarls who ban it argue — also correctly — that a night of sung-madness
in a hamlet of forty can leave the settlement unable to function for three
days, and three days of lost function during the Long Dark can kill people,
and dead people do not benefit from discovered truths.

The argument has no resolution. It has only the same negotiation, repeated
every generation, between the galdr-workers who want to harvest the song
and the leaders who want to keep their people functional. The song does not
care about the negotiation. The song arrives when it arrives, and the
settlement either manages it or it does not, and the settlements that have
learned to manage it — to let the song run in controlled conditions, with
skalds recording and seiðr-wives watching and galdr-workers reading the
seed-lines and house-karls ready to carry out the singers who collapse —
are the settlements that have the deepest archives, the most complete law-
songs, and the richest oral tradition in the Rimevegr.

The settlements that suppress it have order. The settlements that allow it
have knowledge. The Rimevegr, as always, cannot afford both.

## Seidr in Society

Seidr is feared because it works. Not always, not reliably, and never
without cost, but the woman sitting at the edge of the firelight who reads
the smoke and speaks in a voice not entirely her own -- she knows things
she should not know. The Veil between the living and the dead shifted after
the Cracking. Seidr pushes against that Veil. Sometimes the Veil pushes
back.

In settled life, a völva -- a seidr-woman -- occupies a strange space. She
is needed for divination, for reading fate, for speaking with what stirs
beyond the Veil. She is consulted before major decisions: should the
settlement trade with Ashmark? Is the long_dark going to break early? Will
the jarl's sick child survive? The answers come wrapped in ambiguity, as
they always have. But sometimes they are specific enough to be terrifying.
In Thornwall, a völva told a mother her son would die before the ice broke.
The ice broke three days later. The boy had been dead for two.

Galdr-speakers work differently. Galdr is rune-chant, structured and
deliberate -- closer to craft than communion. A galdr-worker carves runes,
speaks them aloud, and the world bends slightly. How slightly depends on
the worker, the runes, and something no one can quantify that might be
talent, might be the Veil's mood, might be the residue of whatever the gods
left behind. Thorne works galdr. He will not call it seidr. The distinction
matters to him, even if it does not always matter to the runes.

Rune-readers are the most common practitioners -- they read existing runes,
interpret old carvings, and occasionally sense when a rune-stone is active.
Every settlement with a rune-stone older than the Cracking wants a
rune-reader nearby. Not because the reader can control what the stone does,
but because the reader can tell the settlement when to stay indoors.

Who practices? Women, mostly. Older women, often. The young seidr- worker
is distrusted not for her gender but for her inexperience -- the Veil does
not forgive errors. Who hires? Everyone, when desperate enough. Jarls hire
for strategic divination. Mothers hire for sick children. Mercenary
captains hire when the Veil closes in and the barrows groan. Who avoids
seidr? Sensible people. The Bone Pack's Pack-Elder Sura will not have a
völva in camp -- she says they draw the attention of things that should not
know where you sleep. The Grave Wardens, by contrast, travel with a
rune-reader at all times. Their reasons are their own.

## Death Attitudes

The Rimevegr thinks about death the way a farmer thinks about winter -- it
is coming, it cannot be bargained with, and the only question is whether
you face it prepared. The good death is simple: a man dies fighting, or
dies at work, or dies having done what needed doing. He does not beg. He
does not weep where others can see. He says what needs saying -- where the
silver is, who gets the axe, what debts remain -- and then he goes. If the
gods are waiting on the other side, he meets them standing. If nothing
waits, he does not know it yet.

The bad death is the opposite. Dying in bed of fever, soiling the blankets,
calling for a mother dead twenty years. Dying by drowning without a fight.
Dying by poison, by ambush, by treachery -- any death where the dying man
had no chance to prove himself. The Rimevegr does not curse the dead who
died badly. It pities them, which is worse. A man who died badly is
remembered less, spoken of less, and eventually forgotten, which in an oral
culture is the true death.

Funeral customs vary by settlement and family. The three forms: cairn
burial (a stone mound over the body, with grave goods -- weapons, tools, a
cup, sometimes food), ship burial (the body placed in a vessel and burned
at the waterline -- expensive, reserved for jarls and captains), and field
burial (the body laid in a shallow grave with a marker stone and whatever
goods the family can spare). In Frostfjord Hollow, the dead are
cairn-buried on the high ground north of the settlement. In Deepholm, they
are placed in old mine shafts and the shafts are sealed. Nobody in Deepholm
explains why.

Cowards -- men who broke and ran, who abandoned shield-siblings, who
refused to fight when the band needed them -- receive no burial rites. The
body is left where it falls, or dragged to the edge of camp and covered
with stones to keep the animals off. No one speaks their name at the
funeral fire. The Rimevegr's worst punishment is not death. It is dying and
then being unmade from memory, as though you were never here. The Iron Tide
Remnant -- what remains of that broken band -- are said to carry their dead
with them because no settlement will let them bury on consecrated ground.
Whether this is true or a story told to shame them further depends on who
you ask.

## Law and the Thing

The thing-assembly is the Rimevegr's closest thing to a court, and it works
about as well as a court run by armed men with personal interests. It meets
when enough people with enough grievances gather at a recognized site -- a
flat field near Skaldhaven, a stone circle above Ashmark, the harbor mouth
at Bleakwater Landing. The jarl or headman who hosts the thing presides. A
law-speaker recites the relevant precedents from memory. Disputants speak
in turn.

Speaking rights follow rank. A jarl speaks first and longest. Free men
speak next. A thrall cannot speak at a thing except through his master. A
woman can speak if she holds property or if the dispute involves her
directly -- and her speech carries the weight of whatever she owns. The
Pale Widow has spoken at three things in Ashen Reach and won every dispute,
because the woman controlling the grain supply in a hungry region has an
argument no jarl can comfortably ignore.

Blood-price -- weregild -- settles killings. The price of a life is
negotiated, not fixed. A jarl's son costs more than a landless drifter. A
skilled smith costs more than a poor farmer. The negotiation is public,
heated, and sometimes more dangerous than the original killing. If the
families agree on a price and it is paid, the feud ends. If not, the feud
escalates. In Grimholt, an unpaid weregild from two winters ago still
simmers between two families. Everyone knows it will end in blood. Nobody
knows when.

Outlawry is the ultimate sentence. A man declared outlaw has no legal
protection. He can be killed without consequence. His property is forfeit.
No settlement may shelter him. In a world where surviving the long_dark
alone is nearly impossible, outlawry is a slow death sentence. Some outlaws
flee to the wilds and join desperate bands. Others turn to the Svarthird --
mercenary life is one of the few paths open to a man with no legal
existence. The Hollow Hall band is rumored to be half outlaws, which is why
no settlement trusts them and every settlement hires them.

Mercenaries and the law have a complicated relationship. A Svarthird has no
legal standing at a thing unless contracted by a party with standing.
Crimes committed under contract are the contractor's responsibility. Crimes
committed between contracts are the band's problem, and the problem is
usually solved with silver or distance before anyone calls a thing. Voss
keeps Gest to handle this: the quarter-master knows which disputes can be
bought quiet and which require the band to be fifteen miles away by
morning.

## Trade and Exchange

The Rimevegr runs on two economies stacked on top of each other. The
gift-economy is older and deeper: a jarl who gives generously binds men to
him. A generous host earns allies. Gifts create debts that are social, not
financial -- you do not repay a gift with coin, you repay it with loyalty,
with service, with a gift in return when the giver needs it. This economy
runs on reputation. Everyone remembers who gave and who kept.

The market economy sits on top, newer and cruder. Hack-silver is the medium
-- arm-rings and ingots cut with a chisel into pieces of the needed weight,
measured on a folding scale. A trader who carries his own scale and weights
is a professional. A trader who trusts someone else's scale is a fool.
Copper coins exist for small transactions -- a meal, a night's ale, a
bundle of firewood. Silver buys weapons, animals, thralls, and alliances.
Gold is rare enough to be remarkable. A man who pays in gold is either
wealthy or robbed someone who was.

Market days happen at the larger settlements. Thornwall holds a market
every twelve days during long_sun -- traders come from as far as Frostfjord
Hollow and Ashmark, bringing salted fish, iron tools, amber scraps, woven
cloth, seal oil, and whatever else the fjords and the forests produce. The
market at Skaldhaven is the largest in the inner fjords, held in the open
ground south of the jarl's hall. Prices shift with the seasons: grain is
cheap at harvest and dear by Ironmoon. Dried fish is cheap when the catch
is good and blank when the cod do not come. A man who pays attention to the
markets at two settlements can profit from the difference. Most people are
too busy surviving to speculate.

Barter fills the gaps. A farmer trades cheese for iron nails. A fisherman
trades smoked herring for a new net. A healer trades her skill for a
winter's supply of firewood. These exchanges do not pass through any market
-- they happen at doorsteps, in cow-byres, across fence lines. The terms
are negotiated face to face, remembered by both parties, and enforced by
the knowledge that you will see this person every day for the rest of your
life.

Mercenary bands buy supplies the hard way. The quarter-master -- Gest, in
the Black Axes -- handles procurement. He arrives at a settlement gate,
asks for the headman, and negotiates the ale-price: what the band needs,
what the settlement can spare, and what it costs. If the negotiation goes
well, silver changes hands and the band eats. If it goes badly, the captain
gets involved, and the conversation acquires an edge that everyone pretends
is still commerce. The Red Tide once skipped the negotiation entirely and
took what they needed from a hamlet near Moor's End. The hamlet was
deserted within a week. The Red Tide ate well for three days and created a
supply problem that lasted a month, because the next settlement heard what
happened and barred the gate.

## Camp Life for Mercenaries

A Svarthird on the march does not make a home. It makes a perimeter. The
camp layout follows a logic that every fighter learns in his first week:
fire at the center, captain's tent on high ground if possible, weapons
within arm's reach, latrine trench downhill and downwind. The Black Axes
set two watches per night -- four men each, rotating. The watch starts when
the fire burns low enough to see past. Sleeping men keep their boots on and
their belts loose but buckled.

The cooking fire is the camp's center of gravity. Dalla -- the band's cook
-- works with what the foragers bring: a hare, a handful of wild garlic,
last night's barley stretched with water. The pot is iron, dented, and has
been carried across more of the Rimevegr than most men in the band. Meals
are eaten in silence or in fragments of conversation. Nobody lingers. The
men who finished eating take over the tasks of the men who have not:
sharpening blades, checking straps, mending the holes in their boots with
awl and sinew. Everything is maintenance. The march erodes gear the way
water erodes stone -- slowly, constantly, and fatally if ignored.

Gambling happens every night the band is not actively dying. Dice and
knucklebones, usually. The stakes are copper, future shares, or petty
favors -- who carries the extra water, who takes first watch. Cheating is
common and dangerous. A man caught palming a weighted die loses his stake
and takes a beating from the nearest veteran. A man suspected but not
caught keeps the table tense. In the Three Wolves, gambling debts between
fighters have led to two killings in the last year. Hadric did not punish
the killers. He docked their shares.

Tensions accumulate like ice on a beam. Men who live in each other's sweat
and silence build grievances that civilians never experience. He chews too
loudly. He does not pull his weight on the march. He owed silver three
weeks ago and pretends he forgot. He speaks too freely about the captain.
These are small things that become large things when there is no privacy,
no comfort, and no way to walk away. The sergeant -- whoever holds that
role -- absorbs the pressure. He hears the complaints, judges which ones
need the captain's ear, and quietly handles the rest. A good sergeant
settles three disputes before breakfast and one more by firelight.

The captain maintains order through the only tools available: pay, fear,
and the arithmetic of survival. The men follow because the alternative is
worse -- alone in the Rimevegr, in the long_dark, with no fire and no
shield-wall. Voss keeps his band together not through charisma but through
the relentless demonstration that the ledger is fair, the watch is honest,
and the man who pulls his weight eats before the man who does not. It is
not loyalty. It is the cold calculation of men who know what happens to
those who wander off alone. The Grave Wardens call it "the chain that has
no links." Every man is free to leave. No man can survive leaving.

## Horse Culture and Breeding Lore

Horses in the Rimevegr are not prestige objects first. They are winter
machines. A beautiful horse that panics on ice, founders on poor forage, or
burns through hay like a jarl's feast is a bad horse no matter how high it
lifts its knees. The culture admires a mount that comes home alive, keeps
its feet in sleet, and does not need three men to hold it. People notice
color and bloodline, but they trust temperament.

The culture also draws a hard distinction between a horse that is merely
owned and a horse that is worked with. A rider who beats a horse for fear
is judged the same way a captain is judged for striking men because he
cannot command them. Hard handling happens. Nobody pretends otherwise. But
a horse with a ruined mouth, saddle sores, or a cracked hoof tells everyone
the owner is short-sighted, wasteful, and probably worse at everything else
too.

Mares are preferred by practical people. They are cheaper to keep, steadier
in close company, and less likely to injure one another in a tight camp.
Stallions exist mostly where prestige or breeding fees matter. Geldings are
rare because the procedure is risky in a poor, cold land with limited
animal medicine, but wealthy households and the best outrider bands still
make them when they want a war-mount without a stallion's temper. A man
insisting every serious rider needs a stallion is usually announcing
status, not competence.

Horse naming follows the same brutal practicality as everything else in the
Rimevegr. Most names come from a visible truth: Frostmane, Bent-Ear, Coal,
Whitefoot, Ragtail, Longstep. Renaming a horse after it has served for
years is treated as ill luck and mild arrogance, as if the rider thinks his
voice matters more than the animal's earned nature. A mount that returns
riderless may keep the dead rider's name attached to it for a generation.
The story matters more than ownership papers nobody has.

### Breeds of the Rimevegr

The Rimevegr does not breed toward elegance. It breeds toward specific
survival niches.

| Breed               | Cultural Reputation                  | Typical Use                                           | Common Saying                                       |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Rimefjord pony      | Honest, calm, ugly, reliable         | General riding, family horse, light war work          | "It looks like winter and thinks like an old wife." |
| Moor-runner         | Fast, sharp, thin-skinned            | Scout horse, messenger work, moor pursuit             | "It sees tomorrow and hates today."                 |
| Pine-cob            | Slow, broad, impossible to embarrass | Pack work, cart pull, camp horse                      | "A pine-cob will outlive your excuses."             |
| Spine pony          | Sure-footed, half-goat, half-spite   | Mountain travel, ridge paths, smugglers, bone-walkers | "Where the path ends, the spine pony begins."       |
| Southblood warhorse | Impressive, expensive, temperamental | Noble guard, shock charge, ceremonial display         | "Fine until the weather remembers where you are."   |

Rimefjord ponies are the local standard and the breed most people mean when
they say "horse." They are compact, heavy-coated, and mentally hard to
surprise. They do not win races, but they rarely waste a rider. Moor-
runners come out of Thornwall and the open moors: narrow-chested, quick,
clever, and often bad-tempered if handled badly. They are admired by scouts
and distrusted by farmers. Pine-cobs are not glamorous at all, which is why
camps love them. They carry kettles, spare shields, and wounded men without
pretending any of it is unfair.

Spine ponies are mountain stock from Deepholm, Raven's Perch, and the
higher passes. They are short, ugly, and worth far more than they look when
the path is shale, ice, and bad judgment. Southblood warhorses are imported
or bred from imported sires. They produce the stories jarls like to tell
about thunderous charges and noble seats, but every local knows they need
better feed, better tack, better farriers, and better weather than the
Rimevegr usually offers.

### Horse Breeding Customs

Breeding is household strategy, not hobby. A family decides whether to
cover a mare the same way it decides whether to salt another barrel of cod:
by calculating winter risk. A foal means future value, but it also means a
pregnant mare that cannot be pushed as hard, a nursing mare that must be
fed, and a year or more before any return is seen. Poor families skip
breeding in bad years because they need the mare working now.

Stud arrangements are verbal contracts witnessed by kin. The mare owner
usually pays with silver, hay, or service. If the mare slips the foal, the
loss is treated as misfortune unless someone can prove negligence. If a
stallion injures a mare, the stallion owner owes compensation. This is
argued with the same seriousness as a damaged boat or broken plow, because
in hard country a breeding mare is productive capital.

People track bloodlines in the way oral cultures track everything: through
memory, witness, and argument. They remember which line throws good feet,
which line goes sour under male riders, which line keeps working in sleet,
which line panics at fire. Nobody speaks of genetics. Everyone practices
it.

Foals are judged early for four things that matter more than beauty: foot,
wind, nerve, and sense. Foot is whether the animal keeps itself upright
where other horses slip. Wind is whether it keeps going after the first
hard burst. Nerve is what it does when surprised, crowded, or hurt. Sense
is the old horseman's term for an animal that notices danger before the
rider does. A horse with all four is talked about for years. A horse
missing two is gelded, sold cheap, or worked to death in harness.

### Horses in Belief and Omen

Horse skulls already carry ritual weight in the Rimevegr, so the living
animal sits close to taboo. A horse refusing a ford, a doorway, or a barrow
approach is taken seriously even by hard men who mock seidr in daylight.
People will joke about cow omens. They do not joke much when a sound horse
plants its feet and starts looking at something nobody else can see.

White markings are read the way people read weather signs: with too much
confidence and just enough accuracy to keep the habit alive. A white
forehead star is lucky for finding the road home. White stockings on hind
legs mean the horse is swift but may not stay. A wall-eyed horse is either
blessed or wrong in the head depending on who owns it. Bone- walkers prefer
dark, plain mountain stock because they distrust anything that looks like
it wants to be noticed.

The old insult "horse-breaker" is double-edged. It can praise a rider who
can settle a dangerous mount, or condemn a brute who mistakes force for
mastery. In the Rimevegr, those are not the same thing, and everybody who
has lived long with animals knows it.

## Dog Culture and Breeding Lore

Dogs sit much closer to ordinary life than horses do. A horse announces
money, distance, and logistical reach. A dog announces household rhythm:
who keeps watch, who hunts, who has children, who sleeps lightly, who can
afford scraps enough to keep another mouth. Even poor people who cannot
dream of owning a horse may keep a cur, a ratter, or a half-trained hound.
The dog is not prestige first. It is warning, warmth, hunting help, and
company for people who would never use that last word aloud.

Because dogs live so close to the hearth, the culture judges how people
treat them with more intimacy than it judges horse handling. A man who
kicks a horse may be called a fool. A man who beats a good dog in front of
the hall is called wrong in the soul. Not soft. Wrong. The dog sleeps by
the door, hears the same storms, smells the same strangers, and warns the
same children. Its loyalty is too visible to abuse without witnesses
remembering.

Dogs are still animals, not little people in hides. Pups are drowned in
hard years. Sick dogs are culled when feed is short. A biter that turns on
its own hall may be killed the same day. But a useful dog earns a kind of
practical affection that the Rimevegr rarely admits to anything. Men who
will not touch each other in comfort will scratch a hound's ear by the fire
and think nobody noticed.

### Dog Kinds the Rimevegr Recognizes

The culture groups dogs by work more than by purity, but a few breeding
lines are distinct enough to carry reputation.

| Breed               | Cultural Reputation                               | Typical Use                                            | Common Saying                                  |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Fjord hound         | Weather-wise, loud, loyal to one yard             | Alarm dog, shore watch, birding and seal-scent warning | "A fjord hound hears fog coming."              |
| Black Pine wolf-dog | Fierce, expensive, half-feared                    | War, winter hunting, wolf-warden work                  | "It obeys until blood says otherwise."         |
| Bog tracker         | Quiet, uncanny, never fooled twice by soft ground | Marsh scouting, trail work, body-finding               | "Trust the bog dog before your own foot."      |
| Hall mastiff        | Slow, ugly, immovable                             | Gate guard, hall defense, stock pen guard              | "If the mastiff stands, the door still holds." |
| Moor lurcher        | Fast, spare, hungry-looking                       | Hare hunting, courier running, open-ground pursuit     | "Blink and the moor dog is over the hill."     |

Black Pine wolf-dogs are the most culturally loaded because Vargheim's
wolf-wardens breed, test, and guard those lines almost like noble stock.
They are not wolves. Calling them wolves in front of a kennel-master is the
sort of mistake that starts with contempt and can end with teeth.

### Dogs in Daily Life

Every part of settled life has a dog version. Fishing hamlets keep alert
hounds that bark at strangers on the quay and scream before storms. Moor
farmers keep lean coursers for hares and flock dogs that harry off foxes.
Forest halls keep bigger dogs to warn of wolves, raiders, and things in the
tree line that no sane person wants named after dark.

Children often bond with dogs before they understand rank. That matters. A
hall dog moves through the social order more freely than a thrall does. It
goes under benches, across thresholds, into private corners. People notice
what dog follows whom, who shares crusts, which child stops crying only
when the old bitch settles at the bedside. None of this is legal evidence.
All of it becomes household truth.

### Breeding Customs

Dog breeding is more common and less formal than horse breeding, but it is
still economic judgment. A breeding bitch is expected to throw working
pups, not just live ones. A bad mating wastes feed. A good one can stock a
hall for years or pay debts in pups and promises.

Stud arrangements for ordinary dogs are casual: a bitch is brought to the
neighbor's proven sire, a fee is paid in scraps, wool, labor, or a pick of
the litter, and everyone pretends the terms were obvious. Valuable lines
are stricter. Vargheim kennel blood is counted, tracked, and argued over by
people who would laugh if you called it bookkeeping.

People judge pups on six things: nose, speed, grit, bite, sense, and voice.
Nose is whether the dog can hold scent through weather. Speed is obvious.
Grit is what it does when hurt, cold, or frightened. Bite is not just
strength but commitment. Sense is whether it reads danger, handler, and
path correctly. Voice matters more than outsiders think. A dog that gives
the right bark at the right time saves lives. A dog that barks at nothing
wastes everyone's sleep until someone wishes it silent forever.

### Dogs in Belief and Omen

Dogs are not sacred in the formal sense, but they are treated as early
warning systems for the unseen. If every dog in a settlement goes quiet at
once, people bar doors. If kennel lines start whining toward the same
ridge, scouts go there in daylight or not at all. The culture accepts that
dogs smell things before people do and hears in that fact the edge of
something older and less comfortable.

Black dogs at crossroads are bad enough to earn proverb status. A dog that
refuses a corpse, a doorway, or a barrow path is taken seriously. A dog
that wants to dig beneath a threshold may be beaten away the first time and
obeyed the second. A howling dog outside a sickroom is an omen people mock
until the patient dies.

People also speak of dogs as witnesses. "The dog saw it" is not lawful
proof, but it means the event happened at close quarters, under the eyes of
something that cannot lie and cannot explain itself. That unsettles people
more than they admit.
