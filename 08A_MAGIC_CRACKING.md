# The Magic of Rimevegr — The Cracking

A reference document for the supernatural layer of the Iron Ledger
setting. Not the mechanical rules (those are in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`)
but the lived reality — what magic feels like, what it costs, what
people believe, and what nobody knows for certain.

## The Cracking and Its Consequences

The gods went silent three centuries ago. Nobody disputes that. What
people dispute — bitterly, violently, with the kind of certainty that
only flourishes in the absence of proof — is why.

Three explanations circulate in the Rimevegr. They pass through
different hands, serve different masters, and none of them is entirely
wrong. That last part is the worst of it. If one theory were obviously
right, the others would die. Instead they all survive, each one fed by
evidence the others cannot explain.

### The Galdr-Workers' Theory: The Withdrawal

The galdr-workers — the rune-carvers, the men and women who cut
binding-marks into iron and stone and wood — believe the gods chose to
leave. Not because they were angry. Not because humanity transgressed.
Because gods are not permanent. They came, they built, they gave the
world its laws and its runes, and then they withdrew to wherever gods
go when they tire of what they have made.

This is the gentlest theory. It is also the most frightening to those
who hold it, because it means the runes are orphaned tools. Every rune
Ash carves into a ward-stone was designed to channel divine power. If
the gods have withdrawn, the channels are drying up. The runes still
work — anyone who has watched a binding-mark flare blue in the dark
knows they still work — but they work less. Each decade, a little less.
Galdr that once held a barrow sealed for a century now fails in twenty
years. Ward-lines that once burned draugr on contact now only slow
them. The thread is fraying.

Galdr-workers who hold this view see themselves as custodians of a
dying inheritance. They count their rune-marks the way a miser counts
coin. Every carving spends something that cannot be earned back. The
workshop is open, the tools are on the bench, but the steel is running
out and no one is making more.

**Supporting evidence:** Old rune-works are measurably stronger than
new ones. The ward-stones around Frostfjord Hollow, carved before the
Cracking, still hum with power that no living galdr-worker can match.
Rune failure rates have increased across the Rimevegr — documented in
the Deepholm archive scrolls, one of the few written records maintained
across generations. Rank-5 galdr effects described in the oldest sagas
cannot be replicated today. Something is diminishing.

**Contradicting evidence:** If magic were a finite reservoir being
drained, all three traditions should weaken at the same rate. They do
not. Seiðr has not weakened. If anything, seiðr-wives report that the
dead are easier to reach now than a generation ago — closer, louder,
more present. A draining model cannot explain why one tradition fades
while another intensifies. Galdr-workers have no answer for this.
Most ignore it.

### The Seiðr-Wives' Theory: The Devouring

The seiðr-wives see it differently. Seiðr is spirit-work — trance,
communion, the art of reaching through the Veil to speak with what
lives on the other side. The seiðr-wives reach through and listen. What
they hear has changed.

Before the Cracking — according to the oral traditions passed from
practitioner to practitioner, unbroken for three hundred years — the
dead were calm. They rested. They answered when called and fell silent
when dismissed. Now the dead are frightened. Not hostile, not angry.
Frightened. Something is behind them in the dark. Something that was
not there before.

The seiðr-wives' theory is simple and terrible: the gods did not
leave. The gods were devoured. Something older than the divine
order — something the gods had spent their existence containing —
broke free and consumed them. The silence is not absence. The silence
is the sound of something wearing the gods' faces and listening through
their empty temples.

This theory is not spoken aloud in most settlements. The seiðr-wives
share it in whispers, in closed rooms, in the private language of their
tradition. It explains too much for comfort. It explains why the dead
are afraid. It explains why the temples feel wrong — not empty, but
occupied by something that does not belong. It explains the Veil's
behavior: not a wound slowly healing, but a membrane being pressed
from the other side.

**Supporting evidence:** The dead are terrified. Every seiðr-wife in
the Rimevegr reports the same thing independently. The dead do not want
to be sent back. They claw at the Veil from the far side not because
they want to return to life but because they are trying to escape what
is behind them. The draugr that walk are not vengeful spirits reclaiming
their graves — they are refugees. Barrows crack open from the inside
because something is pushing the dead out. The old barrow-wards were
designed to keep the living out. Now they need to keep the dead in, and
they were never built for that.

**Contradicting evidence:** If something devoured the gods, it should
be visible. It should be present. It should act. The galdr-workers
point out that three hundred years of silence is a long time to be
eaten and not noticed. Whatever the seiðr-wives hear in trance is
filtered through their own state — half-conscious, drugged on ritual
herbs, freezing in the cold — and their interpretations cannot be
verified. Fear in the dead may simply be confusion. The dead are not
reliable witnesses.

### The Common View: It Doesn't Matter

Most people in the Rimevegr hold neither theory. Most people do not
think about the Cracking the way a galdr-worker or a seiðr-wife thinks
about it. Most people know three things: the sky broke, the cold came,
the dead walk.

The common view is not a theory. It is the absence of one. It is the
practical, bone-tired wisdom of people who have to bank a fire, check
a ward-line, and bar a door every single night of the Long Dark. The
gods may have left. The gods may have died. The gods may be something
else entirely. It does not matter. What matters is whether the
rune-mark on the threshold still glows, whether the salt-line is
unbroken, whether the old woman in the next farmstead can still whisper
the dead back to sleep.

The common folk practice folk magic — scraps of galdr and seiðr worn
down to household habit. Salt at the threshold. Iron nails above the
door. A rune scratched into the hearthstone that grandmother taught and
her grandmother taught before her. They do not call it magic. They call
it sense. The rune-ward works because it has always worked. If the gods
are behind it, good. If not, the ward still held last night and that is
enough.

**What it gets right:** Survival does not require understanding. The
rune-wards work regardless of why they work. The folk practices — the
salt, the iron, the whispered names — have been pressure-tested by
three hundred years of the dead walking, and what is still practiced
is what still functions. There is more hard-won empirical knowledge in
a farmwife's nightly routine than in all the theories of the
galdr-workers and seiðr-wives combined.

**What it misses:** Everything. If the galdr-workers are right and
magic is draining, the folk wards will fail within a generation. If the
seiðr-wives are right and something is behind the silence, the folk
wards were never designed to stop it. The common view works until it
doesn't, and when it stops working there will be no warning.

### The Cracking's Physical Legacy

Whatever the cause, the Cracking's effects on the physical world are
not disputed. The sky split. The rime-mist rolled south. The seasons
collapsed.

Before the Cracking, the Rimevegr had four seasons. After it, two: the
Long Summer and the Long Dark. Sixty days of ferocious growth followed
by three hundred days of cold, diminishing light, and endurance. The
transition between them is violent — ice shatters, rivers flood, the
first flies come in clouds thick enough to choke a horse. The
transition back is worse. The cold does not arrive gradually. It falls
like a door slamming.

The Veil — the rime-mist — is a physical presence. Not fog. Not
weather. Something between the two that behaves like neither. It
swallows sound. It bends distance — a man can walk for an hour in the
Veil and arrive ten minutes from where he started, or three days'
march away. It muffles firelight. It carries sounds that should not be
there: footsteps behind you on an empty trail, voices calling from
directions that do not exist, the creak of oars on a frozen lake.

The landscape itself changed. Barrows that had been sealed for
centuries cracked open. Standing stones that had been decorative
boundary markers began to hum — not with wind, but with a tone that
does not change when the wind shifts. Water sources shifted. Old wells
dried. New springs appeared in wrong places — hilltops, the floors of
barrow-halls, the middle of established roads. The treeline dropped by
a hundred feet. Forests thinned. The ground froze deeper and longer.

Fire behaves differently near Veil-thinning. Flames burn blue at the
tips. The air tastes of old metal — not iron exactly, but the memory of
iron. Temperature drops sharply and without wind. These signs are the
only reliable warning that the boundary is thin tonight.

### The Dead Question

Some dead walk. Most do not.

This is the question that sits at the center of everything and has no
answer. In three hundred years of restless dead, no one has determined
the pattern. It is not violence of death — plenty of murdered men stay
in their graves while someone who died of old age claws out of theirs.
It is not the manner of burial. It is not the strength of the ward. It
is not the season, though the dead walk more often in the Long Dark. It
is not the location, though barrows and battlefields and places where
blood was spilled produce more walking dead than farmsteads and
hearthsides.

The seiðr-wives say it is proximity to Veil-thinning. The dead who
rise are those who died near a thin spot, or whose graves drift into
one as the Veil shifts. This explains most cases. It does not explain
Feldwick, where every corpse in the village walked on the same night
despite the nearest known Veil-thinning being two days' march away. It
does not explain Ash's dead, which he has never spoken about and which
no one in the band asks him to explain.

The galdr-workers say it is decay in the old wards. The dead were
always restless — the Cracking made the world restless — but the old
rune-works kept them down. As those works fade, the dead push through.
This is why the oldest barrows, with the strongest wards, produce the
most dangerous draugr when they finally fail: the ward was holding back
centuries of accumulated pressure.

The common folk say a man walks if he has unfinished business. This is
the oldest explanation and the least reliable. The dead who walk do not
finish business. They do not track down enemies, settle debts, or
deliver messages. They walk. They attack the living. They return to
their graves before dawn. Whatever drives them, it is not purpose.

### The Silence of the Gods

The temples still stand. Every settlement in the Rimevegr that can
afford to maintain one keeps a temple. The carved posts. The altar. The
offering-bowl. The blood-gutter where sacrificial animals bleed out
into the earth. Every element of pre-Cracking worship is preserved,
performed, and attended.

No one answers.

Three hundred years of sacrifice. Three hundred years of ritual. Three
hundred years of blood on the altar and mead in the offering-bowl and
words spoken into the dark. Not once — not in any saga, not in any
seiðr-wife's trance, not in any galdr-worker's rune-reading — has a
god responded.

The rituals continue because the alternative is worse. To stop
sacrificing is to admit the gods are gone. To admit the gods are gone
is to face the Veil, the dead, the Long Dark, and the thing that might
be wearing the gods' silence — alone, without even the fiction of
divine protection. The faith is not faith. It is habit armored against
despair.

The temple-keepers know this. They perform the rites. They speak the
names. They drain the blood and pour the mead and watch the offering
burn on the altar-fire that was lit from a flame that was lit from a
flame that stretches back to before the Cracking. They keep the chain
unbroken because breaking it would be admitting that the chain connects
to nothing.

Some temple-keepers have gone mad. Some have burned their temples and
walked into the Veil. Most simply continue, with the dogged endurance
that characterizes everything in the Rimevegr. You do the thing because
it has always been done. You keep the fire because the fire has always
been kept. You speak the god-names because the names have always been
spoken.

And in the silence that follows, you listen. Because three hundred
years of silence is not proof of absence. It might be proof of
something worse.
