# Minnidraugar — Memory-Ghosts and the Spirit World

## The Dead Are Not Gone

The Rimevegr does not have ghosts the way the old sagas describe
ghosts — transparent figures wringing their hands, bound to a
specific wrong that must be righted before they can rest. The
Rimevegr has the dead. And the dead are not metaphors, not
remnants, not echoes. They are presences. They push against the
Veil from the other side, and the Veil is thinning, and the
push gets stronger every decade.

The standard model — what galdr-workers teach and seiðr-wives
confirm — divides the dead into categories based on coherence,
intent, and danger. But the categories leak. A spirit that behaves
like a shade at dusk may behave like a draugr by midnight. A
memory-ghost that has drifted harmlessly through a settlement for
decades may suddenly coalesce into something with purpose and
weight. The dead are not a filing system. They are a weather
pattern. They shift. They combine. They surprise.

This chapter documents what the Rimevegr knows about the dead —
not the mechanical rules for encountering them, which are in
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, but the lived understanding of what
walks on the other side of the Veil and what happens when it
walks through.

## The Taxonomy of the Dead

### Shades — Aptrgangr Skugga

The simplest and most common form. A shade is the residual
impression of a person who died — not the person, but the shape
the person left in the Veil. The way a hand pressed into wet clay
leaves a hollow, a life lived and ended leaves a shape in the
boundary between the living and the dead. The shape persists.
Under certain conditions — Veil-thinning, seasonal changes, the
proximity of a barrow or a death-site — the shape becomes visible
or sensible to the living.

Shades do not think. They do not respond to speech. They do not
notice the living. They walk paths they walked in life — the road
between the longhouse and the well, the track from the settlement
to the winter pasture — and they walk them at the same time they
walked them alive, which means they are most visible at the hours
when the dead person was most active. A farmer's shade walks at
dawn. A watchman's shade walks at midnight. A child's shade plays
in the dirt at midday, and the women of the settlement step around
it the way they would step around a puddle, because acknowledging
the shade means admitting that the child is dead, and the child
died three winters ago, and the shade has been playing in the same
spot since.

Shades are not dangerous. They are not harmless either. A shade
that intersects a living person — walks through them, occupies the
same space — produces a sensation that veterans describe as "cold
through the spine, not on it." The cold passes in seconds. The
unease lasts hours. A settlement that hosts multiple shades — and
any settlement older than two generations hosts multiple shades —
is a settlement where the living walk carefully, where certain
paths are avoided after dark not because anything will happen but
because the feeling of something walking through you is an
experience that most people prefer to have as rarely as possible.

Shades dissipate over time. A shade from a death thirty years past
is fainter than a shade from a death five years past. A shade from
before the Cracking — three centuries old — should be invisible.
Most are. The ones that are not are cause for concern, because
persistence at that scale means the impression was carved deep,
which means the death was violent, or the life was significant, or
both.

### Memory-Ghosts — Minnidraugar

This is where the taxonomy departs from every other culture's
understanding of the dead, and this is where the Rimevegr's
particular horror lives.

A memory-ghost is not the spirit of a person. A memory-ghost is
the spirit of an event.

The Rimevegr's seiðr-wives were the first to identify them, and
they gave them the name the Rimevegr still uses: _minnidraugar_,
the memory-walkers. A minnidraugr is what happens when an event
is so significant — so concentrated in emotion, in death, in
the raw energy that living beings produce at moments of extremity
— that the event itself leaves a ghost. Not the people who died.
The dying itself. The moment.

A massacre site hosts minnidraugar. Not the shades of the
individuals who were killed — those exist separately, walking
their paths, fading at the normal rate. The minnidraugr is the
massacre. The event persists as a spiritual presence in the Veil,
a knot of impression so dense that it has developed coherence
without developing consciousness. It is not aware. It does not
think. But it pulls. It draws the living toward it the way a
whirlpool draws a boat — not with intent, but with force.

A person who walks into a minnidraugr's radius does not see a
ghost. They relive the event.

### The Memory-Pull — Minnisvarf

The minnidraugr does not attack. It envelops. The living person
enters its radius — measured in paces, not miles, concentrated
around the site where the event occurred — and the memory closes
over them like water over a stone. One moment the person is
standing in a clearing where a battle was fought forty years ago.
The next moment the person is in the battle.

Not watching it. Not observing it from the outside. Living it.

The memory-pull is total sensory immersion. The person sees through
the eyes of someone who was there. They feel the ground under feet
that are not their own. They hear the screaming in a voice that is
not their own. They are inside the event, participant rather than
witness, and the participation is not optional and does not offer
an exit.

The duration varies. A weak minnidraugr — from an event that was
significant but not catastrophic, a murder rather than a massacre
— may hold the living person for seconds or minutes. The person
snaps back to their own body gasping and shaking, with a memory
that feels like their own but belongs to a stranger. They remember
the dead man's last breath as if they were the one breathing it.
They remember the blade entering flesh as if it were their flesh.
The memory does not fade the way a dream fades. It sits in the
mind with the weight and clarity of personal experience, and the
person must carry it alongside their own memories for the rest of
their life.

A strong minnidraugr — from a massacre, a siege-breach, a ship
sinking with all hands — can hold the living person for what feels
like a lifetime. This is the phenomenon that the seiðr-wives call
_lífinnanlíf_: life-inside-life.

The person caught in the memory-pull does not experience the event
in real time. They experience it at the speed of a life. The
memory expands. The person is born — not as themselves, but as
someone who was there. They grow. They live childhood, adolescence,
the years of working life, the slow accumulation of relationships
and losses that constitute a human existence. They arrive at the
moment of the event — the massacre, the sinking, the catastrophic
loss — and they live through it, and they die.

And then they come back.

In the external world, the process takes seconds. Perhaps a minute.
The person's body stands motionless — eyes fixed, breath stopped,
the same respiratory arrest that seiðr-working produces but without
the seiðr-wife's training to survive it. Companions see a person
who has frozen in place, who does not respond to shaking or
shouting, who is not breathing, who is going blue around the lips.
They drag the person away from the site. The person comes back
gasping, weeping, unable to speak, with a lifetime of memories
that are not their own crammed into a skull that already held a
full set.

The trauma is not the event. The trauma is the life. The person
has lived an entire existence — complete, coherent, from birth to
death — inside a stranger's body, in a stranger's time, with a
stranger's family and friends and losses and joys. And then they
are back in their own body, in their own time, and the stranger's
life does not dissolve. It persists. It is real to them. The dead
man's wife is real to them. The dead man's children are real to
them. The dead man's morning routine and evening prayers and the
way he held his cup and the sound of his daughter laughing — all
of it is real, all of it is lodged in the living person's memory
with the same fidelity as their own birth-memories, and the
living person must somehow hold both lives in one skull and
continue functioning.

Most cannot. The lífinnanlíf is the single most devastating
spiritual injury in the Rimevegr. A person who has experienced a
full memory-pull — a complete life-inside-life — is fundamentally
altered. They are quieter. They are distant. They confuse names —
calling their own children by the dead man's children's names,
referring to their spouse by a name that belongs to no living
person. They wake at night speaking a language or a dialect they
never learned, because the dead man spoke it and the dead man's
speech patterns are in their mouth now, the way a seiðr-wife
carries the dead's voice after a trance.

The seiðr-wives can help. Partially. A skilled seiðr-wife — rank
3 or above — can perform a separation ritual that does not remove
the memory-pull's content but creates a boundary around it,
walling off the stranger's life from the host's awareness so that
the memories can only be accessed deliberately, not triggered by
sensory cues. The ritual is imperfect. Smells break through — the
dead man's wife wore a particular herb-sachet, and the scent of
that herb on a market stall can collapse the wall and flood the
living person with an entire marriage's worth of emotion in the
time it takes to pass the stall. Sounds break through. The
laughter of a child the right age, in the right pitch. The creak
of a specific kind of door hinge. The particular sound of rain on
a roof with a particular slope.

Some people seek minnidraugar deliberately. Not many. Not sane
ones. But the knowledge that a memory-pull can grant a full
life's experience — every skill, every language, every memory —
has attracted the occasional desperate person who wants what the
dead knew and is willing to risk the cost. A warrior who enters
a battleground minnidraugr and survives the pull emerges with
a dead warrior's combat experience — not the muscle memory, which
the living body cannot replicate, but the tactical knowledge, the
pattern recognition, the veteran's instinct for when the shield
wall is about to break. The knowledge is real. The price is a
second life's worth of trauma compressed into a body that was
only designed for one.

### Where Minnidraugar Form

Not every death creates a memory-ghost. Not every event, no matter
how terrible, leaves a minnidraugr. The formation requires
concentration — multiple deaths, extreme emotion, compressed into
a single site over a short period. A battle qualifies. A plague
does not, because the dying is distributed across a settlement and
across weeks. A murder may qualify if the murder was sufficiently
charged — a blood-feud killing at a ting, performed in front of
witnesses, carrying the emotional weight of generations of
grievance. A single death in bed does not qualify, no matter how
beloved the person.

The known minnidraugar sites in the Rimevegr are:

**The Bloodfield at Ormsvík.** Site of the worst single-day
battle in post-Cracking history. Three hundred dead in four
hours. The minnidraugr is so strong that the site is marked on
every trader's map and every band's route-chart. Nobody walks
through the Bloodfield. Nobody camps within a mile of it. The
memory-pull reaches further than any other known site — estimated
at thirty paces from the centre, though nobody has measured
precisely because measuring would require walking in.

**The Longhouse at Brennvik.** A settlement that was burned with
its inhabitants inside during a raiding dispute sixty years ago.
The longhouse was rebuilt — the timber was sound, and land is
scarce — and the new inhabitants lasted one winter before
abandoning it. The minnidraugr fills the interior. Anyone who
enters relives the burning. Not a full lífinnanlíf — the event
was too brief for that — but a sensory immersion in smoke and
screaming and the smell of burning hair that lasts minutes in
real time and hours in subjective experience. The building stands
empty. The settlement uses it as a landmark and a warning: _this
is what happens. This is what stays._

**The Drowned Strait.** A shipping channel between two islands
where a merchant fleet went down in a storm forty years ago —
eight ships, two hundred crew. The minnidraugr is in the water.
Boats that cross the strait at the wrong tide report crew members
going rigid at the oars, staring into the water, mouths working
without sound. The memory-pull takes them under the surface —
not physically, but experientially. They feel the cold. They feel
the water. They feel the lungs filling. The pull lasts seconds
and the crew comes back retching seawater that is not there,
coughing up nothing, soaked in a drowning that happened before
they were born.

### Event-Ghosts of the Cracking

The Cracking itself — the moment the gods went silent — produced
minnidraugar that nobody has successfully catalogued. These are
the oldest memory-ghosts in the Rimevegr, and they are also the
most dangerous, because the event they encode is not a battle or
a burning. It is the end of divine contact. The death, if it was
a death, of something larger than human.

Sites that are believed to host Cracking-era minnidraugar include
the ruins of the old temple complexes, the foundation-stones of
Frostfjord Hollow, and at least three standing-stone circles
that pre-date the post-Cracking settlements. Nobody enters these
sites deliberately. Nobody survives a full memory-pull from a
Cracking-era minnidraugr, because nobody survives reliving the
moment when divinity ended, and nobody knows whether what the
pull shows is true — whether the memory it encodes is the actual
Cracking or the Cracking as it was perceived by a human mind that
was never built to perceive the failure of the divine.

One person is reported to have survived a partial pull at a
Cracking-era site. A young galdr-apprentice who stumbled into a
standing-stone circle during a storm and was pulled out by her
teacher before the pull completed. She spoke for a day without
stopping, in a language nobody recognized, describing shapes and
sounds and the colour of the sky at the moment when "everything
that was held together stopped being held." She then stopped
speaking entirely and did not speak again for three years. When
she began speaking again, she could not remember the Cracking
vision. She remembered only the silence afterward — the specific
quality of silence that occurs when something that had been
making a sound for all of human history suddenly stops. She
described it as "the loudest quiet I have ever heard." She
became a seiðr-wife. Not a galdr-worker. The runes had gone
silent for her, replaced by something else, something she heard
in the standing stones that was not rune-song and was not the
dead but was perhaps the space between the two — the gap that the
Cracking opened and that has never closed.

## Artefact Souls — Andavörðr

Items do not have spirits the way people have spirits. A sword
does not dream. A ring does not remember. A longhouse does not
grieve.

Except when it does.

The Rimevegr's understanding of artefact souls — _andavörðr_,
the breath-ward, the spirit that inhabits an object — is not
systematic. It is empirical. Certain items, under certain
conditions, develop properties that can only be described as
ensouled. They respond to their wielder's intent. They resist
being used in ways that contradict their history. They carry
information that was never inscribed in their material, and they
deliver that information in ways that the wielder cannot explain
and the galdr-workers cannot replicate.

### How Objects Become Ensouled

The process is not understood. The conditions are observed.

**Proximity to significant death.** A weapon used to kill a
named person — not a nameless enemy in a skirmish, but someone
whose death carried weight — sometimes absorbs a trace of the
event. The mechanism is unknown. The galdr-workers hypothesize
that the death-energy, passing through the Veil at the moment of
dying, imprints on the nearest solid object the way a rune-mark
imprints on wood. The seiðr-wives disagree: they believe the
dying person's spirit, in the moment of transition, grips
whatever is nearest, and the grip leaves a residue. Neither
theory has been tested. Both explain the observations.

A weapon that has killed many named persons over many years
develops a cumulative ensoulment that galdr-workers describe as
_blóðminni_ — blood-memory. The weapon does not become
conscious. It does not speak. But it carries the accumulated
weight of its history, and the wielder feels that weight. The
sword is heavier than its metal should make it. Not physically —
the scales read the same. Heavier in the hand. Heavier in the
arm. Heavier in the moment before the strike, when the wielder's
will and the weapon's history intersect and the weapon's history
pushes back.

**Long possession by a single owner.** An object carried for
decades by a single person — worn against the skin, held daily,
slept beside — accumulates something that is not spirit and is
not nothing. The owner's habits impress themselves on the object.
A knife that one man used for forty years sits differently in
another man's hand. Not because of wear patterns — the new
owner can see no physical difference. The knife simply prefers
the old grip. It turns toward the angle the old owner used. It
resists the angle the new owner prefers. The resistance is
subtle — a whisper, not a shout — and it fades over months as
the new owner's habits overwrite the old ones. But during those
months, the new owner is holding something that remembers someone
else, and the remembering is not metaphor.

**Divine origin or contact.** The rarest and most potent form.
An artefact that was made before the Cracking, in the age when
divine power flowed freely and the rune-channels carried full
force — such an artefact may carry a soul that is not residual
but structural. The divine power that infused the object during
its creation did not leave when the gods went silent. It was
sealed in. The object is a vessel, and the vessel is still full,
three centuries after the river that filled it dried up.

These artefacts are not common. They are not rare in the way that
rare metals are rare — distributed thinly but predictably across
the landscape. They are rare the way lightning-struck trees are
rare — scattered without pattern, found without warning, and
dangerous in ways that the finder cannot predict from the
outside.

A divine-origin artefact has a will. Not consciousness — the
galdr-workers are precise on this point, and the precision
matters. Will without consciousness. Purpose without thought.
The artefact was made for something, and the divine power that
filled it encoded that purpose into its structure, and the
purpose persists. A blade made for oath-enforcement resists being
used for murder. A ring made for binding resists being removed.
A staff made for warding flares when the dead approach, whether
or not anyone asked it to.

The implication that some artefacts may house fragments of
divine power — actual remnants of the gods who went silent — is
the implication that nobody in the galdr tradition discusses in
public and that the seiðr tradition discusses in whispers. If the
gods left power behind in objects, then the Withdrawal theory is
not a clean departure. The gods left pieces of themselves. And
pieces of gods, sealed in metal and wood and stone, carrying
purpose without thought, acting without awareness — these are not
tools. These are orphans. And orphans, in the Rimevegr, are
dangerous, because an orphan has nothing to lose and no one to
answer to.

## Barrow-Wraiths — Haugvættr

A barrow holds the dead. This is its purpose — to contain, to
seal, to keep the occupants below the earth and the Veil between
them and the living. The galdr-wards on a barrow's entrance stones
are some of the most carefully carved marks in the Rimevegr. The
ward-work on a major barrow — the kind that holds a jarl or a war
chieftain or a galdr-worker — represents weeks of rank-3 work,
carved in Deepholm silver wire, anchored to standing stone chips,
and maintained on a schedule that the nearest settlement treats
with the same priority as roof repair.

When a barrow fails — when the wards decay, when the maintenance
lapses, when the galdr-worker dies and is not replaced — the dead
inside begin to interact. This is the process that produces the
Rimevegr's most dangerous spiritual entities: the haugvættr, the
barrow-wraith.

### How Wraiths Form

A barrow typically holds multiple dead. A jarl's barrow holds the
jarl, his immediate household dead, and sometimes the honoured
dead of the settlement — warriors who earned the right to rest in
the jarl's company. A community barrow holds generations of dead,
layered by period, with the oldest at the bottom and the most
recent at the entrance.

While the wards hold, each of these dead remains individual. The
shade of the jarl walks its path within the barrow's interior.
The shades of the household dead occupy their positions — the wife
by the hearth-position, the warriors by the door-position, the
children in the spaces between. The wards do not just keep the
dead inside. They keep the dead separate. The binding-marks on the
entrance stones create a structure within the barrow that
prevents spiritual combination — the same way that walls in a
longhouse keep separate families in separate spaces.

When the wards fail, the walls come down.

The dead begin to bleed into each other. Not immediately — the
process takes seasons, sometimes years. The jarl's shade, no
longer bounded by the ward's interior structure, drifts toward
the household dead. The household dead drift toward the community
dead. The individual identities do not disappear. They merge.
The jarl's authority combines with the warrior's strength combines
with the child's intensity combines with the accumulated weight of
every person buried in that mound, and the result is a single
entity that is not any of its constituent dead and is all of them
simultaneously.

A haugvættr has the memories of everyone in the barrow. It speaks
with a voice that shifts — the jarl's command flattening into a
child's rhythm flattening into a warrior's cadence, cycling
through its components the way a wheel cycles through its spokes.
It has the galdr-knowledge of any galdr-worker buried within it.
It has the seiðr-sensitivity of any seiðr-wife it absorbed. It
has the physical presence of all its dead combined — not additive
in the way that a troop is stronger than a soldier, but
synthesized, compressed, a single point of spiritual force that
carries the weight of every life that was lived and ended in that
mound.

A haugvættr is, by any measurement the Rimevegr can make, the
most dangerous thing that walks on either side of the Veil.

### What a Wraith Can Do

A shade walks its path and does not notice the living. A draugr
walks with purpose and attacks with the residual combat instinct
of the dead warrior. A haugvættr does neither of these things. A
haugvættr thinks.

Not the way the living think — not with the sequential,
deliberative process that the hugr provides. A haugvættr's
cognition is collective. It processes through all its constituent
minds simultaneously. It remembers everything any of its dead
experienced. It can draw on the tactical knowledge of the
warrior-dead, the political knowledge of the jarl-dead, the
magical knowledge of any practitioner-dead, and the raw emotional
force of every person who ever loved or feared or hated within
the bounds of that barrow.

In practical terms:

**A haugvættr can manipulate the Veil locally.** The combined
spiritual mass of a hundred-year barrow — decades of accumulated
dead, pressed together, amplifying each other — gives the wraith
the ability to thin or thicken the Veil within its immediate
vicinity. It can make itself visible at will. It can hide at
will. It can extend the Veil's effects — the cold, the silence,
the distance-bending — outward from the barrow in a radius that
correlates with the number and significance of its constituent
dead. A small barrow-wraith affects an area of twenty paces.
A major barrow-wraith — the kind that forms in a jarl's mound
that has held dead for a century — affects an area of a hundred
paces or more.

**A haugvættr can use the magic its dead knew.** If a rank-3
galdr-worker was buried in the barrow, the wraith has access to
rank-3 galdr effects — not through a living body, not through
a chisel and silver wire, but through the spiritual channels
that the dead galdr-worker's practice carved during life and
that death did not close. The marks a haugvættr carves are not
wards. They are invitations — rune-work that opens rather than
closes, that draws the living toward the barrow rather than
pushing the dead away. A settlement that discovers fresh rune-
marks on its perimeter stones, carved in a style that matches
no living galdr-worker, has a haugvættr problem, and the
problem is not going to solve itself.

**A haugvættr can generate minnidraugar.** The wraith's
collective memory is so dense that it can project memory-pulls
— not passively, the way a battle-site's minnidraugr pulls
without intent, but deliberately. A haugvættr that wants a
living person to experience something specific can wrap them in
a targeted memory-pull drawn from its archive of lives. The
victim does not relive a random event. They relive the event the
wraith chooses. A jarl who defied the barrow's occupant in life
finds himself reliving his own father's murder — a murder the
wraith's dead witnessed, a memory the wraith carries, deployed
as weapon rather than phenomenon.

**A haugvættr can speak.** Not through a seiðr-wife's mediation
— directly. The Veil is thin enough around a major barrow that
the wraith's combined voice carries across the boundary without
assistance. The voice is wrong. It is multiple voices speaking in
unison, slightly out of synchronization, the way a choir sounds
when each singer is half a beat behind the one before. The content
is coherent — the wraith chooses which of its constituent minds
speaks — but the delivery makes the living listener's skin crawl,
because the ear hears one message and many mouths and the brain
cannot reconcile the two.

### Dealing with a Haugvættr

The Rimevegr has no reliable method for destroying a
barrow-wraith. The wards that should have prevented the wraith's
formation are the same wards that, once the wraith exists, are
insufficient to contain it. The wraith is stronger than its
components. The wards were designed for individual dead, not for
amalgamated spiritual forces.

The methods that work are partial:

**Re-warding.** A galdr-worker of sufficient rank — rank 4 at
minimum — can carve new wards over the barrow entrance that
re-establish the internal structure, forcing the constituent dead
back into individual containment. This does not destroy the
wraith. It disassembles it. The constituent dead resume their
individual patterns and the wraith's collective consciousness
disperses. But the re-warding must be maintained, because if the
new wards fail the dead will re-merge, and a wraith that has been
disassembled and re-formed is reported to be more coherent, more
purposeful, and angrier than a wraith that formed naturally.

**Excavation and individual re-burial.** Dig up the dead.
Separate them physically. Re-bury each one in an individual
grave with individual wards. This is the most reliable method
and the most impractical, because it requires digging into an
active barrow — a barrow whose wraith is present and hostile —
and performing careful ritual work on each set of remains while
the wraith attempts to prevent the separation. The Deepholm
archive records three successful excavations in post-Cracking
history. All three required multiple galdr-workers, armed
escorts, and months of preparation. All three cost lives.

**Seiðr negotiation.** A seiðr-wife of extraordinary skill can
enter trance at the barrow and attempt to communicate with the
wraith — not to destroy it, but to negotiate. The wraith
retains the personalities of its constituents. If the dominant
personality — usually the jarl or the highest-status individual
— can be addressed, reasoned with, offered something that the
dead valued in life, the wraith may voluntarily reduce its
activity. This is not containment. It is diplomacy with the dead.
It works when the dominant personality was reasonable in life. It
fails catastrophically when the dominant personality was not.

## The Unborn Dead — Óskapaðir

The Rimevegr's understanding of when life begins shapes its
understanding of when death can occur, and the two do not align
in ways that the living find comfortable.

A child conceived but not yet born has a spirit. The seiðr-wives
are unanimous on this point, and their unanimity carries weight
because they are the only people in the Rimevegr who can perceive
unborn spirits directly. The spirit is not a full soul — it does
not yet have hugr, hamr, or fylgja in the developed forms that a
living person carries. It is potential. A seed of spiritual
presence that occupies the space between not-existing and existing,
held inside the mother's body, growing as the body grows.

If the child dies before birth — miscarriage, stillbirth, the
various catastrophes that the Rimevegr's brutal conditions inflict
on pregnant women — the spirit does not simply vanish. It was
forming. It was becoming. The process was interrupted, and the
interrupted spirit enters the Veil in a state that is neither the
coherent presence of an adult dead nor the formless absence of
something that never existed. It is between.

The óskapaðir — the unshaped ones — are the most pitied and the
most feared category of the dead, and they are feared precisely
because they evoke pity.

### What the Unborn Want

An adult dead has a completed identity. The jarl's shade walks
the jarl's path because the jarl lived a life and the life
produced a pattern and the pattern persists. The unborn dead have
no completed identity. They have the beginning of one. They have
fragments — the mother's voice as heard through the body's wall,
the rhythm of the mother's heartbeat, the warmth that was the
only world they knew. And they have the absence of everything
that should have come after.

The unborn want completion. Not vengeance — they have no capacity
for vengeance, because vengeance requires a self, and they did not
have time to form one. They want to finish becoming. And the only
way an unborn spirit can finish becoming is by attaching to a
living person — typically the mother, if the mother is alive, or
the nearest living woman of childbearing age if the mother is not
— and attempting to complete the process of development that death
interrupted.

This attachment is not possession. The seiðr-wives are very clear
on this distinction. The óskapaðr does not overwrite the host's
personality. It does not speak through the host's mouth. It
nestles. It curls into the spiritual space that a pregnancy would
occupy — the space between the woman's own hugr and her body, the
space where a forming life would normally develop — and it tries
to grow.

The woman who hosts an óskapaðr feels it as a pregnancy that is
not there. The sensations are physical: nausea, tenderness,
the particular bone-deep tiredness of early pregnancy. A healer
examining the woman finds nothing — no child, no physical cause
for the symptoms. The symptoms persist. They do not progress
the way a real pregnancy would — there is no growth, no
quickening, no movement. There is only the beginning, repeated,
the first weeks played on an endless loop because the spirit
that is driving them does not know how to advance past the point
where its life was interrupted.

The condition can last months. In documented cases, it has lasted
years. The woman's body cycles through early-pregnancy symptoms
without resolution — nauseated, exhausted, tender, empty. The
emotional toll is severe. A woman who has miscarried and who then
experiences the phantom pregnancy of an óskapaðr's attachment
must live through the physical echo of her loss every day, and
the echo does not fade, because the spirit driving it does not
know how to stop.

### Seiðr Treatment of the Unborn Dead

A seiðr-wife can detect an óskapaðr's presence during a standard
trance — the spirit is visible to the trained eye as a faint,
luminous curl in the space around the woman's midsection, like
a sleeping animal that has not yet learned to wake. The seiðr-wife
can communicate with it, though the communication is not verbal.
The unborn have no language. They have sensation. The seiðr-wife
sends warmth, comfort, the spiritual equivalent of the mother's
heartbeat that the óskapaðr lost, and the spirit responds by
relaxing its grip.

The release ritual is among the gentlest things a seiðr-wife does,
and it is among the most painful. The seiðr-wife guides the
óskapaðr away from the host and toward the Veil — not through it,
not into the dead's territory, but to the boundary, where the
spirit can rest in the liminal space that it occupied before
attaching. The spirit does not want to go. The spirit was warm.
The spirit was almost alive. Sending it back to the cold between-
space is an act of mercy that feels like an act of cruelty, and
the seiðr-wife who performs it weeps, and the weeping is not
professional failure. It is the cost.

Some óskapaðir cannot be released. The attachment is too deep, the
spirit too far into its attempted development. In these cases, the
seiðr-wife must monitor the host and ensure that the óskapaðr's
presence does not progress to a point where it begins drawing
vital energy from the woman — the point where the phantom
pregnancy becomes a drain, where the nausea becomes malnutrition,
where the tiredness becomes the kind of exhaustion from which the
body does not recover. This threshold is real. Women have died
hosting óskapaðir, drained by the unborn's desperate, mindless
attempt to complete a development that cannot be completed
outside a living womb.

The woman is told, if she asks. Many do not ask. They know. The
mothers always know. They felt the spirit arrive, even if they
did not have the language for it. They felt the familiar symptoms
begin, and they knew — in the way that the body knows things the
mind refuses — that the child they lost had come back, and that
the coming back was not a second chance. It was a haunting dressed
in the sensation of hope.

_The unborn dead are the Rimevegr's cruelest ghost. Not because
they are malicious — they are not capable of malice. Not because
they are powerful — they are the weakest spirits in the taxonomy.
But because they embody the one loss that the Rimevegr cannot
armour itself against: the loss of what was almost. The galdr-
worker can ward against the dead. The seiðr-wife can negotiate
with the dead. The living can salt their doors and sew their
corpses and maintain the rituals that keep the familiar dead at
bay. But nobody can ward against the child that almost was. Nobody
can negotiate with a spirit that has no language and no self and
no want except the want to exist. The óskapaðir do not haunt.
They wait. And the waiting is worse than any haunting, because
haunting ends and waiting does not._

## The Geography of the Dead

The dead are not distributed evenly. The Veil is not a uniform
barrier. It has topography — thick places and thin places, calm
stretches and turbulent ones — and the dead cluster where the
Veil is thin the way the living cluster where the water is fresh.

**Barrow-fields** are the densest concentrations. A region with
multiple barrows — the kind of region that has been settled for
centuries and has buried its dead in the same hills for
generations — hosts a spiritual population that may outnumber the
living population ten to one. The shades walk their paths. The
minnidraugar sit in their event-knots. The óskapaðir drift near
the settlements where their mothers lived. And if any barrow's
wards have failed, the haugvættr watches from behind the mound's
broken seal, waiting with the patience that only the dead possess.

**Battle-sites** host concentrated minnidraugar surrounded by
diffuse shade populations. The minnidraugr is the event. The
shades are the individuals. Together they create a spiritual
environment that the seiðr-wives describe as "loud" — not in the
audible sense, but in the spiritual density that the trained eye
perceives. A battlefield from fifty years ago, uncleared, is a
place where the Veil is permanently thinned and the dead are
permanently present and the living who walk through it do so at
their own risk.

**Running water** thickens the Veil. Rivers are natural barriers
to the dead — not impassable, but resistant. A shade that
walks to a riverbank turns and walks back. A draugr that reaches
running water hesitates, sometimes for hours, before crossing. A
haugvættr can cross water, but the effort costs spiritual force
that the wraith cannot easily replenish. Settlements built on
riverbanks are safer than settlements built on hilltops, and the
Rimevegr's population distribution reflects this — not
consciously, not as deliberate strategy, but as the accumulated
wisdom of generations who noticed that the farmsteads near the
river lost fewer people during the Long Dark.

**New-growth forest** thickens the Veil for reasons nobody
understands. A forest that has grown since the Cracking — young
trees, dense growth, the aggressive reclamation of cleared land
by birch and pine — is spiritually quieter than old-growth forest
or open ground. The seiðr-wives hypothesize that the growth
itself — the concentrated biological activity of trees that are
actively building wood and spreading roots — creates a kind of
spiritual noise that the dead find difficult to navigate. The
hypothesis is untested. The observation is reliable.

**The deep ice** is where the Veil is thinnest. North of the last
settlements, past the tree line, past the point where the land
becomes ice and the ice becomes the world — the Veil is barely
there. The dead are not behind a barrier. They are here, in the
same space as the living, separated by a membrane so thin that
breathing on it might tear it. The outlaws who walk north do not
walk into the Veil. They walk through the place where the Veil
used to be, and the dead walk with them, and the distinction
between living and dead becomes academic in a landscape where
neither has any advantage and both are equally lost.

_The dead are a nation. They have territory. They have population
density. They have borders — the Veil — that they press against
the way any population presses against its borders. The difference
is that the dead's border is failing. The Veil thins. The dead
push closer. The living build wards and salt doors and maintain
the rituals and pretend that the border will hold, the way people
always pretend about borders. But the people who can see the
border — the galdr-workers who measure it, the seiðr-wives who
feel it, the outlaws who stand on the wrong side of it — know
that the border is not holding. It is giving. Slowly. Unevenly.
In ways that the Allthing does not discuss and the jarls do not
plan for. The dead are coming. Not as an army. Not as an invasion.
As a tide. And tides do not negotiate._
