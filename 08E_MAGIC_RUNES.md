# The Magic of Rimevegr — Runes

## The Rune-Tongue of Rimevegr

The runes are older than the Cracking. They were cut into stone
before the gods went silent, and they still work — diminished,
unreliable, hungry — in the three centuries since. This section
catalogues the runes as the galdr-workers of the Rimevegr know
them: not as a tidy alphabet of power, but as a dying language
spoken by people who learned it from people who learned it from
people who could still hear the gods correcting their
pronunciation.

Every rune has two faces. The galdr-workers call them _dagmál_
(day-speech) and _náttmál_ (night-speech). The day-speech is
what the rune means when the world is working as intended: when
the wards hold, when the fire burns orange, when the dead stay
down. The night-speech is what the rune means when the Veil is
close, when the air tastes of iron, when the carver's hands
move in patterns they did not choose.

The distinction is not metaphorical. A rune carved during
daylight hours with clear intent and steady hands produces the
dagmál effect. The same rune carved in the Long Dark, near a
Veil-thinning, by a galdr-worker whose fingers have gone numb
and whose breath clouds blue — that rune reaches for the
náttmál. The carver does not always choose which face answers.
At rank 1 and 2, the carver controls the voice. At rank 3 and
above, the rune begins to have opinions.

This is the part that the casual practitioners miss and the
masters understand too well: the runes are not tools. Tools do
not care how you hold them. The runes care. They respond to
context — the season, the Veil's proximity, the carver's state
of mind, the blood on the chisel. A rune carved in grief cuts
differently than a rune carved in anger, and both cut differently
than a rune carved in the flat, affectless state that the
Veil-touched carry like a second skin.

### Rune Scribing — The Physical Act

Galdr is not abstract. It begins with material: a chisel, a
surface, a steady hand, and something to say. The galdr-worker
selects the medium — stone for permanence, bone for intimacy
with death, wood for temporary work, iron for war. The medium
matters because the rune reads the material the way a singer
reads a hall: the acoustics change the song.

The carving stroke follows the rune's grain. Every galdr-worker
describes this identically — the sensation that the correct line
already exists inside the material and the chisel's job is to
release it. A wrong stroke resists. The wrist aches. The chisel
catches. The rune buzzes with a low-frequency irritation that
travels through the hand and lodges behind the eyes. A correct
stroke offers no sensation at all. Absence of resistance. The
rune finishes itself.

At rank 3 and above, the carver's hands are not entirely their
own. The rune demands a shape, and the shape may not be what the
carver intended. This is where the failure cascade begins (see
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`). Fizzle means the rune gave up on the
carver. Backlash means the carver fought the shape and the shape
won. Inversion means the rune decided what it wanted to say and
said it regardless of what the carver had planned.

The physical marks accumulate. Thin white lines score the
palms — not cuts, not scars, but frost-patterns that emerge over
months of practice. The fingertips go numb, then cold, then
permanently frostbitten at the tips even in summer. A master
galdr-worker's hands look like birch bark: white-lined, stiff,
cracked at the joints. The runes write themselves on the carver
while the carver writes them on the world.

### The Canonical Rune-Set

Twenty-four runes survive in active use across the Rimevegr. A
further ten are known from saga-fragments, barrow inscriptions,
and the sealed records at Skaldhaven and Deepholm. The twenty-four
are listed here with their dagmál and náttmál meanings. The ten
expansion runes follow.

The rune-names are the pre-Cracking pronunciations preserved in
oral tradition. Some galdr-workers dispute the vowels. Nobody
disputes the shapes — the physical forms are carved into standing
stones across the Rimevegr and have not changed in five centuries.

---

### Fé

**Dagmál (day-speech):** Wealth. Cattle. Stored value. Property.
The honest accumulation of a household that has survived another
season. Fé in its daylight face means the herd is fed, the
storehouse is full, and the jarl's silver-arm weighs what it
should.

**Náttmál (night-speech):** Hoarded power. Inheritance seized
before the body is cold. Wealth that poisons the hand that holds
it. Fé in the dark means avarice — the sickness of a man who
counts his grain while his neighbor's children starve, and the
grain counts him back. The rune whispers: _what you own owns you._
A night-carved Fé on a rival's door curses his wealth to rot from
within — the cattle sicken, the silver tarnishes, the stored
grain fills with weevils that have too many legs.

---

### Úr

**Dagmál:** The aurochs. Strength. Raw endurance. The body's
animal refusal to stop breathing. Úr is the rune carved on
shield-rims and doorframes, the brute promise that this thing
will not break today.

**Náttmál:** Beast-will. The strength that outlasts the mind
controlling it. Úr in the dark is the aurochs that does not know
when to stop charging — muscle and bone moving after the hugr has
gone quiet. Night-carved Úr on a weapon makes the wielder
stronger than he should be, and the strength stays after the
fighting stops, and the man cannot put the weapon down, and his
hands remember the killing grip for days. Berserker-smiths carve
Úr by firelight. They do not carve it twice on the same blade.

---

### Þurs

**Dagmál:** Thorn. Active defense. Giant-force. The dangerous
protection of a threshold that will hurt whatever crosses it.
Ward-posts carved with Þurs are the standard perimeter defense of
every settlement in the Rimevegr.

**Náttmál:** Hostile intrusion. Violent rejection. The thorn that
grows inward. Night-carved Þurs does not merely defend — it
attacks whatever approaches, including the people it was meant to
protect. A ward-post with inverted Þurs has been found at three
abandoned settlements in the eastern waste. In each case, the
inhabitants were found dead inside the perimeter, killed by their
own defenses. The wards held perfectly. Nothing got in. Nothing
got out.

---

### Kaun

**Dagmál:** Fire. The forge. Transformation through controlled
burning. Kaun is the smith's rune — the understanding that
destruction and creation are the same act performed at different
temperatures.

**Náttmál:** Punitive suffering. Cleansing pain. Fire that
chooses what to burn. Night-carved Kaun on a man's threshold does
not set the house ablaze — it burns what the man loves most and
leaves the rest standing. A galdr-worker in Skaldhaven used Kaun
náttmál to burn every written record of a merchant's debts while
the merchant's hall stood untouched. The merchant was ruined. The
galdr-worker's hands smelled of smoke for a year.

---

### Óss

**Dagmál:** Speech. Authority. Wisdom. The voice that carries
weight because the speaker has earned the right to be heard. Óss
is carved on law-stones and speaker's staffs and the posts of
every ting in the Rimevegr.

**Náttmál:** Command language. The voice that compels because the
listener cannot resist. Omen-speech — words that become true
because they were spoken aloud. Night-carved Óss on a speaker's
staff makes every word from that podium land like a verdict,
regardless of truth. The Lawspeaker at Icebreak ordered every Óss
night-staff in the settlement destroyed after a dispute was
settled by a man who was wrong but whose words tasted right in
every listener's mouth. Three of the five were found. The other
two are still missing.

---

### Reið

**Dagmál:** Riding. Motion. The journey that has a destination.
Safe passage. Reið on a traveler's staff smooths the road and
shortens the distance and keeps the wolves at bay.

**Náttmál:** Forced movement. Pursuit that cannot be escaped.
Route-breaking — the destruction of a path so that it cannot be
traveled again. Night-carved Reið on a boundary stone means
anyone who crosses that line walks in circles until they collapse
or until the rune is broken. Outlaw bands have used Reið náttmál
to trap pursuit parties in loops of forest that should take an
hour but take three days. The pursuit party's horses refuse to
eat afterward.

---

### Gjöf

**Dagmál:** Gift. Trust. Reciprocity. The exchange that binds two
people into obligation. Gjöf is the rune of hospitality, carved
on guest-cups and marriage beds and the thresholds of halls where
the host's word is sacred.

**Náttmál:** Binding favor. Debt that cannot be repaid. The gift
that becomes a chain. Night-carved Gjöf on a gift-object means
the recipient owes something and does not know what and cannot
learn what and the debt grows heavier each day until the debtor
does whatever the giver demands just to make the weight stop. The
seiðr-wives call this _gjafaþrál_ — gift-thralldom — and they
consider it worse than physical slavery because the thrall at
least knows who holds the chain.

---

### Vend

**Dagmál:** Bliss. Joy. The warmth of kinship and the comfort of
a hall where the fire burns steady and the ale is passed and
nobody is watching the door. Vend is carved on hearthstones and
cradles and the posts of feasting halls.

**Náttmál:** Enforced contentment. Joy that masks the rot
beneath. Night-carved Vend on a hall's central post makes
everyone inside feel safe and warm and loved, and nobody notices
that the wards have failed, and nobody checks the perimeter, and
the dead that have been gathering at the tree-line for three
nights walk through the unwatched doors. Dalla would call this
the most dangerous rune in the set, and she would be right, and
nobody would listen because they feel so warm.

---

### Hagall

**Dagmál:** Hail. Disruption. The storm that clears the air and
leaves the ground changed. Hagall is transformation through
violence — the understanding that some things must be broken
before they can be rebuilt.

**Náttmál:** Disaster. Ruin. The storm that does not stop.
Night-carved Hagall on a settlement's ward-post invites
catastrophe — not a specific disaster, but the _inclination_
toward disaster. Accidents multiply. Joints fail. Livestock
stumble into ravines that were not there yesterday. A year of
Hagall náttmál does not destroy a settlement. It exhausts one.
It makes every day slightly worse than it should be, until the
people inside begin to believe that the land itself has turned
against them. They are not wrong.

---

### Nauð

**Dagmál:** Need. Hardship. Endurance under pressure. The
grinding survival of the Long Dark, when the food is low and the
fire is small and the only virtue left is the stubborn refusal to
die. Nauð is carved on ration-posts and survival caches.

**Náttmál:** Compulsion. Need so deep it overrides the will.
Night-carved Nauð on a person's belongings makes them need
something — anything — with a desperation that has no object. The
man does not know what he needs. He only knows the absence is
killing him. He will do terrible things to fill it. The outlaws
in Chapter 14 who walk toward the Veil are feeling Nauð náttmál
whether they know it or not — the need for something the Veil
promises and the Veil does not name and the Veil does not deliver.

---

### Íss

**Dagmál:** Ice. Stillness. The calming of what was turbulent.
Íss is the rune of mediation and cooling, carved on fever-stones
and placed on wounds and scratched into the posts of halls where
tempers run hot.

**Náttmál:** Emotional freezing. Arrested life. The stillness
that is not peace but paralysis. Night-carved Íss on a man's
hearth-post stops the household — not through violence but
through the absence of all motion. The family stops speaking.
The children stop playing. The fire burns but gives no warmth.
Everyone sits and stares and waits for something that does not
come. Settlements near Veil-thinnings report this effect without
any rune being carved — the Veil itself is Íss náttmál, the
cosmic stillness that the ice-rune merely echoes.

---

### Ár

**Dagmál:** Year. Harvest. The long cycle that rewards patience.
Ár is the rune of seasons and timing, carved on field-stones and
granary posts and the boundary-markers of land that has been
tended for generations.

**Náttmál:** The cycle that will not break. The harvest that
comes due whether you planted or not. Night-carved Ár on an
enemy's land means the land remembers every debt, every
trespass, every drop of blood, and the year's turning brings the
reckoning. An Ár náttmál carved into a battle-site means the
battle repeats — not literally, but in the dreams of everyone
who sleeps within a day's walk, every year on the anniversary,
until someone pays the price the land is owed.

---

### Dagur

**Dagmál:** Day. Brightness. Clarity. The light that reveals
what the dark was hiding. Dagur is carved on truth-stones at the
ting and on the lintels of halls where honest dealing is expected.

**Náttmál:** Exposure. Scrutiny without mercy. The light that
burns away every shadow a man was hiding behind. Night-carved
Dagur on a liar's threshold means his secrets walk out of his
mouth while he sleeps — he speaks his crimes aloud in the dark,
and anyone listening hears the truth he cannot suppress. Three
Lawspeakers have used Dagur náttmál in the last century. All
three condemned the practice afterward. All three used the
evidence.

---

### Yr

**Dagmál:** Yew. The bow. Patience at the edge of mortality.
Yr is the rune of the dead — not the restless dead, but the
properly dead, the quiet ones, the ancestors who stay where they
were put.

**Náttmál:** Death-mark. The arrow that has already been loosed.
Yr náttmál carved on a living person's possession means the
death is coming and the death knows where to find them. Not
murder — the rune does not kill. It removes the small accidents
of luck that keep a person alive in the Rimevegr: the foot that
lands on solid ground instead of rotten ice, the instinct that
says _duck_ a heartbeat before the branch falls. Yr náttmál
strips the target of the casual grace that separates the living
from the nearly dead. After that, the Rimevegr does the rest.

---

### Peorð

**Dagmál:** The dice-cup. Chance. Hidden outcome. The
uncertainty that makes every decision a wager and every wager
a prayer.

**Náttmál:** The loaded die. Fate-shaking — the manipulation
of outcome by a hand that should not be in the cup. Night-carved
Peorð on a set of gaming lots ensures that the lots always favor
the carver, but the favor comes from somewhere, and somewhere is
keeping a ledger. Every rigged game borrows luck from the
carver's future. A season of cheating at dice costs a year of
misfortune — injuries that should not have happened, illnesses
that should not have caught, falls on clear ground. The luck-debt
compounds. The interest is physical.

---

### Óðal

**Dagmál:** Inheritance. Estate. Homeland. The land that knows
your name because your father's father bled into it. Óðal is the
deepest rune — the one that ties a family to a place and a place
to a family across generations.

**Náttmál:** Ancestral judgment. The land that remembers what the
family did. Night-carved Óðal on a family's boundary-stone
wakes the inheritance — not the silver and the cattle, but the
debts and the crimes and the oaths broken three generations ago
that nobody living remembers but the land never forgot. Óðal
náttmál is the rune used in the most devastating legal curses
in the Rimevegr: the curse that does not punish the man but
punishes his line, reaching backward through the dead and forward
through the unborn, because the land does not distinguish between
the grandfather who sinned and the grandson who inherited.

---

### Elgur

**Dagmál:** Elk. Protection. The antlered boundary that says
_this space is defended_. Elgur is the ward-rune — passive,
patient, enduring. Every settlement's inner perimeter carries
Elgur marks.

**Náttmál:** Defensive authority that will not let go. The
sanctuary that becomes a prison. Night-carved Elgur on a
shelter's posts means nothing harmful can enter — and nothing
inside can leave. The distinction between protection and
imprisonment is a line drawn by intent, and intent is the first
thing the Veil erodes. Three cave-shelters in the eastern waste
carry Elgur náttmál carved by outlaws who wanted safety. The
outlaws are still inside. The rune held. They starved.

---

### Sól

**Dagmál:** Sun. Wholeness. Vitality. The revelation that comes
with light and the health that comes with warmth. Sól is carved
on healing-stones and recovery beds and the east-facing posts of
every longhouse.

**Náttmál:** Total exposure. Life-force that burns what it
touches. Sól náttmál is the light that has no shadow — the
scrutiny that does not reveal truth but obliterates privacy. A
person under Sól náttmál cannot hide, cannot rest, cannot close
their eyes without the brightness finding them. Seiðr-wives use
Sól náttmál to force a spirit to manifest — the dead cannot hide
in the dark when the dark is burning — but the light damages
everything it touches, living and dead alike. There is a reason
the sun sets. Sól náttmál forgets that reason.

---

### Týr

**Dagmál:** Law. Sacrifice. Justice. The warrior's honor that
says _I will pay the price of what is right_. Týr is carved on
oath-stones and weapon-hilts and the pillars of every ting in the
Rimevegr.

**Náttmál:** Binding judgment. The law that has no mercy. Týr
náttmál carved on an oath-breaker's weapon means the weapon
turns — not physically, not visibly, but at the moment of crisis,
the blade finds the wrong target, the shield drops a heartbeat
too soon, the arm does not answer. Warriors who carry
Týr-cursed weapons do not die in battle. They die in the pause
after battle, when the adrenaline fades and they discover the
wound they did not feel and the wound is from their own blade.

---

### Bjarkan

**Dagmál:** Birch. Growth. Recovery. The green returning after
the Long Dark. Bjarkan is carved on cradles and sick-beds and
the posts of midwifery halls.

**Náttmál:** Growth that does not know when to stop. Recovery
that overshoots. Night-carved Bjarkan on a wound makes the
flesh close faster than it should, and the flesh that grows is
not quite right — too thick, too pale, too warm to the touch.
The seiðr-wives call Bjarkan náttmál healing _lífsvǫxtr_ (life-
overgrowth) and they warn against it: the body heals, but the
body changes, and the change is not reversible. A man healed
three times by Bjarkan náttmál has skin that moves independently
of the muscle beneath it, as though the skin has developed its
own intentions.

---

### Maður

**Dagmál:** Mankind. Community. The social order that separates
the human from the beast. Maður is carved on assembly-posts and
treaty-stones and the doors of halls where disputes are settled
by words instead of iron.

**Náttmál:** The crowd that turns. Public standing as a weapon.
Night-carved Maður on a man's property makes the community
forget him — not his name, not his face, but the instinct to
include him. He speaks and no one responds. He enters a room and
no one looks up. He calls for help and the sound arrives but the
urgency does not. Social erasure. The outlaws call it _dreaming
alive_ — being present in a room full of people who cannot
perceive that you are present. It is worse than exile because
exile is a decision. This is an oversight that never corrects.

---

### Eykur

**Dagmál:** Horse. Loyal motion. The partnership between rider
and mount that makes distance manageable. Eykur is carved on
saddles and harnesses and the gate-posts of steadings.

**Náttmál:** Momentum that cannot be stopped. The horse that
will not halt. Night-carved Eykur on a mount means the horse
runs and the rider cannot slow it and the destination is chosen
by the rune, not the rider. Three riders in the Frostmark
disappeared on Eykur-cursed horses. One was found two weeks
later, ninety miles north of his intended path, the horse dead
under him and his hands fused to the reins by dried blood and
cold. He could not explain where he had been. He could not
explain why the horse's eyes were open.

---

### Lögur

**Dagmál:** Water. Flow. Memory. The depth that holds what the
surface has forgotten. Lögur is carved on wells and boat-keels
and the stones placed at river-crossings.

**Náttmál:** Drowning memory. The current that pulls the mind
under. Night-carved Lögur on a drinking vessel makes the drinker
remember — not what they choose to remember, but what the water
chooses. The worst thing. The thing they buried. The face they
cannot look at. The sound of the ice breaking under someone they
loved. Lögur náttmál is used by seiðr-wives during interrogation:
a cup of water, a carved rune, and the prisoner drinks and weeps
and tells you everything because the memory will not stop until
the truth comes out.

---

### Ing

**Dagmál:** Fertility. Peace. Health. Safety. The blessing of a
household that is growing as it should. Ing is carved on
marriage-beds and birthing-stools and the hearth-stones of
halls where children are expected.

**Náttmál:** Safeguarded growth that serves the wrong master.
Night-carved Ing on a pregnant woman's threshold ensures the
child survives — ensures it with a ferocity that overrides
everything else. The mother may not survive. The household may
not survive. The child _will_, because the rune has decided, and
the rune's priority is the new life, and everything that is not
the new life is fuel. The midwives of the Rimevegr refuse to
carve Ing without daylight and steady hands. The stories of what
happens when Ing náttmál takes hold during a difficult birth are
not told in public.

---

### The Expansion Runes

Ten additional runes survive in fragments — barrow inscriptions,
sealed Skaldhaven records, the oral traditions of remote
settlements. They are not universally known. A galdr-worker may
know three or four of them. Nobody living knows all ten. Their
inclusion here is a reconstruction, assembled from multiple
sources, and some of the náttmál meanings are speculative.

---

### Nið

**Dagmál:** Shame. Disgrace. The public naming of what a man
has done wrong. Nið is the rune of social accountability — the
carved declaration that says _this person has failed and the
community knows_.

**Náttmál:** Social death. The shame that eats the name. Nið
náttmál carved on a man's door does not merely humiliate — it
unmakes his social existence. His name loses weight. His oaths
lose binding force. His word at the ting becomes noise. The
guðníðing described in Chapter 14 carve Nið into their own
flesh, deliberately, as the first step of the god-scorned path.
They are writing their own erasure.

---

### Hrafn

**Dagmál:** Raven. Omen. Battlefield intelligence. The bird that
sees the dead before the living know they have fallen. Hrafn is
carved on scout-staffs and message-poles.

**Náttmál:** Carrion-vision. The sight that cannot stop seeing
the dead. Night-carved Hrafn on a warrior's helm gives him the
raven's perspective — he sees the battlefield as a field of
meat, and the living are merely meat that has not stopped moving
yet. The sight persists after the battle ends. The warrior looks
at his companions and sees future corpses. He looks at children
and sees small corpses. The raven's gift is truth. The truth is
that everything alive is a body that does not yet know it is a
body. The sight fades in days. The knowledge does not.

---

### Blóð

**Dagmál:** Blood. Kinship. Sacrifice. The price paid in the
only currency the gods ever respected. Blóð is carved on
oath-rings and sacrifice-altars and the stones where blood-
brotherhood is sworn.

**Náttmál:** Blood-debt. The price that never finishes being
paid. Night-carved Blóð on a compact between two parties means
the betrayer bleeds — not metaphorically, not symbolically.
The wounds open without a blade. Small at first: nosebleeds,
bleeding gums, cuts that reopen. Then larger. A woman in the
Uppmark broke a Blóð-sealed trade agreement and bled from her
eyes for three days. She honored the agreement. The bleeding
stopped. She never trusted a galdr-worker again.

---

### Móðr

**Dagmál:** Fury. Courage. Battle-temper. The red heat that makes
a man stand when his body wants to fall. Móðr is the warrior's
rune, carved on weapon-hilts and shield-bosses.

**Náttmál:** Wrath without direction. The fury that does not
know friend from enemy. Night-carved Móðr on a warrior's weapon
makes him impossible to cow and impossible to control. The
berserker state — Úr for the body, Móðr for the spirit — is the
most feared rune-combination in the Rimevegr. The warriors who
carry both do not come back. Not dead. They come back _wrong_.
The fury does not leave when the battle ends. It lives in the
muscle memory. It lives in the dreams. It lives in the way the
man's hand tightens on the haft when someone raises their voice
in the hall.

---

### Mórk

**Dagmál:** Boundary. Marked land. Threshold. The line between
this and that, here and there, ours and theirs. Mórk is the
foundation of property law in the Rimevegr — carved on every
legal boundary-stone.

**Náttmál:** The boundary that seals. The edge that cuts. Night-
carved Mórk on a settlement's perimeter means the perimeter
becomes absolute — not a ward but a wall, not a defense but a
severance. Everything inside the boundary is one world.
Everything outside is another. The two do not communicate.
Messages do not cross. Sound does not carry. The settlement
exists in a pocket of silence, and the people inside do not
know the outside world still exists, and the outside world
forgets the settlement was ever there.

---

### Steinn

**Dagmál:** Stone. Permanence. The mound. What lasts when
everything else has rotted. Steinn is carved on grave-markers and
foundation-stones and the pillars of halls meant to stand for
generations.

**Náttmál:** The immovable curse. The weight that holds the
target in place. Night-carved Steinn on a man's boots means his
feet will not carry him from the spot where he stands. Not
paralysis — the muscles work, the joints bend — but the ground
will not release him. He pulls and the earth holds. Steinn
náttmál is used to anchor barrow-wards: the dead who are
Steinn-bound cannot rise because the stone remembers them as
stone and the stone will not let go. When Steinn-wards fail
after centuries, the draugr that emerge have skin like granite
and joints that crack like breaking rock.

---

### Salt

**Dagmál:** Preservation. Cleansing. Anti-corruption. Salt is
the rune of purity — the carving that keeps the rot at bay and
the boundary between clean and unclean intact.

**Náttmál:** Preservation without mercy. The refusal to let the
dead be dead. Night-carved Salt on a corpse keeps the body fresh
indefinitely — skin supple, eyes clear, no bloating, no stink.
The body looks asleep. It is not asleep. It is dead, perfectly
dead, and the perfection is obscene. Families have kept Salt-
preserved bodies for years, unable to grieve because the evidence
of death is absent. The rope-readers insist on burning any body
found with Salt náttmál carved into the burial wrappings. The
families resist. The argument never ends well.

---

### Mjǫðr

**Dagmál:** Mead. Poetry. Inspiration. The word-gift that
transforms speech into something that matters. Mjǫðr is the
skald's rune, carved on drinking-horns and speaker's posts.

**Náttmál:** Intoxicating insight. The truth that poisons the
speaker. Night-carved Mjǫðr gives the speaker eloquence that
comes from somewhere deeper than training — the words arrive
fully formed, devastating in their precision, impossible to
argue against. The speaker does not compose the speech. The
speech composes itself, using the speaker as a vessel. Afterward,
the speaker cannot remember what they said. Others can. Others
always remember. The speech changes them. It changes the
speaker too, but the speaker does not know how, because the
speech has erased the memory of its own delivery. Skaldhaven's
archive contains fourteen transcribed Mjǫðr-speeches. Reading
them is restricted. The restriction is not political. It is
medical.

---

### Skuggi

**Dagmál:** Shadow. Concealment. The ambiguity of things half-
seen. Skuggi is carved on thief-tools and spy-staffs and the
cloaks of men who need to be elsewhere.

**Náttmál:** Hidden identity. The doubled self. Night-carved
Skuggi on a man's cloak does not make him invisible — it makes
him _someone else_. Witnesses see a person. They describe a
person. The description is consistent and detailed and
completely wrong. The man they describe does not exist. Skuggi
náttmál is the forgery of presence — a false person layered over
the real one like frost over stone. The false person persists in
memory even after the rune is broken. Witnesses swear they saw
someone who was never born.

### Hlið

**Dagmál:** Gate. Passage. Threshold crossing. Hlið is the rune
of entry and exit — carved on door-frames and bridge-posts and
the entrance-stones of halls where guests are expected.

**Náttmál:** The sealed crossing. The door that opens one way.
Night-carved Hlið on a doorway means anyone may enter but no one
may leave — or anyone may leave but no one may enter, depending
on which face of the frame the rune is carved. Outlaws have used
Hlið náttmál to trap pursuit parties inside buildings: the door
opens inward, the pursuit enters, the door does not open outward.
The galdr-worker walks away. The building becomes a box.

### Mál

**Dagmál:** Speech. Measure. Legal speech. Agreement. Mál is the
rune of the lawspeaker — the spoken word that carries the weight
of decision.

**Náttmál:** Verdict without trial. The word that becomes law
because it was spoken in the right voice at the right time with
the right rune on the right post. Night-carved Mál on a ting-
stone means the first person to speak from that stone speaks
binding truth — not their opinion, not their argument, but the
underlying reality of the dispute, dragged into the open whether
the parties want it or not. Both sides leave destroyed. Justice
is served. Nobody is happy. This is what justice looks like in
the Rimevegr: a verdict that punishes everyone and satisfies no
one and is, nonetheless, correct.

### Vetr

**Dagmál:** Winter. Endurance. Survival under the weight of cold.
Vetr is carved on storehouse doors and winter-camp markers and
the walking-staves of people who travel the Long Dark.

**Náttmál:** The season that does not end. Patient ruin. Night-
carved Vetr on a settlement's well means winter touches the
water — not ice, but cold that persists into the Long Summer.
The crops falter. The ground stays hard. The livestock huddle.
Vetr náttmál is the Veil's favorite rune, and the galdr-workers
know this, and they carve it last and erase it first and they
handle it the way a smith handles a crucible of molten iron:
quickly, carefully, and with deep respect for what happens
when it spills.

## Galdr Vocalization — The Throat-Singing

The rune carved into material is half the working. The other
half is the voice.

Galdr is not spoken. It is not sung in the way a skald sings or
a mother hums a lullaby. Galdr is _throat-voiced_ — a guttural,
resonant, compressed vibration that originates in the chest and
exits the throat as something between speech and growl. The
galdr-worker does not choose the pitch. The pitch chooses itself,
rising from the gut the way the rune's shape rises from the
material. The correct tone is the one that makes the carved line
glow. The incorrect tone is the one that makes the carver's nose
bleed.

The old galdr manuals — fragments survive at Skaldhaven —
describe the voice as _rúnamunnr_ (rune-mouth): the physical
apparatus that translates carved symbol into activated power.
The carving is the letter. The voice is the breath that makes
the letter a word. Without the voice, the rune is dormant. With
the wrong voice, the rune inverts. With the right voice, the
rune remembers what it was meant to do and does it, and the
world around the carver shifts to accommodate the demand.

### What the Singing Does to the Singer

The physical effects accumulate. Short-term, a galdr-working
leaves the singer's throat raw, the voice hoarse, and the chest
aching as though the ribs have been compressed. The taste of
copper persists for hours. The breath clouds even in warm air,
as though the body is exhaling something colder than the
surrounding temperature.

Long-term, the throat changes. A galdr-worker of ten years has a
speaking voice that carries a permanent undertone — not a pitch,
but a texture, as though the vocal cords have developed a second
resonance that runs beneath the normal voice. People hear it
without identifying it. It makes them uneasy. Children cry.
Dogs lower their ears and back away. The galdr-worker sounds
_inhabited_ — as though the voice is not entirely one voice.

At rank 4 and above, the throat-singing produces involuntary
effects on listeners. The galdr is not aimed at them — the rune
is the target, the carving is the focus — but the vibration
spills. The overflow manifests as:

**Dark emotional resonance.** Listeners within earshot
experience a sudden, sourceless shift in mood. Not fear
exactly — deeper. A memory of grief surfaces without trigger. An
old anger flares without cause. The feeling is
indistinguishable from genuine emotion, and that is the danger:
the listeners believe the grief is theirs, the anger is theirs,
and they act on it. A hall full of armed men hearing galdr at
rank 4 may erupt into violence not because the galdr targeted
them but because the sound woke something in each man that was
already there, waiting for permission.

**Compulsive joining.** The most disturbing effect. At rank 4
and above, some listeners begin to sing along. Not consciously.
The throat vibrates. The chest resonates. A low hum emerges from
the listener's body before the listener's mind has agreed to
produce it. The hum harmonizes with the galdr — not perfectly,
but enough. In a room of twelve people, three or four will begin
humming. They cannot stop until the galdr-worker stops. They do
not know they are doing it until someone tells them, and when
told, they cannot explain why their body obeyed a sound their
mind did not authorize.

**Physical arousal.** The galdr's resonance moves through the
body. At certain frequencies — the náttmál register, the low
growl that activates the dark-face runes — the vibration
triggers involuntary physical responses. Pulse accelerates.
Skin flushes. Sexual arousal manifests without stimulus,
without desire, without the listener's consent or participation.
It is a body responding to a frequency the way a tuning fork
responds to its note — mechanically, impersonally, and with no
regard for the person inside the body. This effect is most
pronounced during extended workings (thirty minutes or longer)
and most dangerous during group-galdr, when multiple voices
produce harmonic interference that amplifies the base frequency.
The old manuals describe this as _holdboð_ — flesh-command —
and the galdr-workers who still know the term do not use it
casually.

**Numbness and pressure.** Listeners report tingling in the
extremities — fingers, toes, lips — followed by numbness that
climbs. At thirty minutes of exposure, the hands are insensate.
At an hour, the arms. The galdr-worker himself feels this too —
the singing numbs the body from the outside in, as though the
vibration is replacing nerve-signal with rune-signal. A galdr-
worker who has been singing for two hours cannot feel his own
hands on the chisel. He carves by muscle memory and by the
rune's guidance, and if the rune decides his hand should slip,
his hand slips, and he does not feel the blade enter the palm.

**Blood pressure and the divine stroke.** The most severe
physical consequence. Extended galdr — rank 4 or 5 workings
that last hours — produces symptoms identical to acute
hypertension: pounding headache, visual disturbance, nausea,
chest pressure, ringing ears. The galdr-workers call this
_guðslag_ (god-stroke). It is not metaphorical. Galdr-workers
die of it. The throat-singing forces blood pressure upward
through a mechanism that the seiðr-wives say is spiritual
(the body trying to contain divine energy) and the galdr-
workers say is physical (the vibration constricting blood
vessels in the throat and chest). Both are probably right.

Guðslag at its worst produces hemorrhagic stroke. The galdr-
worker collapses mid-chant. One pupil dilates. The face goes
slack on one side. The rune on the stone glows brighter than
it should — brighter than any rune the witnesses have seen —
and then it goes dark, and the galdr-worker is dead or wishes
they were. Survivors of guðslag lose function on one side of
the body and lose access to the higher ranks of galdr
permanently. Their hands still know the shapes. Their throat
still knows the tones. The runes no longer answer. The body
paid and the runes took and the runes did not give back.

Three galdr-workers in the last century have died of guðslag
during rank 5 workings. Two more survived with permanent
disability. The Deepholm archive records each case with clinical
precision and does not editorialize, because the Deepholm
archivists understand that the data speaks for itself: the most
powerful magic in the Rimevegr kills the people who practice it,
and the mechanism is indistinguishable from natural disease, and
this is either coincidence or design, and neither answer is
comforting.

### Poetic Form — The Galdr Chant Structure

Every galdr working should have a voiced form: the _kvæði_
(verse-chant) that the galdr-worker speaks or growls while
carving. The kvæði is not decorative. It is the targeting system.
The rune carved into stone says _what_. The kvæði says _where_,
_when_, _how hard_, and _to whom_.

A well-formed kvæði has four elements:

1. **Name the rune.** Speak the rune's name aloud. This is the
   invocation — the act that tells the rune it is being called.
2. **State the image.** Give the rune a concrete anchor in the
   physical world: a place, a person, a thing. The more specific
   the image, the more precise the effect.
3. **State the change.** Say what the rune should do. Not in
   abstract terms — in physical, observable, sensory terms.
4. **Leave room.** The kvæði should not close every door. A line
   of ambiguity — a space for the rune to interpret — is the
   difference between galdr that works and galdr that works
   exactly as intended and nothing more.

The tone is guttural, compressed, rhythmic. The lines should
feel like they cost something to speak. The Rimevegr's kvæðir
sound like commands issued through clenched teeth — not because
the carver is angry, but because the voice is carrying weight
that vocal cords were not designed to carry.

## Rune-Script Construction — The Grammar of Galdr

Rune magic is a language. Each rune is a word. Combining runes
creates a sentence, and the sentence is the spell.

### Script Size and Power

The number of runes in a script determines its scope:

- **3 runes:** A single, clean effect. One sentence. One demand.
  Most galdr in the Rimevegr is three-rune work.
- **5 runes:** A layered effect. Two sentences that reinforce
  each other. Rank 3+ galdr-workers use five-rune scripts for
  complex wards and bindings.
- **7 runes:** Major scene control. The galdr-worker is not
  asking the world to change — they are telling it. Rank 4
  minimum. The kvæði for a seven-rune script takes twenty
  minutes to chant.
- **9 runes:** Mythic-level working. The old sagas describe
  nine-rune scripts that sealed entire valleys, raised storms
  that lasted seasons, and bound the dead to obedience for
  generations. No living galdr-worker has successfully completed
  a nine-rune script. The last verified attempt was in year 247.
  The galdr-worker's name was Eiríkr. The script was meant to
  seal a barrow-field permanently. The galdr succeeded. Eiríkr
  died of guðslag before the final line was cut. His apprentice
  finished the chant. The barrow-field is still sealed. The
  apprentice cannot speak above a whisper and has not been able
  to since.

### Script Construction Pattern

Every script follows a grammar:

**Pattern 1 — Noun + Verb + Consequence:**

- `Fé + Óss + Gjöf` — Cattle obey the voice and return
  home through trust.
- `Kaun + Hagall + Nið` — Fire falls like a storm on a
  house of shame.
- `Yr + Mál + Hrafn` — The grave speaks through omen
  and carrion-sign.

**Pattern 2 — Object + Force + Outcome:**

- `Steinn + Nauð + Elgur` — Stone endures and becomes
  sanctuary.
- `Skuggi + Reið + Hlið` — Shadow travels and the gate
  opens one way.
- `Blóð + Týr + Nið` — Blood demands justice and the
  betrayer is shamed.

**Pattern 3 — Social + Natural:**

- `Gjöf + Ár + Vend` — Gift and harvest build household
  bliss.
- `Mál + Vetr + Nauð` — Law endures through winter's
  hardship.
- `Nið + Kaun + Hagall` — Shame burns and the storm
  takes what's left.

### Dark Script Examples — Rimevegr Workings

These are the galdr-scripts that galdr-workers whisper about
and Lawspeakers pretend do not exist.

#### 1. Seal the Dead Who Will Not Stay

Runes: Yr + Steinn + Elgur

Kvæði:

> "Yr in the bone, Steinn on the lid, Elgur at the mouth.
> What sleeps here stays sleeping. The stone remembers the
> weight."

Effect: The barrow-ward holds the dead in place. Standard
defensive galdr. Every settlement has at least one of these.
The dark part is not the script — the dark part is that they
need refreshing every twenty years now, and twenty years ago
it was thirty, and thirty years before that it was fifty.

#### 2. Burn the Oath-Breaker's Hall

Runes: Kaun + Nið + Óðal

Kvæði:

> "Kaun in the rafter, Nið on the name, Óðal in the ash.
> Let the fire know what the line has done. Let the land
> forget the house."

Effect: The hall burns. The fire targets the structure, not the
people — but the people are inside the structure, and the fire
does not distinguish between the two after the first minute.
The land where the hall stood will not support a building for
a generation. The family name becomes a cautionary tale.

#### 3. Curse the Betrayer's Blood

Runes: Blóð + Týr + Nið + Ár + Óðal

Kvæði:

> "Blóð on the oath, Týr in the breach, Nið on the tongue,
> Ár in the debt, Óðal in the line. The blood remembers. The
> law remembers. The land remembers. The year comes due."

Effect: A five-rune curse-script. The betrayer bleeds for the
broken oath. The shame follows the family. The land records the
transgression. The year brings the reckoning. This is rank 4
galdr — expensive, dangerous, and devastating. The galdr-
worker who carves this pays 3 Willpower and 3 HP minimum, and
the script must be carved in the betrayer's blood (obtained
legally — a nick during a witnessed ting, a wound from an
honor-duel, a deliberate cut offered by the betrayer themselves
before they knew what the blood would be used for).

#### 4. Trap the Living Behind Their Own Walls

Runes: Hlið + Þurs + Mórk

Kvæði:

> "Hlið at the door, Þurs in the frame, Mórk at the line.
> The guest may enter. The guest may not leave. The boundary
> does the rest."

Effect: The building becomes a box. The door opens inward.
People can enter. No one exits until the rune is broken.
Used historically in three recorded cases: twice against
outlaws who occupied a settlement building, once against a
jarl's household during a coup. The third case is not
discussed in public because the jarl's household included
fourteen children, and the building was sealed for three days
before anyone broke the rune.

#### 5. Drown a Man in His Own Memories

Runes: Lögur + Nauð + Hrafn

Kvæði:

> "Lögur in the skull, Nauð in the need, Hrafn on the
> shoulder. The water rises. The raven watches. Drown in
> what you did."

Effect: The target remembers. Not selectively — totally. Every
cruelty they performed, every betrayal they committed, every
face they tried to forget. The memories arrive with full
sensory detail — the smell, the sound, the wet. The target
cannot eat, cannot sleep, cannot function until the memories
run their course, which takes between three days and three
weeks depending on how much the target has to remember. Two
men subjected to Lögur-Nauð-Hrafn scripts killed themselves
before the effect ended. The galdr-workers who carved the
scripts were acquitted at ting because the suicides were ruled
self-inflicted. The legal distinction between killing a man
and making him remember enough to kill himself is one of the
more disturbing features of Rimevegr jurisprudence.

#### 6. Erase a Man from His Community

Runes: Maður + Íss + Skuggi

Kvæði:

> "Maður in the gone, Íss in the voice, Skuggi on the face.
> Stand among them. They stand on."

Effect: Social erasure. The target is not invisible — he is
unremarkable. Present but unperceived. He speaks and the
words arrive but the urgency does not. He stands in a room
and the room forgets to include him. This is the rune-based
version of the outlaw's terminal loneliness described in
Chapter 14 — but achieved in minutes rather than years, and
inflicted from outside rather than grown from within.

#### 7. Make the Unborn Pay for the Father's Sin

Runes: Óðal + Blóð + Ár + Ing + Nið

Kvæði:

> "Óðal remembers the sin. Blóð carries the price. Ár turns
> the wheel. Ing bears the weight. Nið names the shame."

Effect: A five-rune generational curse. The most forbidden
galdr in the Rimevegr. Targets not the man but the man's line
— children, grandchildren, the family's future. The effects
are subtle and persistent: fertility drops. Livestock yield
decreases. Luck sours. The family's fortunes decline across
decades in ways that could be coincidence and are not. The
galdr-worker who carves this is legally niding if caught, and
no ting in the Rimevegr will hear a defense. The practice
survives because the evidence is indistinguishable from natural
misfortune, and because the families who suffer do not know a
rune was carved, and because the galdr-workers who carve them
do not talk.

### Bind-Runes — Permanent Workings

A bind-rune is a compound rune: two or more rune-forms
overlaid into a single carving. Where a script is a sentence,
a bind-rune is a single word made from multiple roots. The
binding compresses the effect but makes it permanent — or as
permanent as anything is in the Rimevegr, which means it lasts
until the material fails or the Veil eats the power.

**Ward bind-runes** — Protection and defense.

- `Þurs + Elgur + Úr` — Active defense, warding, bodily
  endurance. Kvæði: "Þurs at the flank, Elgur on the
  ridge, Úr in the ribs. Stand and do not break."
- `Mórk + Steinn + Salt` — Boundary sealed, permanent,
  purified. The standard barrow-ward. Kvæði: "Mórk at the
  edge, Steinn in the deep, Salt on the seal. The line
  holds. The dead sleep."

**Witness bind-runes** — Memory and truth.

- `Dagur + Mál + Óss` — Clarity, speech, authority. Carved
  on ting-stones. Kvæði: "Dagur sees, Mál measures, Óss
  speaks. The truth has no shade."
- `Hrafn + Yr + Steinn` — Omen, death, permanence. Carved
  on grave-markers to record how the dead died. Kvæði: "The
  raven saw. The yew remembers. The stone keeps."

**Burden bind-runes** — Curses and weight.

- `Nið + Blóð + Óðal` — Disgrace, blood-debt, lineage.
  The generational curse in compact form. Kvæði: "Nið on
  the house, Blóð on the name, Óðal remembers what the
  line has done."
- `Nauð + Vetr + Íss` — Need, winter, stillness. The
  binding of despair. Kvæði: "Need in the dark, winter in
  the bone, ice on the will. Wait. Wait. Wait."

**Battle bind-runes** — War and force.

- `Týr + Móðr + Úr` — Justice, fury, strength. The
  warrior's compound. Kvæði: "Týr in the hand, Móðr in
  the blood, Úr in the bone. Strike true and stand."
- `Kaun + Hagall + Þurs` — Fire, storm, thorn. The
  siege-breaker. Kvæði: "Kaun opens, Hagall breaks, Þurs
  punishes. Nothing holds."

## Rune-Blight — The Corruption of Galdr

The runes are not safe. They were never safe. The pre-Cracking
galdr-workers had divine patronage — the gods corrected the
pronunciation, steadied the hand, absorbed the overflow. Post-
Cracking galdr-workers have none of that. They carve with
orphaned tools and channel power through a system designed for
divine supervision that has been running unsupervised for three
centuries. The runes still work. They work wrong, sometimes.
They work _more than intended_, sometimes. And the wrongness
accumulates in the carver's body the way the cold accumulates
in the Rimevegr: slowly, specifically, and without mercy.

The galdr-workers call it _rúnavíti_ — rune-blight.

### What It Is

Rune-blight is the physical and mental corruption that results
from prolonged, excessive, or improper rune use. It is not the
same as the normal costs of galdr (the white hand-lines, the
numb fingertips, the permanent vocal undertone). Those costs
are the expected price. Rune-blight is what happens when the
price is not enough and the runes take more.

The distinction is cumulative exposure versus acute damage.
A galdr-worker who carves within their rank, rests between
workings, and respects the material's grain will pay the
standard costs and live to old age with strange hands and a
stranger voice. A galdr-worker who overreaches — carving above
rank, forcing runes into wrong materials, chanting náttmál
without understanding what the night-face wants — accumulates
blight.

### The Stages of Rune-Blight

**Stage 1 — The Tremor.** The galdr-worker's hands develop a
fine tremor that appears only during carving. The chisel
jumps. Lines waver. Simple runes that should be second nature
require concentration. The galdr-worker compensates by gripping
harder, which makes the tremor worse. At this stage, galdr
checks take a -5 penalty and the failure cascade shifts upward
by 10 on the table (see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`).

**Stage 2 — The Echo.** The runes begin to speak back. Not
audibly — the galdr-worker hears the rune's resonance in
peripheral attention, the way you hear a sound in a dream. The
echo is the rune's night-face offering opinions. A galdr-worker
carving Fé for a simple livestock blessing hears, beneath the
dagmál chant, the náttmál whisper: _what you own owns you_. The
echo cannot be silenced. It can be ignored, but ignoring it
requires Will checks, and each failure means the rune tilts
toward its dark face regardless of the carver's intent.

**Stage 3 — The Mark.** The blight becomes visible. The white
hand-lines that every galdr-worker develops begin to spread —
up the wrists, along the forearms. They are no longer thin.
They are raised, textured, and they glow faintly in the dark
with the same blue-white flicker as active rune-lines. The
galdr-worker's hands look like ward-stones. The galdr-worker
_is_ a ward-stone — the runes have decided that the carver's
body is a valid medium, and they are writing on it the same way
the carver writes on wood and bone and iron.

At this stage, the galdr-worker's presence begins to affect the
environment. Fires burn blue within arm's reach. Small metal
objects drift toward the hands. The air around the worker smells
of old iron permanently, not just during carving. Animals refuse
proximity. Other galdr-workers can feel the blight at a
distance — a pressure, a wrongness, the sensation of standing
too close to an active rune-stone.

**Stage 4 — The Speaking.** The galdr-worker's voice splits.
The permanent undertone that all experienced galdr-workers
develop separates into a distinct second voice — audible,
recognizable, and not the carver's. The second voice speaks
in rune-fragments: partial kvæðir, rune-names, the beginning
of chants the carver did not start and cannot stop. The
speaking happens during sleep, during conversation, during
silence. The galdr-worker is chanting constantly, beneath the
threshold of conscious control, and the chant is building
toward a working that the carver has not chosen and cannot
identify.

Other people hear it. The effect is the same as galdr overflow —
dark emotional resonance, compulsive humming, the physical
effects described above — but constant, low-level, unending.
Living with a Stage 4 galdr-worker is living inside a
permanent low-grade rune-working. The household adapts. The
household changes. The household stops noticing things that
visitors notice immediately: the blue fire-tips, the drifting
objects, the children who hum in their sleep.

**Stage 5 — The Rune Becomes the Person.** Terminal blight.
The galdr-worker's body completes its transformation into a
medium. The white lines cover the entire body — face, torso,
legs. The eyes develop the blue-white flicker. The voice is no
longer human in any recognizable sense; it is rune-speech,
constant, a low guttural chant that does not stop even when the
galdr-worker is unconscious. The person inside the body is still
present — this is not possession — but the person is now living
inside a rune-stone, and the rune-stone is active, and the
rune-stone is running a working that no one authorized.

No Stage 5 galdr-worker has been documented in the last century.
The Deepholm archive records three cases from the first century
post-Cracking, when galdr-workers pushed harder because the
failure of the old systems demanded more power than human bodies
could safely channel. All three were killed by their settlements
— not out of malice, but out of necessity. A Stage 5 galdr-
worker warps the environment in a radius of approximately
fifty paces. Within that radius, wards fail, the Veil thins,
the dead wake, and the living experience continuous galdr
overflow: dark emotions, compulsive humming, physical arousal,
numbness, headache. The settlement cannot function. The galdr-
worker cannot stop. The only recorded resolution is death, and
even then the body must be burned completely and the ashes
scattered in running water, because Stage 5 corpses have been
known to continue chanting for hours after the heart stops.

### Connection to the God-Scorned Path

The guðníðing described in Chapter 14 sometimes pursue rune-
blight deliberately. Where a normal galdr-worker accumulates
blight through overwork and overreach, the god-scorned carve
inverted runes into their own flesh, chant náttmál without
target or intent, and force the blight's progression as a form
of spiritual self-destruction.

The overlap between Stage 3 rune-blight (the Mark) and the
guðníðing's inverted ward-marks is not coincidental. Both
involve the runes writing on the carver's body. The difference
is direction: the blighted galdr-worker's marks are the runes'
initiative, carved from outside in. The guðníðing's marks are
self-inflicted, carved from inside out. The result is similar
but not identical. The blighted worker is being consumed by the
magic. The guðníðing is consuming themselves with the magic.

The three soulbanes described in Chapter 14 — mannát, dýrríða,
villimennska — interact with rune-blight in specific ways:

- **Mannát (the Eating)** kills the hugr. A galdr-worker whose
  hugr is dead cannot hear the rune's dagmál face. All galdr
  defaults to náttmál. The rune-blight accelerates because the
  brake is gone.
- **Dýrríða (the Riding)** kills the hamr. A galdr-worker
  whose hamr is dead has no barrier between the rune's will
  and the body. The speaking (Stage 4) arrives immediately.
  The runes write freely.
- **Villimennska (the Wilding)** kills the fylgja. A galdr-
  worker whose fylgja is dead has no divine inheritance, no
  residual connection to whatever the gods left behind. The
  galdr stops working entirely — not because the runes reject
  the carver, but because the carver has nothing left for the
  runes to channel through. A villimaðr cannot carve. The
  hands still move. The chisel still cuts. The runes are
  shapes on stone. They do not glow. They do not answer. The
  magic is gone.

This creates a bitter irony: the god-scorned who pursue rune-
blight as self-destruction find that the path of corruption
leads not to greater dark power but to the complete loss of
power. The runes are not tools of rebellion against the divine
order. They are fragments of the divine order, and destroying
the divine inheritance within oneself destroys access to the
fragments. The wolfshead who expected to become a dark galdr-
master becomes, instead, a man with scars on his hands and
silence where the runes used to speak.

_This is the Rimevegr's cruelest joke, and it is a joke nobody
tells because nobody finds it funny: the magic that is killing
the world is also the only thing keeping the world alive, and
the people who try to weaponize the killing find that the weapon
does not work without the thing it is destroying._
