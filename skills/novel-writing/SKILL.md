# Novel-Writing Skill — Iron Ledger Voice

Use when drafting, revising, or reviewing fiction prose for the
Iron Ledger: Mercenaries of the Rimevegr project. Applies the
voice, structure, diction, and anti-AI rules so prose reads like
native dark military fantasy — harsh, clear, physical, atmospheric,
and free of generated-text tells.

Covers novel chapters, vignettes, character scenes, dialogue,
battle aftermath, and the integration of simulation results into
narrative prose.

---

## The Problem This Skill Solves

AI writing fails in dark military fantasy in three stacked ways:

1. **Surface voice.** The AI produces "dark fantasy flavor" — shadow,
   whisper, ancient power, grim destiny — without physical grounding.
   The result is mood fog that teaches nothing and feels generic.

2. **Structural flattening.** The AI writes every paragraph the same
   way: topic sentence, elaboration, softened conclusion. Real
   paragraphs have shape — statement then consequence, condition then
   pressure then cost. The AI's paragraphs are interchangeable.
   The prose that works is not.

3. **Invisible AI tells.** Even when the AI avoids obvious slop,
   it leaks: em-dash overuse, rule-of-three padding, copula
   avoidance ("serves as" instead of "is"), elegant variation
   (cycling synonyms to avoid repetition), negative parallelisms
   ("not just X, but Y"), hedging stacks, and generic positive
   conclusions. These are detectable and they break trust.

This skill attacks all three layers.

## Part 1: What Glen Cook Does

### The Annals Voice

Cook invented the military fantasy first-person chronicle
in _The Black Company_. The narrator, Croaker, is the
company physician and annalist. He writes like a man
keeping records in camp by firelight with ink he mixed
himself. The voice has five structural properties:

**1. Radical understatement.** The worst things are stated
in the fewest words. Cook describes a sorcerous massacre
and the narrator writes: "It was pretty bad in there." A
man is disemboweled and Croaker notes the wound with
clinical brevity and moves to logistics. The horror is in
the gap between what happened and how little the narrator
says about it. The reader fills the gap. Cook trusts the
reader to do more emotional work than most fantasy authors.

**2. Present-tense logistics.** The Company exists as a
sequence of practical problems: food, pay, route, weather,
who is on watch, who is sick, which wagon has the bad
axle. Cook spends more time on these than on magic or
battle. The reader lives inside the daily grind. Violence,
when it arrives, is an interruption of the grind rather
than the point of it. The mundane is the baseline.
Everything else is deviation.

**3. Character through habit.** Croaker identifies people
by what they do repeatedly: One-Eye cheats at cards, Goblin
retaliates with petty magic, the Lieutenant counts supplies.
Cook rarely describes a face, body, or emotional state. He
describes behavior patterns. A new character is introduced
by the thing they always do — the gesture, the tic, the
ritual, the compulsion. Two lines of habit and the character
exists.

**4. Moral neutrality in narration, moral weight in
consequence.** Croaker does not judge. He records. The
Company does terrible things and serves terrible masters
and the narrator describes this the same way he describes
setting up camp. The moral weight arrives through
consequence: the village that is empty next season, the
recruit who stops talking, the captain who sleeps badly.
Cook never tells the reader something is wrong. He shows
what the wrong thing costs, later, quietly, in a detail
the narrator mentions because it is in the annals and
the annals record everything.

**5. Humor as survival mechanism.** The Company jokes
constantly — crude, dark, petty humor between soldiers who
have seen too much. The jokes are never witty. They are
functional. They defuse tension, establish hierarchy
(who can mock whom), and create the only warmth the
narrative allows. When the jokes stop, the reader knows
things are genuinely bad.

### Cook's Sentence Architecture

Cook writes short. His default sentence is 8–14 words.
Compound sentences are rare and almost always joined by
"and" — never by semicolons, rarely by em-dashes. The
effect is flat, even, reportorial. The narrator is not
performing. He is recording.

When Cook does write a longer sentence, it is a list:
things carried, things seen, places passed through. The
length comes from accumulated concrete nouns, never from
subordinate clauses or psychological elaboration.

Cook's paragraph breaks are abrupt. He will drop a single
sentence as its own paragraph — a fact, a punchline, a
hard turn. The effect is staccato and reader-propulsive.
The page physically moves.

### Cook's Limitations (and What the Vignettes Correct)

Cook's prose is deliberately thin. He sacrifices atmosphere
for pace. His settings are often sketched in two words
("a village," "swampy ground," "cold"). _The Black Company_
achieves mood through accumulation across hundreds of pages
rather than through any single passage.

The Iron Ledger does not have hundreds of pages of ramp-up
time. Each vignette stands alone. The vignettes therefore
add atmospheric density that Cook does not provide —
specific place names, weather that fills a sentence,
material textures (clay track, oilcloth, porridge scraped
from the pot with a knife blade) — while keeping Cook's
flat emotional register and logistical obsession.

## Part 2: What Joe Abercrombie Does

### The Close-Third Confessional

Abercrombie writes close third-person point of view. The
narrator sits inside the character's head and the prose
takes on the character's vocabulary, prejudices, rhythm,
and self-deception. When Glokta narrates, the sentences
are bitter and clipped. When Dogman narrates, the sentences
are plain and tinged with exhaustion. The voice of the
prose _is_ the character.

**1. Interior commentary that contradicts the exterior.**
A character says something reasonable while thinking
something ugly, or performs a brave act while internally
cataloguing why it is stupid. The gap between surface
and interior is where Abercrombie lives. His characters
are never what they appear.

**2. Physicality as psychology.** Mental states rendered
through the body. Confidence is how a man sits, where his
hand rests, how his weight shifts. Fear is a cold feeling
in the guts. Shame is in the way someone avoids eye
contact. Abercrombie almost never names an emotion. He
describes where the character feels it and what the body
does about it.

**3. The bathetic pivot.** A passage builds toward something
grand — a heroic moment, a noble speech — and then the
character undercuts it. The sword that was "father's blade"
turns out to have a notch from when it was used to chop
firewood. The noble stand was taken because the character
was too tired to run. This is not parody. It is a claim
about how heroism actually feels to the people performing
it.

**4. Violence rendered in claustrophobic detail.** Fights
from inside the fighter's body. The weight of a swing,
the breath, where the ground is slippery. Attacks as bodily
sensation, not tactical summary. Fights are ugly, confusing,
exhausting, and they end in a way that makes the winner
feel sick. Combat prose gets shorter, more fragmentary.
Sentences break down as the character breaks down.

**5. Dark humor that is character-sourced.** Glokta cracks
bitter jokes about his own mutilated body. Logen makes dry
observations about the stupidity of combat while covered
in blood. The humor is defense mechanism, characterization,
and atmospheric tool simultaneously. Never cute. Never a
wink at the reader.

### Abercrombie's Sentence Architecture

Medium-length by default (14–22 words) but wide variation.
Very short sentences (2–6 words) as percussive beats:
"He was done." "That was that." "Not much of a plan."
Long sentences (30+ words) for sensory accumulation during
physical scenes.

Paragraphs run 3–6 sentences. The paragraph-closing short
sentence as tonal hammer: a long paragraph of building
tension lands on a flat, short closer. "So that was that."
"He'd had worse days. Not many, though."

Fragments at high-pressure moments. "Blood. Everywhere."
"The cold. The weight." Fragments signal cognition broken
to pure sensation.

## Part 3: The Composite Voice — Operational Rules

These synthesize Cook and Abercrombie into the Iron Ledger
voice. Norse setting, all-human cast, sorcerers as rare
and feared Norse casters (galdr, seidr, rune-work).

### POV and Narration

- Default to **company-level close-third omniscient** —
  the narrator sees the camp, follows the viewpoint
  character's thoughts when interiority is needed, but
  can pan across the column to observe others. Cook's
  annalist freedom with Abercrombie's close-third depth
  available on demand.
- Do not head-hop within a paragraph. Stay in one
  perspective until a paragraph break.
- Interior thoughts as free indirect speech, not italicized
  monologue. "She wondered whether the bridge would hold"
  — not _Will the bridge hold?_

### Sentence Targets

- **Default sentence:** 10–18 words. Plain subject-verb-
  object. The Cook baseline.
- **Short percussive:** 2–7 words. Closers, fragments,
  punchlines, tonal pivots. Abercrombie's hammer.
- **Long accumulative:** 25–40 words. Lists of objects,
  physical description during movement or weather, sensory
  detail during violence. Length from stacked concrete
  nouns, never from nested clauses or psychology.
- **Ratio target:** 60% default, 25% short, 15% long. More
  than two long in sequence = pacing problem. More than
  three short in sequence without a default = telegram.

### Paragraph Targets

- **Default paragraph:** 3–5 sentences. One job per
  paragraph.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs:** Percussive emphasis,
  scene endings, tonal shifts. Maximum one per scene.
- **Long paragraphs (6–8+):** Only for logistics
  accumulation — the morning count, kit distribution,
  march description.

### Dialogue Mechanics

- No dialogue tags beyond "said." No "growled," "muttered,"
  "remarked," "intoned." Cook and Abercrombie use "said"
  almost exclusively. The verb is invisible.
- Beat tags (physical action) over attribution where
  possible. "Voss looked at him. 'That's a problem.'"
- Dialogue is terse. Characters do not explain themselves.
  Questions are direct. Answers shorter than questions.
- **No speeches.** When someone speaks more than two
  sentences, they are giving orders or negotiating — not
  conversing. Rarity purchases power.

### Emotion Budget

- **One emotional beat per scene or vignette.** One moment
  of acknowledged interior state. The rest is logistics,
  dialogue, and physical action.
- The beat must be rendered through **body, action, or
  cost** — never through named emotions.
- Acceptable: a repeated gesture, a calculated cost, a
  physical sensation, a behavioral shift.
- Banned: "She felt sad." "Grief washed over him." "A sense
  of unease settled over the camp." The narrator does not
  feel. The narrator observes.

### Time and Pacing

- Scenes are **moments, not setpieces.** Begin with the
  situation in progress. End when the transaction closes.
  No setup paragraphs. No epilogues.
- Time through logistics: "They made nine miles that day."
  "Every seventh day." Time measured in miles, days, meals,
  quarter-days — not abstract duration.
- **No flashbacks.** Backstory as referenced consequences
  in the present. The past is mentioned, never visited.

### World-Building Method

- Names and specifics over generics. "Frostfjord Hollow,"
  not "a town." Named places and named contracts create the
  sensation of real operational history.
- **Geography is practical.** Places described by travel
  time, what they contain, what they lack.
- **Culture shown through transaction.** The elder's
  economic calculation, the village's method of quartering,
  how people stand when armed strangers arrive.

### The Abercrombie Pivot (Use Sparingly)

The bathetic undercut — the moment where something grand
is revealed as petty or practical.

Good: "Voss scraped the last of the porridge from the pot
with his knife and ate it off the blade. There would be
nothing else until evening."

Good: "The oath was not magic. It was Voss's blood on the
ground and the silence of ten men."

If every scene undercuts its gravity, the voice becomes
cynical instead of honest. Use in about one third of
scene closings. Two thirds end on weight without
undercutting.

### The Cook Catalogue (Use Freely)

Lists of concrete objects are the primary atmospheric tool.
Gear, terrain, food, physical description. Nearly impossible
to overuse within the style constraints.

Only rule: every item must be a concrete noun the reader
can picture. No abstractions in catalogues. No "a sense of
weariness" among the objects. Just the objects.

### Violence

Violence is aftermath, not performance.

- "He took a blow that broke him across the middle" —
  result, not process.
- "Kell's cut ran from elbow to wrist, shallow but long,
  crusted with dried blood and barrow dirt" — forensic
  observation.
- The pillaged village: "Three dead — two men and a woman,
  cut clean, which meant swords" — deduction from the wound,
  not description of the act.

Show the wound. Show the cost. Do not choreograph the swing.

### Norse Sorcery

Sorcerers (galdr-workers, seidr-speakers, rune-carvers) are
rare, feared, and expensive. They are not wizards in robes.
They are the person everyone avoids at the fire. Their power
is real but poorly understood, even by them.

- Magic is **physical**: rune-stones hum, blood seeps from
  carved marks, the air turns wrong near a working.
- Magic is **costly**: a seidr-speaker bleeds from the nose
  after a divination. A galdr-worker loses sleep for days.
  The price is always bodily.
- Magic is **witnessed through consequence**: the men do not
  see the spell. They see what was broken, healed, or changed
  after. The POV character experiences magic the way a
  layperson does — terror, awe, and the practical question
  of what it means for the column.
- **No spell descriptions.** Describe the approach, the
  physical cost, and the aftermath. The working itself is
  elided. What the reader imagines is worse than what you
  could write.
- Sorcerers negotiate their terms like mercenaries. Their
  dialogue is a pitch: capabilities, price, conditions.

## Part 4: The Ten Core Techniques

### Technique 1: Logistical Baseline, Emotional Exception

The dominant mode is logistical. Food, coin, distance,
headcount, pay schedule, route, shelter. Emotion arrives
as exception to the logistical baseline — a moment where
behavior deviates from the practical pattern.

Build every scene on a practical spine. The feeling lives
in what deviates from the spine, not in sentences about
feeling.

### Technique 2: Character Through Repeated Gesture

Every named character has 2–3 concrete repeated behaviors.
Physical habits that appear across multiple scenes and
communicate inner life without psychologizing it.

Do not explain what the behaviors mean. Let them recur.
The reader builds the psychology from the pattern.

### Technique 3: The Loaded Silence

Silence as structural device. Not atmosphere but the
pointed, witnessed fact that no one spoke.

Name the silence, then name what is happening inside it.
"The kind of silence where ten men recalculate everything
they know." Do not break it. Do not explain what anyone
concluded.

### Technique 4: The Unbearable Specific Detail

Anchor powerful moments in a single hyper-specific detail
that is too precise to be decorative.

Every scene must contain at least one detail so physically
specific that the reader can reconstruct the moment that
created it. If the detail could be replaced with a generic
equivalent without losing information, it is not specific
enough.

### Technique 5: The Unsaid Judgment

Moral logic never stated as moral logic. Characters do
arithmetic, draw conclusions, act. The reader infers the
moral framework from calculations and choices.

Characters do not have moral epiphanies, do not articulate
ethical positions. They make choices that cost them
something. The cost tells the reader what the character
values. The morality is in the price paid.

### Technique 6: The Final-Line Hammer

End scenes on a short, flat declarative that drops the
emotional weight of everything preceding.

The line is shorter than what came before. It states a
physical fact, a cost, or a worldview without elaboration.
It does not explain. It lands.

### Technique 7: Dialogue as Power Negotiation

Every line of dialogue is a transaction. No one speaks to
share information or express feelings. They speak to
establish position, negotiate terms, test limits, close
a deal.

If a line could be removed without changing the balance
of power in the scene, remove it. Characters do not chat.
They negotiate.

### Technique 8: The Implied Backstory

Reference events before the narrative without explaining
them. Single concrete details — a consequence, a habit,
a place avoided — that give the world history.

The reader's imagination fills the event. What the reader
imagines is always worse than what you could describe.

### Technique 9: The Structural Absence

When a scene has an emotional center that would normally
be expressed through dialogue, reflection, or commentary
— do not provide it. Show the logistics surrounding the
emotion. Show the routines that continue. Show the space
where the emotion should be and leave it empty. The
emptiness communicates.

### Technique 10: The Material Catalogue

Lists of physical objects that serve as world-building
through accumulation. Pure Cook.

Objects as biography: "a copper ring, a scrap of letter
from someone whose handwriting nobody recognized, two
silver coins hidden in his boot lining, and a lock of
hair tied with leather cord."

When building atmosphere, weight, or emotional density
— list concrete objects. Do not describe the mood.
List what is in it. The mood emerges from the list.

## Part 5: Anti-Patterns — What Breaks the Voice

### 1. Psychologizing

"Voss felt the weight of leadership pressing down on his
shoulders." — Interiority without physical grounding. The
voice never names a feeling. Show a person doing math,
eating scraps, or staring at rain.

### 2. Heroic Framing

"Kell, the bravest of them all, shouldered the body
without a word." — The narrator has no opinion about who is
brave. The narrator describes behavior. The reader assigns
the adjective.

### 3. Symmetrical Moral Commentary

"She had done terrible things, but in a world like this,
perhaps there were no clean choices." — Modern novelistic
commentary. The narrator does not philosophize.

### 4. Atmospheric Fog

"A heavy silence settled over the camp as darkness crept
through the trees." — Generic mood. Anchor silence in what
it contains (men calculating). Anchor darkness in what it
costs (watch shifts, cold). Mood without logistics is fog.

### 5. Clever Narrator

"The irony, of course, was that Ketil's greatest theft was
of the company's trust." — The narrator is not clever.
The narrator is the ledger. If there is irony, the reader
finds it.

### 6. Explaining the Subtext

"She had paid him what the contract owed. She had simply
chosen the currency. This was, in some way, her truest form
of mercy." — The first two sentences work. The third
explains the subtext. Never explain the subtext. If it
needs explaining, the surface is wrong.

### 7. Dialogue That Expresses Feelings

"I'm tired of this, Gest. Tired of the blood and the
counting and the mud." — Nobody says how they feel. They
say what they want, what they expect, what they will do.

### 8. The Noble Suffering Beat

"She carried the burden so her men would not have to." —
Heroic framing. Show economic calculations that happen to
have moral weight. She is not noble. She is competent,
and competence sometimes looks like kindness.

## Part 6: Hard Rules

Non-negotiable. Violating any one disqualifies a draft.

1. **No mood without object.** Every atmospheric sentence
   contains a physical noun — body part, material, tool,
   terrain, weather. "Darkness gathered" banned.
   "The cold crept through the seams of his mail" allowed.

2. **No paragraph without a job.** Name the job in one
   word: define, describe, distinguish, warn, demonstrate,
   bridge. If you cannot name the job, do not write the
   paragraph.

3. **No AI tells.** Final pass for: em-dash clusters,
   rule-of-three, copula avoidance, elegant variation,
   negative parallelisms, hedging stacks, sycophantic
   qualifiers, filler phrases, generic positive endings.
   Split-sentence negative ("It is not X. It is Y.") is
   a hard AI tell. Collapse or rewrite every time.

4. **No synonym cycling.** If the band calls it silver,
   call it silver. Repetition is cleaner than variation
   when the repeated word is precise.

5. **No therapy, no corporate, no blog.** The prose does
   not say "meaningful," "impactful," "engage with,"
   "it's worth noting," "basically," "in other words,"
   "this means that," or "the key takeaway."

6. **End on weight.** Sentences land on concrete nouns,
   bodily consequences, hard verbs, or material facts.
   Trailing qualifiers and abstract summaries are the most
   reliable sign of generated text.

7. **Earn every adjective.** One strong noun beats two
   modified ones. "The blade" beats "the sharp, gleaming
   blade."

## Part 7: Simulation-to-Prose Integration

The Iron Ledger novel is backed by a simulation engine
(Python scripts in `scripts/`, YAML state in `data/`).
The simulation generates outcomes. The prose renders those
outcomes as lived experience.

### The Iron Rule of Simulation Fiction

**The simulation decides what happens. The prose decides
how the characters experience it.**

Never override a simulation result for narrative
convenience. If the dice kill a Named Man, the Named Man
dies. If foraging fails, the band goes hungry. If morale
breaks, men desert. The simulation is the world's physics.
The writer's job is to make the physics feel like a story.

### Rendering Simulation Results

- **Weather:** Do not state the weather system output
  directly. Translate: if `weather.py` returns "blizzard,
  -15°C, visibility 20m," write: "The snow came sideways,
  hard as thrown gravel. Visibility dropped to arm's length."

- **Foraging:** Results become character action. A failed
  forage roll is not "the foraging check failed." It is
  Petra returning at dusk with empty hands and a cut on
  her thumb from a snare that caught nothing.

- **Morale:** Morale shifts appear as behavior changes.
  Morale 4 is men who follow orders. Morale 2 is men who
  hesitate, who look at each other before they move, who
  eat in silence.

- **Combat:** Combat results rendered as aftermath. The
  simulation determines who lives, who dies, who takes
  wounds. The prose shows the cost — the silence after,
  the counting of the living, the division of the dead
  man's kit.

- **Treasury:** Financial state appears through logistics.
  Gest's entries in the ledger, the quality of meals, the
  captain's decisions about what can be afforded.

- **Hidden events:** Chinese-encoded secrets trigger without
  explanation. The prose shows consequences. The reader
  (and the viewpoint character) experiences the mystery.

### Chapter-Level Simulation Planning

Before writing a chapter:

1. Run the simulation for the chapter's timeframe
2. Record all outcomes: weather, food, morale, encounters,
   combat results, treasury changes
3. Identify which outcomes create the most narrative pressure
4. Structure the chapter around those pressure points
5. Let the simulation results that don't create pressure
   appear as background logistics — the daily grind that
   Cook uses as his baseline

## Part 8: Diagnostic Checklist

Before finalizing any prose:

- [ ] Does the scene have a practical spine (food, coin,
      distance, contract, logistics)?
- [ ] Is there exactly one emotional beat, rendered through
      body or cost?
- [ ] Does every named character appear through repeated
      gesture, not description?
- [ ] Does dialogue advance a negotiation or transaction?
- [ ] Does the final line land on a concrete fact, cost,
      or flat declarative?
- [ ] Are past events referenced through consequences,
      never described in flashback?
- [ ] At least one hyper-specific physical detail that
      implies a story the narrator does not tell?
- [ ] Silence used at least once as structural device?
- [ ] Scene ends without explaining its subtext?
- [ ] No adjective the narrator has not earned through
      prior physical grounding?
- [ ] No emotion named? No "felt" in a sentence?
- [ ] Violence rendered as aftermath, not choreography?
- [ ] Zero moral commentary from the narrator?
- [ ] Simulation results appear as lived experience,
      not as system output?
- [ ] Could this passage sit beside the existing
      vignettes without sounding imported?

## Vignette Examples

These are the voice standard. New prose must be able to
sit beside them without sounding imported.

### The Ford Count

She counted heads at the ford and came up one short. The
river had not taken him — the river was knee-deep and slow,
the kind of crossing that kills no one. She turned in the
saddle, scanning the column, and Gest met her eye from the
rear guard and held up five fingers. Five where there should
have been six.

Ketil was gone. He had been behind Kell at the last count.
Before that, behind Orm. Nobody could say with certainty,
because nobody watched Ketil the way Ketil watched everyone
else.

She did not send men back. The road ahead was paid for. The
road behind was not. Gest noted the name in the ledger with
a stub of charcoal and asked no questions aloud. He had been
doing this longer than questions helped.

The column moved on. One lighter. The arithmetic was the same
as always: feed the ones who stayed, pay the ones who fought,
bury the ones who fell, and forget the ones who walked off
in the dark. That was the work.

### The Ledger Stone

Every seventh day, Gest sat on a rock or a fallen trunk or
whatever was nearest to flat, and the men came past one at
a time.

The ritual never changed. Gest had the purse on his left
side and the ledger on his right and a small knife stuck in
the wood beside him, because once — years ago, before Voss's
time — a man had grabbed the purse and run. The knife was
not for the purse. It was for the hand.

Each man received his retainer in copper, counted it in front
of Gest, and walked away. Some counted slowly. Some did not
count at all, because they trusted Gest, which was a kind of
compliment the sergeant did not acknowledge.

Kell never counted. He put the coin in his belt without
looking at it and walked back to whatever he had been doing.
If you had asked him the exact amount, he could have named
it. He simply did not need to look.

### Orm's Hiring

The new man's name was Orm, and he had the look of someone
who had been eating regularly until about three weeks ago.
Good shoulders. Clean teeth, which was unusual. A scar across
the back of his left hand that said he had blocked a blade
once and learned from it.

"What can you do?" Voss asked.

"I can hold a line. I can dig. I can walk thirty miles if
someone feeds me at the end."

"Can you keep your mouth shut when the job isn't what you
were told?"

Orm looked at him. Then at Gest. Then at the eight men behind
them who were not looking at him, because nobody cared about
new men until they had survived something.

"I've done that before," he said.

Voss held out a coin. Half-silver, clipped at the edge — the
advance. He took it. That was the whole ceremony. No oath,
no contract, no handshake. He paid him, which meant he was
his, which meant that if he ran with the advance Voss would
put his name on a board somewhere and somebody down the road
would collect. He understood this without being told. The men
who needed it explained were the men who did not last.

Gest wrote him into the roster that evening. Common. No rank.
Shares last.

### The Door

The thing Kell did at the Ashfall barrow was not complicated.
The contract was going wrong — two men down, the kill ground
inside the ruin compromised, and the thing they had been hired
to clear between them and the exit. The air had turned wrong
an hour ago — hot and close, the way stone gets above a forge
after the bellows have run all day, and the walls wept
moisture that was not rain.

Kell was on the right side near a gap in the wall, and when
the thing came through he did not wait to be told. He heard
it before he saw it — a heavy dragging over flagstone, wet
underneath, and the heat hit his face before the shape did.
He put himself in the gap — not heroically, the way men
talked about it later. He stepped into it the way you step
into a queue, because nobody else was there and somebody
needed to be. He braced his shield and the impact drove him
back a foot on the stone floor. The shield held. Whatever
scraped across its face left a gouge he could fit two fingers
into afterwards. He held for long enough that Voss could
close the passage and Gest could turn the column into
something that functioned again.

He did not drive the thing back. He held long enough that
the men who could do those things had room to do them.

After, when it was done and two men were bleeding and Thorne
was kneeling beside the worst of them with his hands pressed
flat on the wound, Voss looked at Kell.

"That was right," he said. Two words. From Voss, it was
a speech.

That evening at the fire Orm sat down next to Kell instead
of across from him, which he had never done before. Within
a week, three men were calling him Kell the Door — not a
title, not a rank, but a story compressed to a single word.

Kell did not use it himself. He did not need to.

### The Sorcerer at the Ford

The woman at the ford had the look of someone who had not
eaten well in a long time and did not care. Thin through
the shoulders, sharp at the jaw, hands that moved like
someone used to working with things smaller than a sword.
She smelled of birch bark and something metallic, like old
blood or fresh lightning.

"I hear you need a caster," she said.

Voss looked at her the way he looked at a bridge before
crossing — testing what would hold. "What can you do?"

"Galdr. Wound-closing, enough to save a man who would
otherwise bleed out. Rune-reading at a distance of two
days. Ward-laying — nothing complex, but enough to make a
camp unwelcome to things that walk the barrows."

"What do you cost?"

"Full share. My own fire. No man touches my kit without
asking."

Gest wrote nothing down yet. He watched.

"If you're worth the share, you'll get it," Voss said.
"If not, you walk."

"I've walked before," she said. No smile. No pitch. She
stated facts the way Gest stated accounts.

She came with nothing but a leather satchel and the smell.
The first time someone bled in the field, she pressed her
palms flat on the wound and something happened that nobody
wanted to describe afterwards. Kell stopped bleeding. That
was the fact that mattered. How it happened stayed between
the galdr-worker and whatever she drew on, and the men
did not ask because asking was the beginning of knowing,
and knowing was the beginning of being afraid of the person
who shared their fire.

They called her Ash, after the tree. Not after anything
she burned. Though she did that too, once, and nobody
spoke about it.

### The Village Tribute

The village was smaller than Frostfjord. Nine houses, a
ditch, and a palisade that leaned in three places. The
elder was a woman with forearms like a smith's and a face
that suggested she had heard speeches before.

Voss did not give a speech.

"Your village needs a patrol," he said. "The contract at
Bleakwater is finished. We can stay through the season.
One meal per man per day, drawn from your stores. Shelter
in the barn. No looting. In exchange, we walk the road
and answer whatever comes from the north."

The elder looked at Gest. Then at Kell, who was leaning
on his spear twenty paces back and looking at nothing, the
way Kell looked at nothing when he wanted someone to think
about what he could be looking at.

"We cannot feed ten for a season," the elder said.

"Eight, then. I'll rotate. Eight mouths for eight weeks."

"Six."

"Eight, for ten weeks, and my foragers hunt your tree line
— none of it touches your stores."

The elder's jaw moved. She was calculating: not whether the
offer was fair, but whether refusing it was more expensive.
An unprotected village on that road had lost two goats the
previous season and a man the season before.

"Eight," the elder said. "But your men stay out of the
houses."

"Done."

Neither of them shook hands. Neither needed to.

### Haldr's Kit

Haldr's boots went to Lump, because Lump's were rotting and
Haldr's were the right size. His belt knife went to Orm, who
had been using a borrowed blade since he signed. His cloak
went to stores. His shield — a good one, banded iron, two
seasons of service on it — went to the company inventory,
where Gest marked it and did not assign it until the next man
was hired, because a dead man's shield on a new man's arm on
the same day was bad for morale in ways that did not show
immediately but showed eventually.

What Haldr had in personal effects: a copper ring, a scrap
of letter from someone whose handwriting nobody recognized,
two silver coins hidden in his boot lining, and a lock of
straw-fair hair tied with leather cord. These went into a
pouch. The pouch went to Voss.

If they passed through Vargheim in the spring, the pouch
would go to the sister Kell had mentioned. If they did not
pass through Vargheim, the pouch would stay in the strongbox
until it did not matter anymore, and eventually the ring
would be spent, the letter would rot, and the lock of hair
would be one more thing that outlived the person it was
meant for.

The space in the column where Haldr had marched filled in
within a day. The space around the fire where he had sat
took longer. Lump sat there on the fourth night and nobody
said anything about it, and after that it was Lump's spot,
and the dead man's spot belonged to the band the way
everything eventually did.

### The Blood Oath

Voss knelt down and drew his knife across his palm. Not
deep. Deep enough. The blood welled and he held the hand
over the bare earth and let it fall.

"I will leave nothing in that hole," he said. "No man of
mine stays behind. We go in, we come out, and whatever the
barrow has, we take. I am binding this with blood because
words do not hold in the dark, and the dark is where we are
going."

The men watched. Some gripped their weapons. Some looked
at the ground. None of them spoke, because speaking during
a blood-oath is the kind of thing that gets remembered in
ways that benefit no one.

The blood soaked into the earth. It did not steam. It did
not glow. It was blood on cold ground, red turning dark,
and the weight of the moment was not in what it meant but
in the fact that every man there had agreed, separately and
silently, that the oath was binding.

The oath was not magic. It was Voss's blood on the ground
and the silence of ten men who had decided, each separately
and all at once, that they were in.
