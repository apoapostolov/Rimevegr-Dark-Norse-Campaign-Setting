# Scene Transitions — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** How to write the transition itself -- the first and
last sentence of a scene-within-a-chapter when it is not the chapter
opening or closing. Chapter-structure.md covers chapter-level anatomy
(opening grounding, body, cost-accounting closing). This file covers
the seams between scenes inside a chapter body.

> **Cross-reference:** Chapter openings and closings are in
> `chapter-structure.md`. This file handles only mid-chapter
> scene boundaries.

---

## The Problem Transitions Solve

A chapter has 2-4 scenes. Between them, time passes, location
changes, or focus shifts. The reader needs to cross these gaps
without losing physical orientation or momentum. Bad transitions
kill pacing -- they either jar the reader out of the scene or
create dead space where attention drifts.

In the Iron Ledger, transitions carry an additional burden: every
scene operates at least one simulation system (weather, supply,
morale, money, injury, time, supernatural). The transition must
hand off from one system's dominance to the next without making
the handoff feel mechanical.

---

## Transition Types

### Type 1: Time Skip (Same Location)

Time passes. The band is still where it was. The world has changed
around them.

**Exit the previous scene** with a physical detail that will decay:

- A fire burning. Food cooking. A wound being dressed.
- Something that implies a process in motion.

**Enter the next scene** with the result of that process:

- The fire is embers. The food is eaten. The wound is bound and
  the man is sleeping.
- The result tells the reader how much time passed without stating
  a number.

**Rule:** Never state the time gap directly ("Three hours later").
Show what three hours did to the environment. If the rime has
crept further up the palisade, three hours passed. If the candle
has burned to a stub, an evening passed. If the watch changed, a
shift passed. The Rimevegr measures time in physical decay.

**Examples:**

Exit: _Gest scratched the last number into the ledger and set
the charcoal down. The candle was half-gone._

Enter: _The candle was a pool of tallow and a drowned wick. Gest
was still at the ledger, but the numbers had changed._

---

### Type 2: Location Change (Same Time Frame)

The POV character moves from one place to another. The scene
boundary is the journey -- but the journey is compressed.

**Exit the previous scene** with a departure action:

- Standing up. Picking up a weapon. Pulling on a cloak.
- The physical act of leaving, not the decision to leave.

**Enter the next scene** with arrival sensory data:

- What the new location feels like on the body. Temperature
  change. Light change. Sound change. Smell.
- The body registers the new location before the mind catalogs it.

**Rule:** Never narrate the walk unless the walk is the scene.
If Voss leaves the fire and arrives at the sentry post, the reader
does not need the thirty paces between them. Cut from departure to
arrival. The gap is the walk. The reader fills it.

**Exception:** If something changes during the walk -- a sound, a
figure in the dark, a shift in weather -- the walk becomes a
micro-scene and the location change is handled as a continuous
transition (Type 4 below).

**Examples:**

Exit: _Voss took his axe from beside the fire and stood. The
cold hit his knees first._

Enter: _The sentry post was a notch in the palisade where the
wind funneled through. Dagfinn was there, hunched into his cloak
like a wet bird._

---

### Type 3: Focus Shift (Same Time, Same Location)

The camera stays in the same place but shifts attention from one
activity or group to another. The POV character's gaze moves.

**Exit the previous scene** with the POV character's attention
releasing:

- A conversation ending. A task completed. A silence falling.
- The previous focus losing its pull on the character's attention.

**Enter the next scene** with a new sensory intrusion:

- A sound from across the camp. A figure approaching. A change
  in the fire's behavior. Something new entering the POV
  character's awareness.
- The new focus announces itself physically before the POV
  character decides to engage with it.

**Rule:** The shift must feel involuntary. The POV character does
not decide to pay attention to the new thing -- the new thing
demands attention. This maintains the sense that the world is
operating around the character, not for the character.

**Examples:**

Exit: _Snorri finished the story. Nobody laughed. The fire
popped once and settled._

Enter: _Somewhere past the firelight, a mule stamped and blew.
Dalla's voice, low and sharp, carried a word Voss did not catch._

---

### Type 4: Continuous (No Break)

The scene shifts without a break. Time flows. This is not a
transition in the structural sense -- it is a scene that evolves
into a different scene. The boundary exists but is invisible.

**When to use:** When the change is gradual and the reader should
feel the shift happening, not notice it happened. A camp scene
that becomes a confrontation. A march that becomes an ambush. A
negotiation that becomes a threat.

**Technique:** Change the dominant sensory channel. If the scene
was auditory (conversation), shift to visual (someone standing,
a hand on a weapon). If the scene was visual (the landscape),
shift to auditory (the sudden silence). The sensory shift signals
the tonal shift without a structural break.

**Rule:** Continuous transitions work only when the new scene
raises the stakes. If the new scene is lower-energy than the
previous one, use a hard break (Type 1, 2, or 3). Continuous
transitions that lose energy feel like the narrative is drifting.

## Hard Breaks vs Soft Breaks

### Hard Break

A blank line or section divider between scenes. The reader
registers a gap. Time, location, or both changed.

**When to use:**

- Time skip of more than a few minutes.
- Location change where the journey is not narrated.
- Tonal shift that needs a breath (combat aftermath to camp
  logistics, for example).
- When the previous scene ended on a strong beat that needs
  to land before the next scene starts.

**The blank line is a cost.** Every hard break loses a small
amount of momentum. Use them deliberately. A chapter with four
hard breaks feels fragmented. A chapter with zero hard breaks
feels relentless.

### Soft Break

No structural marker. The transition happens within the flow of
prose. A new paragraph shifts the focus, but the reader does not
register a "scene change" -- they register a continuation.

**When to use:**

- Focus shifts within the same location and time.
- Continuous transitions where the shift is gradual.
- When maintaining momentum matters more than clarity.

## Transition Anchors

Every transition needs an anchor -- a concrete physical detail
that bridges the gap. The anchor appears on both sides of the
transition, changed by the passage of time or space.

### Anchor Types

| Anchor Type    | Exit Version                      | Entry Version                       |
| -------------- | --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Fire           | Burning, crackling, warming       | Embers, cold ash, rebuilt           |
| Weather        | Current state                     | Changed state (worse, usually)      |
| Sound          | Present (conversation, wind, axe) | Absent or replaced                  |
| Body state     | Current discomfort                | New or worsened discomfort          |
| Light          | Current quality                   | Changed quality (darker, usually)   |
| Object in hand | Held, used, active                | Put down, changed, or in other hand |
| Another person | Present and engaged               | Gone, asleep, or changed posture    |

**Rule:** The anchor must be physical. No emotional anchors
("His worry deepened" does not bridge a scene gap). No abstract
anchors ("The situation worsened" is not a transition). The anchor
is something the body can feel, see, hear, or smell.

## Transition Pacing Rules

1. **Exit short, enter shorter.** The exit sentence of a scene
   can afford one physical detail. The entry sentence of the next
   scene gives one sensory hit. Longer transitions slow the
   chapter. The reader does not need help crossing the gap -- they
   need permission.

2. **Never explain the gap.** Do not tell the reader what happened
   between scenes unless what happened is the point. "While Voss
   slept, the snow deepened" is acceptable (the snow matters).
   "While Voss slept, Gest counted the remaining provisions" is
   only acceptable if the count is shown in the next scene.

3. **Match energy across the gap.** A high-energy scene followed
   by a hard break followed by a low-energy scene creates
   deliberate contrast (useful for aftermath). A high-energy scene
   followed by a soft transition to another high-energy scene
   creates sustained tension (useful for escalation). Know which
   effect the chapter needs.

4. **The first scene sets the chapter's baseline.** Every
   subsequent transition either maintains, raises, or deliberately
   drops from that baseline. Random energy fluctuation across
   scenes reads as structural confusion.

5. **End scenes on cost, begin scenes on sensation.** The exit
   line registers what the scene spent. The entry line registers
   what the new scene feels like. Cost, then sensation. Ledger,
   then body.

## Common Transition Failures

| Failure                | Symptom                                                 | Fix                                          |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Floating transition    | Reader loses spatial orientation after the break        | Add a physical anchor on entry               |
| Narrator intrusion     | "Meanwhile" or "Later that evening" stated explicitly   | Show the time through environmental change   |
| Energy cliff           | High-energy scene drops to dead calm with no bridge     | Add a decompression beat before the break    |
| Redundant re-grounding | Entry repeats information from the previous scene's end | Cut the first sentence of the new scene      |
| Invisible scene change | Reader does not realize a new scene started             | Add a hard break or a stronger sensory shift |
