# Anti-AI Detection Library

A catalogue of AI writing tells, how to find them, and how to
kill them. Use after every chapter draft. If a passage triggers
any item below, it fails the quality gate and must be rewritten.

---

## Category 1: Structural Tells

### 1.1 Paragraph Sameness

**The tell:** Every paragraph follows the same shape — topic
sentence, two elaborations, softened conclusion. Swap any two
paragraphs and the reader would not notice.

**Detection:** Read five consecutive paragraphs aloud. If they
all _feel_ the same length, start the same way (noun-verb), and
end with a summary beat, the structure is AI-flattened.

**Example of failure:**

> The wind cut through the camp with brutal efficiency. Voss
> pulled his cloak tighter, knowing the fire would not last the
> night. The cold was a reminder — in the Rimevegr, warmth was
> borrowed, never owned.

Every sentence is 10-15 words. All three follow
statement-elaboration-reflection. The third sentence is a
thematic summary that nobody asked for.

**Fix:**

> Wind found the gaps in the tent. Voss's cloak was wool and it
> was not enough. He fed the fire a boot — Orm's spare, the dead
> man's. It burned slow and smelled of feet.

Vary the sentence length (5, 10, 14, 8). Open with the physical
thing, not the character. End on a concrete detail, not a theme.
The fourth sentence earns its existence by adding information.

### 1.2 Three-Beat Ritual

**The tell:** Everything arrives in threes. Three adjectives.
Three items in a list. Three parallel clauses. Three beats before
the turn. AI loves the rule of three because it is the safest
rhythmic structure.

**Detection:** Ctrl+F for commas separating three parallel items.
If the pattern appears more than twice per page, it is padding.

**Example of failure:**

> The hall was dark, cold, and unwelcoming.

**Fix:**

> The hall was dark. Cold came up through the stone floor.

Two beats. The second earns its place by being physical. The
adjective "unwelcoming" was summary — cut it.

### 1.3 Mirror Openings

**The tell:** Consecutive paragraphs begin with the same
syntactic pattern. "He looked... He saw... He felt..." or
"The camp... The fire... The wind..."

**Detection:** List the first three words of each paragraph on a
page. If more than two share a pattern, restructure.

**Fix:** Rotate entry points. Start with action, then
environment, then dialogue, then a body sensation, then a logistic
detail. Never repeat the same opening gambit within a page.

### 1.4 Symmetric Dialogue Blocks

**The tell:** Character A speaks 2-3 sentences. Character B
responds with 2-3 sentences. A replies with 2-3 sentences. The
turns are mechanically equal length. No one interrupts. No one
trails off. No one says something and then walks away before the
other responds.

**Detection:** Count words per dialogue turn. If the variance is
less than 30%, the dialogue is synthetic.

**Fix:** Unbalance dialogue. One character speaks a full
paragraph; the other grunts. Someone begins to answer and is
cut off by an action beat. Someone says something that gets no
response at all, and that silence is the answer.

## Category 2: Lexical Tells

### 2.1 Copula Avoidance

**The tell:** The AI avoids "is" and "was" by substituting
fancier constructions. "The fire served as their only protection"
instead of "the fire was all they had." "The rune-stone acted as
a warning" instead of "the rune-stone was a warning."

**Detection:** Search for: "serves as," "acts as," "functions
as," "proves to be," "stands as," "represents." These are
AI-speak for "is."

**Fix:** Use "is" and "was" freely. Cook uses them constantly.
Abercrombie uses them. Plain copulas are invisible to the reader.
Substitutes are not.

### 2.2 Elegant Variation

**The tell:** The AI cycles through synonyms to avoid repeating
a word. The sword becomes "the blade," then "the weapon," then
"the steel," then "the edge." A character is named, then called
"the mercenary," then "the older man," then "the captain."

**Detection:** Highlight every reference to the same object or
person in a paragraph. If three different labels appear in five
sentences, the AI is synonym-cycling.

**Fix:** Repeat the word. Cook repeats "sword" four times in a
paragraph and the reader does not notice because the reader is
not an English teacher — the reader is watching a fight.

### 2.3 Hedging Stacks

**The tell:** "Perhaps," "it seemed," "almost as if,"
"something like," "what might have been." AI hedges because it
does not want to commit. Cook commits. Abercrombie commits.

**Detection:** Ctrl+F: "perhaps," "seemed," "almost,"
"something," "might have," "as if," "as though." More than one
per page is suspicious. More than two is synthetic.

**Fix:** State it or do not state it. "The fire was dying" not
"the fire seemed to be dying." "Kell was afraid" not "something
almost like fear crossed Kell's face."

### 2.4 Inflated Verbs

**The tell:** Characters do not "look" — they "survey," "assess,"
"regard," "contemplate." They do not "walk" — they "traverse,"
"navigate." They do not "say" — they "declare," "articulate,"
"intone."

**Detection:** Read a page and circle every verb that would sound
wrong in a bar conversation. Those are inflated.

**Fix:** Use simple verbs. Look. Walk. Say. Sit. Stand. Eat.
The prose should sound like a veteran telling a story to another
veteran. Plain words do hard work.

### 2.5 AI Vocabulary

**The tell:** Words that AI uses far more often than humans:
"tapestry" (of something abstract), "landscape" (of emotions),
"testament" (to something), "underscored," "nuanced,"
"multifaceted," "delve," "intricate," "resonated," "compelling,"
"stark" (as positive adjective), "palpable," "tangible"
(of abstract things), "visceral," "deftly," "pivotal,"
"profound."

**Detection:** If the word sounds like a book review rather than
a novel, cut it.

**Fix:** Replace with the specific physical thing the word was
trying to dress up. "A palpable sense of dread" becomes "the
silence that came before the barrow opened."

## Category 3: Rhythmic Tells

### 3.1 Em-Dash Overuse

**The tell:** AI loves em-dashes. It uses them for asides,
interruptions, elaborations, and dramatic pauses. Three per page
is a maximum. More than that is a tic.

**Detection:** Ctrl+F for "—". Count per page. Also check for
patterns: if every dialogue line has an em-dash interruption,
the rhythm is mechanical.

**Fix:** Replace most em-dashes with periods. Use commas for
genuine subordinate clauses. Reserve the em-dash for one
powerful interruption per page.

### 3.2 Sentence-Length Monotony

**The tell:** AI defaults to 12-18 word sentences. Real prose
varies: 4 words. 22 words. 6 words. The variation creates
rhythm. AI's monotone sentence length creates sleep.

**Detection:** Count words in ten consecutive sentences. If the
standard deviation is less than 4, the rhythm is flat.

**Fix:** Deliberately shatter the pattern. After a 20-word
sentence, write a 4-word sentence. The short sentence hits like
a fist because it breaks the established rhythm.

### 3.3 Conjunction Addiction

**The tell:** AI starts sentences with "And," "But," "Yet,"
"Still," "However," "Moreover," "Furthermore." These are
transition crutches. Real prose often omits transitions entirely
and lets the reader make the connection.

**Detection:** Highlight sentence-initial conjunctions. More than
three per page is a dependency.

**Fix:** Delete the conjunction. If the sentence still makes
sense, the conjunction was unnecessary. If it does not, the
problem is the sentence structure, not the missing conjunction.

## Category 4: Content Tells

### 4.1 Generic Positivity

**The tell:** AI wants to resolve discomfort. Dark scenes get a
note of hope at the end. Loss is followed by a lesson. Failure
produces growth. The Rimevegr does not work this way.

**Detection:** Check the last sentence of every dark passage. If
it contains any hint of silver lining, comfort, or meaning, the
AI has softened the world.

**Fix:** End dark passages on the cost. The payment. The thing
that is now gone and will not come back. No lessons. No growth.
The character survives and that is all the good news there is.

### 4.2 False Depth

**The tell:** "In that moment, he understood..." or "It was then
that she realized..." or "The truth was simpler than that." The
AI manufactures epiphanies because it thinks fiction requires
insight. Cook never provides insight. Things happen. Characters
note them. Meaning is the reader's problem.

**Detection:** Search for: "realized," "understood," "knew in
that moment," "the truth," "what it meant."

**Fix:** Delete the epiphany. Show the physical evidence.
Instead of "he realized the barrow was not empty," write "the
sound came again. From below."

### 4.3 Therapeutic Framing

**The tell:** Characters process trauma in healthy ways. They
reflect, they name their feelings, they reach moments of
acceptance. This is therapy, not the Rimevegr. Voss does not
process. Voss continues.

**Detection:** Any passage where a character introspects on their
emotional state using modern psychological language — "he needed
to come to terms with," "she had been carrying," "he allowed
himself to feel."

**Fix:** Replace introspection with action. A man who is grieving
does not reflect on grief — he sharpens his axe until the edge is
too thin and then he goes out in the rain and stands there because
the cold makes the inside noise stop.

### 4.4 Significance Inflation

**The tell:** Everything is important. The morning meal is
"meaningful." A walk is "charged." A glance is "loaded with
unspoken tension." AI escalates significance because it does not
trust flat moments.

**Detection:** Circle every adjective modifier that signals
importance: "meaningful," "significant," "charged," "weighted,"
"heavy," "loaded." If a meal is not plot-relevant, it is just
porridge.

**Fix:** Let flat moments be flat. Voss ate. The porridge was
thin. Kell did not eat. That is a scene. The last detail does
more narrative work than any amount of significance-inflation
because the reader asks _why_ Kell did not eat.

### 4.5 Negative Parallelism

**The tell:** "Not just X, but Y." "Not merely a soldier, but a
leader." "Not only cold, but a cold that seeped into the bones."
AI loves this rhetorical structure because it creates the
appearance of complexity without adding substance.

**Detection:** Ctrl+F: "not just," "not merely," "not only,"
"more than," "beyond."

**Fix:** State the stronger thing directly. "A cold that went
through wool." The weaker thing did not need to be negated
first.

### 4.6 Split-Sentence Negative

**The tell:** "It was not X. It was Y." Two sentences where the
first negates and the second asserts. "It was not fear. It was
something older." "The sound was not wind. It was breath." AI
uses this structure to create false profundity — the negation
makes the assertion feel deeper than it is.

**Detection:** Ctrl+F sentence pairs starting with "It was not"
or "This was not" or "He did not" followed immediately by
"It was" or "This was" or "He."

**Fix:** State the thing directly. "Something older moved in
his chest." The reader does not need to be told what it is
not. If the distinction matters, earn it through context, not
through the negation-assertion pair.

## Category 5: Dialogue Tells

### 5.1 Thesis Statement Dialogue

**The tell:** Characters say their theme out loud. "This is what
we've become," or "That's the price of command." Real people do
not summarize their narrative arc in conversation. They say
"Pass the salt" and the way they say it tells you everything.

**Detection:** Read each dialogue line and ask: "Would someone
actually say this aloud to another person?" If the answer is
no — if the line is really for the reader — it is thesis
statement dialogue.

**Fix:** Cut the line entirely or replace with the oblique thing
the character would actually say. Instead of "This is what
command costs," Voss says "Kill the fire. We march at dark."
The cost is in the order, not the explanation.

### 5.2 Equal Eloquence

**The tell:** Every character speaks with the same level of
articulation. Complete sentences. Good grammar. Varied vocabulary.
Real people do not sound alike. Kell speaks in half-sentences.
Gest speaks in calculations. Snorri barely speaks at all.

**Detection:** Cover the dialogue attribution and read three
different characters' lines. If you cannot tell who is speaking
from the words alone, the voices have collapsed.

**Fix:** Give each character a speech budget. Kell gets 5 words.
Gest gets 15. Voss gets 10 but half of them are orders. The
budget shapes the voice more than vocabulary choices.

### 5.3 Wrap-Up Lines

**The tell:** A conversation ends with a line that summarizes
what was discussed or signals the emotional conclusion. "I
suppose we both know what that means." "And that's all there
is to say." Real conversations end mid-thought, end with
someone walking away, end with silence.

**Detection:** Read the last line of every dialogue exchange.
If it sounds like a chapter ending, it is artificial closure.

**Fix:** Cut the last line. End on the penultimate exchange.
Or end on an action: "Gest stood and went to check the horses.
The conversation was over."

## Self-Audit Checklist

Run after every chapter draft. Failure on any item requires
targeted rewrite.
