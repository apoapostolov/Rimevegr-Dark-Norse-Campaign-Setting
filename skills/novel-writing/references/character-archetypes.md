# Character Archetypes — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** Reusable character templates drawn from mercenary and
military campaign fiction — Cook's Black Company, Abercrombie's
First Law, historical free companies, and the medieval Norse
warrior-band tradition. Each archetype defines a behavioral
pattern and a structural role within the band's economy.

Use when creating new characters, assigning arc trajectories, or
checking that the band's composition creates sufficient narrative
friction.

---

## Archetype Design Rules

1. **An archetype is a job, not a personality.** The Ledger-Keeper
   keeps the ledger. What makes him a character is the specific
   friction between the job and the person doing it.

2. **Every archetype has a structural role in the band's economy.**
   Captain, sergeant, fighter, specialist, dead weight. Remove the
   archetype and something breaks. The break is the proof the
   archetype matters.

3. **Archetypes create friction in pairs.** A single archetype is
   a role. Two archetypes in proximity are a story. The pairing
   table below maps which combinations generate narrative pressure.

4. **Archetypes are not exclusive.** A character can hold two — the
   Quiet Blade who is also the Political Animal. The tension between
   the two archetypes is itself a source of story.

## The Archetypes

### 1. The Captain Who Holds by Competence

**Source:** Cook's Lady, Abercrombie's Dogman as reluctant leader.
Historical analog: condottieri captains who led by contract
performance, not birthright.

**Behavioral signature:** Commands through demonstrated ability,
not charisma or fear. Speaks rarely. Means every word. His
authority is real but fragile — one bad call in front of the men
and the ledger starts working against him. He does not explain
decisions. The men follow because the alternative is worse.

**Dialogue pattern:** Declarative. Short. Commands without
softeners. "We march." "Keep it clean." "That's a problem."
Never asks for consensus. Never explains the strategy behind an
order. When he speaks more than two sentences, it is a
negotiation with an employer, not a conversation with his men.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Top. But unlike a tyrant, his
position is conditional. The band's tolerance is a continuous
calculation: Is this captain better than the next one? The
moment the answer changes, the captain knows before anyone tells
him — from the silences, the eye contact, the micro-delays in
response.

**Typical arc:** The Loyalty Test. What does the captain sacrifice
for the band versus what does the band cost him?

**Current holder:** Voss

### 2. The Ledger-Keeper

**Source:** Cook's Croaker (the annalist who records everything),
real-world company clerks. The person who turns human events into
numbers and treats the numbers as more honest than the events.

**Behavioral signature:** Methodical. Precise. Views the world
through arithmetic. People are entries. Events are transactions.
The ledger is the band's true law — more reliable than oaths,
more honest than speeches. The Ledger-Keeper's authority comes
from being the person who knows, not the person who decides.

**Dialogue pattern:** Numbers as speech. "Down to nine. Retainer
savings: twelve copper. Food loss: half a day." Reports facts,
not opinions. When he offers advice, it is framed as arithmetic:
"Three weeks at current burn. After that we're choosing between
paying and feeding."

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Second to the captain, but in a
different dimension. The captain commands. The Ledger-Keeper
constrains. His power is the veto of arithmetic — the captain
cannot order what the numbers cannot support.

**Typical arc:** The Erosion. The numbers get harder. The man
behind the numbers gets thinner. The work does not decrease. The
capacity does.

**Current holder:** Gest

### 3. The Quiet Blade

**Source:** Abercrombie's Logen (the practical killer), Cook's
Raven (the silent professional). The fighter who is very good at
violence and very quiet about it.

**Behavioral signature:** Economy of everything. Language, motion,
expression. Communicates through positioning — where he sits,
which exit he faces, who he stands next to. Violence is
mechanical, not emotional. He does not boast. He does not
threaten. He does what the situation requires and then he stops.

**Dialogue pattern:** Minimal. When he speaks, the words carry
more weight because of the silence that surrounds them. "That
was right" from the Quiet Blade is worth more than a speech from
anyone else. Often replaces speech with gesture — a look, a
shift of weight, the sharpening of a blade that does not need
sharpening.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** The captain's best tool. Positioned
where the danger is. The relationship is mutual respect expressed
through placement, not words. If the Quiet Blade starts speaking
more, something is wrong.

**Typical arc:** The Competence Trap. Reliability leads to
overuse. Overuse leads to breaking.

**Current holder:** Kell

### 4. The New Blood

**Source:** The ubiquitous recruit figure in military fiction.
The person who has not yet been shaped by the band's culture and
therefore reveals it by contrast. The audience's entry point.

**Behavioral signature:** Watches everything. Learns by
observation, not instruction. Competent at basic tasks but
untested in the band's specific demands. The New Blood's primary
quality is potential — the narrative has not decided what this
person becomes. They could be the next Quiet Blade or the next
deserter.

**Dialogue pattern:** Short, practical answers. "I can hold a
line. I can dig. I can walk thirty miles." Not self-promotional.
Not self-deprecating. Factual. The New Blood does not yet have
a voice — the voice develops as the band shapes them.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Bottom or near-bottom. Tolerated
until proven. The shift from tolerated to accepted is the same
mechanism as the Grudging Rise, but for the New Blood it may
happen faster or not at all.

**Typical arc:** Fork — Grudging Rise (earns place) or Quiet
Betrayal (was always leaving).

**Current holders:** Orm (competent New Blood, fork undecided),
Dagfinn (raw New Blood, just signed), Lump (non-combatant New
Blood — carries packs, cannot fight, earns place through labor
not steel; Grudging Rise arc; see `dialogue-patterns.md` for
voice profile and `character-arcs.md` for arc assignment)

### 5. The Old Campaigner

**Source:** Cook's entire supporting cast of veterans. The
character who has seen too many captains to be impressed by
another and whose survival is itself a form of expertise.

**Behavioral signature:** Talks when the ale loosens him. Stories
that begin with specifics and end without conclusions. Offers
advice exactly once — if ignored, does not repeat. His knowledge
is experiential and practical: not "courage wins battles" but
"be useful, not brave." The body is degrading. The knees are
going. The knowledge appreciates while the capacity depreciates.

**Dialogue pattern:** Fireside mode — activated by ale, deactivated
by sobriety. Stories told in counting idiom: "Six captains. Three
buried. Two deserted. One killed myself." Numbers as biography.
Advice given as plain fact, not wisdom. "Find the one thing and
do it every day."

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Lateral. Not competing. Watching.
The Old Campaigner is the band's institutional memory — the
person who remembers how things were done before. His value is
historical, not operational.

**Typical arc:** The Erosion (physical version — the body gives
out while the knowledge remains).

**Current holder:** Snorri

### 6. The Caster Tolerated Not Trusted

**Source:** Cook's One-Eye and Goblin (sorcerers who are part of
the company but never fully of it). The specialist whose ability
is necessary and whose nature is unsettling.

**Behavioral signature:** Operates on different terms from the
rest of the band. Full share, own fire, own rules. The tolerance
is conditional — useful is tolerated, useless is expelled, too
powerful is feared. The caster's social position is a continuous
negotiation: enough utility to justify the unease, never enough
power to threaten the hierarchy.

**Dialogue pattern:** States capabilities and price the way a
contractor bids a job. "Galdr. Wound-closing. Rune-reading.
Ward-laying." No sales pitch. No mystification. The caster is
a professional, not a mystic. When the caster speaks about their
craft, the language is practical. When the craft itself
manifests, the prose does not describe it — only the cost and
the aftermath.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Parallel. Not inside the normal
chain of command. The caster answers to the captain by contract,
not by rank. This creates structural tension — the caster can
refuse. The captain cannot compel. The negotiation is ongoing.

**Typical arc:** The question of whether utility can become
belonging when the utility is something the band fears.

**Current holders:** Ash (galdr-worker), Thorne (rune-reader —
a partial holder; Thorne is more integrated but still orbits
the band's edge)

### 7. The Deserter

**Source:** Historical military reality. The person who is
calculating the exit while performing the job. Not a traitor —
a rational actor whose personal arithmetic has diverged from
the group's.

**Behavioral signature:** Professional competence. Nothing wrong
with the work. The tell is in the margins: slightly lighter pack,
coin hidden in the boot lining, a route suggestion that serves
no obvious purpose. The Deserter eats alone not because they are
antisocial but because eating alone prevents monitoring.

**Dialogue pattern:** Terse. Factual. "That won't hold." "She's
lying." Observations without investment. The Deserter does not
argue for courses of action because they do not plan to be
present for the consequences.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Outwardly compliant. The Deserter
follows orders because following orders is cheaper than conflict.
The compliance is genuine — until it is not, and by then the
Deserter is gone.

**Typical arc:** The Quiet Betrayal. The departure is small,
practical, and quietly devastating.

**Current holder:** Ubbe (active), Ketil (departed — Vignette 22)

### 8. The Tyrant

**Source:** Abercrombie's Bethod, Cook's various warlords.
Historical analog: mercenary captains who rule through fear.

**Behavioral signature:** Commands through intimidation.
Discipline is immediate and visible. The band performs well in
the short term because fear is efficient. In the long term, Named
Men build exit plans, commons do not report problems, and the
information flow that keeps a band alive degrades.

**Dialogue pattern:** Commands. Threats. The Tyrant speaks more
than the Competence Captain because fear requires performance.
"You will do this or you will answer for it." Short sentences
but more of them.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Top by force. The hierarchy holds
only while the Tyrant is present and perceived as dangerous.
Absence or weakness triggers immediate recalculation.

**Typical arc:** When the band — rarely in the Iron Ledger cast.
Relevant for rival band leaders (Hadric Red Jaw of the Three
Wolves).

**Current holder:** Hadric (Three Wolves, rival band)

### 9. The Political Animal

**Source:** Abercrombie's Bayaz, Cook's Lady. The person who
sees the band as a piece on a larger board and manipulates
accordingly.

**Behavioral signature:** Offers better deals than the
alternative. Asks for less than expected in return — at first.
The Political Animal's currency is dependence: create it, deepen
it, exploit it when the time is right. They are charming in the
way a well-constructed trap is elegant.

**Dialogue pattern:** More words than anyone else, but every word
is a move. Proposals framed as favors. Conditions framed as
generosity. "I can offer you this" is always "I need you to do
that." The fluency is itself a warning the band may not read.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** External, above. The Political
Animal hires the band, patronizes the band, or maneuvers the
band. They are never inside it. Their power is asymmetric —
they have resources the band needs and information the band
lacks.

**Typical arc:** The employer who reveals their true price at
the worst possible moment.

**Current holders:** The Pale Widow (Arc 4), various jarls

### 10. The Functional Infrastructure

**Source:** Cook's background company members who are identified
by what they do, not who they are. The cook, the healer, the
mule-driver. Characters whose identity is their function.

**Behavioral signature:** Does the job. Does it every day. Does
it with the precision that comes from repetition rather than
talent. The narrative identifies the character by the function
first: "Dalla managed the cook-fire." The person behind the
function is implied by the quality of the work.

**Dialogue pattern:** Corrective instructions. "Smaller pieces."
"Two more days." Statements of fact about the specialty domain.
These characters speak in the language of their craft, not in
the language of the band's politics.

**Relationship to hierarchy:** Below Named Men, but protected
by irreplaceability. Killing the cook is a worse tactical
outcome than losing a common fighter. The band may not
articulate this. The serving order suggests it.

**Typical arc:** Background steady state. The Infrastructure
character's arc is not tracked because they are the ground
everyone else walks on. If they die, the gap is felt through
what breaks.

**Current holders:** Dalla (cook; also holds partial Caster
through seidr — see `character-bible.md` and `pantheon.md`),
Petra (partially — hunter role is infrastructure, but the
sister-agenda adds arc depth)

## Archetype Pairing Map

Pairs that generate narrative friction when in proximity:

| Pair                                      | Friction Type                                                                                                                                                  |
| ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Captain + Ledger-Keeper                   | Authority vs. arithmetic. The captain commands; the ledger constrains. Power negotiation through numbers.                                                      |
| Captain + Quiet Blade                     | Competence recognizing competence. The captain positions the blade; the blade trusts the captain's judgment — until one bad call.                              |
| Quiet Blade + New Blood                   | The new man sits next to the veteran. Proximity as unspoken apprenticeship. The veteran tolerates; the new man learns from observation, not instruction.       |
| Ledger-Keeper + Deserter                  | The Ledger-Keeper sees the Deserter's arithmetic. The Deserter knows the Ledger-Keeper sees. Neither speaks about it. The silence is the story.                |
| Old Campaigner + New Blood                | The old man talks at the fire. The new man listens. Not mentorship — coincidence of timing. The old man was going to talk anyway.                              |
| Caster + Band                             | Permanent edge-tension. Every transaction between the caster and the band is a renegotiation of tolerance.                                                     |
| Captain + Tyrant (rival)                  | Mirror pair. Same role, different method. The contrast defines both captains for the reader.                                                                   |
| Political Animal + Captain                | The employer and the employed. Asymmetric power. The Animal has resources; the Captain has men. Each needs the other and trusts neither.                       |
| New Blood + Deserter                      | Two exit trajectories. One is learning to stay. One is planning to leave. If the reader cannot tell which is which until the act, the characterization worked. |
| Functional Infrastructure + Ledger-Keeper | They share the language of margin. The cook counts calories; the Ledger-Keeper counts coin. Between them, the band's survival reduces to arithmetic.           |

## Generating New Characters from Archetypes

When `recruitment.py` or narrative need produces a new band member:

1. **Assign a primary archetype.** What structural role does this
   person fill in the band's economy?
2. **Select 2-3 gestures** from the archetype's behavioral
   signature. Make them specific to this person — not the
   archetype's generic version but a concrete physical habit.
3. **Choose a pairing.** Which existing character creates the most
   friction with this archetype? Place them in proximity.
4. **Assign an arc trajectory** from character-arcs.md. Not every
   character needs a full arc — commons may run on archetype alone.
5. **Write a hiring vignette** (see Vignette 4, 32, 37 for
   patterns). The hiring scene establishes the archetype through
   dialogue, physical description, and the band's reaction.

### New character checklist

- [ ] Primary archetype assigned
- [ ] 2-3 specific gestures defined
- [ ] Pairing with existing character identified
- [ ] Arc trajectory selected (or marked as background)
- [ ] Hiring vignette written or chapter introduction planned
- [ ] Added to character-bible.md
- [ ] Added to band_state.yaml
