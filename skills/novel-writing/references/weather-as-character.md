# Weather as Character — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** Weather in the Rimevegr is not atmosphere. It is a
physical antagonist with operational consequences. Every weather
state acts on the characters' bodies, their gear, their morale,
and their ability to move, eat, and fight. This document
establishes how to render each weather type as a force that
does things to people, and how seasonal context changes the
prose voice itself.

> **Cross-reference:** `simulation-rendering-guide.md` § Weather
> contains the simulation-to-prose rendering table for weather
> states. This file extends those states into multi-day arcs,
> escalation, and supernatural weather.

---

## The Weather Principle

Weather is not metaphor. Weather is logistics.

When it snows, the band moves slower, eats more, sees less,
and fights worse. The snow is not reflecting anyone's emotional
state. The snow is making their lives materially harder, and
the prose renders the material hardship as physical experience.

The reader should finish every weather passage knowing:

1. What the weather is doing to the band's bodies
2. What the weather is doing to the band's plans
3. What the weather costs

## Weather Types as Antagonists

### Clear Grey

**What it does to bodies:** Cold. Not the dramatic cold of
a storm — the patient, persistent cold of a grey sky that
gives nothing. The cold that lives in fingers, in ears, in
the gap between collar and neck. Breathing is sharp. The
ground is hard. The light is flat and colorless and it ages
the men — everyone looks older under a clear grey sky.

**What it does to plans:** Good marching weather. The band
moves at full pace. Forage is normal. But the clarity is
deceptive — clear grey means they can see how far they have
to go, and that knowledge has its own weight.

**What it does to gear:** Metal is cold to touch. Leather
stiffens. Straps need working. The men's hands are slow
until they are warmed by work.

**Prose approach:** Spare. Factual. The weather does not
demand attention and the prose does not give it more than
a line or two. It is the default state and the characters
treat it as the default state — this is what life looks
like.

### Rime-Fog

**What it does to bodies:** The world shrinks. Visibility
drops to arm's length, then less. The fog enters lungs as
cold air and leaves as visible breath. Rime forms on
eyebrows, on beards, on the edge of shields. Hair freezes
into shapes. Nostril hairs become brittle. The fog is not
wet like lowland fog — it is crystalline, a suspension of
ice particles that coats everything it touches with a thin
white film.

**What it does to plans:** Marching slows. The trail
disappears. Navigation becomes the point man's best guess.
Forage is impaired — the foragers cannot see what is around
them and go shorter distances. The band walks closer together
because losing contact in rime-fog means losing a man.

**What it does to combat:** Ambush becomes possible. The
fog is sound-wrong — sounds carry but direction is
impossible. A shield wall cannot form properly because the
men cannot see their own line. Individual fights become
intimate and terrifying because the opponent materializes
from nothing at close range.

**Prose approach:** Proximity. Everything is described in
close terms. The man in front is a shape. The man behind
is footsteps. The world beyond ten feet does not exist.
Sentence length shortens because the world has shortened.

### Light Rain

**What it does to bodies:** Wet. Persistent wet. Not a
downpour that passes but a steady soak that gets into
everything. Wool becomes heavy. Leather darkens and chafes.
Hair plasters to foreheads. Water runs into eyes. The cold
is different from dry cold — it finds the skin and stays.
The hands wrinkle. The feet soften in wet boots and then
the blisters start.

**What it does to plans:** Marching continues but costs
more. The ground gives way underfoot. Streams swell. River
crossings that were manageable become dangerous. The camp
is miserable — the fire hisses, the smoke lies low, nothing
dries.

**What it does to morale:** Rain is the universal
misery-maker. It is not dramatic enough to produce fear
and not gentle enough to be ignored. It produces
irritability, shorter tempers, petty arguments. Men who
had nothing to complain about now have the rain.

**Prose approach:** Sensory accumulation. The wetness
builds across the passage. First the hair. Then the
collar. Then the shoulders. Then everything. The reader
should feel the slow invasion of water into every layer.

### Driving Snow

**What it does to bodies:** The snow hits sideways. It
fills boot-tops. It packs into eye sockets when the face
turns into the wind. Eyelashes freeze. Breathing through
the nose becomes impossible because the nostrils seal
with ice. Breathing through the mouth means swallowing
snow. Every exposed surface of skin becomes a burn within
minutes. The arms go up reflexively to shield the face
and then the arms are no longer holding weapons or packs
properly.

**What it does to plans:** March speed halves. The trail
disappears in real-time — a man's footprint fills within
minutes. Navigation becomes survival-first. The band does
not march _to_ somewhere; they march _through_ something.
Forage is negligible. Combat ability drops severely — men
cannot see the swing coming, cannot grip properly, cannot
maintain formation.

**What it does to the camp:** If they camp in driving
snow, the tent is a battle. Pegs will not hold. Fabric
cracks. The fire is nearly impossible and when it exists
it is a small thing crouching against the wind. The men
pack close for warmth and the tent becomes a space of
shared misery where rank breaks down because everyone is
equally cold.

**Prose approach:** Physical assault language. The snow
_hits_, it _drives_, it _buries_. The sentences fight the
weather the way the characters fight the weather — short
bursts between impacts. The prose rhythm is the rhythm of
a man walking into a headwind: effort, pause, effort.

### Rime Storm

**What it does to bodies:** They cannot march. This is not
a choice. The wind is a wall. The snow is horizontal. The
cold is lethal — frostbite chance climbs to near-certainty
for any exposed skin. Men dig into whatever shelter exists
and they stay there. If they are on open ground, they dig
into the snow itself because the snow is warmer inside than
the air outside.

**What it does to plans:** All plans stop. The storm is
the only plan. Survival. Days are lost — one, two, three.
The food stores drop because the band eats but does not
move. The calendar turns and the destination gets no closer.
Every day of rime storm is a day that the runway shortens.

**What it does to minds:** Confinement. The men cannot go
outside. They sit in darkness and listen to the storm
trying to get in. The sound is continuous and it wears at
the mind. Conversations become circular. Arguments ignite
over nothing. Someone starts weeping and pretends it is
the cold.

**Prose approach:** Confinement prose. Interior focus. The
outside world does not exist — there is only the tent, the
men, the sound. Sentences are claustrophobic. The same space
is described repeatedly because the characters cannot leave
it. Time distorts.

### The Hush

**What it does to bodies:** Nothing physical. That is the
problem. The body reacts to the absence of stimulus as a
threat. Hair stands up. The gut tightens. Breathing slows
voluntarily because breathing feels loud. The heartbeat
becomes audible in the ears. Hands go to weapons without
conscious decision.

**What it does to minds:** Fear. Not the fear of a visible
threat but the fear of something _wrong_. The Hush is the
absence of the world's normal noise — wind, birds, insects,
the creak of trees, the sound of water. All of it stops.
The silence is not empty. The silence is _listening_.

Men who have experienced the Hush before react faster.
Their reaction is the warning for the others. The scout
raises a fist. The sergeant's hand goes to his sword. They
do not explain. There is no time and no words that cover
it.

**What it does to the supernatural layer:** The Hush is
the first marker that the Veil is thin. Thorne registers
it through the runes — his hand goes to his amulet, his
warning to Voss is flat: "Something is listening." Dalla
knows it through seidr. Her behavior changes — she
becomes still, her eyes go to the middle distance, she
may make a small gesture or say a word under her breath.
Thorne reads. Dalla responds. What she does during the
Hush is not explained to the men and not fully explained
to the reader. (See `simulation-rendering-guide.md`,
Supernatural Rendering Pipeline.)

**What it costs:** Duration matters. A short Hush — minutes —
costs nothing but nerve. A long Hush — ten minutes or more —
costs morale (−1). A Hush that ends badly — something emerges,
something is _there_ — costs more.

**Prose approach:** Absence prose. Describe what is _not_
there. The birds that stopped. The wind that died. The
stream that is still audible but sounds wrong because it
is the only sound. Sentence rhythm slows. Spaces between
sentences widen. The prose itself becomes quiet.

### Veil-Thinning

**What it does to perception:** Reality is unreliable.
Small things are wrong. A shadow falls at an angle that
does not match the light source. Something moves in the
periphery that is not there when looked at directly. A
sound — footsteps, breathing, whispering — comes from a
direction where nobody stands. The quality of light changes
as if a cloud crossed the sun, but there is no cloud.

Men begin to doubt their senses. "Did you see—" "Was
that—" The questions trail off because the answers are
worse than the uncertainty.

**What it does to operations:** Travel becomes dangerous
not because of physical obstacles but because the landscape
may not be trustworthy. A path that was straight now seems
to curve. Distances feel wrong — a tree that was far is
now close, or the reverse. The band stays together not
for protection from enemies but for confirmation of reality.

**How the band responds:** Thorne interprets — he reads
the wrongness through rune-signs and delivers flat
assessments to Voss: what to avoid, what to respect,
when to leave. Dalla acts — she perceives more than the
men and she is less afraid, but not unafraid. Her
methods — runes drawn on a hand, a word spoken into the
fire, herbs burned that smell of something sharp — are
the band's only response to a threat they cannot fight
with steel. Thorne warns. Dalla wards. (See
`simulation-rendering-guide.md`, Supernatural Rendering
Pipeline.)

**Prose approach:** Wrongness prose. Every description
carries a slight distortion. The adjectives are almost
right but not quite. The landscape is familiar but _off_.
The reader should feel the uncanny through accumulated
small discomforts, not through dramatic revelations.

### Blood Sun

**What it does to the sky:** The sun turns red. Not
sunset-red. Not dust-red. The red of something that is
wrong at a scale larger than human. The light on the
snow is the color of old blood. Shadows are sharp and
black and look solid. The sky itself feels lower, as if
the ceiling of the world has dropped.

**What it does to men:** Fear that has no tactical
response. Men who face steel without flinching are
unmade by a red sun because steel is a problem they
understand. This is not a problem. This is a _condition_.
The world is afflicted and they are inside the
affliction.

Render through regression. Grown men make gestures their
grandmothers taught them. Someone prays who has not
prayed since childhood. The instinct is to hide, to be
small, to not attract the attention of whatever is
causing the sky to bleed.

**What it costs:** Blood Sun is the highest-tier weather
event. It is rare and it is expensive. Morale drops.
The Hush often precedes it. Whatever happens under a
Blood Sun carries weight — not because of game mechanics
but because the characters will remember it. A fight
under a Blood Sun, a death under a Blood Sun, a decision
under a Blood Sun — these become the stories the band
tells later. "It was the day the sun bled."

**Prose approach:** Scale prose. The description starts
large — the sky, the horizon, the color of everything —
then contracts to the human response. The contrast
between the cosmic event and the small human standing
under it is the emotional engine. Short sentences for
human responses. Long sentences for the sky.

## Seasonal Prose Voice

### Long Summer (Days 1–60)

The prose voice in Long Summer is slightly more expansive.
Sentences can be longer. The world is wider — visibility is
better, days are longer, the land opens up.

But Long Summer in the Rimevegr is not gentle. The light
is disorienting — too much, too long, never fully dark.
Men from the south lose track of bedtime. The midnight
sun turns sleep into a discipline. Green things grow but
the green is aggressive — thick moss, dense undergrowth,
flies and biting insects. Mud replaces ice and the mud is
its own antagonist.

**Exhaustion is different.** Summer exhaustion is wet
and insect-bitten and green-rotten. Winter exhaustion is
dry and cold and clean. The prose should feel the
difference — summer fatigue is accompanied by sweat,
stink, the weight of wet clothing. Winter fatigue is
accompanied by numbness, the economy of movement, the
effort of staying warm.

**Key prose markers:**

- Longer sight-lines (the men can see what is ahead)
- Biological activity (insects, animal sounds, growth)
- Wet ground (mud, bog, stream crossings)
- Heat that is never comfortable (humid, close, buggy)
- Sleep disruption (the light does not stop)

### The Long Dark (Days 61–360)

The prose voice in the Long Dark contracts. Sentences
shorten. The world shrinks. The cold is a presence in every
scene — not mentioned every time but always there, the way
gravity is always there.

**Key prose markers:**

- Short days, long nights (the darkness has weight)
- Cold as physical presence (on skin, in lungs, in metal)
- Fire as centerpiece (the fire is where life happens)
- Visibility contraction (the world is close and dark)
- The economy of movement (nothing is wasted because
  wasted movement costs warmth)
- Sound carries differently in cold air (sharper, further)

### The Transition

The shift from Long Summer to the Long Dark happens over
roughly a week. The light changes first — shorter days,
longer shadows. Then the cold sharpens. Then the green
things die. Then the first frost that does not melt by
midday.

Render the transition as a week of small changes that
accumulate into a transformation. Not a dramatic switch
but a creeping change. One morning a man can see his
breath. The next morning the puddles have skin ice. The
morning after that the puddles are solid. By the end of
the week, the world is different and the men are dressed
differently and the prose sounds different because the
world sounds different.

## Weather as Operational Constraint

### The Decision Framework

Every weather state forces decisions. The rendering of
weather is not complete until the decision it forces is
visible.

**Can they march?** If no: they stay. The scene is about
what happens when armed men cannot go somewhere.

**Can they forage?** If limited: the food math changes.
The scene carries the weight of diminishing stores.

**Can they fight?** If impaired: vulnerability. The scene
carries the knowledge that if someone attacks now, the
band is worse off than usual.

**Can they navigate?** If not: they are lost. The scene
carries the weight of not knowing where they are or how
far they have gone wrong.

### Weather Stacking

Weather compounds with other states. Driving snow plus low food
plus wounded plus far from settlement equals crisis. The weather is one
factor among several, but it is the factor that cannot be
negotiated with, fought, or outrun. Everything else — enemies,
contracts, feuds — has a response. Weather has only endurance.

Render stacking through accumulation. Do not list the problems.
Show one problem being dealt with and then the weather making
it harder. "Gest was counting the food when the snow started.
By the time he finished, the fire was half its size and the
number was worse because they would need to eat more to stay
warm."

## Weather Continuity Rules

1. **Weather persists.** A multi-day weather state does not
   need re-describing every scene, but its presence must be
   felt. If it has been snowing for three days, the snow is
   knee-deep. The camp is buried. The men are different than
   they were three days ago.

2. **Weather changes are events.** When the weather changes,
   someone notices. The wind shifts. The sky changes color.
   The temperature drops or rises. The change is a moment —
   brief, specific, noticed.

3. **Weather history matters.** Three days of rain followed
   by a clear day means the ground is still wet. The camp is
   still damp. The gear is still not dry. The clear day does
   not erase the rain — it provides a window to dry things
   and repair damage.

4. **Weather memory.** Characters remember weather. A rime
   storm that cost them three days of march and a man to
   frostbite is referenced later. "Last time the sky looked
   like that, we lost Haldor's toes." Weather creates history
   and history creates character.
