# Rewrite Calibration Examples

Before/after pairs showing AI draft transformed to human-quality
prose for the Iron Ledger voice. Each pair names the failure type,
the rule violated, and shows the exact fix.

---

## Failure Type 1: Voice Failure

### Example 1.1 — Mood Fog

**AI draft:**

> The ancient darkness of the barrow seemed to breathe with a
> malevolent purpose. Shadows danced across the rime-covered
> walls, whispering secrets that had been buried for centuries.
> Voss felt the weight of ages pressing down on him, a palpable
> dread that transcended mere fear.

**Failures:** Mood fog (no physical grounding). "Palpable dread
that transcended mere fear" is significance inflation + hedging.
"Whispering secrets" is generic dark-fantasy atmosphere. "Ancient
darkness" is a nothing-phrase.

**Rewrite:**

> The barrow smelled of standing water and something Voss could
> not name — animal, maybe, but long dead. His torch reached four
> paces. The walls were wet. The ceiling was lower than the
> entrance had promised, and he felt the stone through his helmet
> when he straightened.

Grounded in senses: smell, light range, touch. "Lower than the
entrance had promised" does atmosphere work through a physical
observation. The unnamed smell does more dread-work than
"malevolent purpose" because the reader fills in what it might be.

### Example 1.2 — Lost POV Anchor

**AI draft:**

> The camp settled into an uneasy quiet as night fell. Each
> mercenary found their own way of coping with the uncertainty
> that hung in the air. Some sharpened weapons, others stared
> into the fire, all carrying the burden of tomorrow's march.

**Failures:** Omniscient summary. "Each mercenary" breaks the
close-third POV. "Uncertainty that hung in the air" is abstract.
"Carrying the burden" is metaphorical weight with no physical
detail.

**Rewrite:**

> Voss counted heads. Kell was at the fire, turning the same
> piece of kindling in his hands. Orm slept already — or pretended
> to. Gest had the ledger open on his knee but had not written
> in it for an hour. Voss stopped counting. It did not help.

Everything filtered through Voss. Each person rendered by one
specific habit-detail. "Stopped counting. It did not help." is
the emotional content — delivered through action and its failure,
not through summary.

## Failure Type 2: Structure Failure

### Example 2.1 — Paragraph Sameness

**AI draft:**

> The morning brought grey light and a thin wind from the north.
> Voss rose early, as he always did, checking the perimeter
> before the camp stirred. The ground was frozen solid, making
> every step a negotiation between speed and noise.
>
> Breakfast was a quiet affair, porridge thinned with meltwater.
> Kell ate mechanically, his eyes tracking the tree line. The
> men ate in silence, each absorbed in the routine of fueling
> bodies that would march until dark.
>
> The march began without ceremony, as it always did. Voss took
> point, Orm beside him, both scanning the trail ahead. The
> forest closed around them, black pine and granite, a tunnel
> with no clear end.

**Failures:** All three paragraphs are the same length (3
sentences), the same shape (setting → character action →
elaboration), and start with "The [time/thing]." Interchangeable.

**Rewrite:**

> Grey light. Thin wind. Voss was up before it.
>
> He walked the perimeter on frozen ground that cracked under
> his boots no matter how he placed them. Kell was already awake,
> sitting with his porridge. He had not eaten any of it. Voss did
> not ask. The kind of morning where asking cost more than not
> knowing.
>
> They marched.

Three paragraphs, three shapes: staccato fragment (3 words,
3 words, 6 words), long observation paragraph with a withholding
beat, single-sentence action paragraph. The reader feels the
rhythm change.

### Example 2.2 — Symmetric Dialogue

**AI draft:**

> "We should move before dawn," Voss said, studying the horizon.
> "The Three Wolves know this route."
>
> "That's risky," Gest replied carefully. "We don't know the
> terrain south of the river. Moving blind is how bands die."
>
> "Staying is how bands die too," Voss countered. "Every hour
> we wait gives them time to close the distance."

**Failures:** Equal-length turns. Each character speaks
exactly two sentences. The rhythm is metronomic. Also: "countered"
is a dialogue tag that tells instead of showing.

**Rewrite:**

> "Before dawn," Voss said.
>
> Gest opened the ledger. "The south bank — "
>
> "Before dawn. That's the decision."
>
> Gest closed the ledger. He had not needed to open it. The
> numbers were in his head. They both knew that.

Unbalanced turns. Voss speaks in fragments. Gest starts to argue
and is shut down. The action beats (opening/closing the ledger)
carry the subtext: Gest performs calculation as authority, Voss
overrides it. The last two sentences are Voss's POV reading the
situation.

## Failure Type 3: Tell Failure

### Example 3.1 — Copula Substitution + Elegant Variation

**AI draft:**

> The weapon served as both tool and symbol in Svarthird culture.
> A mercenary's blade represented his station within the band.
> The steel carried meaning beyond its function — it stood as
> testament to battles survived and debts owed.

**Failures:** "Served as," "represented," "stood as testament" —
three copula substitutions in three sentences. "The weapon," "a
mercenary's blade," "the steel" — elegant variation for the same
sword. "Meaning beyond its function" is abstract inflation.

**Rewrite:**

> Kell's sword was notched where he had used it to chop kindling
> last winter. The blade was good enough. He kept it because it
> was his and because replacing it cost four silver he did not
> have.

"Was" used three times — invisible. One concrete detail (notch
from kindling) does more characterization than the entire
original paragraph. The economics of swords (four silver) grounds
the object in the world. No symbolism. No meaning. Just a man
and his tool and what it costs.

### Example 3.2 — Hedging + Generic Positivity

**AI draft:**

> The settlement seemed almost welcoming after weeks on the road.
> Perhaps it was the smoke rising from the chimneys, or maybe the
> distant sound of children playing. In that moment, Voss allowed
> himself something that might have been hope.

**Failures:** "Seemed almost" + "perhaps" + "or maybe" + "might
have been" = four hedges in three sentences. "Something that
might have been hope" is generic positive closure. "In that
moment" signals a manufactured epiphany.

**Rewrite:**

> Smoke. Chimneys. The settlement had a gate and someone had
> bothered to close it. Voss counted buildings. Twelve. An inn
> with a sign he could not read from this distance. He would
> know the price of a bed in ten minutes. That was worth walking
> faster for.

Zero hedges. The "welcoming" quality is rendered through specifics
(closed gate = someone cares about defense, an inn exists). Voss
does not feel hope — he does logistics. "Worth walking faster
for" is as close to hope as this voice gets.

### Example 3.3 — Em-Dash Overuse + Negative Parallelism

**AI draft:**

> The rune-stone — half-buried in the frozen earth — pulsed with
> a light that was not just illumination but something deeper — a
> resonance that spoke not merely to the eyes but to something
> ancient within the blood. Thorne — ever watchful — reached for
> his charm.

**Failures:** Three em-dashes in two sentences. Two negative
parallelisms ("not just X but Y," "not merely A but B"). "Ancient
within the blood" is mood fog.

**Rewrite:**

> The rune-stone glowed. Faint. Blue-white, like ice under
> moonlight. Thorne's hand went to his charm before the rest of
> him decided to be afraid.

Three short sentences for the visual. The last sentence gives
Thorne's reaction through the body-before-mind pattern: his hand
moved first, the fear came second. No em-dashes. No parallelisms.

## Failure Type 4: Subtext Failure

### Example 4.1 — Thesis Statement Dialogue

**AI draft:**

> "That's the problem with command," Voss said, staring into the
> fire. "Every decision costs something. Every man you send
> forward is a life you're spending. And the worst part is, the
> ledger never balances."

**Failures:** Voss is stating his theme out loud. No commanding
officer says this. This is a speech for the reader, not a
conversation with another character. "The ledger never balances"
is a thesis statement.

**Rewrite:**

> "Orm. Take two. Check the south ridge."
>
> Orm stood. He did not ask which two.
>
> Later, when they had gone and the fire was smaller, Gest said,
> "Three would have been safer."
>
> "Three would have left us with four."

The math is present (send two, keep four, three is better but
costs more). The theme — command as arithmetic — is visible only
in the numbers, never stated. Orm not asking "which two" tells
us he trusts Voss to have already decided, which is a detail
about relationships. Gest's critique comes _after_ — he does
not argue before the order, only notes the math afterward.

### Example 4.2 — Emotional Explanation

**AI draft:**

> Dalla sat apart from the others, as she often did. The visions
> had left her shaken, though she would never admit it. She
> missed her sister — missed the warmth of their shared bed,
> the sound of breathing in the dark. This was the loneliness
> that seidr brought: the knowing without comfort.

**Failures:** "Though she would never admit it" tells the reader
what the character hides — the point was to show it without
telling. "This was the loneliness that seidr brought" is thesis.
The entire paragraph explains Dalla's emotional state rather
than rendering it.

**Rewrite:**

> Dalla was near the fire but not at it. She had her hands in
> her sleeves. The other side of the fire, where the warmth was,
> was crowded. She did not move.

No emotional vocabulary at all. "Near the fire but not at it"
is the entire emotional state: present but excluded. "The warmth
was" on the other side and "she did not move" tells the reader
everything: she could be warm, she chooses not to be, or
something prevents her. The reader does the emotional work.

### Example 4.3 — Over-Explained Backstory

**AI draft:**

> Kell had served under three captains before Voss, each one
> worse than the last. The first had been a drunk who got half
> the band killed in a ford crossing. The second sold them to
> the highest bidder — literally, as mercenaries. The third had
> simply vanished one night with the treasury.

**Failures:** All backstory is loaded into one expository dump.
Cook never does this. Backstory leaks through present-moment
details and offhand references.

**Rewrite:**

> "Ford crossing," Kell said. He was looking at the river.
>
> Voss looked at the river. Deep. Moving fast. "Bridge is two
> hours east."
>
> "Good."
>
> Kell said nothing else.

"Ford crossing" is the entire backstory. Two words. The reader
does not know the full story yet. But they know Kell has a
reason for not wanting to ford. The information arrives when it
is needed, not before. The rest of the backstory appears in
other chapters, one detail at a time.

## Quick Reference: Fix Patterns

| Problem               | Signal                         | Fix                             |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Mood fog              | Abstract dark-atmosphere words | Replace with sense data         |
| Lost POV              | "Everyone felt" / omniscient   | Filter through one pair of eyes |
| Paragraph sameness    | Same shape three times         | Vary length and entry point     |
| Copula substitution   | serves/acts/stands as          | Use "is" or "was"               |
| Elegant variation     | Three labels for one sword     | Repeat the word                 |
| Hedging               | seemed/perhaps/almost          | State or cut                    |
| Em-dash clusters      | Three per paragraph            | Replace with periods            |
| Negative parallelism  | not just X but Y               | State the stronger thing        |
| Thesis dialogue       | Characters state theme         | Show through action/orders      |
| Emotional explanation | Named feelings in narration    | Render through body/action      |
| Silver-lining endings | Comfort after darkness         | End on the cost                 |
| Backstory dump        | Three sentences of history     | One oblique reference           |
