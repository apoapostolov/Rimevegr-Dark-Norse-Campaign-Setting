# Chapter Structure — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** Structural templates for planning and writing chapters.
Every chapter has the same anatomy but not the same shape. The
anatomy ensures consistency. The chapter type determines which
systems dominate and what the reader should leave with.

---

## Chapter Anatomy

Every chapter has three parts. No exceptions.

### 1. Opening — Physical Grounding (1-3 sentences)

The reader must know three things within the first three
sentences: where the body is, what the weather is doing to
that body, and what the body is doing about it. The opening
is physical, present, and specific. No abstraction. No
reflection. No flashback.

The POV character's relationship to the environment is the
opening's entire job. If Voss is on watch, the opening is
the cold in his hands and the weight of the axe on his hip.
If Gest is at the ledger, the opening is the charcoal's
scratch, the candlelight on the page, the numbers that will
not balance.

Rules:

- First sentence: sensory grounding — weather, terrain, or
  physical discomfort. What the body feels.
- Second sentence: context — what the POV character is doing
  and why. The practical task, not the emotional state.
- Third sentence (optional): the complication or pressure —
  what makes this moment different from the baseline. If
  nothing is different, the chapter is a baseline chapter
  and the opening stays flat.

### 2. Body — The Event (2-4 scenes or 1 extended scene)

The body of the chapter contains what happens. "What happens"
is always a combination of the simulation state and the
characters responding to it. A chapter where nothing happens
is a chapter where the simulation state is stable — and that
stability is itself the point (establishing the baseline
that later chapters will disrupt).

Body structure rules:

- Maximum 4 scenes per chapter. Most chapters have 2-3.
- One scene carries the chapter's primary event. The other
  scenes are setup, logistics, or aftermath.
- Every scene must render at least one simulation system as
  lived experience: weather, food, morale, money, time,
  injury, or supernatural presence.
- Scenes end when the cost becomes visible. A negotiation
  scene ends when the price is known. A combat scene ends
  when the damage is counted. A camp scene ends when the
  fire dies or the watch changes.

### 3. Closing — Cost Accounting (1-3 sentences)

Every chapter ends with the reader knowing what the event
cost. Cost is always concrete: food eaten, silver spent,
a man wounded, morale shifted, an opportunity lost, a
relationship changed. The closing does not moralize. It
does not summarize. It tallies.

The narrator closes the way Gest closes a ledger entry —
the number, the balance, the consequence.

Rules:

- The closing references a specific resource or relationship
  that changed during the chapter.
- If the chapter advanced a character arc, the closing
  registers the behavioral change through a concrete detail:
  who sits where, who speaks to whom, whose pack is lighter
  or heavier.
- The final sentence may be a single physical image that
  carries the chapter's weight: the fire burning low, the
  empty seat, the road ahead.
- Never close with a thought, a realization, or an emotional
  summary. Close with what exists after the event.

## Chapter Types

### March Chapter

**What happens:** The band moves from one place to another.
The march is logistics — distance, weather, terrain, food
consumption, who lags, who is injured, what the column looks
like from the front versus the rear.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** Weather and terrain. The road or lack of
   road. What the column looks like.
2. **Scene 1:** The march itself — rendered through physical
   detail. Pack weight, mud depth, the sound of footsteps.
   Time compressed by listing what passes: hill, ford,
   treeline, stone marker.
3. **Scene 2:** A disruption — weather turns, someone falls
   behind, a decision point (which route, whether to stop).
4. **Closing:** Camp. What was consumed. How far they got
   versus how far they needed to get.

**Simulation requirements:** Weather for the march days.
Food consumption. Injury states (who is slow). Morale
(who is talking, who is not).

**POV recommendation:** Voss (sees the column as a tactical
problem), Gest (counts the cost per day), Kell (reads the
terrain for threats).

**Pacing target:** 2,500-4,000 words. Compressed. Time
moves in blocks — morning, midday, evening. The march
chapter is the novel's metronome.

---

### Camp Chapter

**What happens:** The band is stationary. Camp chapters are
where relationships shift, morale surfaces, and the small
politics of proximity play out. The fire-circle is the
stage. Who sits where is the dialogue.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** The fire. The pot. The sky. Who is on watch.
2. **Scene 1:** A routine camp action — cooking, gear repair,
   firewood — that reveals the current hierarchy through who
   does what work.
3. **Scene 2:** A conversation or confrontation. Camp is where
   people talk. The talk is practical but the subtext is
   political — grievances, debts, alliances.
4. **Scene 3 (optional):** The watch shift. The quiet after the
   fire dies. Private moments or overheard fragments.
5. **Closing:** What changed. A bedroll moved. A seat taken or
   yielded. The morale state rendered through silence or noise.

**Simulation requirements:** Food state (what is in the pot,
how much). Morale. Current grievances. Injury states.
Weather (cold camps versus comfortable camps).

**POV recommendation:** Voss (watches the men, assesses the
political state), Gest (camp is where the ledger is updated
and reports are made).

**Pacing target:** 2,000-3,500 words. Slower than march
chapters. The pace is the fire — it burns steadily and
reveals things as the light shifts.

---

### Contract Chapter

**What happens:** The band negotiates a job. The contract
chapter is a transaction scene — employer, steward, or
village elder across the table from Voss, with Gest's
numbers running beneath every word.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** The employer's space — hall, field, town
   square. What the space says about the employer's wealth,
   stability, and desperation.
2. **Scene 1:** The negotiation. Voss speaks. The employer
   responds. Gest's numbers are the invisible third voice —
   the reader knows what the band needs because Gest has
   already stated it.
3. **Scene 2:** The aftermath. Voss and Gest alone. The
   actual assessment — what the contract is worth versus
   what it will cost versus what the alternative is. The
   decision is arithmetic, not principle.
4. **Closing:** The terms. What was agreed. What was given
   up. The margin.

**Simulation requirements:** Treasury state. Food reserves.
Current contracts or lack thereof. Band size and condition.
The employer's offer versus the band's minimum.

**POV recommendation:** Voss (the negotiation is command
made visible — every word is strategy), Gest (the numbers
behind the words).

**Pacing target:** 2,000-3,000 words. Tight. Negotiation
scenes are compressed — the transaction moves in offers and
responses. No filler. Every line of dialogue advances or
retreats the position.

---

### Combat Chapter

**What happens:** Violence. The combat chapter renders
fighting as physical experience — weight, impact, pain,
exhaustion — not choreography. Combat is ugly, costly,
and over faster than it felt.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** The moment before. Hands on weapons. The
   sound that should not be there. The body's reaction
   before the mind catches up.
2. **Scene 1:** The fight. Blow-by-blow rendered through the
   POV character's body: what the weapon feels like, what
   the impact does, where the ground is slippery with what.
   Compression is key — real fights are short. A single
   combat is one scene, not three.
3. **Scene 2:** The aftermath. Counting the living. Counting
   the wounded. Counting what was lost. Gest's voice — the
   cost in bodies and silver.
4. **Closing:** The dead. The injured. The new arithmetic.

**Simulation requirements:** Combat resolution results.
Wounds sustained (who, severity, duration). Deaths or
desertions. Equipment lost or gained. Morale change.

**POV recommendation:** Kell (the mechanics of violence are
his language — positioning, threat, the weight of iron),
Voss (the tactical picture — what the fight means for the
band's survival).

**Pacing target:** 2,500-4,500 words. The fight itself is
short — 500-1,500 words. The rest is before and after.
The aftermath is always longer than the violence.

### Settlement Chapter

**What happens:** The band enters a village, town, or
settlement. The settlement chapter is about the band's
relationship with civilian space — trade, resupply, shelter,
reputation, standing.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** The settlement as seen from the approach.
   What the palisade state says about the village's history.
   The elder's face. The way the gates open or do not.
2. **Scene 1:** The transaction — buying supplies, selling
   labor, negotiating shelter. The prices tell the reader
   about the village's state without exposition.
3. **Scene 2:** The band in civilian space — how the men
   behave when there are people who are not soldiers.
   Who drinks. Who steals. Who is careful.
4. **Closing:** The departure or the consequence. What the
   band took. What the village remembers. What the standing
   record says now.

**Simulation requirements:** Settlement data — population,
resources, standing, prices. Band's treasury and supply
needs. Previous interactions with this settlement (standing
changes are permanent).

**POV recommendation:** Voss (managing the band's behavior
is his job — one theft and the gates close), Gest (the
prices, the resupply arithmetic).

**Pacing target:** 2,500-4,000 words. Medium pace. The
settlement is a contrast — civilian rhythm against military
rhythm. The contrast reveals things about the band that
the road does not.

---

### Barrow Chapter

**What happens:** The band enters a ruin, barrow, or
supernatural space. The barrow chapter introduces the
uncanny — not as explanation but as physical wrongness.
The Hush. Temperature shifts. Shadows misaligned.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** The threshold. The moment the air changes.
   Thorne's hand on his rune-amulet. The silence that is
   not quite silence.
2. **Scene 1:** The descent or the approach. The physical
   space rendered through what it does to the body — cold
   that goes through wool, a smell that is not rot but is
   not right, the way sound behaves.
3. **Scene 2:** The event — what the barrow contains. Not
   always a fight. Sometimes a discovery. Sometimes a price.
   The supernatural is rendered through physical symptoms,
   never through explanation.
4. **Scene 3:** The extraction. Getting out. What was taken
   and what was left behind. The loot is never free.
5. **Closing:** The light outside. The count. What was
   gained. What it cost in sleep, in morale, in Thorne's
   face.

**Simulation requirements:** Barrow encounter results.
Loot gained. Wounds or deaths. Morale impact. Supernatural
event log. Thorne's knowledge state.

**POV recommendation:** Voss (the tactical problem of a
space he does not understand), Kell (the immediate physical
threat).

**Pacing target:** 3,000-5,000 words. The longest chapter
type. The barrow earns its length through sustained
atmospheric pressure. The pacing is slow at entry,
compressed at the event, decompressed at extraction.

---

### Transition Chapter

**What happens:** Between major events. The transition
chapter handles logistics that do not deserve their own
march or camp chapter but must be accounted for: a time
skip rendered as summary, a roster change, a seasonal
shift, a quiet stretch where nothing happens except the
passage of time.

**Structural template:**

1. **Opening:** A time marker — the season, the distance
   covered, the calendar.
2. **Scene 1:** A compressed account of what happened in the
   gap — who healed, who left, what was spent, how the
   band's state changed. Rendered as Gest's ledger or Voss's
   assessment, not as narrated summary.
3. **Scene 2 (optional):** One specific moment from the gap
   that matters — a conversation, a small event, a shift.
   The moment that stands for the whole stretch.
4. **Closing:** The new baseline. Where the band is now.
   What the arithmetic says.

**Simulation requirements:** Time passage data. Resource
consumption over the gap. Wound healing timelines. Morale
drift. Any events the simulation generated during the
skipped period.

**POV recommendation:** Gest (the summary is his voice —
the ledger compressed), Voss (the assessment of what the
gap cost or gained).

**Pacing target:** 1,000-2,000 words. Short. The transition
chapter's job is to move time without losing the reader's
trust in the simulation's consistency.

## Scene Transition Rules

### Hard Breaks

A hard break (marked by `* * *` or equivalent scene
divider) means a change in at least one of: time, location,
or POV focus. Hard breaks are the default for scene
transitions within a chapter.

Rules:

- Hard break when time jumps more than one hour within a
  chapter.
- Hard break when location changes within a chapter (camp
  to road, tent to fire-circle, inside to outside).
- Hard break when the POV character's attention shifts to a
  fundamentally different situation.
- After a hard break, re-ground the reader: physical
  location, time marker, what the body is doing. One to
  two sentences.

### Continuous Transitions

A continuous transition flows from one moment to the next
without a break. Time moves forward in real-time or
near-real-time. Used within a single scene.

Rules:

- Continuous transitions work through physical movement:
  the character walks from one space to another, the
  conversation shifts from one topic to another, the task
  progresses from one stage to the next.
- Never use "later" or "after a while" for continuous
  transitions. Use physical markers: "The fire had burned
  to coals." "The watch had changed." "The skin of water
  was half-empty."
- If a continuous transition spans more than 30 minutes
  of in-world time, compress with a concrete summary:
  "Three loads of firewood. The axe handle was slick."

### Time Compression Techniques

When a chapter covers more than a single day:

1. **The catalogue.** List what passed: terrain features,
   weather changes, camps made and broken. The list moves
   time through accumulated nouns. "Two fords. A burned
   village. Three days of rain that did not stop."

2. **The ledger entry.** Gest's voice compresses time
   into arithmetic: "Seven days. Forty-two rations consumed.
   Three silver in resupply. One minor wound, healing."

3. **The gesture shift.** Show time through behavioral
   change: a wound that was fresh is now scabbed. A man who
   was talking is now quiet. A pack that was heavy is now
   light. The change implies the time.

4. **The seasonal marker.** "The frost had come" covers
   weeks. "The river was high with spring melt" covers
   months. Environmental change is the longest-range time
   compression tool.

Rules:

- Never state the number of days as naked exposition
  ("Three weeks passed"). Embed the time in a physical
  fact.
- When compressing time, always account for at least one
  simulation system: food consumed, silver spent, morale
  shifted, wounds healed or worsened.
- After compression, re-ground the reader with a specific
  scene — the first concrete moment after the gap.
