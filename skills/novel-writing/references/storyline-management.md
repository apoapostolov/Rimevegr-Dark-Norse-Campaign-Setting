# Storyline Management — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** Practical timeline management when the novel runs
simultaneous storylines with different characters in different
locations. The band sometimes splits. Parallel events happen.
Characters who are not in the current scene continue to exist
and act. This file defines how to track, synchronize, and narrate
concurrent storylines without losing the reader.

> **Cross-reference:** Arc interlacing rules are in
> `plot-planning.md`. POV selection triggers are in
> `pov-selection-logic.md`. This file covers the logistics of
> running multiple timelines.

---

## When Simultaneous Storylines Occur

### Band Split

The most common structural cause. Voss sends a group on a
separate task: scouting, foraging, negotiation, or escape.
The band exists as two groups in different locations, acting
under different conditions, for a defined period.

**Duration rule:** A band split rarely exceeds 3-5 chapters.
Longer splits risk the reader losing connection with the
inactive group. The band is the novel's social unit; fragmenting
it too long fragments the reader's investment.

### Parallel Settlement Events

The band is in a settlement while events happen elsewhere that
affect them: another jarl's army moving, a rival band taking a
contract, a feud escalating in a town the band left. These events
reach the band through rumor, messenger, or consequence.

### Intercut Action

Two events happen simultaneously: Voss negotiates in the hall
while Kell stands guard and witnesses something outside. The
events are separated by location but united by time. The reader
needs both to understand what happens next.

## Timeline Tracking

### The Anchor Timeline

One storyline is the anchor. All other storylines are measured
against it. The anchor is always the storyline containing Voss.
If Voss is in both groups (impossible with a split but possible
with parallel events), the anchor is wherever the primary
narrative tension lies.

**Rule:** Never narrate a secondary storyline without the reader
knowing where the anchor timeline stands. If the last Voss chapter
ended at nightfall, the reader must be able to locate the secondary
storyline relative to that nightfall -- before it, during it,
or after it.

### Time Markers

The Rimevegr does not use clocks. Time is marked by physical
phenomena:

| Marker        | Approximate Time         | Usage                        |
| ------------- | ------------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| Watch change  | ~4 hours                 | Camp-based time measurement  |
| Meal (stew)   | Evening                  | Daily anchor point           |
| Fire state    | Hours since last tending | Short-duration tracking      |
| Light quality | Time of day (crude)      | March-based time measurement |
| Weather shift | Variable                 | Synchronization signal       |
| Moon phase    | Days                     | Multi-day tracking           |
| Supply count  | Days of marching rations | Duration measurement         |

**Synchronization technique:** When cutting between storylines,
use a shared time marker. If both groups can see the same moon,
the moon synchronizes them. If both groups experience the same
weather system, the weather synchronizes them. A blizzard that
hits both groups at the same time tells the reader the timelines
are concurrent.

## Cutting Between Storylines

### The Intercut Pattern

When running two simultaneous storylines, alternate chapters
(or scenes within chapters) between them. The pattern is not
strict alternation but follows narrative pressure.

**Cut toward tension.** When one storyline reaches a point of
rising tension, cut to the other. The reader's anxiety about the
unresolved tension carries across the cut, creating momentum in
both lines.

**Cut away from resolution.** When one storyline resolves a beat,
stay with it long enough for the reader to register the cost, then
cut. The resolution's implications carry into the other storyline
as dramatic irony (the reader knows something the other group
does not).

### Cut Frequency

| Storyline Structure     | Recommended Pattern                            |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Two active lines        | Alternate every 1-2 chapters                   |
| One active, one waiting | Active line gets 2-3 chapters, then 1 check-in |
| Brief divergence        | One intercut chapter, then reunite             |
| Extended split          | Alternate every chapter, maximum 5 total       |

### The Check-In

When one storyline is less active (the group waiting in camp
while the other group acts), it does not need full chapters. A
check-in is a single scene, usually at the start or end of the
active storyline's chapter, that shows the waiting group's state.

**Check-in anatomy:**

1. Physical state of the waiting group (cold, hungry, bored).
2. One character detail (what someone is doing to pass time).
3. One supply or morale detail (the fire, the food, the mood).
4. One sentence of tension (what they are waiting for and how
   long they have been waiting).

The check-in is 4-8 sentences. It maintains the reader's awareness
of the second group without claiming a full chapter's worth of
narrative real estate.

## Rejoining Storylines

### The Reunion Chapter

When split storylines reconverge, the reunion is a structural
event. The reader has been tracking two versions of reality. Now
they collapse into one.

**Rules:**

1. **Information exchange is a scene.** The groups have been
   apart. Each knows things the other does not. The scene where
   they share information is not skipped -- it is rendered.
   Because what each group chooses to report (and omit) reveals
   character.

2. **The POV character is whoever was with the anchor timeline.**
   Voss narrates the reunion because Voss narrates the anchor.
   The secondary group's experience reaches the reader through
   their report, filtered through Voss's assessment of whether
   to trust it.

3. **Physical details mark the passage of time.** When the
   secondary group returns, they look different. Thinner. Dirtier.
   Wounded. Wearing different boots. The reader sees the gap in
   their bodies.

4. **One chapter for reunion logistics.** Do not stretch the
   reunion across multiple chapters. The novelty of the rejoined
   band wears off quickly. Get the information exchanged, register
   the changed dynamics, and move on.

### Information Asymmetry After Reunion

Each group knows things the other does not. This asymmetry is
a resource for the next 2-3 chapters:

- Voss may not know what Gest discovered about a contract clause.
- Kell may not report something he saw because he does not
  understand its significance.
- Gest may soften the financial picture to avoid Voss making a
  desperate decision.

Track what each group knows and ensure character decisions reflect
their actual knowledge, not the reader's omniscient view.

## Parallel Event Narration

### Events the Band Does Not Witness

When events happen elsewhere that affect the band, the reader
learns about them the way the band does: late, incomplete, and
possibly wrong.

**Delivery methods:**

| Method    | Reliability | Delay    | Usage                         |
| --------- | ----------- | -------- | ----------------------------- |
| Messenger | Medium      | Days     | Official news, contract calls |
| Rumor     | Low         | Variable | Market talk, traveler stories |
| Evidence  | High        | After    | Burned village, dead bodies   |
| Scout     | High        | Hours    | Direct observation, scouting  |

**Rule:** The band never learns distant news instantly or
accurately. Every piece of information from outside the band's
immediate experience arrives with a delay and a reliability
question. The reader should share the band's uncertainty about
distant events.

### When to Show the Parallel Event Directly

Rarely. The Iron Ledger's default is to stay with the band and
let the outside world arrive as news. However, direct narration
of a parallel event is justified when:

1. **The event directly threatens the band** and the reader needs
   dramatic irony (an army marching toward the band's position).
2. **A secondary POV character is at the event** (Gest sent to
   negotiate in a different town).
3. **The event's outcome is already known** and the reader needs
   to see how it happened (a flashback-as-revelation, used
   sparingly).

If none of these conditions apply, the event is not shown. It
arrives as news, filtered through the band's information channels.

## Timeline Consistency

### The Day Count

Maintain an internal day count for each storyline. The count does
not appear in the text but must be tracked by the writer. When
storylines converge, the day counts must match.

**Tracking fields:**

- Day number (from story start or chapter event).
- Weather state (must be consistent across concurrent storylines
  in the same region).
- Light phase (Long Dark / Long Summer, with current day length).
- Supply state (each group's food/silver independently tracked).
- Wound states (injuries must be at the correct healing stage
  when groups reunite).

### Common Consistency Errors

| Error                  | What Goes Wrong                             | Prevention                            |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Timeline drift         | Group A has 3 days pass, Group B has 5      | Track day count per storyline         |
| Weather mismatch       | Blizzard for Group A, clear sky for Group B | Same weather system unless distant    |
| Wound healing mismatch | Injury too healed or not healed enough      | Track wound stages per character      |
| Supply inconsistency   | Group ate 4 days of food in 2 days          | Track rations per group per day       |
| Knowledge bleed        | Character knows something from wrong group  | Track information silos per character |

### The Sync Line

When cutting between storylines, include a physical detail that
anchors the reader to the shared timeline. This is the sync line.

**Sync line examples:**

- "The second watch had just changed." (Both groups use the same
  watch system.)
- "The snow had started again." (Both groups experience the same
  weather.)
- "Three days since the split." (Explicit time anchor, used once
  per storyline.)

Place the sync line in the first or second sentence of the new
storyline's scene. The reader orients temporally before
experiencing the scene.

## Structural Limits

### Maximum Concurrent Storylines: Two

The Iron Ledger does not run three simultaneous storylines. If the
band splits into three groups, two of them are combined into one
narrative track. The reader tracks Voss's group and one other.
The third group's experience arrives as news.

### Maximum Split Duration: Five Chapters

After five chapters of alternating between splits, the storylines
must converge or one must resolve. Extended parallel narration
dilutes the reader's investment in both lines. The band is the
novel's social unit -- the longer it is fragmented, the weaker
the narrative engine.

### Minimum Split Justification: Irreplaceable Perspective

A band split is a structural investment. It doubles the tracking
load for both writer and reader. The split is only justified if
the secondary storyline shows something the reader cannot get
any other way -- not convenience, not variety, but irreplaceable
perspective.

**Justified:** Gest discovers a contract betrayal at the employer's
town while Voss walks into the trap. The dramatic irony is
irreplaceable.

**Not justified:** Kell's group forages while Voss's group camps.
Nothing in the foraging party's experience is narratively
irreplaceable. Summarize it in a check-in.
