# Pacing and Rhythm — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** The tempo controls for prose at three scales:
sentence, chapter, and act. Pacing is not about speed — it
is about the ratio between compressed time and expanded time.
The reader unconsciously calibrates what matters by how long
the narrator spends on it. A march that takes two paragraphs
and a meal that takes two pages tells the reader the meal
matters more than the miles.

---

## Chapter-Level Pacing

### When to Compress Time

Compression means covering hours or days in sentences or
short paragraphs. Compress when the simulation state is
stable and nothing changes the band's trajectory.

**Compress these:**

- Routine marches without incident. Catalogue terrain and
  weather. Two to four sentences cover a day.
- Uneventful camp nights. One paragraph: fire, food, watch
  order. Done.
- Healing periods. State the wound's progress through
  physical markers: "The stitches held. Kell's arm was
  still stiff but he carried his own pack." Skip the days
  between.
- Resupply stops where no conflict occurs. Gest's ledger
  entry covers the transaction.
- Travel between contracts when the band's morale is
  stable and the treasury is adequate.

**Compression tools:**

- The Cook catalogue: "Three days north. Rain. Mud track
  that was not a road. A village that was not there anymore."
- The ledger summary: "Seven rations consumed. No incidents.
  Morale steady."
- The gesture shorthand: "Kell's arm was working again.
  Nobody remarked on it."

### When to Expand Time

Expansion means spending more page time than the event's
clock time warrants. Expand when the event changes the
band's state or advances a character arc.

**Expand these:**

- Combat. Always. Even a short fight gets full sensory
  rendering. The fifteen seconds it takes to kill a man
  deserve a page because the cost lasts longer than the act.
- Contract negotiations. The words matter because money
  matters. Every offer and counter-offer is rendered because
  the margin between survival and failure lives in the terms.
- Confrontations within the band. Morale breaks, grievances
  voiced, loyalty tested. These scenes expand because the
  band's cohesion is the novel's structural integrity.
- Barrow entries. The supernatural earns expansion through
  atmospheric pressure. The uncanny requires time to register
  in the body. Rush it and the reader feels nothing.
- The Hush. Any scene where the supernatural intrudes.
  Silence is expanded by describing what fills it — the
  absence of wind, the absence of birdsong, the way sound
  behaves when something is wrong.
- Meals and fire-circle scenes where hierarchy shifts. If
  someone sits in a new seat, the moment expands to let the
  reader notice.

### The Baseline Principle

Compression establishes the baseline. Expansion marks the
deviation. If every chapter is expanded, nothing stands out.
If every chapter is compressed, the reader never engages.

Target ratio: for every 3 chapters, approximately 2 should
contain significant compression (march, transition, routine
camp) and 1 should be substantially expanded (combat,
negotiation, supernatural, confrontation).

The compressed chapters are not filler. They are the
metronome that makes the expanded chapters feel like
disruptions.

## Sentence-Level Rhythm Patterns

### The Cook Catalogue

A list of concrete nouns — things carried, things seen,
things passed through — followed by silence. The list
accumulates weight through specificity. The silence after
lets the weight settle.

**Pattern:** Noun, noun, noun. Short sentence.

**Example:**

> Mud track. Stone marker. A burned shrine with no roof
> and the smell of old ash. We made camp where the trees
> thinned.

**When to use:** March sequences, inventory scenes, any
moment where the accumulation of physical detail IS the
point. The catalogue says: this is what exists. The short
sentence that follows says: and that is all.

**Danger:** Overuse makes the prose feel listy. Maximum
two catalogues per chapter. Space them with normal
paragraphs between.

### The Abercrombie Pivot

A setup that the reader expects to resolve one way,
followed by a reversal that resolves it the other way.
The pivot works through sentence rhythm — the setup is
longer, building expectation, and the reversal is short,
cutting the expectation off.

**Pattern:** Long setup sentence. Short reversal.

**Example:**

> Voss considered the offer, the elder's careful language,
> the steward's position by the door, the quality of the
> bread they had been served, the state of the palisade
> that told him more about the village's finances than any
> number the elder had produced. "No."

**When to use:** Decision moments, character reveals,
moments where a character's competence is demonstrated
through what they do not accept. The pivot shows the
character thinking and then acting — the gap between the
two is the character.

**Danger:** The pivot requires setup. If the long sentence
is not loaded with specific concrete detail, the short
reversal has no weight to cut against. A weak pivot is
a cliché.

### The Loaded Pause

Dialogue followed by silence followed by a physical
gesture. The silence between the words and the gesture
is where the meaning lives. The reader fills the pause
with inference.

**Pattern:** Dialogue. Beat of silence (described as
absence). Gesture.

**Example:**

> "The barrow is wrong," Thorne said.
>
> Nobody answered. The fire popped.
>
> Voss's hand moved to his axe-haft.

**When to use:** Group scenes where a statement changes
the room's temperature. The pause forces the reader to
register the statement's weight before the response
arrives. The gesture IS the response — not a verbal
one but a physical one.

**Danger:** The loaded pause requires genuine tension.
If the dialogue line is not consequential, the pause
reads as artificial slowness. Use only when the
statement actually matters.

### The Flat Report

A sequence of short declarative sentences with no
variation in rhythm. The narrator reports without
emphasis. The flatness is the point — the content is
terrible but the voice does not flinch.

**Pattern:** Subject verb object. Subject verb object.
Subject verb object.

**Example:**

> They found the village empty. The grain stores were
> ash. The well had a body in it.

**When to use:** Aftermath scenes. The discovery of
cost. Moments where the appropriate emotional response
is too large for the narrator to perform. Cook's
technique — the voice stays flat because the content
is too heavy for emphasis.

**Danger:** More than four flat-report sentences in
sequence becomes monotonous. Break the sequence with
one concrete sensory detail that is longer: what the
body in the well smelled like, what the ash felt like
underfoot. The detail interrupts the flatness and the
interruption is what the reader remembers.

### The Interior Fragment

A short, unpunctuated interior thought that sits inside
an action paragraph. Not a full interior monologue — a
flash of the POV character's assessment.

**Pattern:** Action. Interior fragment (no quotation marks,
present thought embedded in past narration). Action.

**Example:**

> Voss watched the men file past the elder's hall. Kell
> had his hand near the axe. Good. The elder's steward
> was counting them. That number would reach someone by
> nightfall.

**When to use:** Voss's POV chapters. His assessments
are compressed — one or two sentences embedded in an
action sequence. The reader hears Voss thinking without
the narration stopping for a thought.

**Danger:** Interior fragments must be distinct from the
narrator's voice. If the fragment sounds like narration,
the reader does not register it as thought. Keep
fragments shorter than the surrounding narration. Use
sentence structure the narrator would not use — the
fragment can be a single word ("Good.") or a judgment
the narrator would not make.

## Sentence Distribution Targets

SKILL.md defines quantitative targets for sentence-length
distribution across every chapter:

| Band    | Word Count | Target % | Primary Patterns                                    |
| ------- | ---------- | -------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Default | 10-18      | 60%      | Flat Report, narration                              |
| Short   | 2-7        | 25%      | Cook Catalogue items, fragments, Interior Fragments |
| Long    | 25-40      | 15%      | Abercrombie Pivot, Loaded Pause setups              |

**How the named patterns generate the distribution:**

- **The Cook Catalogue** generates short-band sentences (the
  list items) followed by a silence that is either short or
  default. A full catalogue produces 4-6 short sentences and
  1 default sentence. Two catalogues per chapter contribute
  roughly 8-12% of the short-band target.
- **The Abercrombie Pivot** generates one long-band sentence
  (the setup) followed by one short-band sentence (the
  reversal). Each pivot contributes to both ends of the
  distribution.
- **The Flat Report** generates default-band sentences. Four
  flat-report sentences in sequence is the maximum before
  rhythm flattens. This pattern is the backbone of the 60%
  default target.
- **The Loaded Pause** generates one default or long setup,
  then silence (rendered as a beat tag or gesture), then one
  short response. Three elements, three bands.
- **The Interior Fragment** generates short-band sentences
  (typically 2-5 words) embedded inside default narration.

**Paragraph targets:** 3-5 sentences per paragraph. Shorter
paragraphs (1-2 sentences) at pressure points. Longer
paragraphs only during catalogues or extended narration.

**Diagnostic:** If a chapter's sentence-length standard
deviation is below 4 (measured in word count), the rhythm
is too flat. See `anti-ai-detection-library.md` for the
StdDev threshold.

## Act-Level Pacing Across a Contract Arc

The novel's primary structural unit is the contract arc:
the band is hired, does the work, and gets paid (or does
not). A contract arc spans 3-8 chapters and has a
characteristic pacing shape.

### The Six Phases

The canonical phase structure is defined in `plot-planning.md`
(Contract Arc Template). The pacing profile for each phase:

| Phase           | Chapters | Pacing     | What Dominates                                  |
| --------------- | -------- | ---------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Contact      | 0-1      | Compressed | The band learns a contract exists. Rumor, word. |
| 2. Negotiation  | 1        | Expanded   | The deal. Terms, prices, conditions.            |
| 3. Approach     | 1-2      | Compressed | March to the work site. Time and supply cost.   |
| 4. Execution    | 1-2      | Expanded   | The job itself. Combat, barrow, escort, guard.  |
| 5. Complication | 0-1      | Mixed      | What went wrong. Counting cost. Wounded, dead.  |
| 6. Resolution   | 1        | Compressed | Payment or betrayal. Resupply. New baseline.    |

### Rhythm Rules Across the Arc

- **Never expand two consecutive phases.** If the negotiation
  was expanded, the approach must be compressed. If the
  execution was expanded across two chapters, the
  complication compresses the count into one scene.
- **The execution is always the pacing peak.** It gets the
  most page time and the most sensory detail. Everything
  before it builds toward it. Everything after it measures
  its cost.
- **The approach sets the metronome.** The first chapter of a
  contract arc is a march chapter — compressed, logistical,
  establishing the baseline. The reader calibrates "normal"
  from this chapter. Every deviation from normal in later
  chapters registers against this baseline.
- **The resolution phase resets.** After the cost is counted
  and payment received, a transition chapter or a camp chapter
  resets the band's state. This is the breathing space before
  the next arc begins.

### Multi-Arc Pacing

When contract arcs overlap with political or supernatural
arcs:

- The contract arc provides the surface structure (the
  timeline the reader follows).
- The political arc provides tension between contracts
  (faction pressure, employer betrayal, internal dissent).
- The supernatural arc provides escalation across contracts
  (each barrow worse than the last, each Hush longer).

The contract arc compresses and expands on its own schedule.
The political and supernatural arcs insert beats into
contract chapters without changing the contract arc's
pacing. A camp scene during a contract march might contain
a political beat (Ubbe testing the others' willingness to
desert) without the camp scene expanding beyond its normal
pacing.

### The Novel's Macro Rhythm

Across the full novel (multiple contract arcs):

- **Early contracts:** Short arcs (3-4 chapters). Small
  stakes. Establishing the band, the voice, the systems.
  The reader learns what normal looks like.
- **Middle contracts:** Longer arcs (5-6 chapters). Higher
  stakes. The band's state degrades — treasury thinner,
  morale lower, wounds accumulating. The arcs overlap.
- **Late contracts:** The longest arc (6-8 chapters). The
  stakes are existential. The contract, the political
  pressure, and the supernatural threat converge. Expansion
  dominates. The compressed baseline from early chapters
  makes the expansion feel earned.

The macro rhythm is a slow tightening. The gaps between
contracts get shorter. The breathing space gets thinner.
The reader should feel the margin narrowing the way Gest
feels it — in the numbers.
