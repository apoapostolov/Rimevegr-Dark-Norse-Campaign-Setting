# Adaptation Notes — Audio and Screenplay

Guidance for translating the Iron Ledger prose voice into other
media without losing what makes it work. The Cook-Abercrombie
DNA is a prose instrument — flat understatement, POV constraint,
ledger-closing, physical grounding. Not all of it survives media
translation. This document identifies what must survive, what
must transform, and what to cut.

---

## Part 1: Audiobook Adaptation

### What the Voice Sounds Like Read Aloud

The Iron Ledger prose is already close to oral storytelling.
Short sentences. Flat delivery. A veteran telling war stories
to someone who was there. The narrator does not perform
emotions — the narrator reports.

#### Narrator Selection Criteria

| Requirement                                        | Why                                                                                     |
| -------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Male, 35-50, low register                          | The voice of a man who has seen enough and is not impressed                             |
| Flat affect by default                             | The prose does its own work; vocal gymnastics fight it                                  |
| Capable of contained intensity                     | The 10% moments (supernatural encounters, combat turns) need subtle lift, not drama     |
| Comfortable with silence                           | Pauses between paragraphs are structural, not accidental                                |
| Multiple character voices — subtle, not cartoonish | Kell is clipped. Gest is precise. Dalla is quiet. Differences are in rhythm, not accent |

**Anti-pattern:** A narrator who performs the text — rising
pitch for tension, dramatic pauses for revelations, vocal
warmth for emotional moments. This prose was written cold. It
should sound cold.

#### Pacing Rules for Audio

- **Short sentences get silence after them.** A 4-word sentence
  followed by a 20-word sentence needs a beat of silence between
  them. The pause is the rhythm.
- **Combat passages accelerate.** Shorter pauses between
  sentences. The narrator's breathing should feel slightly
  shortened. Not shouting — compressed.
- **Ledger-closing paragraphs slow down.** These are the
  chapter's final accounting. The narrator reads them with the
  pace of a man writing in a book by poor light.
- **Dialogue is underplayed.** Characters do not emote through
  the narrator. "Kell said" is flat. The words themselves carry
  the emotion. If the narrator adds vocal emotion, the
  understatement is destroyed.

#### What Changes for Audio

| Prose Element                     | Audio Adaptation                                                                                          |
| --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| POV-internal thoughts             | Narrator delivers in same register as description — no "inner voice" shift                                |
| Simulation data rendered as prose | Works as-is; the reader-listener absorbs the numbers through the narrator's flat delivery                 |
| Silence beats in dialogue         | Literal silence. 1-2 seconds. The narrator does not fill it                                               |
| The closing ledger line           | Slight tempo reduction. The last sentence of each chapter lands with weight through pace, not performance |
| Em-dashes (the few we allow)      | A micro-pause, shorter than a period-pause. Not a dramatic break                                          |

#### What Does Not Work in Audio

- **Dense supply lists.** "Twelve sacks of grain, eight of
  salt, four barrels of ale" works on the page (the eye scans).
  In audio it becomes a grocery list. **Fix:** Reduce to the
  two most characterful items. "Twelve sacks of grain and the
  ale that cost more than the grain."
- **Simultaneous physical details.** "His left hand on the
  pommel, right on Kell's shoulder, boot wedged against the
  door-frame" works on the page (the eye constructs the image
  simultaneously). In audio it is sequential and loses the
  snapshot quality. **Fix:** Lead with the most important
  detail. Cut to two maximum.

## Part 2: Screenplay Adaptation

### The Translation Problem

The Iron Ledger voice lives in narration — POV-filtered
observation, physical grounding, understatement, the closing
ledger. Screenplay has no narrator. Everything must be
externalized: shown, spoken, or heard. The challenge is
preserving the voice without its primary instrument.

### What Survives Translation

#### Physical Grounding → Camera Language

The prose opens every chapter with a physical detail. The
camera does the same work.

| Prose                                           | Screen                                                                                  |
| ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Grey light. Thin wind. Voss was up before it." | DAWN. EXT. CAMP — grey light through fog. A tent flap stirs. Voss is already standing.  |
| "The barrow smelled of standing water"          | INT. BARROW — water drips from stone. Torchlight reaches four paces                     |
| Weather grounding every scene                   | Every establishing shot includes weather: rain on tents, frost on armour, mud underfoot |

**Rule:** If the prose opens with a sense-detail, the camera
opens with the visual equivalent. Weather is always visible.

#### Understatement → Performance Direction

The prose's flat affect translates to acting direction. The
screenplay must specify: no emoting. The actors underplay.

```text
VOSS
    (flat, not looking up)
  Before dawn. That's the decision.
```

Direction notes should include: "Play this like a tired man
giving an order, not a commander rallying troops."

#### The Ledger → Visual Motif

The closing ledger paragraph has no dialogue equivalent. It
becomes a visual motif: Gest writing in the actual ledger.

Every episode ends with a shot of Gest's hand, the book, the
numbers. No voice-over. The quill on parchment. The candle.
The closing of the book. This is the series' signature image.

#### Consequence Persistence → Production Design

Wounds persist in the screenplay as they do in the prose.
A character injured in Episode 2 wears bandages in Episode 3.
A burned settlement stays burned when the camera returns to
it six episodes later. The production design department
maintains a continuity bible derived from the series bible.

### What Must Be Invented for Screen

#### Subtext Made Visible

The prose conveys subtext through what the POV character
notices and does not notice. The camera needs physical
equivalents.

| Prose Subtext                                  | Screen Equivalent                                                                  |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Kell did not eat" (character observed by POV) | Camera holds on Kell's untouched bowl while others eat. 2 seconds. No dialogue     |
| "Gest had the ledger open but had not written" | Close-up: blank page, ink dry on the quill                                         |
| "Dalla was near the fire but not at it"        | Wide shot: the group clustered at the fire, Dalla at the edge of the light's reach |

**Rule:** Subtext that lives in narration on the page must
live in composition, blocking, and silence on screen. Never
in dialogue. Characters do not explain their feelings in this
world.

#### Sound Design as Voice Substitute

The prose's physical grounding (smell, touch, cold) cannot
all be shown. Sound design fills the gap.

| Prose Sense | Sound Design                                                                          |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Cold        | Wind. Cracking ice. The crunch of frozen ground                                       |
| Exhaustion  | Heavy breathing. Armour clanking arrhythmically. Silence where conversation should be |
| Dread       | Absence of birdsong. Distance between sounds growing                                  |
| The Veil    | Low-frequency hum just below conscious hearing. Not music — a pressure                |

**Rule:** The soundtrack is sparse. No orchestral score during
character scenes. Ambient sound only. Music reserved for
transitions and the 10% supernatural moments — and even then,
minimal.

#### Dialogue Expansion (Controlled)

The prose uses minimal dialogue (under 30% per chapter). The
screenplay needs more — characters must occasionally speak
where the narrator would have observed. The expansion rules:

1. **Never add thesis-statement dialogue.** No character says
   their theme. If the adaptation team adds lines, the lines
   must be functional: orders, logistics, questions about the
   immediate situation.
2. **Preserve talk budgets.** Kell speaks in fragments. Gest
   speaks in numbers. Voss speaks in orders. New lines must
   match the speech profiles from `dialogue-patterns.md`.
3. **Silence is still dialogue.** A scene can be 90 seconds
   with no words. The camera watches people work, march, eat.
   This is not dead time — it is the voice of the adaptation.
4. **New lines come from action, not emotion.** "Hand me
   that." "The bridge is out." "Three days, maybe four."
   Not: "I can't do this anymore." Not: "We've come too far
   to stop now."

### What to Cut for Screen

| Prose Element                                        | Screen Treatment                                                                           |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| POV internal narration                               | Cut. Show through behavior and camera                                                      |
| Extended supply logistics                            | Compress to one visual beat: Gest's ledger, a number, a look                               |
| Multiple simultaneous physical details               | Camera picks the one that matters most                                                     |
| Backstory delivered through present-moment callbacks | Flashbacks are forbidden. If the audience needs context, a character says one oblique line |
| The closing ledger prose                             | Replace with the Gest-writing visual motif. No voice-over                                  |

## Part 3: Cross-Media Constants

These elements must survive in any adaptation, regardless
of medium.

1. **Consequences persist.** The dead stay dead. Wounds show.
   Burned settlements stay burned. The adaptor does not get
   to soft-reset between episodes or volumes.

2. **The economics are visible.** The audience must always
   know, roughly, how much silver the band has and what it
   costs to exist. This is not background — it is the engine.

3. **Understatement is the voice.** The worst things are
   described/shown with the least emphasis. If an adaptation
   makes the violence louder or the emotions bigger, it has
   lost the voice.

4. **The supernatural is deniable.** Every weird event has a
   non-supernatural explanation available. Adaptation cannot
   show ghosts clearly. Show shadows. Show reactions. Let the
   audience decide.

5. **No heroes.** No swelling music. No slow-motion combat.
   No speeches before battle. No redemption arcs that resolve
   cleanly. These are working men in a dying trade. The
   adaptation must feel like work, not adventure.

6. **The ledger closes.** Every chapter/episode ends with
   cost. What was spent. What was lost. What the day cost in
   silver, food, time, or men. This is the series' structural
   signature and it does not change medium.
