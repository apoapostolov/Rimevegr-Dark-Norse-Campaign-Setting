# Dialogue Patterns — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** Character-specific dialogue rules, forbidden patterns,
and attribution mechanics for the novel. Dialogue in the Iron Ledger
is flat, transactional, and character-diagnostic — a reader should
be able to identify the speaker from the line alone.

---

## Universal Dialogue Rules

These apply to every character, every scene.

### Attribution

- **"Said" only.** No murmured, growled, snapped, whispered,
  hissed, declared, exclaimed.
- **Omit the tag when possible.** If two people are talking and
  the reader can track who is speaking, the tag is clutter. Drop
  it.
- **Beat tags over dialogue tags.** Instead of "said Gest,"
  write "Gest opened the ledger." The gesture replaces the tag
  and does double work — attribution plus characterization.

### Rhythm

- **Short.** Most spoken lines are one sentence. Two is a speech.
  Three is a monologue and should occur no more than twice in
  the entire novel.
- **No eloquence.** These men are not articulate. They speak in
  fragments, facts, and functional language. If a line sounds
  like it was composed, it belongs to a different book.
- **Silence is the default.** Most communication in the band is
  non-verbal — a look, a gesture, a shift in seating. Dialogue
  is what happens when silence is insufficient.

### Subtext

- **Characters never say what they mean directly.** Agreement
  comes through action, not words. Disapproval comes through
  silence or withdrawal, not argument.
- **No feelings vocabulary.** No character says "I'm afraid,"
  "I'm angry," "I trust you," or "I'm worried." Emotion is
  expressed through body, through action, through the thing they
  do instead of talking about the feeling.
- **Dialogue is transaction.** Every spoken line either requests,
  reports, commands, or negotiates. If a line does none of these,
  cut it.

## Character-Specific Dialogue Rules

### Lump

**Voice:** Minimal. Practical. The voice of a man who learned
that speaking draws attention, and attention is usually bad.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Offers labor, not opinions: "I can carry it." "I'll dig."
- Answers questions with the shortest possible true statement.
- When surprised, says nothing. Silence is his default response
  to the unexpected.

**Forbidden:**

- Lump does not make jokes. If he develops humor, it comes late
  and is dry, observational, and said once.
- Lump does not explain himself. If asked why he did something:
  shrug, or "Seemed right."
- Lump does not argue. He accepts or he endures.

**Sample lines:**

> "I can carry it."
>
> "Not well. But I've done it."
>
> [When praised — rare] A nod. Not even that. A half-nod.

### Gest

**Voice:** Numeric. Precise. Gest speaks the way the ledger reads
— entries, balances, deficits. When he speaks in non-numeric
terms, the structure is still transactional.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Leads with numbers: "Down to nine." "Three weeks at current
  burn." "Twelve copper per week."
- Frames all decisions as arithmetic: "We lose more keeping him
  than we gain."
- Gives advice in conditional structures: "If we move now, we
  make the ford by nightfall. If we wait, we don't."
- When the numbers are bad, his voice gets flatter, not louder.
  The worse the situation, the more clinical the language.

**Forbidden:**

- Gest does not use metaphor. Numbers are his metaphor.
- Gest does not make speeches. If he speaks more than three
  sentences, something is catastrophically wrong.
- Gest does not express concern for individuals. He expresses
  concern for the margin.

**Sample lines:**

> "Down to nine. Retainer savings: twelve copper per week. Food
> loss: half a day's supply."
>
> "Three weeks at current burn. After that we are choosing
> between paying the men and feeding them."
>
> "Keep it short. They don't need a speech. They need to know
> you know."
>
> [Writing in the ledger] "Two silver, signing cost. Dagfinn.
> He signed with an X."

---

### Kell

**Voice:** Silence is the primary mode. When he speaks, the words
carry disproportionate weight because of the silence that
surrounds them.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Observations rather than opinions. "That's a problem." (Not
  "I think that's a problem.") Stated flat, as measurement.
- Agreement expressed through action, not words. Kell does not
  say "yes." He picks up his axe, or he does not.
- Under pressure, shorter. One word. A look. The hand moves
  to the weapon. The weapon is the answer.
- When he voices a rare full thought, the band listens because
  Kell spoke and Kell does not speak.

**Forbidden:**

- Kell does not explain his reasoning. If he says "No," that
  is the entire communication.
- Kell does not speak about his feelings, his past, or his
  agenda. The missing fingers are visible. The story behind
  them is not offered.
- Kell does not threaten. Threats are declarations of intent.
  Kell acts without declaration.

**Sample lines:**

> "That's a problem."
>
> [When asked for an opinion] Silence.
>
> [When someone says something correct] A single nod. Not
> approval — acknowledgment.
>
> [When necessary] "Hold the gap." Two words. Then he does it.

---

### Voss

**Voice:** Command voice. Short, declarative, without softeners.
Voss does not request, ask, or suggest. He states what will
happen. The absence of qualification is what makes it an order.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Present-tense imperatives: "We march." "Keep it clean."
  "You cross first."
- Speaks to externals (employers, villagers) in a different
  register — slightly more words, the tone of negotiation
  rather than command. "Your village needs a patrol." This is
  both statement and sales pitch.
- Two-word evaluations: "That was right." (highest praise)
  "Not enough." (judgment)
- Never explains strategy to the men. Explanation implies
  uncertainty. Certainty is performed through brevity.

**Forbidden:**

- Voss does not apologize. If he makes a bad call, the band
  absorbs it. He does not acknowledge the error — he adjusts
  the next decision to compensate. The adjustment is the
  apology.
- Voss does not share personal information with the band. The
  blood-debt is private. If it surfaces, it surfaces through
  action, not confession.
- Voss does not raise his voice. The quieter he gets, the more
  dangerous the situation.

**Sample lines:**

> "We march at first light."
>
> "You cross first. If it holds your weight, it holds ours."
>
> "That was right."
>
> "We buy fair and leave clean. One theft and the gates close."

**POV interiority (when POV chapter):**

Voss's internal voice is compressed assessment. He does not
narrate feelings. He reads his men the way Gest reads the
ledger — who is reliable, who is close to breaking, where the
margin is. Judgments land as short, unpunctuated thoughts:
"Kell would hold. Orm might." The blood-debt surfaces only in
quiet moments and never as explanation — fragments the reader
assembles across chapters.

### Thorne

**Voice:** Private and flat. Thorne delivers warnings the way
Gest delivers numbers — as facts, not emotions. His specific
quality is catching people alone to tell them things the group
should not hear.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Warnings framed as fact: "The barrow is wrong." "Do not lie
  to a man with rune-lore." "Build your will."
- Speaks of the supernatural in practical terms — wards, signs,
  costs. No mystification. The runes are a tool. Knowledge of
  the unseen is a trade, like Gest's arithmetic.
- When translating runes or reading signs, reports findings in
  the same register as a scout report: "Two days old. Binding
  ward. Someone wanted this door closed."
- His advice to Lump is specific and flat: "Build your will.
  Pray if you know how. And if the whispers come again, do not
  answer."

**Forbidden:**

- Thorne does not perform mystery. He does not speak in riddles
  or prophecy. If the runes say something, he translates it
  into plain language.
- Thorne does not make public warnings. His approach is always
  private — a hand on the arm, a word by the fire when others
  sleep.
- Thorne does not discuss his burn scars or the hall fire. The
  scars speak. Thorne does not explain what they say.

**Sample lines:**

> "Do not lie to a man with rune-lore."
>
> "Build your will. Pray if you know how."
>
> "Something walked here. Not recently. But it remembers the
> path."
>
> [To Voss, privately] "The men need to know. Not what I saw.
> Just that the door stays shut."

### Petra

**Voice:** Report voice — the same register as Gest but applied
to terrain, game, and sign rather than silver and headcount.
Petra speaks to communicate findings, not to connect.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Observations delivered as intelligence: "Hadric's band. They
  burned Feldwick last month." "He went south. Fast. He planned
  this."
- Borrows Gest's idiom when food is involved: "Fourteen mouths.
  That gives us one more day before deficit."
- Does not speak about hunts afterward. Returns with the kill.
  The kill is the report. No narrative added.
- When correcting someone's fieldcraft, states fact: "Wrong
  tree. That one's wet inside."

**Forbidden:**

- Petra does not boast about kills or hunts. The game strung
  on her shoulder is the statement.
- Petra does not discuss the missing sister in casual
  conversation. The agenda is private. If it surfaces, it
  surfaces through behavior — a trail followed too far, a
  question asked in a village that has nothing to do with the
  contract.
- Petra does not complain. Problems are reported. Solutions are
  implemented. The emotional cost is invisible.

**Sample lines:**

> "Hadric's band. They burned Feldwick last month."
>
> "Fourteen mouths. That gives us one more day before deficit."
>
> [Returns from hunt. Drops two hares by the fire. Silence.]
>
> "He went south. Fast. He planned this."

### Ash

**Voice:** Contractor voice. States capabilities and price.
Does not sell. Does not mystify. The galdr is a craft. She
describes it the way a blacksmith describes ironwork.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Capability statements: "Galdr. Wound-closing, enough to save
  a man who would otherwise bleed out. Rune-reading at a
  distance of two days."
- Terms stated once: "Full share. My own fire. No one touches
  my kit without asking." This is not repeated. If violated,
  the consequence is departure, not argument.
- "I've walked before." — her answer to skepticism. The past is
  sufficient evidence. She does not elaborate.
- After performing galdr, she speaks less, not more. The work
  takes something. The silence afterward is the cost becoming
  visible.

**Forbidden:**

- Ash does not explain how the galdr works. The men do not ask.
  This is the arrangement both parties prefer.
- Ash does not negotiate her terms. They were stated. Acceptance
  or rejection. No middle ground.
- Ash does not express vulnerability after galdr use. The cost is
  shown through body — a slower walk, a hand that shakes when
  she lifts the satchel — not through admission.

**Sample lines:**

> "Galdr. Wound-closing. Rune-reading. Ward-laying."
>
> "Full share. My own fire."
>
> "I've walked before."
>
> [After healing Kell, walks away without looking back. No
>
> > dialogue. The transaction is complete.]

---

### Dalla

**Voice:** Instructional. Dalla speaks in the language of her
craft — food, fire, portions. She corrects. She does not
converse.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Instructions framed as physical fact: "Smaller pieces. Small
  burns hot and fast. Big burns slow and smoky."
- Does not address people by name. Addresses them by function
  or position: "You — stir." "Next." "Done."
- Silence as the default. The stew speaks for her. The serving
  order speaks for her. If the food is good, she has done her
  job. If the food is bad, circumstances are bad. Either way,
  discussion is unnecessary.

**Forbidden:**

- Dalla does not engage in band politics. The cook-fire is
  outside politics.
- Dalla does not make small talk. Every word from Dalla is an
  instruction or a correction.
- Dalla does not complain about her workload. The work is the
  work.

**Sample lines:**

> "Smaller pieces. Small burns hot and fast."
>
> [Serves Voss first. Named Men. Veterans. Commons. No words
>
> > needed. The order is the communication.]
>
> "Next."

---

### Snorri

**Voice:** Fireside voice, ale-activated. Snorri speaks in
stories and counts. The stories begin with specifics and end
without morals. The listener does the work.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Counts as biography: "Six captains. Three I buried. Two
  deserted. One I killed myself."
- Advice given as fact, stated once: "Being useful. Not brave.
  Not strong. Useful."
- Stories that start with "I knew a man who..." and end with
  a silence that means more than the conclusion would.
- When sober, much quieter. The difference between sober-Snorri
  and ale-Snorri is itself a characterization tool.

**Forbidden:**

- Snorri does not give advice twice. If it is ignored, the
  account is closed.
- Snorri does not speak at length when sober. The ale is the
  mechanism. Without it, he is another quiet veteran.
- Snorri does not sentimentalize the past. His stories about
  dead captains are not eulogies. They are inventory.

**Sample lines:**

> "Being useful. Not brave. Not strong. Useful."
>
> "Six captains. Three I buried. Two deserted. One I killed
> myself."
>
> [After the second ale] "I knew a captain once who marched his
> band three days without water because a jarl's man told him
> there was a hall with silver at the end. There wasn't."
> [Silence. No moral. Lump does the work.]

---

### Orm

**Voice:** Developing. Orm has not found his voice yet because
the band has not yet decided what he is. His dialogue is
functional and carefully neutral — he gives nothing away.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Competence statements: "I can hold a line. I can dig."
- Non-committal responses: "Does it matter?" (Shrug.)
- Echoes the speech patterns of whoever Orm is near — a subtle
  chameleon quality that may indicate adaptability or may indicate
  concealment.
- When uncertain, defaults to silence. The silence is Kell's
  pattern. The proximity is not accidental.

**Forbidden:**

- Orm does not discuss his past in detail. "I've done that
  before" is the maximum disclosure.
- Orm does not express opinions about the band's direction. He
  follows. Whether the following is loyalty or calculation is
  the open question.
- Orm does not yet have distinctive verbal tics. He acquires
  them as the band shapes him — or as his true nature surfaces.

**Sample lines:**

> "I can hold a line. I can dig. I can walk thirty miles if
> someone feeds me at the end."
>
> "Does it matter?"
>
> [Shrug. The shrug is his primary response to questions that
>
> > ask for more than he is willing to give.]

---

### Ubbe

**Voice:** Terse fact. Ubbe speaks only when silence would cost
more than words. Every line is an observation delivered without
investment.

**Characteristic patterns:**

- Observations without opinion: "That won't hold." "She's
  lying." "We're short."
- Does not engage in debate. States the fact and stops.
- When speaking about plans or future actions, uses second
  person: "You'll need more men." Never first person plural.
  The "we" is what he is not committing to.

**Forbidden:**

- Ubbe does not speak about his plans. His plans are visible
  only through behavior — the hidden coin, the eating alone,
  the pack that is always ready.
- Ubbe does not express loyalty or attachment. He professes
  nothing and promises nothing.
- Ubbe does not argue about decisions. He complies or he
  disappears. There is no middle state.

**Sample lines:**

> "That won't hold."
>
> "She's lying."
>
> "You'll need more men." [Note: "you," not "we."]

## Oath-Phrases and Invocations

The band invokes the gods through casual oaths, not prayer.
These are verbal habits — swearing, emphasis, superstition —
not theological statements. See `pantheon.md` for the full
pantheon and rendering rules.

### Common Oath-Phrases

| God    | Oath-Phrase             | Usage Context                        |
| ------ | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Thurr  | "Thurr's cracked hands" | Exasperation, physical hardship      |
| Odynn  | "Odynn's rope"          | Acknowledging fate, inevitability    |
| Hael   | "Hael's chair"          | Proximity to death, dread, the sick  |
| Fraeja | "Fraeja take me"        | Rare — grief, exhaustion, surrender  |
| Tyvr   | "Tyvr's stump"          | Before combat, acknowledging cost    |
| Skathi | "Bone-Walker"           | Whispered — wilderness fear, barrows |

### Who Uses Them

- **Voss:** Rarely. One oath per several chapters. When Voss
  swears, it registers.
- **Gest:** "Thurr's cracked hands" when the numbers are bad.
  His only expressive indulgence.
- **Snorri:** The most frequent invoker — oaths in fireside
  stories, casual speech. Part of his Old Campaigner idiom.
- **Kell:** Never. Kell does not invoke the gods.
- **Thorne:** Never invokes — he reads runes but treats gods
  as conditions, not beings to call upon.
- **Dalla:** Never invokes aloud. Her relationship with the
  gods (especially Fraeja) is private and fearful.
- **Others:** Commons use oaths freely. Named Men use them
  sparingly. Frequency correlates inversely with authority.

### Rules

- Oaths are exclamations, not addressed prayers. Nobody asks
  the gods for anything. They use god-names the way modern
  people say "Jesus Christ" — as emphasis, not petition.
- Never have an oath answered or acknowledged by the narrative.
- Keep oath-phrase vocabulary stable per character — Gest
  always uses Thurr, not a rotating selection.

## Dialogue Scene Construction

### Two-Person Scenes

The default dialogue scene is two characters, 4-8 lines total.

Structure:

1. **Setting beat** — one sentence establishing physical context.
   Not atmosphere. Location and action. "Gest had the ledger
   open on his knee."
2. **Exchange** — 2-4 lines of dialogue. Short. One person talks
   more than the other.
3. **Gesture close** — the scene ends on a physical action, not
   a spoken line. Someone stands, someone opens the ledger,
   someone walks away.

Never end a dialogue scene on a spoken line. End on the silence
after. The silence carries the meaning the words did not.

### Group Scenes

Rare. When necessary (planning, contract negotiation, pay
ritual):

- Maximum 4 speaking characters. Everyone else is present but
  silent.
- One person dominates (usually Voss or Gest). Others contribute
  single lines.
- The silent characters are described by position and gesture,
  not by unexpressed thoughts.
- End on dispersal: the group breaks up, the fire dies, someone
  leaves first.

### Forbidden Dialogue Patterns (Universal)

| Pattern                                         | Why it fails                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Speeches longer than 3 sentences                | Nobody in a mercenary band gives speeches. Speeches are for politicians and the dead.                                                                                                                          |
| Rhetorical questions                            | Implies performative thinking. These men do not perform.                                                                                                                                                       |
| Pet names, terms of endearment                  | The band uses call-names, given names, or "you." Nothing softer.                                                                                                                                               |
| Expository dialogue                             | "As you know, we are heading north to..." — characters do not explain what both speakers already know.                                                                                                         |
| Argument as dialogue                            | Two characters shouting at each other. Rejected. Band conflicts are expressed through silence, withdrawal, looking away. If a conflict becomes verbal, it is two sentences before it becomes physical or ends. |
| Dialogue tags beyond "said"                     | Already covered above. Repeated for emphasis. No alternatives. Ever.                                                                                                                                           |
| Internal monologue presented as muttered speech | Characters do not mutter their thoughts. If the reader needs to know a character's reaction, show it through body.                                                                                             |
| Humor explained                                 | If a line is funny, the next line does not acknowledge it. Other characters may or may not react. The humor succeeds through understatement, not through the audience laughing.                                |
| Accents or dialect written phonetically         | No "ye" for "you," no dropped consonants, no apostrophe-heavy dialect. Voice is expressed through vocabulary and rhythm, not spelling.                                                                         |
| "Listen," "Look," "Hey" as dialogue openers     | Filler that real speech uses, but written dialogue must not. Start on the information.                                                                                                                         |
