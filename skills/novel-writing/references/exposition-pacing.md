# Exposition Pacing — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** How to distribute world-building information across
chapters without info-dumping. The Iron Ledger's core principle is
"world-building through transaction" -- the reader learns about the
Rimevegr by watching people buy, sell, negotiate, suffer, and survive
within it. This file defines the information budget, delivery methods,
and pacing rules that prevent exposition from overwhelming narrative.

> **Cross-reference:** What the world contains is in
> `01_RIMEVEGR_SETTING_BIBLE.md`. How chapters are structured is in
> `chapter-structure.md`. This file covers how to distribute that
> world content across those chapters.

## The Information Budget

### Per-Chapter Limit

Every chapter can introduce a maximum of two new world-building
concepts. A "concept" is any piece of information the reader has
not encountered before: a social custom, a political relationship,
a geographical fact, a historical event, a supernatural rule, an
economic convention.

| Chapter Type | New Concepts Allowed | Reason                                    |
| ------------ | -------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| March        | 1                    | Physical experience dominates; one detail |
| Camp         | 2                    | Conversation and observation make space   |
| Negotiation  | 2                    | Terms reveal social and economic rules    |
| Combat       | 0-1                  | Action cannot pause for world-building    |
| Settlement   | 2                    | New location justifies new information    |
| Barrow       | 1                    | Supernatural focus; one lore detail only  |
| Aftermath    | 1                    | Cost accounting, not exposition           |
| Interlude    | 2                    | Lower-energy chapters can absorb more     |

### Cumulative Load

Track the reader's information load across consecutive chapters.
After two chapters that each introduced two new concepts, the next
chapter should introduce zero. The pattern is: load, load, breathe.
Three consecutive concept-heavy chapters exhaust the reader's
willingness to absorb new information.

**The breathing chapter** is not empty. It reinforces previously
introduced concepts by showing them in action. The feud escalation
system explained in Chapter 5 is demonstrated without comment in
Chapter 8. The currency rules introduced during a negotiation
reappear when the band counts its pay. Reinforcement feels like
story. Repeated introduction feels like a textbook.

## Delivery Methods

### Method 1: Transaction (Primary)

The reader learns a world-building fact because a character is
doing something that requires that fact.

**How it works:** Voss negotiates a contract. The negotiation
requires understanding weregild pricing. The reader learns
weregild pricing by watching the negotiation happen -- not through
a narrator aside, but through the numbers Voss and the jarl argue
over.

**Rules:**

- The transaction must be real. A character is buying, selling,
  hiring, paying, punishing, rewarding, or exchanging. The
  information is the price, the terms, or the context.
- The character does not explain the system. The character acts
  within the system. The reader infers the system from the action.
- If the concept is too complex for the reader to infer from a
  single transaction, it appears across multiple transactions in
  different chapters. The reader assembles the picture.

**Example:** The reader learns that coins are weighed, not
counted, because Gest puts a coin on a balance and the merchant
argues about the weight. The word "weighed" appears in an action
sentence, not an explanatory one.

### Method 2: Correction (Secondary)

A character gets something wrong and another character corrects
them. The correction delivers the world-building fact.

**How it works:** Kell assumes a thrall was captured in battle.
A veteran says, "Debt. Lost a crop bet." Two words deliver the
concept that thralldom is economic.

**Rules:**

- The correction must be brief. One sentence. Two at most.
- The corrector does not lecture. They state the fact and move
  on. The fact is obvious to them and boring.
- The character being corrected must be someone who would
  plausibly not know. Kell is the primary vehicle for corrections
  because he is new to the band and ignorant of its world.

### Method 3: Contrast (Tertiary)

The reader learns what the Rimevegr is by seeing what it is not.
A character expects one thing and encounters another.

**How it works:** A settlement has no temple. No one remarks on
the absence. The reader who has been assuming temples exist now
understands the Cracking's cultural impact -- not through
explanation, but through the missing building.

**Rules:**

- The contrast must be silent. No character says "There is no
  temple here because the gods went silent." The absence speaks.
- This method works only for readers who have baseline knowledge
  of Norse culture (the target audience). The contrast depends
  on the reader's existing frame creating the expectation.
- Use sparingly. More than one silent contrast per chapter
  requires too much reader inference and begins to feel like a
  puzzle rather than a story.

### Method 4: Ritual Observation (Situational)

The reader watches a social ritual happen without explanation.
The ritual's meaning emerges from context.

**How it works:** The band arrives at a settlement. The headman
does not open the gate. Voss and the headman talk through the
palisade. Only after terms are agreed does the gate open. The
reader has learned the quartering protocol without being told
it exists.

**Rules:**

- Show the full ritual. Do not skip steps for efficiency. The
  ritual's meaning is in the sequence of actions.
- No narrator gloss. Do not insert "This was the custom" or
  "As was traditional." The characters do not comment on customs
  they find normal.
- The first occurrence teaches. Subsequent occurrences can be
  abbreviated because the reader now knows the pattern.

## Pacing Rules

### Rule 1: Front-Load Nothing

Chapter 1 introduces zero world-building concepts explicitly. The
reader enters through Voss's body: cold, axe, weight, fire. The
world reveals itself through physical sensation. The Rimevegr is
not explained -- it is felt. By the end of Chapter 1, the reader
knows it is cold, dark, dangerous, and poor. They do not know why.

### Rule 2: Escalate Complexity Gradually

| Novel Section  | Concept Complexity     | Example                              |
| -------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Chapters 1-5   | Physical and immediate | Weather, food, shelter, hierarchy    |
| Chapters 6-12  | Social and economic    | Currency, contracts, feud, thralldom |
| Chapters 13-20 | Political and cultural | Factions, thing-assembly, oaths      |
| Chapters 21+   | Historical and cosmic  | The Cracking, the Veil, the gods     |

The reader learns the world in concentric circles: body first,
then band, then settlement, then region, then history. Each circle
contains the previous one. The reader never encounters a concept
they lack the foundation to understand.

### Rule 3: Settlement Chapters Are Not Catalogs

Settlement chapters are the highest-risk chapters for info-dumping.
The band arrives at a new place with new people, new politics, new
geography. The temptation is to describe everything.

**Prevention:**

- The band does not tour the settlement. They go where necessity
  takes them: the gate, the hall, the market, the quartering.
  Each location reveals exactly what the scene requires.
- Voss notices threats and resources. Not architecture, not
  history, not local color. What can feed the band. What can
  kill the band. What is the gate situation.
- New characters are introduced through function, not
  description. "The headman" is enough. His name comes when he
  matters. His appearance comes when Voss has reason to study
  him.

### Rule 4: Repeat Through New Context

A concept introduced in one chapter should reappear in a different
context 3-8 chapters later. The reappearance tests whether the
reader absorbed the concept and deepens it.

**Example:** Weregild is introduced during a negotiation
(Chapter 7). It reappears when the band must pay one after killing
a freeman in a fight (Chapter 12). The second occurrence adds
emotional weight to an economic concept the reader already
understands.

### Rule 5: Trust the Reader After Two Exposures

After a concept has appeared twice in distinct contexts, treat it
as known. Do not re-explain. Do not provide reminders. The reader
who paid attention knows. The reader who did not will either figure
it out from context or accept the gap.

## Information Density by Scene Type

### High-Density Scenes (2 concepts)

- **Negotiation:** Contract terms reveal economic and social rules.
- **Thing-assembly:** Legal proceedings reveal political structure.
- **First arrival at a new settlement:** Geography and social
  layout are implicit in the band's navigation.

### Medium-Density Scenes (1 concept)

- **Camp:** A single custom, tradition, or social dynamic.
- **March:** One geographical or weather detail that matters.
- **Aftermath:** One consequence that reveals a social rule.

### Low-Density Scenes (0 concepts)

- **Combat:** No new information. Everything here should have been
  established before the fight. Combat tests what the reader knows,
  it does not teach.
- **Chase / escape:** Pure action. The reader's existing knowledge
  of terrain and weather creates the tension.
- **Grief / loss:** Emotional processing. The world recedes. The
  character's interior is the only landscape.

## The Exposition Debt

Information the reader needs but has not received yet creates
exposition debt. The longer a debt is held, the more interest it
accrues -- the reader's confusion or assumptions compound.

### Managing Debt

| Debt Age      | Reader State      | Action Required                  |
| ------------- | ----------------- | -------------------------------- |
| 1-3 chapters  | Mild curiosity    | None. Healthy tension.           |
| 4-7 chapters  | Active question   | Begin delivery. Transaction.     |
| 8-12 chapters | Frustration risk  | Deliver or confirm it is mystery |
| 13+ chapters  | Assumption locked | Too late for clean delivery      |

**Rule:** Never let exposition debt exceed 12 chapters unless the
information is intentionally withheld as a plot mechanism (the
Cracking's true cause, the nature of draugr). Functional
world-building information -- how things work in this world --
must be delivered within the window or the reader builds wrong
assumptions that later corrections feel like retcons.

### Deliberate Debt (Mystery)

Some information is intentionally withheld:

- What exactly happened during the Cracking.
- Whether the gods are truly gone or merely silent.
- What draugr actually are.
- Whether Dalla's voices are real.

These debts are never paid. They are the novel's ongoing questions.
The reader should know they are questions, not gaps. The difference:
a gap feels like the author forgot. A question feels like the
author chose.

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern              | What It Looks Like                              | Fix                                       |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| The History Lesson        | A paragraph of backstory mid-scene              | Break into 3-4 transactions across chs    |
| The Knowledgeable Newbie  | Kell knows things he should not know            | Route info through correction instead     |
| The Tour Guide            | Settlement described feature by feature         | Character goes only where necessity sends |
| The As-You-Know           | Characters explain things to each other         | Cut the dialogue. Show through action.    |
| The Footnote              | Narrator aside explaining a custom or term      | Let the custom appear without comment     |
| The Front-Loaded Prologue | Chapter 1 explains the world before story       | Delete and redistribute across Act I      |
| The Repeated Explainer    | Same concept re-explained chapter after chapter | Trust the reader after two exposures      |
