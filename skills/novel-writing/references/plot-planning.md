# Plot Planning — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** Templates for structuring multi-chapter narrative arcs.
The Iron Ledger operates on three interlocking arc types: contract
arcs (the economic spine), political arcs (faction pressure), and
supernatural arcs (escalating wrongness). Every chapter belongs to
at least one arc. Most chapters serve two. The logistical baseline
runs beneath all of them.

---

## The Contract Arc

The novel's primary structural unit. The band is hired, does the
work, and gets paid — or does not. Every contract arc follows the
same economic logic: someone has a problem they cannot solve with
their own people, the band has swords and a price, and the margin
between the two determines whether the contract is worth the blood.

### Contract Arc Template (3-8 chapters)

| Phase           | Chapters | Function                                                                                           |
| --------------- | -------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Contact      | 0-1      | The band learns a contract exists. A messenger, a rumor at a settlement, Gest's network.           |
| 2. Negotiation  | 1        | Voss and the employer across a table. Terms, price, conditions. Gest's numbers running beneath.    |
| 3. Approach     | 1-2      | March to the work site. Supply consumption. Weather. The column moving toward something.           |
| 4. Execution    | 1-3      | The job itself. Combat, escort, guard duty, barrow clearance. The event that earns the silver.     |
| 5. Complication | 0-1      | The contract was not what it appeared. The employer lied. The target changed. The price was wrong. |
| 6. Resolution   | 1        | Payment or betrayal. The cost counted. The margin assessed. The band's state after.                |

### Contract Arc Rules

1. **Every contract changes the band's state.** The band that
   finishes a contract is not the band that started it. Someone
   is wounded, dead, richer, poorer, more loyal, or gone. If the
   band's state did not change, the contract was not worth
   narrating.

2. **The margin determines the tone.** If the contract pays well
   and costs little: a brief, efficient arc (3-4 chapters). If
   the margin is thin: a tense arc where every decision is
   weighed. If the contract costs more than it pays: a grim arc
   where the question is whether to finish or walk away.

3. **The complication is optional but expected.** Not every contract
   has a twist. Some jobs are exactly what they appear. But the
   reader expects complications, and the absence of one is itself
   a kind of tension — the reader waits for the other boot and
   it never drops.

4. **Contracts stack.** The consequences of Contract A are still
   active during Contract B. Wounds from the first fight have not
   healed. The silver from the first payment is already spent.
   The settlement the band passed through remembers what happened.

### Contract Complexity Scale

| Complexity | Chapters | When to Use                                                                                |
| ---------- | -------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Simple     | 3-4      | Early novel. Establishing rhythm. Low stakes, clear terms.                                 |
| Standard   | 5-6      | Mid novel. Higher stakes. Complication present. Margin thinner.                            |
| Complex    | 7-8      | Late novel. Multiple complications. Political or supernatural overlay. Existential margin. |

## The Political Arc

Faction dynamics that operate between and during contracts. The
political arc answers: who holds power, who wants it, and what the
band's presence changes about that equation.

### Political Arc Template

| Phase              | Function                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Faction reveal  | The power structure is shown through observable facts: who owns the hall, who controls the grain.                                                                               |
| 2. Band positioned | The contract places the band on one side of a factional line. The band may not know which side.                                                                                 |
| 3. Pressure        | One or more factions attempt to use, buy, threaten, or redirect the band. Offers arrive.                                                                                        |
| 4. Decision        | Voss chooses. The choice is always economic first, political second. The band goes where the silver is, unless the silver's source is poisoned enough to kill future contracts. |
| 5. Consequence     | The faction the band did not serve remembers. Standing changes. Gates close or open.                                                                                            |

### Political Arc Rules

1. **Factions are shown, not explained.** The narrator does not
   map the political landscape. The reader assembles it from
   observable facts: who sits at the high table, who controls the
   granary, whose men carry better weapons, who sends servants to
   deliver messages instead of coming themselves.

2. **The band is a tool, not a faction.** The band does not have
   political opinions. It has a price. When factions try to make
   the band an ally, Voss's calculation is always: what does this
   cost, what does it pay, and who will remember when it is over.

3. **Political arcs are longer than contract arcs.** A political
   arc might span two or three contracts. The faction that hired
   the band for Contract A may be the faction the band fights
   against in Contract C. The political arc connects the contracts
   into a larger pattern.

4. **Betrayal is arithmetic.** When an employer betrays the band
   (short payment, changed terms, ambush), Voss does not rage.
   He adjusts the ledger. The betrayal becomes a debt. Debts are
   collected or written off. Both options cost something.

5. **Standing is permanent.** If the band burns a village's trust,
   that village does not quietly forgive. The consequence persists.
   Political arcs do not reset between contracts.

## The Supernatural Arc

Escalating wrongness that operates beneath the contract and
political layers. The supernatural arc is the novel's deep
structure — the pressure that makes the world stranger and more
dangerous as the story progresses.

### Supernatural Arc Template

| Phase        | Function                                                                                      |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Whisper   | The first sign. A detail that is wrong — temperature shift, a sound that should not be there, |
|              | rune-stones humming, Thorne's hand on his amulet. The band notices. Nobody names it.          |
| 2. Event     | The supernatural becomes undeniable. A barrow that breathes. A dead man's shadow in the wrong |
|              | place. Something the band's swords cannot answer. Thorne speaks. The men listen.              |
| 3. Cost      | The supernatural demands a price. Not a bargain — an extraction. Morale drops. A man breaks.  |
|              | Sleep goes wrong. The band's practical systems (food, march, discipline) degrade.             |
| 4. Crisis    | The supernatural and the practical collide. A contract in a barrow that is actively wrong.    |
|              | The band must choose between the silver and the safety. The choice costs either way.          |
| 5. Aftermath | What the supernatural left behind. Not resolution — residue. The band survived it             |
|              | but the surviving changed something. Thorne knows more than he did. The men trust less.       |

### Supernatural Arc Rules

1. **Physical symptoms only.** The supernatural is rendered through
   what the body feels, not what the mind understands. Temperature
   shifts. The Hush — the absence of natural sound. Shadows
   misaligned with their sources. A smell that is not rot but is
   not right. The narrator describes symptoms. The narrator never
   describes causes.

2. **Thorne is the interpreter, not the explainer.** Thorne reads
   the signs. His warnings are flat and factual: "The barrow is
   wrong." "Do not sleep near the south wall." He does not explain
   the mechanism. His knowledge is practical: what to avoid, what
   to respect, when to leave. If pressed for why, his answer is
   a variant of "Because I said so and I am the one who knows."

3. **Escalation across the novel.** The first supernatural event
   is small — a feeling, a misplaced shadow, a night where sleep
   goes wrong. The second is larger. The third is undeniable. The
   escalation is not linear — there are quiet stretches between
   events. The quiet is when the men decide the last event was
   nothing. The next event proves them wrong.

4. **The supernatural does not follow the band's arithmetic.**
   Gest cannot price it. Voss cannot outmaneuver it. Kell cannot
   fight it. The supernatural is the one force in the novel that
   does not respond to competence. This is what makes it
   frightening in a world where competence is the only currency.

5. **No explanations. Ever.** The reader, like the band, does not
   learn what the barrows contain, why the dead do not rest, or
   what the Hush means. The supernatural is rendered as experience,
   not as lore. If the reader wants to understand, the reader does
   the work — the same work Thorne does, from the same insufficient
   evidence.

## Interlacing Rules

How to weave contract, political, and supernatural arcs together
without losing the logistical baseline.

### The Layer Principle

At any point in the novel, the reader should be able to identify:

1. **The active contract** — what the band is being paid to do
   right now. If between contracts, what the band is looking for.
2. **The political pressure** — which factions are relevant and
   what they want from the band. If no active faction, the
   absence is the political state.
3. **The supernatural state** — is the world currently quiet or
   wrong? If quiet, how long since the last event? If wrong,
   what symptoms are present?
4. **The logistical baseline** — what the treasury holds, what the
   food state is, what the morale is, who is wounded.

### Rules for Interlacing

1. **One arc dominates per chapter.** A chapter is primarily a
   contract chapter, a political chapter, or a supernatural
   chapter. The other arcs are present as pressure, not as focus.
   A contract negotiation chapter may include a political beat
   (the employer's faction allegiance revealed through observable
   detail) without becoming a political chapter.

2. **The logistical baseline is always present.** No matter which
   arc dominates, the band's practical state is rendered. If the
   chapter is a supernatural barrow scene, the reader still knows
   what the supply state is, who is wounded, and whether the
   column can march tomorrow. The baseline is the foundation
   that makes the arcs feel real.

3. **Arc beats do not compete within a chapter.** If the chapter
   advances the contract arc (a fight, a negotiation, a march),
   the political and supernatural arcs do not also advance. They
   may apply pressure — a political faction's messenger arrives
   during a contract march, creating tension — but the beat
   belongs to the contract arc.

4. **Transitions between arc dominance happen at chapter
   boundaries.** If Chapter 7 is a contract chapter and Chapter 8
   is a supernatural chapter, the transition happens between
   chapters, not within them. The hard break is the shift. The
   opening of Chapter 8 re-grounds the reader in the new
   dominant arc.

5. **The supernatural arc overrides when it manifests.** When
   the Hush falls or a barrow wakes, the contract and political
   arcs pause. The men stop caring about silver when the dead
   are moving. After the supernatural event passes, the other
   arcs resume — but altered. The contract may be abandoned. The
   faction dynamics may have shifted. The supernatural does not
   just interrupt — it changes the landscape the other arcs
   operate in.

### Arc Density by Novel Phase

| Novel phase | Contract | Political | Supernatural | Baseline weight |
| ----------- | -------- | --------- | ------------ | --------------- |
| Opening     | Primary  | Absent    | Whisper      | Heavy           |
| Early       | Primary  | Emerging  | Quiet        | Heavy           |
| Middle      | Primary  | Active    | Event        | Medium          |
| Late-middle | Shared   | Primary   | Escalating   | Medium          |
| Climax      | Shared   | Active    | Crisis       | Light           |
| Resolution  | Closing  | Settling  | Aftermath    | Heavy           |

The baseline weight column indicates how much page time the
logistical systems receive. Early and late, the baseline is
prominent — the reader lives in the band's daily reality. During
the climax, the baseline lightens because the events have
outpaced the logistics. After the climax, the baseline returns
heavy — the cost accounting.
