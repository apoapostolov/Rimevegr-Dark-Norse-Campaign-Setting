# Medieval Skills and Authenticity — Iron Ledger

**Purpose:** The real-world knowledge base that characters
carry in their hands and habits. Everything a 10th-century
Scandinavian fighting man, cook, hunter, or camp-follower
knew how to do — the granular, physical, unglamorous skills
that Cook and Abercrombie embed as ambient competence in
their prose. These details make the world feel inhabited
rather than described.

**Usage:** Mine this file for incidental detail, character
gestures, logistical texture, and the material substrate of
daily life. A character who sharpens a blade should sharpen
it correctly. A character who lights a fire should light it
the way a person who has lit ten thousand fires lights one.

> unique to the Rimevegr. This file covers what is universal
> to pre-industrial northern European life — the baseline
> competence that every character possesses.

## Fire

### Starting a Fire

**Fire-steel and flint.** The universal method. A C-shaped
striker of high-carbon steel struck against a sharp edge of
flint. The steel sheds tiny curls of metal that are hot
enough to ignite — flint does not spark; the steel does.
The sparks land on tinder held directly below the strike
point. One-handed operation: flint pinched between thumb
and finger with the tinder nest pressed against it, striker
in the dominant hand. The motion is a sharp downward
scrape, not a tap. An experienced man gets a catch in
three to five strikes. A cold, wet, shaking man might
take thirty.

**Tinder.** The critical variable. Char-cloth (linen
scorched in a sealed tin until black) is the best tinder
— catches a spark instantly. Birch bark (the papery outer
layer, peeled dry) is second-best: natural oils make it
flammable even slightly damp. Dried bracket fungus
(amadou), scraped to a felt-like surface, catches and
holds an ember longer than anything else. Cattail fluff,
dried grass, and pine resin splinters all work when dry.
Nothing works when wet. Every experienced traveler carries
tinder in a waterproof pouch — a small leather bag sealed
with beeswax or fat. Losing the tinder pouch in rain
country is a survival emergency.

**The nest.** Tinder catches an ember, not a flame. The
ember must be transferred to a prepared nest of fine dry
material — shredded bark, dried grass, or hair-fine wood
shavings. The nest is held in cupped hands (the heat will
burn the fingers — this is normal) and blown on with
steady, controlled breaths aimed at the ember's base. The
sequence: glow, smoke, more smoke, then a sudden whomp of
flame. Drop the flaming nest into a prepared fire lay
immediately. The whole process, start to flame, takes one
to three minutes for a competent person in good conditions.
In rain or wind, it may take twenty or fail entirely.

**Fire lay.** Structure matters. A teepee of finger-thin
dry sticks over the flaming nest. The flame needs air
beneath and fuel above. Thick logs smother a young fire.
Build up gradually: tinder → pencil-thin kindling →
thumb-thick sticks → wrist-thick pieces → forearm logs.
A fire takes ten to fifteen minutes to establish to the
point where it will sustain itself on substantial fuel.
Impatience kills fires.

**Wet-weather fire.** Split wood. The interior of a
twelve-inch log is dry even after days of rain. Split with
an axe or a knife batoned through with a baton-stick.
The dry inner wood is shaved into kindling. Resinous wood
(pine, spruce) is best — the resin burns hot even when
damp. Birch bark burns in the rain. Standing dead wood is
drier than fallen wood (ground contact wicks moisture).
A man who knows this can light a fire in a rainstorm. A
man who does not know this cannot light a fire next to a
river in July.

### Maintaining a Fire

**Banking.** To keep a fire alive overnight without burning
through fuel: push the coals together into a tight pile,
cover with ash (the camp's own ash, raked back over). A
banked fire will hold coals for eight to twelve hours. In
the morning, scrape the ash aside, add tinder over the
exposed coals, blow gently. Flame in two minutes. This is
why the man on last watch is responsible for the fire —
letting it die cold means starting from scratch. In deep
cold, this is a punishable failure.

**Smoke.** Green wood or wet wood smokes heavily. Dry
hardwood produces heat and little smoke. When concealment
matters (enemy nearby, trying to sleep), dry hardwood
only. When warmth is the priority and smoke does not
matter, burn anything. Smoke follows the low-pressure side
— the saying "smoke follows beauty" is camp humor for
whoever the smoke keeps finding.

**Fire size.** A common mistake is building too large.
A cooking fire is small — knee-high, arm's width. Just
enough to heat a pot and provide a nucleus of warmth.
A warming fire is larger but still controlled. A fire
so large a man cannot stand near it wastes fuel and
announces position. The experienced fire-builder builds
the smallest fire that does the job.

### Torches

**Construction.** A torch is not a stick with fire on it.
A greenwood shaft (living wood resists burning through)
wrapped at one end with material soaked in fat, pitch,
or resin. Birch bark strips wound tightly and smeared
with pine resin provide 15-20 minutes of light. A proper
pitched-cloth torch (linen dipped in pine pitch or animal
tallow) lasts 30-45 minutes. A torch drips — hot fat
runs down the shaft. Hold it high and slightly tilted so
the drips fall away from the hand.

**Limitations.** A torch lights a circle of about ten feet.
Beyond that, it makes the bearer visible to anyone in
the dark while blinding the bearer to everything outside
the light. Carrying a torch at night is advertising your
location. It destroys night vision for ten to fifteen
minutes after extinguishing. Men who know this carry
torches only when they must — inside buildings, inside
barrows, in places where footing kills. Overland at night,
experienced men prefer moonlight and adjusted eyes.

**Resin.** Pine resin is the universal adhesive, sealant,
and fire-starter. Collected as hardened lumps from wounded
trees (the sticky amber blobs on pine bark). Softened by
gentle heat, it can be pressed onto a torch head, smeared
on leather for waterproofing, or chewed (it tastes of
turpentine and is mildly antiseptic). Every camp in pine
country collects resin automatically. It is too useful
to leave behind.

## Blades and Weapons

### Sharpening

**The whetstone.** Every fighting man carries one. A flat
stone of fine-grained sandstone, slate, or imported
quartzite. Wetted with water or spit (the liquid floats
away metal particles and prevents the stone from loading).
A dry whetstone cuts slower and glazes.

**Technique.** The blade is held at a consistent angle —
roughly 15-20 degrees for a cutting edge (knife, sword),
25-30 degrees for a chopping edge (axe). The stroke runs
the length of the stone, leading with the edge, not
trailing. Alternate sides: five strokes per side is
standard. The burr (a tiny lip of metal rolled past the
edge by sharpening) is felt with the thumb running from
spine to edge, never along the edge. The burr is removed
by a few light alternating strokes. A properly sharpened
axe shaves arm hair. A sword should cut a free-hanging
thread.

**The sound.** A dull blade drags on the stone with a
heavy, grinding tone. A sharp blade whispers. An
experienced man listens to his edge. He knows when it
is done before he tests it. The sound of a whetstone on
steel in a quiet camp at dusk — this is one of the novel's
ambient textures.

**Frequency.** A working axe (chopping wood, clearing
brush) needs touching up daily. A fighting weapon that
was used needs attention that night — dried blood corrodes
steel, and nicks left unaddressed propagate into cracks.
A weapon in its sheath and not used needs sharpening
weekly at most. Over-sharpening wastes metal. Each pass
of the stone removes steel that cannot be replaced.

### Weapon Maintenance

**Rust.** Northern climate. Constant damp. Steel rusts
visibly overnight if left unprotected. After use, a blade
is wiped dry with a cloth or a fistful of grass, then
lightly oiled. Animal fat (from the cook's pot, from a
meal's remnants) is the standard lubricant. Linseed oil
or fish oil if available. A man who neglects his blade
in the Rimevegr will see orange bloom on the steel within
two days.

**Nicks and damage.** A nicked blade is ground out with
the coarse side of the whetstone. This removes material
and shortens the blade's useful life. A deeply nicked
blade requires a smith's grinding wheel. A blade with a
crack is done — the crack will spread under impact. The
owner either discards the blade or saves it for a
throwing weapon or utility knife where structural failure
is not fatal.

**Handles and hafts.** Axe heads loosen on their hafts.
Wood shrinks in dry cold, swells in wet heat. A loose
head is fixed by soaking the haft end in water (it
swells) or by driving a wooden or iron wedge into the
top of the haft where it meets the eye of the head.
Checking haft tightness is a morning ritual. A loose
axe head in combat kills the wielder, not the enemy.

**Leather wraps.** Sword grips and axe hafts wrapped in
leather strip (rawhide, wet-applied — it shrinks as it
dries, tightening the grip). The leather is replaced when
it stretches smooth and loses purchase. In between, it is
roughened with a knife point or rubbed with pine resin
for extra grip. A sweaty hand on a smooth haft is how
weapons are dropped in fights.

### Shields

**Wooden shields.** Made of planks (linden, poplar, or
whatever is light and does not split easily) glued and
sometimes riveted edge to edge. The face may have a
leather covering (raw hide, stretched and glued) that
holds splinters together when the wood breaks. The iron
boss covers the hand-grip hole at the center.

**Damage.** A shield in battle is a consumable. A heavy
blow splits planks. Multiple blows in the same area break
through. An axe blow to the rim can hook and drag the
shield aside. A shield that has taken several heavy hits
is noticeably lighter — because pieces of it are on the
ground. After a fight, a damaged shield is repaired
(new plank fitted, leather re-glued) or discarded if the
break is central. A man without a shield looks for the
next dead man who has one.

**Weight.** A round shield of 30-inch diameter weighs
7-10 pounds. This does not sound heavy. Hold ten pounds
at arm's length for five minutes. Now hold it there while
someone hits it with an axe. Shield work is exhausting.
The arm tires before the man does. In a long fight, the
shield drops lower and lower. This is how men are killed
— not through the shield but over it, when the tired arm
cannot hold it high enough.

## Armor and Clothing

### Leather Care

**Leather dries and cracks.** Leather is skin. It needs
moisture and fat to stay supple. A leather jerkin left
unattended in dry cold becomes stiff and brittle — it
will crack at the stress points (armpits, elbows, across
the back) within weeks. Regular treatment: animal fat
(pig lard, tallow, bear grease — whatever is available)
rubbed in by hand, working it into the grain, paying
attention to seams and folds. The fat absorbs overnight.
Excess is wiped off in the morning.

**Water and leather.** Wet leather stretches. Leather
dried too fast (near a fire) shrinks and hardens
irreversibly — the collagen cooks. The correct method:
stuff the garment with dry grass to hold its shape, hang
it at a distance from the fire where it is warm but not
hot, and let it dry slowly. A leather boot dried by
shoving it against the coals will be an unwearable claw
by morning.

**Stitching.** Leather is sewn with heavy linen thread
or sinew, using a curved needle or an awl (a short
metal spike that punches the hole) followed by a straight
needle. Sinew thread swells when wet, which tightens the
stitches — a design feature. Every man carries a needle
and thread for emergency repairs. The thread is usually
kept wound around a piece of bone or antler. Replacing
a burst seam on a boot is a half-hour job done by
firelight without ceremony.

### Wool Care

**Lanolin.** Raw wool retains its natural oils (lanolin),
which make it partially water-resistant. Washed wool
loses lanolin and becomes more absorbent. Do not over-wash
wool garments. They should smell somewhat of sheep. This
is correct. The lanolin is doing its job.

**Wet wool.** Heavy. A soaked wool cloak weighs two to
three times its dry weight. It still insulates when wet
(unlike linen, which becomes useless), but the weight
drags. Drying wool is slow — hang near the fire but not
close enough to scorch. The smell of drying wool in a
camp tent is one of those sensory details that anchors a
scene instantly.

**Repair.** Wool tears at stress points. Patch with a
needle and wool thread. Felting (matting wool fibers into
a solid fabric by heat, moisture, and friction) is used
for soles, padding, and patches where woven cloth has
given out. A patched garment tells its history in visible
stitches.

### Chainmail

**Weight.** A short-sleeve haubergeon (thigh-length mail
shirt) weighs 20-30 pounds. It feels heavier than plate
because all the weight hangs from the shoulders. A broad
leather belt cinched at the waist transfers some weight
to the hips. Without the belt, wearing mail for a full
day's march produces bruised shoulders and a stiff neck.

**Sound.** Mail jingles. Constantly. The rings shift
against each other with every movement. A mail-wearing man
cannot sneak. At night in camp, mail is removed and hung
(never piled — piled mail tangles and takes twice as long
to put on). The sound of mail rustling in a pack is one
of the sounds of the march.

**Maintenance.** Rust is the enemy. Every few days, the
mail is dumped into a leather bag with sand and vinegar
(or just sand and whatever acidic liquid is available —
sour ale works). The bag is rolled and tumbled. The
abrasion scours rust off the links. The result is bright
steel with a faint grit. After scouring, oil the mail
with a fat-soaked cloth. Then store it wrapped in oiled
cloth.

**Repair.** Individual links break. They are replaced on
the spot with pliers and wire. A smith does this properly
with riveted links. In the field, a man replaces broken
links by threading in a new ring and pinching it closed
with pliers. It holds. Not perfectly, but it holds.

## Camp Skills

### Setting Camp

**Site selection.** Not flat ground. Slightly elevated
ground, so rain drains away. Sheltered from the wind on
the north and west (prevailing cold-weather wind
directions). Near water but not on the bank (flooding,
damp ground, mosquitoes). Near fuel (deadfall timber) but
not under dead trees that could fall in wind. Hard ground
or gravel preferred — mud sucks heat out of a sleeping
body faster than cold air.

**Wind-breaks.** A simple wind-break of stacked brush or
a stretched hide reduces wind-chill dramatically. In snow
country, a wall of packed snow blocks wind completely. The
fire goes between the wind-break and the sleeping area.
Radiant heat bounces off the break and back toward the
sleepers. A single man can make himself significantly
warmer in an hour of work building a proper wind-break
than by burning twice the fuel without one.

**Sleep system.** Ground insulation matters more than
blankets. A man lying on bare frozen ground loses heat
downward faster than he can generate it, no matter what
is on top of him. Spruce bows, dry leaves, straw, cut
bracken — anything between the body and the ground.
Four inches of compressed spruce bough insulation is the
minimum for reasonable sleep below freezing. The cloak
goes over the man; the ground padding goes under him.
This is the priority most amateurs get backward.

**Latrine.** Downhill from camp. Downhill from the water
source. A trench dug with a short-handled shovel or an
axe. Each use covered with a scoop of loose earth (kills
smell, discourages flies, prevents disease). Within two
days of occupation, a camp without a trench stinks. Within
a week, men get sick. Armies that ignored latrine
discipline suffered more casualties from dysentery than
from combat — a historical fact that applies with full
force in the Rimevegr. Dalla knows this. She enforces it.

### Rope

**Material.** Walrus hide, seal skin (coastal), or plaited
plant fibers — hemp, nettle, lime bast. Making rope is a
camp industry: strands are twisted by hand or on a simple
frame, then two or three twisted strands are counter-
twisted together. The counter-twist is what prevents
unraveling. A man who knows how to lay rope can make
fifty feet in an evening. A man who does not will produce
a tangled mess.

**Care.** Wet rope is weaker than dry rope and harder to
untie. Knots tightened under load when wet may become
permanent. Rope stored wet rots within weeks — the fibers
break down. Dry rope thoroughly before coiling for storage.
Frozen rope is brittle and must be thawed gently before
use (breath, not fire — fire burns through rope fibers
that were softened by ice).

**Knots.** A fighting man needs four knots: a bowline (a
loop that will not slip), a clove hitch (fast attachment
to a post or tree), a trucker's hitch or variant (for
mechanical advantage in tensioning), and a round turn and
two half-hitches (securing a load). Everything else is
ornamental. The speed at which a man ties a bowline in the
dark tells you how many winters he has marched.

### Navigation

**By day.** Sun position. Moss on the north side of trees
is a half-truth — it grows on the shadowed, damp side,
which in the northern hemisphere is roughly north, but
terrain and moisture complicate this. More reliable:
prevailing wind direction (consistent in a region — men
who live there know it), the direction of watercourses
(rivers run downhill to the sea — follow them to find
settlements), tree-line on ridges (windward side has
stunted growth), and simple dead reckoning
(direction + pace-count = approximate position).

**By night.** The Pole Star. If it is visible, north is
known. The Pole Star does not move. Everything else
rotates around it. Finding it: the two rear stars of the
Big Dipper's cup point to it. This is the single most
important piece of astronomical knowledge for a
pre-compass traveler. Cloud cover removes this option.
On overcast nights, experienced travelers stop and wait
for dawn rather than walk in circles.

**Trail-reading.** Broken branches at chest height mean a
mounted rider. At waist height, a man on foot. A snapped
twig shows the direction of travel (the break faces the
direction of force). Disturbed moss recovers in two to
three days — tracks in moss older than that are invisible.
Tracks in mud show depth (heavy load = deep heel strike),
spacing (running vs walking), and freshness (sharp edges
= recent; crumbled edges = hours old; water-pooled = a
day or more). Petra reads these. Most men cannot.

## Woodcraft and Timber

### Reading a Tree

A man who needs wood looks at a tree and sees material,
not scenery. The assessment is automatic:

**Species.** Hardwoods (oak, ash, elm, birch) versus
softwoods (pine, spruce, fir). Hardwoods burn hotter,
last longer, and make stronger tool handles. Softwoods
are easier to cut, split cleanly, and contain resin
for fire-starting. Birch is the universal utility wood
of the north — hard enough for tools, light enough to
carry, bark useful for a dozen purposes.

**Soundness.** A standing dead tree may be dry and ready
to burn, or it may be rotten through. Check by pressing
a knife point into the base — solid resistance means good
wood, the blade sinking in means punk. Kick the trunk.
A solid tree gives a dull thud. A hollow one booms. A
rotten one gives nothing — the boot sinks in. Fungal
brackets on the bark mean the heartwood is compromised.
Woodpecker holes mean insects have colonized the interior,
which means the interior is softening.

**Size.** A man with an axe can fell a tree up to about
eighteen inches in diameter in thirty to forty-five
minutes. Larger trees require hours. Before felling, the
calculation: is the wood worth the effort and the noise?
Felling a large tree announces your position within half
a mile. The sound carries.

**Grain.** Straight-grained wood splits cleanly along the
fibers. Twisted grain (the fibers spiral around the trunk)
fights the axe and splits unpredictably — wedges pop out,
the split runs sideways, the wood tears instead of
cleaving. A man who needs split wood for a shelter or a
fire examines the bark first. Straight bark furrows mean
straight grain. Spiral bark means spiral grain. Choose
the straight tree.

### Felling

**The notch-and-back-cut.** Universal method. The faller
first decides which direction the tree should fall — look
up. Where do the branches weight the tree? Is the trunk
leaning? The tree wants to fall in the direction of its
lean and its branch weight. Fighting this is dangerous
and usually unnecessary.

Cut a notch (a V-shaped wedge) on the side facing the
intended fall direction. The notch goes roughly one-third
of the way through the trunk. Two cuts: one horizontal,
one angled downward to meet it. Remove the wedge chunk.

The back cut comes from the opposite side, slightly above
the horizontal line of the notch. Cut straight in,
leaving a hinge of uncut wood between the back cut and
the notch — roughly two inches for a twelve-inch tree.
This hinge controls the fall direction. When the tree
begins to tip, step away at an angle — not directly
behind, not directly to either side, but at forty-five
degrees to the fall line. Trees kick back off the stump
when they fall. The butt end swings upward and backward
with enough force to kill. This is how fallers die.

**The sound.** The cracking of the hinge wood carries
across a camp. Everyone looks. A falling tree gathers
speed — the rush of branches through air, the ground
impact. If the tree hangs up on a neighbor (a "widow-
maker"), do not stand under it. The hung tree can slide
free at any time. Cut the hanging tree's contact points
from the side, never from beneath.

**Limbing.** After felling, the branches are removed by
chopping from the base toward the tip. Stand on the
uphill side of the trunk. Work from the butt toward the
crown. Each limb is a three-stroke job: two angled cuts
to notch, one horizontal to sever. The stubs are left —
trimming flush is wasted effort if the trunk is for
firewood or rough construction.

### Splitting

**Technique.** The log is stood on end. The axe strikes
the center of the end grain — the round face of the cut
log. A straight-grained log splits with one blow. A
knotted log requires a wedge: the axe opens the split,
a hardwood or iron wedge is tapped into the crack, then
driven with the back of the axe head. The log opens
along the grain.

**Batoning.** For smaller work — producing kindling,
making stakes, splitting staves — a knife is placed edge-
down on the end of a piece of wood and hammered through
with a baton (a wrist-thick piece of hardwood used like
a mallet). The knife splits the wood along the grain.
This produces controlled, thin splits that an axe is too
coarse for.

**Quarter-splitting.** A log is split in half, then each
half is split again, producing quarters. These quarter-
rounds are the standard firewood shape — they stack,
they dry faster than rounds (the exposed inner wood
breathes), and they fit in a fire without rolling. A man
splitting wood for a two-day camp produces twenty to
thirty quarter-rounds and stacks them bark-down to shed
rain.

### Processing Green Wood

**Green wood versus seasoned.** Freshly cut wood
(green) contains water — sometimes more than half its
weight. Green wood bends before it breaks. Seasoned
wood (dried for months) is lighter, harder, and snaps
cleanly. Green wood is used for anything that needs to
bend: shelters (bent frames), baskets, wattle, tool
handles shaped while flexible. Seasoned wood is used for
anything rigid: planks, posts, weapon hafts, bowls.

**Shaping.** A drawknife (a blade with two handles,
pulled toward the user) flattens and smooths a piece
of green wood in minutes. Without a drawknife, an axe
hews the rough shape and a knife shaves the surface.
The wood is held in a notched log (a crude vise) or on
the ground with a foot on it. Everything from a spoon to
a shield plank starts this way.

**Steam bending.** Wood can be bent permanently by
heating it with steam or soaking it in hot water. The
heat softens the lignin (the glue that holds fibers
rigid). Bent around a form and tied, the wood holds
its curve as it dries. Shield rims, boat ribs, and
furniture curves are made this way. In the field, a
simplified version: hold the green wood over the fire,
rotating it slowly, until it becomes pliable. Bend to
shape. Hold until cool. This works for snowshoe frames,
fishing hooks, and trap triggers.

### Charcoal Making

**Why charcoal.** Charcoal burns hotter than raw wood
(twice the temperature) and produces almost no smoke.
It is essential for metalwork (a wood fire cannot reach
forging temperature) and valuable for cooking where
smoke is a problem. Making charcoal is a settlement craft,
but a man who understands the principle can improvise.

**The principle.** Wood heated in the absence of air
drives off water and volatile compounds, leaving nearly
pure carbon. The simplest method: build a fire, produce
a deep bed of coals, then smother it with earth or green
leaves. Cut off the air. Let it cool overnight. Dig out
the coals in the morning — they are charcoal. Wasteful
(half the wood is lost) but functional. A proper charcoal
kiln (a sealed mound) is more efficient but takes a day
to build.

## Shelter Building

### The Lean-To

The simplest effective shelter. One man, one axe, one
hour.

**Construction:** A ridge-pole (a straight trunk or thick
branch, eight to ten feet long) is propped at one end
against a tree fork or a boulder, creating a sloped
frame. One end is chest-high. The other is on the ground
or braced on a lower support. Shorter poles are leaned
against the ridge-pole at close intervals (a hand's width
apart), forming a sloped wall. Over these, layers of
material: spruce boughs (laid like shingles, thick end
up, overlapping so water runs off), leaves, bark sheets,
or whatever the terrain provides. The layers must be
thick — four inches of compressed spruce bough or six
inches of loose leaf litter to be reasonably waterproof.

**Orientation.** The open face of the lean-to faces the
fire. The closed back faces the wind. Heat from the fire
reflects off the sloped roof and radiates down onto the
sleeper. A reflector wall of stacked logs behind the fire
doubles the effect — the heat bounces off the reflector,
across the open space, and into the lean-to. This
arrangement keeps a man alive in temperatures well below
freezing without extraordinary fuel consumption.

**Limitations.** A lean-to is open on one side. Wind that
shifts during the night drives rain or snow directly onto
the sleeper. The roof leaks in prolonged heavy rain
(water migrates along boughs to the low point and drips).
A lean-to for one man is snug. A lean-to for six is a
wide, shallow structure that burns fuel fast trying to
heat the open face. For a band, multiple small lean-tos
or a different shelter type is more efficient.

### The A-Frame

A two-sided shelter. Takes two to three hours to build
properly but provides superior protection.

**Construction:** A ridge-pole is supported at both ends
— propped in tree forks, on stacked stones, or on tripod
frames of shorter poles lashed together. The ridge-pole
height at the peak should be waist-high (higher wastes
material and interior heat rises to the peak and is lost).
Rafters are leaned against both sides of the ridge-pole.
Covered with the same materials as a lean-to — boughs,
bark, leaves — layered thickly. The entrance is at one
end, the other end is closed with more material or stacked
brush.

**Interior.** The ground inside is covered with insulation
— four to six inches of dry material. The space is tight.
A man lies lengthwise under the ridge. Two men lie side
by side. The body heat of the occupants warms the enclosed
space significantly. An A-frame for two men in calm air
stays ten to fifteen degrees warmer than the outside
temperature even without a fire (body heat plus
insulation).

**Ventilation.** A sealed A-frame with a fire inside
fills with smoke immediately. The fire stays outside.
Heat enters through radiation and through heated stones
placed inside (see bed-warming below). Alternatively,
a small fire at the entrance with the far end open a
crack for draft creates airflow that carries smoke out —
but this requires attention and is not suitable for
sleeping.

### The Debris Hut

A one-man survival shelter. Built when caught out without
tools or time.

**Construction:** A ridge-pole propped against a stump
or rock, long enough for the man to lie beneath it. Short
sticks leaned against both sides, packed as densely as
possible. Then covered with every available piece of
debris — leaves, ferns, moss, pine needles, grass, bark
scraps. The covering must be thick. Very thick. A man's
arm pushed into the debris should disappear past the
elbow. Two feet of loose leaf litter on the outside is
the minimum for rain resistance. The interior is also
filled with dry leaves — the man buries himself in the
debris like an animal. The dead air trapped in the leaves
is the insulation.

**No fire.** A debris hut is too flammable for a fire
nearby. The insulation is the body heat trapped in dead
air. It works. A properly built debris hut keeps a man
alive in subfreezing temperatures without fire if the
debris is dry. If the debris is wet, the man gets wet
and cold and the shelter fails.

### Snow Shelters

In deep snow country, snow itself is the building
material.

**The snow trench.** Dig a body-length trench in deep snow
(deepness: at least waist-deep to work). Lay branches or
poles across the top. Cover with snow. Leave one end open
for entry. The interior temperature stays near freezing
(32°F / 0°C) regardless of how cold it is outside — snow
is a superb insulator. At -30°F outside, 32°F inside
feels warm. The man's body heat even raises the interior
above freezing slightly. A candle or tallow lamp inside
a snow trench raises the temperature by several degrees and
provides light. Ventilation: poke a hole through the roof
with a stick. Without ventilation, exhaled CO2 accumulates
and the man dies in his sleep. Always ventilate.

**The quinzhee.** A mound of loose snow, piled and packed,
left to harden for an hour (the snow crystals bond
through a process called sintering — medieval men did not
know the word but knew the principle: piled snow hardens).
Then hollowed out from one side. The walls should be a
forearm's thickness. Smooth the interior (drips follow the
smooth curve instead of dripping from bumps directly onto
the sleeper). A platform inside for sleeping — the
sleeping platform must be higher than the entrance because
cold air sinks. The man sleeps on the upper shelf while
cold air pools at the entrance level.

**Igloos and snow blocks.** Cutting snow into blocks and
stacking them in a spiral requires specific snow
conditions (wind-packed snow that holds its shape when
cut) and significant skill. This is an Inuit technique,
not a Norse one. But the principle — snow as building
material — is understood by any northern people. Norse
settlers in Greenland and Iceland used turf-over-stone
for the same insulation principle.

### Semi-Permanent Shelters

When a band stays in one place for more than two days,
the shelters improve.

**The turf-and-timber frame.** Common in Norse
settlements. A wooden frame (posts at corners, ridge-pole
across the top, rafters from ridge to ground or to low
side-walls) covered with stacked turf — sod cut in
blocks, grass-side out, layered like bricks. Turf is
waterproof, insulating, and fireproof. A turf-covered
shelter with a central hearth stays warm in temperatures
that would kill a man in a lean-to. The downside: turf is
heavy, cutting and stacking it is labor-intensive (a
day's work for two men to roof a one-room shelter), and
the structure is not portable. This is for a semi-
permanent camp — a winter stop, a watching post, a supply
depot.

**Wattle and daub.** A frame of vertical stakes with
flexible withies (hazel, willow) woven between them
horizontally, then plastered with daub (a mixture of
clay, mud, straw, and dung). Wattle walls are
surprisingly strong and reasonably weatherproof when
properly daubed. The daub must be applied thickly (two
inches minimum) and allowed to dry before occupation.
Cracks that develop as the daub dries are filled with
a second coat. In wet climate, daub walls need a roof
overhang to prevent rain dissolving them.

**Thatching.** A roof of bundled straw, reeds, or
heather laid in overlapping courses. Working from the
eave upward, each course overlaps the one below by two-
thirds. The overlapping layers shed water like feathers —
rain runs across each layer and drips off the edge onto
the layer below, continuing downward until it drips free
at the eave. A well-thatched roof at the correct pitch
(45 degrees or steeper) sheds all but the most prolonged
rain. A badly pitched or thin thatch leaks. Thatching
is skilled work. Every Norse man has seen it done and
most have helped, but the person who sets the angle and
the overlap is the one with the eye for it.

## Digging

### Graves

A reality of mercenary life. Graves are dug after losses.

**Dimensions.** Body-length, shoulder-width, and deep
enough that animals cannot dig it out — a minimum of
three feet, ideally four. In frozen ground, this is
nearly impossible. The top six inches may be frozen hard
as stone. Break through the frost layer with an axe or
a fire (build a fire on the grave site for two hours,
scrape away the thawed earth, repeat). Below the frost
line, the ground is softer. In summer, a competent man
with a digging tool can dig a grave in two to three
hours. In deep winter, it can take a full day — or the
band gives up, builds a cairn (piled stones), or a
pyre.

**The pyre.** More common in Norse tradition than burial.
A body on a bed of logs, more logs stacked over and
around, with kindling and tinder. A death-pyre consumes
an enormous amount of fuel — a full day's wood-cutting
for two men. The fire must be intense and sustained for
four to six hours to reduce a body to manageable ash and
bone fragments. In deep forest, fuel is available. On the
open steppe or above the tree-line, it may not be. The
captain decides based on material and time.

**Cairns.** A pile of stones large enough to cover the
body and heavy enough that wolves cannot shift them. This
requires hundreds of pounds of stone, carried and stacked.
In rocky terrain, easy. On flat ground, impractical. A
shallow grave topped with heavy stones is the compromise.

### Ditches

**Defensive.** A ditch around a camp slows attackers and
provides a raised position for defenders behind the
spoil-pile (the dirt thrown up on the camp-side of the
ditch). A ditch three feet deep and four feet wide takes
two men an hour per ten-foot section in soft ground,
twice that in clay or rockfill. Stakes driven into the
bottom (fire-hardened points upward) make the ditch
treacherous. A ditch alone is not a fortification. A ditch
plus a log palisade is.

**Drainage.** Camp in a hollow fills with rainwater. A
drainage ditch uphill of the camp, angled to divert runoff
around the occupied area, keeps the sleeping ground dry.
This is a labor investment that pays for itself on the
first wet night. Experienced men dig the drainage ditch
before setting up shelter. Inexperienced men wake up in a
puddle and learn.

## Bridges and Water Crossings

### Assessing a Ford

Not every shallow stretch of river is a safe ford. The
assessment:

**Depth.** Probe with a long staff before committing.
Knee-deep is comfortable. Thigh-deep is manageable with
effort and risk to gear. Waist-deep is dangerous — the
current pushes hard against the broad surface of the body,
footing is uncertain, and a stumble means swimming.
Chest-deep is swimming and the ford is not a ford.

**Current.** A river that looks slow from the bank may
have strong current at mid-channel. Look for surface
disturbance — standing waves, upstream V-shapes around
submerged rocks, a smooth glossy surface (paradoxically
indicates deep fast water). Wade in at an angle, facing
upstream, staff planted on the upstream side. If the
current pushes a man sideways at knee-depth, it will
knock him over at thigh-depth.

**Bottom.** Sand and gravel: stable footing. Silt and
mud: feet sink, suction holds them, balance fails.
Round slippery stones: treacherous — each step is
uncertain. Bedrock: excellent if flat, dangerous if
sloped (algae-slicked bedrock is frictionless).

**Crossing with gear.** Gear goes high. Packs are held
overhead or on the head. If the ford is deep enough to
risk wetting gear, send the strongest man across first
with a rope. The rope is staked on both banks. Subsequent
crossers hold the rope for balance. The mule crosses last
(mules are better at fords than horses — lower center
of gravity, more cautious foot placement — but a mule
that decides the ford is too deep will stop and refuse,
and no force on earth moves a mule that has refused).

### Improvised Bridges

**Log bridge.** A tree is felled across a narrow gap
(ravine, stream). The tree must be long enough to reach
both banks with several feet of overlap on each side.
The bark is left on (better grip for feet). Crossing a
round log over water requires balance — arms out, short
steps, eyes on the far bank (not down at the water).
Hewing a flat top with an axe takes an hour but makes the
crossing walkable for everyone including the mule.

**Corduroying.** For boggy ground: logs laid perpendicular
to the direction of travel, side by side, creating a
wooden road. Each log is five to eight inches in diameter
and the length of the path's width. The logs are held in
place by stakes driven at each end. Corduroy road is bumpy,
slippery, and labor-intensive — but it prevents men and
animals from sinking thigh-deep in bog. The Svarthird
builds corduroy when the route passes through unavoidable
marsh. The logs are cut, dragged, laid, and staked by every
available hand. A fifty-foot section takes four men an
hour.

## Fencing and Perimeter Work

### Camp Perimeter

**Abatis.** Felled trees with branches left on, dragged
into a rough perimeter with branches facing outward.
An abatis is not a wall — it is an obstacle. A man
trying to move through an abatis of interlocked branches
makes noise, moves slowly, and presents himself at
awkward angles. It channels attackers into gaps that can
be watched.

**Sharpened stakes.** Waist-high stakes driven into the
ground at an outward angle around a camp or a defensive
position. Fire-hardened points (hold the tip in the fire
until the surface chars and hardens). The stakes slow a
charge, force attackers to pick their way through, and
funnel them toward defended positions. Setting a ring of
stakes around a camp is a two-hour job for four men if
wood is available. The stakes are pulled and carried on
the march if the band expects to need them again.

**The alarm line.** A cord of rope or rawhide strung
ankle-height around the camp perimeter, threaded through
small noise-makers (bones, wooden toggles, scraps of
metal). A man stepping over the cord trips it; a man
stepping into it catches his ankle and the noise-makers
rattle. This is not foolproof — a careful man can step
over it. It is a system for waking sleeping men when
large animals or careless attackers approach in the dark.

### Corrals and Holding Pens

**For the mule.** A quick pen of poles lashed between
trees, or stacked brush dense enough to discourage the
animal from pushing through. The mule is hobbled as
backup (forelegs tied with a short rope so it can walk
but not run). A loose mule in the night is a lost mule
by morning — and with it, the band's supplies, tools, and
reserve food. Securing the mule is as important as posting
a watch.

## Food Preparation

### Butchering

**Field dressing.** An animal must be gutted immediately
after the kill or the meat spoils within hours. The
abdominal cavity is opened from pelvis to sternum with
a short blade, point-up to avoid puncturing the
intestines (punctured intestines contaminate the meat
with bacteria and bile — the smell is unmistakable and
the meat is ruined). The organs are removed in one mass
by cutting the diaphragm and windpipe, then pulling
downward. Heart and liver are kept (dense nutrition,
eaten first — often raw or barely cooked). The remaining
offal is left for scavengers unless the band is starving,
in which case lungs and kidneys are boiled into the stew.

**Skinning.** Cuts around the ankles, a slit up the inner
legs to meet the belly cut, then the skin is pulled off
rather than cut off — pulling leaves a clean carcass
without wasting meat. The skin is scraped of fat and
membrane while fresh (it stiffens within hours). Salted
or stretched on a frame to dry if the band has time.
Pelts have trade value. A good deer hide is worth
keeping.

**Quartering.** The carcass is broken into portable
pieces. Joints are the natural disassembly points — the
knife finds the cartilage between bones rather than
cutting through bone (bone dulls a blade faster than
anything). The pieces are wrapped in the animal's own
skin or in cloth and distributed among several packs so
no one man carries the entire weight.

### Preservation

**Smoking.** A smoking rack is improvised from green
sticks over a low, smoky fire (green wood or wet wood for
maximum smoke). The meat is cut into thin strips — thinner
means faster drying. Four to eight hours of dense smoke
produces meat that will keep for a week in cool weather.
True preservation requires 24-48 hours of continuous
smoking. The resulting jerky is hard, dark, chewy, and
tastes of smoke and salt. It keeps for months. It is not
pleasant to eat. It is not meant to be.

**Drying.** In dry, cold, windy conditions, meat dried in
thin strips on a rack (without smoke) produces wind-dried
meat. This works in the Rimevegr's Long Dark but fails in
humid Long Summer. The strips must be thin enough that
flies cannot lay eggs in the center before the surface
crusts. Fly-blown meat is visibly infested within hours
in warm weather.

**Salting.** Salt is valuable. When available, rubbing
coarse salt into meat and fish draws moisture and prevents
bacterial growth. Salt fish is a staple of coastal
settlements — split, salted, and dried on wooden racks
in the wind. It keeps for months. It requires soaking in
fresh water before cooking (otherwise inedibly salty). The
soaking water is not wasted — it has salt in it.

### Cooking

**The pot.** The single most important piece of camp
equipment after weapons. An iron or bronze cauldron
suspended on a tripod or hung from a horizontal stick
over the fire. Everything goes in: water, whatever meat
is available (cut small for fast cooking to save fuel),
root vegetables, wild greens, grain if they have it.
The stew cooks for hours. The longer it cooks, the more
it extracts from the bones (marrow, gelatin — these are
calories). A pot that has been simmering a stew for three
days develops a base flavor that improves everything
added to it.

**Bread.** When the band has grain, flat bread is cooked
on a hot stone placed in the fire. No yeast (leavened
bread requires an oven). The grain is ground between two
stones (a hand-quern — upper stone rotated on lower stone,
grain fed through a center hole). The flour is coarse.
The bread is dense, gritty, and filling. It goes stale
in two days and hard as wood in four. Hard bread is
soaked in stew or ale to soften it. Teeth broken on
four-day-old field bread are a real occupational hazard.

**Porridge.** Grain boiled in water until it thickens.
The foundational camp meal. Barley, oats, or whatever
cereal is available. Cooked in the pot, eaten off a knife
blade or a wooden spoon. Seasoned with salt if they have
it, with nothing if they do not. A man who has eaten
porridge every morning for three years can tell you the
grain type and the water quality blindfolded.

**Roasting.** Meat on a stick over fire is the most
primitive method and the most wasteful — dripping fat
is lost (in a pot, it becomes broth). Roasting is for
fresh kills eaten immediately, when the pot is not
available, or when the meat is too large for the pot.
A spit (a green-wood rod through the carcass, turned by
hand) over a bed of coals (not flames — flames char the
outside while leaving the inside raw) produces edible
meat in one to two hours depending on size.

## Hunting

### Deer Stalking

The primary large-game hunting technique in northern
forests. Not a chase — a slow, patient approach to within
bow or spear range.

**Wind.** The single most important factor. Deer smell
a man at two hundred paces downwind. The hunter must
approach from downwind — always. If the wind shifts, the
stalk is over. Experienced hunters test the wind
constantly: a pinch of dry dust or a thread of wool tied
to the bow. When the thread points toward the deer, stop.
Circle around and approach from another angle, or wait
for the wind to change.

**Movement.** Deer detect movement before shape. A
motionless man in plain view may be ignored. A moving man
behind cover is spotted instantly. The stalk is: move
when the deer has its head down (grazing), freeze when
the head comes up (scanning). A step takes three seconds
— weight shifts forward slowly, the foot is placed toe-
first, feeling the ground for sticks before committing
weight. Fifty yards of approach in good cover takes thirty
minutes. This is not dramatic. This is the reality of
hunting.

**Range.** A hunting bow (lighter than a war bow, for
easier draw during a long stalk) is effective to about
thirty yards. Beyond that, accuracy drops and the arrow
may not penetrate deeply enough for a clean kill. The
hunter works to get as close as possible. Twenty yards is
good. Fifteen is better. Under ten — close enough to see
the animal's eye — is the mark of an exceptional stalker.

**The shot.** Behind the front shoulder, angled slightly
forward. This puts the arrow through the lungs and
sometimes the heart. A lung-shot deer runs for fifty to
two hundred yards, then collapses. A gut-shot deer
(the arrow too far back) runs for miles and dies slowly
over hours or days. The hunter follows the blood trail.
Lung blood is bright red and frothy. Gut blood is dark
and mixed with stomach contents — the smell tells the
tracker immediately that the shot was bad and the follow-
up will be long.

**Waiting.** After a shot, an experienced hunter waits.
Fifteen to thirty minutes. A lung-shot deer that is left
alone lies down within two hundred yards, stiffens, and
dies. A lung-shot deer that is pushed (the hunter follows
immediately) runs on adrenaline far beyond where it would
have stopped. Pushing a wounded deer is the most common
hunting mistake and one of the hardest disciplines to
learn — the impulse to pursue is almost irresistible.
Petra knows this. She sits. She waits. She reads the
blood and follows when the blood says the animal has
stopped.

### Boar Hunting

Different from deer hunting in almost every way.

**Danger.** A boar is armed and aggressive. A wounded boar
charges. Tusks five to eight inches long, razor-sharp on
the edges, driven by three hundred pounds of low-centered
muscle. A boar tusk opens a man's thigh to the bone. Boar
hunting is the most dangerous non-combat activity in the
Rimevegr.

**Method.** A boar is found by sign: rooted earth (they
dig for roots and tubers, leaving torn-up patches), wallow
pits (muddy depressions where they roll to control
parasites), and tracks (a boar's track is wider and
rounder than a deer's, with deeper impressions because
the animal is heavier for its leg length).

**The drive.** One or two men with dogs (or men shouting
and beating brush) push the boar toward hunters waiting
with spears. The spear is the weapon — not the bow. An
arrow may not stop a charging boar (they have a thick
cartilaginous shield under the hide on the shoulders).
A spear braced against the ground, point toward the
charge, catches the boar in the chest as it rushes. The
cross-guard on a boar spear (a horizontal bar below
the head) prevents the boar from running up the shaft
to reach the hunter. Without a cross-guard, this happens.
The boar impales itself to the hilt and still gores the
man holding the shaft.

**Solo hunting.** Riskier. The hunter finds a boar in
thick cover, gets a spear or heavy javelin into it, and
then waits for the boar to decide between fight and
flight. They often choose fight. A man alone against a
charging boar needs a tree to get behind, a second weapon,
and nerve. This is where scars come from.

### Small Game

When large game is scarce or the band cannot afford the
time for a full stalk.

**Rabbits.** The most common small game in northern
terrain. Hunted with snares (see trapping section),
thrown sticks, or a quick dog. A rabbit provides enough
meat for one man's meal — a mouthful of stew flavoring,
not a feast. A dozen rabbits feed the band for a day.
Getting a dozen rabbits reliably requires either an
experienced trapper with time to set a line of snares or
a dog bred and trained for coursing.

**Birds.** Grouse and ptarmigan in upland terrain. They
sit tight in cover and burst out when nearly stepped on
— the noise is startling every time. Killed with a thrown
stick, a quick arrow, or by stalking while they sit
(ptarmigan in winter are white on snow and nearly
invisible, but they sit tight enough that a man can walk
to within arm's reach if he moves slowly). A bird caught
is plucked immediately (feathers come out easily while
the body is warm, much harder when cold). Feathers are
saved for arrow fletching or bedding stuffing.

**Squirrels.** Hunted with blunt arrows (a knob-ended
arrow that stuns rather than pierces — a pierce-arrow
pinned to a tree branch is difficult to retrieve and
damages the skin). Squirrel pelts are trade goods. The
meat is lean and has a nutty flavor. A day's effort might
produce six to ten squirrels — enough pelts to be worth
trading, enough meat for a supplemental stew.

## Trapping

### Snares

A snare is a slip-knot noose set on a game trail. The
animal walks into the noose, its body tightens the loop,
and it is held.

**Construction.** Wire is ideal (thin, strong, holds the
noose open) but rare. Cordage of braided sinew or rawhide
works. The noose must be large enough for the target
animal's head to enter but not so large that the body
passes through without catching. For rabbits: a fist-
sized loop, set two inches off the ground, on a well-used
trail (look for droppings, worn ground, fur on thorns).

**The anchor.** The noose is tied to a stake in the ground
(the animal struggles against it until exhaustion), a
bent sapling (a spring-pole — the animal's weight
triggers a toggle that releases the sapling, which snaps
upright and lifts the animal off the ground, killing it
quickly by neck-break or suffocation), or a drag (a heavy
stick that the animal pulls but cannot carry far — the
trapper follows the drag-trail to find the catch).

**Placement.** On natural funnels — narrow points on
trails where the animal has no choice but to walk through
the snare. Between two logs, in a gap in a brush fence
that the trapper builds to channel movement, at the
entrance to a burrow. Animals use the same paths
repeatedly. Finding the trail is the skill. Setting the
snare is mechanics.

**Snare line.** A trapper setting a serious line places
ten to twenty snares over a half-mile to one-mile loop.
The line is checked daily. An unchecked snare means a
dead animal rotting (wasted), a live animal attracting
predators (which may destroy the snare and the catch),
or an animal that has chewed through the cordage and
escaped (with a noose mark that teaches it to avoid
snares). Petra checks her snare line at dawn.

### Deadfall Traps

A heavy weight (a flat stone, a log) propped up by a
trigger mechanism. The animal takes the bait, the trigger
releases, the weight falls and kills the animal.

**The figure-four trigger.** Three sticks notched to
interlock into a shape resembling a figure four. One
vertical stick supports the weight. One horizontal stick
holds the bait. One diagonal stick connects them. The
bait stick is the trigger — when the animal tugs on the
bait, the figure-four collapses and the weight drops.
The carving of the notches is precise work — too loose
and the trigger collapses on its own; too tight and the
animal pulls the bait free without triggering the fall.

**Deadfall weight.** Must be at least five times the
target animal's body weight for a clean kill. For
rabbits, a flat stone of five to ten pounds. For
marten or fox, a log of twenty to forty pounds. An
inadequately weighted deadfall injures without killing —
the animal suffers and the noise attracts scavengers.

**Bait.** Species-specific. Rabbits: fruit, root
vegetables, grain. Marten and fox: meat scraps, fish.
Birds: seeds, berries. Bait must be fresh enough to
smell appealing. Rotted bait attracts flies but not
target animals (except wolverines, which eat anything).

### Pit Traps

A hole in the ground, covered with a lattice of thin
sticks and leaves. The animal walks onto the covering,
breaks through, and falls into the pit.

**Size.** For large game (deer, boar), the pit must be
deep enough that the animal cannot jump out: six feet
minimum. Wide enough that it cannot brace against the
sides: four to five feet. Digging this is a major labor
investment — four to six hours for two men in soft
ground. In rocky or frozen ground, impractical.

**Stakes.** Sharpened stakes in the bottom turn a
restraining trap into a killing trap. Fire-hardened
points angled upward. The animal falls onto the stakes.
This is also a weapon of war — pit traps along an
expected approach route.

**Covering.** The covering must look natural. Sticks
laid across, then leaves, then a thin layer of dirt.
Fresh-cut vegetation wilts and changes color within a
day, revealing the trap. Use dead material that matches
the surrounding ground. Scent: the trapper walks
around the pit, not over it, and touches the covering
as little as possible. Game animals avoid areas that
smell of human.

## Fishing

### Line Fishing

**Hooks.** Carved from bone (a small curved sliver, barbed
with a notch filed against the curve), forged from iron
wire (heated, bent, and hammered to a point), or
improvised from thorns (hawthorn spines are surprisingly
strong). A gorge hook (a short straight piece of bone
sharpened at both ends, tied in the middle) is the oldest
design — the fish swallows it, the line pulls taut, the
gorge turns sideways and catches in the throat. Gorge
hooks work but do not release cleanly and cannot be set
or struck — the fish must swallow the bait completely.

**Line.** Twisted horsehair (several strands braided —
strongest natural line), sinew, or fine cordage of
nettle fiber. The line must be strong enough to hold
the target fish but fine enough not to be visible
(fish in clear water avoid thick, dark lines). In
murky water, line visibility matters less.

**Bait.** Worms (dug from rich soil near water), grubs
(found under bark and in rotting wood), small fish
(caught first and used as bait for larger fish), insects,
and pieces of meat or offal. Salmon take flies — an
artificial fly made of thread and feather wrapped on a
hook imitates the insects that salmon feed on at the
surface. Fly-tying is a specialized skill.

**Where.** Fish hold in predictable locations: behind
rocks in current (the eddy reduces the work of swimming
against the flow), under overhanging banks (shade, cover
from predators), at the junction of fast and slow water,
at the mouths of tributaries (converging food sources),
and in deep pools. A man who reads water sees fish before
he casts. A man who does not read water fishes the first
convenient spot and catches nothing.

### Net Fishing

**Gill nets.** A curtain of netting stretched across a
current. Fish swim into it and their gills catch in the
mesh when they try to back out. Mesh size determines what
is caught — small mesh catches small fish, larger mesh
catches larger fish. A gill net across a known salmon
run during migration season catches more fish in one
night than a line fisher catches in a week.

**Seining.** A net dragged through shallow water by two
people, one at each end, sweeping toward the shore. The
net corrals fish against the bank and is lifted, trapping
them. Effective in shallow rivers, estuaries, and lake
edges. Requires a long net (twenty feet minimum) and two
coordinated people.

**Dip nets.** A net on a frame (hoop of bent wood), used
to scoop fish. Works at waterfalls where salmon are
jumping (the fisher stands at the fall's lip and nets
fish mid-leap), in weirs where fish are funneled into
a narrow space, and in tide pools.

### Fish Weirs

A permanent or semi-permanent structure that channels
fish into a trap where they can be easily caught.

**Construction.** A V-shaped fence of stakes and woven
withies built across a stream, narrowing to a point.
The point opens into a basket or pen. Fish swimming
upstream follow the fence, funnel into the narrow
point, and enter the trap — the opening is easy to
enter but difficult to exit (a funnel effect). A well-
placed weir on a productive stream catches fish
continuously and is the closest thing to automation
that medieval fishing offers.

**Maintenance.** The current pushes debris against the
fence, which must be cleared daily. Floods can destroy
the structure. In freezing conditions, ice forms on and
around the stakes, breaking them. A weir is a permanent
camp investment — worth building if the band plans to stay.

### Spear Fishing

**The leister.** A three-pronged fishing spear — the
center prong is rigid, the two outer prongs are flexible
and barbed, spreading slightly so they grip the fish when
it is struck. The fisher wades in shallow water or stands
on a rock and strikes downward at fish passing below.

**Refraction.** Water bends light. The fish is not where
it appears to be — it is deeper and slightly closer to
the fisher than the eye reports. An experienced spear-
fisher aims below the apparent fish. Learning this
adjustment takes dozens of missed strikes. The eye never
fully corrects for it — the hand learns the compensation
separately from the eye's judgment.

**Torch fishing.** At night, a torch or fire held over
shallow water attracts fish (they are drawn to light).
The fisher stands with the light behind him and strikes
at fish illuminated in the shallow water below. This
technique is best in dark, clear water — murky water
scatters the light and the fish are invisible.

### Ice Fishing

Winter-specific. When rivers and lakes freeze.

**Finding the fish.** Fish in winter move to the deepest
parts of the lake where the water stays above freezing
(deep water is denser and settles at 4°C). The fisher
must get through the ice to reach these depths. Shoreline
ice is thickest — mid-lake ice may be dangerously thin
without being visibly different.

**Cutting the hole.** An axe or an ice chisel (a heavy
iron point on a shaft, dropped repeatedly to fracture the
ice). The hole must be wide enough to pull a fish through
— fist-width for line fishing, arm-width for net or
spear. Ice thickness varies: four inches supports a man's
weight safely. Less than that — stay off. A man who falls
through lake ice in winter has two to three minutes of
useful consciousness before hypothermia takes his muscle
control and he cannot climb out.

**The jig.** A baited hook or a lure (a carved bone or
bright piece of metal that wobbles on the line, imitating
a small injured fish) lowered through the hole. The fisher
jiggles the line — small regular movements that make the
bait or lure look alive. The takes are felt as a pull
on the line. Set the hook immediately — cold-water fish
are sluggish but they release bait quickly when they feel
resistance.

## Foraging

### Berries and Fruits

Northern forests produce seasonal fruit that supplements
the diet when available and provides critical vitamins
that preserved food lacks.

**By season:**

| Season       | Available                                      |
| ------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Early summer | Strawberries (tiny, intense), crowberries      |
| Midsummer    | Blueberries, cloudberries (gold), bilberries   |
| Late summer  | Lingonberries (tart, keep well), raspberries   |
| Autumn       | Rowan berries (bitter, cooked), rosehips       |
| Winter       | Rosehips (dried on bush), frozen lingonberries |

**Identification.** The rule for unknown berries: if you
do not know it, do not eat it. Red is dangerous (many
toxic berries are red or white). Blue and black are
usually safe (blueberries, blackberries, elderberries).
White is almost always toxic. This is a rough guide, not
a guarantee — a man who eats the wrong berry may have
stomach cramps (uncomfortable) or may die (several Nordic
berry-like plants are lethal). Dalla knows which is which.
Most men know the common edible berries of their region
and are cautious about the rest.

### Nuts

**Hazelnuts.** The most common edible nut in northern
Europe. Ripe in autumn. Found in hedgerows and forest
edges. The shell is cracked between two stones or with
the teeth (medieval teeth were stronger than modern ones
from harder use, but cracking nuts still chipped them).
Hazelnuts keep well through winter if stored dry.

**Acorns.** Technically edible but bitter — they contain
tannins that must be leached out by soaking in cold water
for several days (changing the water daily) or boiling
repeatedly. After leaching, the acorns are ground to
flour. The flour makes dense, slightly sweet bread. This
is famine food — labor-intensive and unpleasant to
process, but available in abundance under every oak tree.

**Pine nuts.** From stone pines (not common in
Scandinavia, but present in southern ranges). Small,
labor-intensive to extract from the cones (heat the cone
over fire to open the scales, shake out the seeds), but
extremely calorie-dense.

### Roots and Tubers

Underground plant parts that provide starch — the caloric
backbone of foraged food.

**Cattail roots.** Cattails grow in marshes and at pond
edges. The root is starchy, peeled, and boiled or roasted.
Also: cattail pollen (collected by shaking the flowering
head into a bag) is edible and can be mixed with flour.
Cattail is one of the most useful wild plants — root for
food, fluff for tinder and insulation, leaves for weaving.

**Burdock root.** Dug from the first-year plant (before
it flowers). Peeled, sliced, and boiled. Tastes earthy.
Substantial. A forager who knows burdock can find a meal
where others see weeds.

**Wild garlic (ramsons).** Grows in woodland shade in
spring. The leaves smell of garlic when crushed — this
is the identification. The bulbs are small but strongly
flavored. Used as seasoning rather than staple food.
Finding a patch of wild garlic transforms a bland stew
into something men remember.

**Angelica.** A robust plant of northern regions. The
stems are eaten raw or candied (boiled in honey). The
roots are used medicinally — a warming herb that the
Norse valued for its ability to "drive out cold humours."
The hollow stems make serviceable drinking straws and
pea-shooters.

### Mushrooms

**The safe ones.** Chanterelles (golden, funnel-shaped,
smelling faintly of apricot — common in northern birch
and pine forests, very difficult to confuse with anything
dangerous). Porcini (thick-stemmed, brown-capped, growing
under pines and spruces — a prized find that dries well).
Birch polypore (a shelf fungus growing on birch — tough,
cut into strips and boiled for broth; also used as
strop for sharpening).

**The deadly ones.** The death cap (looks like several
edible mushrooms, is lethal — one cap kills a man over
several agonizing days of liver failure). The destroying
angel (white, beautiful, deadly). The fly agaric (red
with white spots — toxic but rarely lethal; causes
hallucinations, vomiting, and confusion; associated
with Norse berserker rituals in modern speculation though
this is debated).

**The rule.** A man who is not certain does not eat the
mushroom. Dalla knows. Most men know two or three safe
species in their home terrain and eat nothing else. In
unfamiliar terrain, mushrooms are avoided unless someone
with knowledge prepares them.

### Seaweed and Shore Foraging

For coastal bands or bands near tidal rivers.

**Seaweed.** Dulse (red, flat, rubbery — dried and chewed
like jerky; genuinely nutritious). Kelp (brown, leathery
— boiled into broth or dried and crumbled into food;
provides iodine, which prevents goiter — a real problem
in inland medieval populations). Sea lettuce (green, thin,
edible raw or cooked like greens).

**Shellfish.** Mussels, clams, limpets, and periwinkles
collected from rocks and tidal flats. Eaten raw or
steamed over a fire on a flat stone. Shellfish from
standing water or near settlement sewage outflows cause
violent illness — the smell test is unreliable; location
is the indicator.

**Crabs and lobsters.** Caught in tidal pools by hand
or in simple traps (a baited basket lowered to the
bottom). Boiled alive (they must be alive when cooked —
dead crustaceans spoil almost immediately and the
toxins cannot be cooked out).

### Wild Greens and Herbs

**Nettles.** The most nutritious wild green in northern
Europe. Stinging when raw (the tiny hairs inject formic
acid), harmless when cooked — boiling for two minutes
destroys the sting. Nettle soup is rich in iron, vitamins,
and calories. Nettles grow everywhere humans have been
(they thrive in nitrogen-rich soil — near latrines,
old camps, animal pens). Finding a nettle patch also
means finding the ghost of a previous settlement.

**Sorrel.** Sour-tasting leaves (oxalic acid — the
same sharpness as rhubarb). Eaten raw as a trail nibble
or added to stew for flavor. In small quantities,
harmless and refreshing. In large quantities over weeks,
the oxalic acid can cause kidney problems — this is not
a concern for occasional foraging.

**Clover.** Both red and white clover are edible — flowers,
leaves, and roots. Bland but nutritious. Available in
meadows throughout the growing season. Dried clover
flowers make a mild tea.

**Dandelion.** The entire plant is edible — leaves (bitter,
best young), roots (roasted and ground as a drink — not
coffee but a hot, bitter substitute), flowers (sweet,
eaten raw or fried in batter if batter exists). Dandelions
grow everywhere and are one of the first and last green
things available in the season.

### Honey Hunting

**Wild bees.** Northern forests contain wild bee colonies
in hollow trees. Finding them: watch for bee flight lines
(bees travel in straight lines between hive and food
source) and follow. The hive is usually in a hollow
at least ten feet up the trunk.

**Harvesting.** Smoke subdues the bees (drives them
deeper into the hive). A fire of damp grass or punk wood
at the base of the tree, or a smoking brand held near the
entrance. The harvester climbs to the hollow and reaches
in (wearing a leather wrap on the arm helps but does not
eliminate stings). Break off sections of honeycomb. The
comb contains honey, wax, pollen, and larvae — all are
edible. The larvae are high in protein.

**Dangers.** Bee stings in quantity are dangerous —
thirty or more stings can cause a systemic reaction
even in a man who is not allergic. A man who is allergic
(a small percentage, unknown until the first major
stinging event) can die from a dozen stings. Dalla
carries no treatment for this. Falling from the tree
while being stung is the more common injury.

**Value.** Honey is one of the most valuable wild
products. Calorie-dense, naturally antiseptic (wound
treatment), preservative (fruit preserved in honey keeps
for months), and the basis for mead (fermented honey
water — the culture's primary celebratory drink). Finding
a wild beehive is a small windfall.

### Insects and Emergency Protein

When all other food sources fail.

**Grubs.** Found under bark (beetle larvae) and in
rotting wood. White, fat, soft-bodied. Eaten raw
(they taste of the wood they ate — pine grubs taste of
resin, oak grubs taste of nothing much) or roasted on
a hot stone until they pop (better texture, nuttier
flavor). A man pulling bark from a dead log can collect
a handful in ten minutes. It is not a meal. It is
survival.

**Ants.** Eaten alive or crushed and added to water
(the formic acid gives a sour, slightly citrus flavor —
an emergency vitamin C source). Ant colonies are easily
found. The quantity needed for real nutrition is large.
This is starvation food.

**Earthworms.** Dug from rich soil. Purged by keeping
them in clean water for several hours (they expel the
soil from their guts). Boiled or dried. High in protein.
Unpleasant in every way. Eaten when the alternative is
not eating.

## Water

**Finding.** Follow animal tracks downhill — every
animal in the landscape knows where water is. Listen for
moving water in quiet conditions (a stream can be heard at
200 paces in still air). Look for vegetation concentrations
in dry terrain — a line of green in brown land means water
below. In winter, snow is water but costs body heat to
melt (eating snow lowers core temperature — melt it over
fire first). Ice from streams or lakes is clean if the
water source is clean.

**Treatment.** Medieval people did not understand germ
theory, but they understood that some water made you sick.
Moving water preferred over standing water. Clear water
preferred over cloudy. Upstream of settlements and latrines
preferred over downstream. Boiling water was practiced
(for making broth, herb infusions) and had the accidental
benefit of sterilization, but was not understood as
purification. Weak ale was safer than water in settled
areas because the brewing process killed pathogens — this
is why armies drank ale, not because they preferred it.

**Carrying.** Leather water skins (animal stomach or
bladder, or sewn leather sealed with pitch). A skin holds
about a liter. It imparts a taste — leather and whatever
fat was used to seal it. Wooden canteens (hollowed
section of trunk, stoppered with a carved plug) are
heavier but do not flavor the water. A man needs roughly
two liters per day at rest, four or more on the march
in cold weather (cold air is dry; the body loses moisture
through breathing faster than in warm humid air).

## Sanitation and Hygiene

### Bodily Functions

**Urination.** Downwind of camp. Downhill of water
source. In freezing conditions, the steam column from
urine is visible at distance — a concealment concern.
Urine was collected in some camp settings: it is high in
ammonia, useful for treating (fulling) wool, softening
leather, and as a mordant for plant-based dyes. This is
not romanticized. It is mentioned because it is true.

**Defecation.** The latrine trench. In active camp,
always the same location. On the march (no established
camp), each man digs a small hole, uses it, covers it.
Leaves, moss, smooth stones, or snow serve as cleaning
material — cloth is too valuable to waste. Diarrhea from
bad water, bad food, or illness is the single most
dangerous non-combat condition a mercenary company faces.
It weakens men, slows the march, fouls the camp, and
spreads. When two men have it, four will have it tomorrow.
This is why latrine discipline is military discipline.

**Menstruation.** Managed practically. Rags (hence the
term), moss, or wool strips. Washed and reused. In the
band, Petra, Ash, and Dalla deal with this without
comment, assistance, or accommodation. It is not a topic
of conversation. It does not appear in the novel as a
subject — but the incidental detail of laundry, privacy,
and the small logistics of managing a body in the field
are part of the authentic texture.

### Washing

**Full bathing.** Rare on the march. When the band camps
near a stream in warm enough weather, men wash. The
process is quick — strip, wade in, scrub with sand or a
wad of rough cloth, get out. No soap in the field (soap
exists — made from animal fat and wood ash lye — but is a
settlement product, not a marching supply). Hair is washed
by dunking. Beards are rinsed. The medieval Norse combed
their hair regularly — ivory or bone combs are common
grave goods. Keeping hair untangled prevents louse
infestation (tangled hair is where lice breed).

**Hand washing.** Before meals, men rinse hands in
whatever water is available. This is hygiene as habit, not
as germ theory. The habit is real and documented — Norse
hospitality included offering guests water for hand-
washing on arrival.

**Teeth.** Chewing sticks — a twig of hazel, birch, or
willow, chewed at one end until the fibers fray into a
crude brush. Rubbed against the teeth and gums. Willow
contains salicin (natural aspirin) — the gums feel
better after. Medieval Norse teeth were surprisingly good
by later-medieval standards because the diet was low in
refined sugar. Grain-heavy diets produced more wear but
less decay. A broken tooth that abscesses is a serious
medical event — the infection can spread to the jaw and
kill.

### Lice, Fleas, and Parasites

**Inevitability.** In pre-industrial communal sleeping
arrangements, body lice and fleas are not a sign of
filth. They are a condition of existence. Every man on
the march has lice. The question is how many.

**Delousing.** Communal activity. Men sit by the fire and
pick through each other's clothing, crushing lice between
thumbnails (the pop is audible). Heating clothing over
a fire drives out lice and eggs (the seams are where they
concentrate — eggs are glued to fibers in the seams and
folds). Boiling clothing kills everything but is hard on
the fabric. In the Rimevegr, delousing is a fireside
activity like knife-sharpening — ambient, unstigmatized,
and social.

**Intestinal parasites.** Roundworm and whipworm from
undercooked meat or contaminated water. Universal in
pre-modern populations. Symptoms: gut pain, weight loss
despite eating, visible worms in stool. Treatment:
controlled fasting (starves the parasites) or herbal
purgatives (tansy, wormwood — both genuinely
anthelmintic, both toxic in excess). Dalla would know
these. The line between medicine and poison is dosage.

## Medical Skills

### Wound Treatment

**Stopping bleeding.** Direct pressure with cloth, held
firmly. If the cloth soaks through, add another on top
(do not remove the first — disturbing a forming clot
restarts the bleeding). Elevate the wound above the heart
if possible. Pressure points: the inner arm above the
elbow (brachial artery), the groin crease (femoral
artery) — pressing hard here reduces blood flow to the
limb. A tourniquet (a strip of cloth twisted tight with
a stick) is a last resort — it saves the life but may
cost the limb. The decision to tourniquet is a decision
about amputation. In the field, this decision is made in
seconds.

**Wound cleaning.** Irrigate with clean water. Wine or
ale if available — the alcohol content is low but it is
better than pond water. Pick out visible debris (cloth
fibers from clothing pushed into the wound by the weapon,
dirt, splinters). A wound that is not cleaned festers.
"Festering" is not a euphemism: the wound edges swell
red, the discharge turns yellow-green, the skin around it
becomes hot to the touch and streaked with red, and the
man develops a fever. Without antibiotics, a festered deep
wound is often fatal. This is why wound cleaning in the
field is not gentle — it is thorough, it is painful, and
it is the difference between a scar and a grave.

**Stitching.** A curved needle (bone or iron) and sinew
or linen thread. The wound edges are pressed together and
the needle passes through both edges, roughly a finger-
width apart, pulling the thread through and tying each
stitch individually. Good stitches hold the edges together
without pulling them too tight (overtight stitches cut
through the flesh and leave gaps). Fourteen stitches on
Kell's forearm means the cut was roughly six inches long.
The stitching took ten to fifteen minutes and Kell held
still for it because the alternative was bleeding until
he did not have the blood to spare.

**Poultices.** A paste of herbs, clay, or plant material
pressed against a wound and bound in place with cloth.
Plantain leaf (a common weed — anti-inflammatory, mildly
antimicrobial) is the most referenced medieval wound herb.
Yarrow (staunches bleeding — the plant name _Achillea_
comes from Achilles treating his soldiers' wounds with
it). Comfrey (promotes tissue regrowth — called "knit-
bone" for a reason). Honey, if available, is genuinely
antiseptic and was used on wounds for millennia before
anyone understood why. Dalla's herb pouch contains these
or their local equivalents.

### Bone Setting

**Diagnosis.** A broken limb has unnatural mobility where
it should be rigid. The man cannot bear weight on it.
Crepitus — the grinding sound of bone ends moving against
each other — confirms the break. A compound fracture
(bone through skin) is visible and terrifying.

**Setting.** Traction (pulling the limb straight to align
the bone ends) and then splinting. Two straight sticks or
boards, padded with cloth, bound on either side of the
break with strips of cloth or leather. The splint must
immobilize the joint above and below the break. A set
forearm cannot bend the elbow or wrist. An unset fracture
heals crooked — the limb works but works wrong, forever.

**Recovery.** Six to eight weeks for a clean break to
bear weight. The man is non-functional for that entire
period. In a mercenary band, this means he eats but does
not earn. The ledger records this as a cost. Whether the
band waits for him or leaves him in a settlement depends
on who he is and what the band can afford.

### Fever and Infection

**Recognition.** Hot skin, shivering despite the heat,
rapid pulse (felt at the wrist or throat), confusion,
sweating. A man with a wound who develops a fever within
two days of the injury has an infected wound. A man with
fever but no wound has caught something — respiratory
infection, waterborne illness, or the beginning of
something worse.

**Treatment.** Willow bark tea (contains salicin — genuine
fever reducer). Cool cloths on the forehead and wrists.
Fluids — broth, water, weak ale. Keep the man warm but
not overheated. There is no medieval cure for serious
systemic infection. Dalla knows this. She does what she
can and does not promise what she cannot deliver. When a
man is going to die of fever, she keeps him comfortable
and keeps the camp from panicking.

## Horses and Pack Animals

### Mule Care

**Feeding.** A working mule needs 15-20 pounds of forage
per day — grass, hay, grain. On the march, the mule
grazes when the band stops. If grazing is poor, the
mule's load is reduced or grain is shared from the band's
supply. A starved mule collapses under load and takes a
day to recover, if it recovers. The men eat less before
the mule eats less, because the mule carries everyone's
gear.

**Hoof care.** A mule's hooves grow continuously and must
be trimmed every six to eight weeks. A farrier (the band
does not have one — someone learns the minimum) uses a
rasp and a hoof knife to pare excess hoof wall. A stone
bruise (from stepping on a sharp rock) lames a mule for
days. A thrown shoe on a horse (the band rarely has
horses) requires a smith. The shoe is nailed through the
insensitive hoof wall — done correctly, painless; done
badly, the nail hits living tissue and the animal goes
immediately lame.

**Loading.** Pack saddle balanced on either side. Weight
must be equal — a mule with an unbalanced load develops
saddle sores on the heavy side within three days. The
sores are raw, weeping patches where the skin has been
rubbed through. They take weeks to heal and the mule
cannot be loaded during that time. Proper loading is a
skill. Gest checks the mule's back daily.

## Textile and Repair Skills

### Sewing

**The needle kit.** Every man carries one. A bone or iron
needle (bone for thick leather, iron for finer work), a
length of linen thread or sinew wound on a bone spool,
and a small block of beeswax to draw the thread through
(waxing strengthens the thread and makes it water-resistant
and less likely to tangle). Alternatively, a strip of
pitch to stiffen the thread end into a point.

**Common repairs.** A torn boot sole is restitched from
the inside. A split seam is closed with a whip stitch
(fast, rough, holds). A worn elbow or knee is patched —
cut a piece from a worn-out garment, lay it over the hole,
stitch around the edges. Nobody's clothing fits properly
or looks new. Everything is patched. The patches have
patches.

**Waterproofing.** Leather treated with fat (see armor
section). Wool cloaks brushed with tallow and hung taut
to let the tallow melt into the fibers near a fire.
Seams sealed with pine pitch or beeswax run along the
stitching with a heated knife tip. A properly treated wool
cloak sheds moderate rain for an hour before saturating.
This hour of dryness is the difference between functional
and miserable.

### Cobbling

**Boots.** The most critical garment on the march. A man
with bad boots is a man who cannot march. A man who
cannot march is left behind.

**Medieval boot construction.** Turnshoe method: the boot
is sewn inside-out (the seam is on the inside, where it
does not contact the ground) and then turned right-side
out. The sole is a single piece of thick leather. It wears
through in 300-500 miles of rough terrain. When the sole
wears thin, a new layer of leather is sewn (or riveted)
over the old sole. Multiple resolings make the boot heavier
and stiffer with each repair. Eventually the upper
separates from the accumulated sole layers and the boot
is done.

**Daily care.** Boots are removed at camp. Wet boots are
stuffed with dry grass (absorbs moisture, holds shape) and
dried slowly near the fire (not on it — see leather
section). The insides are aired. In deep cold, boots left
outside freeze stiff and must be warmed before putting
on — a man who staggers into frozen boots in an emergency
loses ten minutes and possibly toe-skin to the rigid
leather. Camp wisdom: sleep with boots under the cloak,
near the body, if temperatures drop below freezing.

## Smithing and Metalwork

### What Every Man Knows

No one expects every man to forge a sword. But every man
in the Rimevegr understands basic metal behavior — because
metal tools break, and a band without a working axe-head
is a band in trouble.

**Heat changes metal.** Iron heated in a fire becomes soft
enough to bend. The hotter the fire, the softer the metal.
A normal wood fire reaches about 700°C — enough to heat
iron to a dull red, enough to straighten a bent blade
or close a split in a tool head. Forging temperature
(bright orange to yellow, over 1000°C) requires charcoal
or forced air (bellows). This is smith's territory.

**Quenching.** Hot metal plunged into water hardens. But
hardness brings brittleness — a fully quenched blade
shatters under a hard blow instead of bending. The smith
controls this by tempering: reheating the quenched metal
to a lower temperature and letting it cool slowly. The
temper color (straw yellow, bronze, blue, purple) tells
the smith the hardness. Every man has watched this. Few
can do it reliably.

**Riveting.** The field-repair technique. Two pieces of
metal (or metal to wood) are joined by a rivet — a short
iron pin pushed through aligned holes and hammered flat
on both ends. Repairing chainmail is riveting. Repairing
a cracked tool head is riveting. Reattaching a shield
boss is riveting. Any man who has served in a band has
hammered rivets. The skill: getting the rivet tight
enough to hold but not so tight that the surrounding
material cracks.

### Bog Iron

The Rimevegr's primary iron source.

**Formation.** Iron dissolved in groundwater precipitates
in bogs and marshes, forming lumps of iron-rich ore
mixed with sand, clay, and organic material. These lumps
are found by probing boggy ground with a stick — the
lumps feel different from rocks (lighter, rougher
texture). The reddish-brown staining in bog water is
the indicator — where the water bleeds orange, iron is
present.

**Smelting.** The ore is roasted over a fire to drive off
moisture. Then smelted in a bloomery — a chimney-shaped
clay furnace. Layers of charcoal and roasted ore are
stacked in the furnace. Bellows force air through the base.
The temperature climbs to 1200°C or higher. The iron
melts partially, the slag (waste) runs liquid to the
bottom, and the iron forms a spongy mass called a bloom
at the center. The bloom is pulled from the furnace
(still white-hot) and hammered on an anvil to compress
it and drive out trapped slag. This wrought iron can
then be forged into tools and weapons.

\*\*The entire process — gathering ore, roasting, building
the furnace, smelting, and pulling the bloom — takes two
to three days and produces enough iron for perhaps two
axe-heads or a hundred nails. Iron is not cheap. Every
nail has cost labor.

### Nails, Hooks, and Simple Hardware

The most common smithing products. A functioning camp
needs nails (for repairs, building, shoe-caulks), hooks
(for hanging, fishing, pot-hanging), hasps (for securing
doors and chests), and hinges (for anything that swings).

**Nail-making.** A smith heats a rod of iron, draws it to
a point on the anvil, cuts it to length with a chisel-
cut, inserts the cut end in a nail header (a plate with
a hole), and hammers the protruding end flat to form the
nail head. An experienced smith makes a nail every thirty
seconds. A competent apprentice takes two minutes. A man
at a campfire with a rock for an anvil takes ten minutes
and produces an ugly but functional nail.

### Tool Repair

**Axe-head reshaping.** A cracked or chipped axe-head is
reheated (red heat — a sustained fire with bellows or a
charcoal bed), the damaged section is hammered back to
shape on a flat stone or stump-anvil, and the edge is
reground. If the crack is through the eye (the hole for
the handle), the head is finished — no amount of hammering
closes a through-crack in the eye. A new head must be
forged.

**Handle replacement.** When a tool handle breaks (and
they break — overstriking, dry rot, impact fatigue), the
stub is driven out of the head by heating the head (the
wood chars and shrinks) and punching the remainder through.
A new handle is shaped from a billet of straight-grained
hardwood (ash preferred — it absorbs shock, is strong in
tension, and splits predictably). The handle is shaped
oval in cross-section (not round — an oval handle tells
the hand which way the blade faces without looking). The
head is fitted by driving the handle into the eye and
wedging it tight: a hardwood wedge driven into a slot cut
in the handle end, expanding the wood inside the eye.
A properly fitted handle is immovable. A loose handle
means a flying axe-head, which means injury or death.

## Bone, Antler, and Horn Working

### Bone

Every butchered animal produces bone. Bone is hard, can
be carved and polished, and does not rust.

**Needles.** A flat sliver of bone, sharpened to a point,
with an eye drilled through using a flint awl or a fine
iron drill bit. Bone needles are not as strong as iron
ones but they are free and replaceable. Every man carries
at least one for mending clothing and gear.

**Toggles and buttons.** Small shaped pieces of bone
used as fasteners. A toggle is a cylinder of bone with
a groove around the middle — a cord tied in the groove
holds it to the garment, and the toggle is pushed through
a loop to fasten. Simpler and more durable than most
other fastener methods.

**Dice.** Carved from dense leg bones (cattle, horse).
Six-sided, marked with drilled pits or carved lines.
Well-made bone dice are balanced enough for fair play.
Poorly made ones favor certain faces — which is why
experienced gamblers test dice by spinning them in water
before agreeing to play.

**Combs.** Antler or dense bone, carved and polished.
A comb is not vanity — it is parasite control. Regular
combing pulls nits (louse eggs) from hair and catches
adult lice. Every Norse man carries a comb. Losing the
comb is a hygiene problem that leads to infestation
within a week.

### Antler

Shed antler is collected in spring. Fresh antler from a
killed deer is available when hunting succeeds.

**Working.** Antler is soaked in water for several days
to soften the outer layer, then carved with a knife
(cuts more easily than unsoftened antler), drilled,
and polished. The tines (pointed branches) are natural
handles and pressure-flakers. The beam (main shaft) is
cut into sections for tool handles, cheek-pieces for
bridles, or decorative plaques.

**Pressure-flaking.** An antler tine is pressed against
the edge of a flint blade to chip tiny flakes free,
refining the edge. This is primarily a flint-working
technique (see stone tools) but the antler tine is the
universal pressure tool.

### Horn

Horn from cattle, goats, and sheep is heated (boiled or
steamed) until pliable, then shaped. Unlike antler, horn
is a keratin sheath (like fingernails) — it can be
flattened, bent, molded, and polished to a translucent
finish.

**Drinking horns.** The horn is cleaned (the pith scraped
from the interior), the open end trimmed smooth, and the
interior sealed with beeswax or pitch. A drinking horn
does not stand upright — it must be emptied when filled.
This is cultural: filling and draining a horn at a feast
is performance.

**Horn plates.** Flattened horn (boiled until soft, then
pressed between flat boards until cool) produces thin,
translucent sheets used for lantern windows (horn-lanthorn
— light passes through but wind cannot reach the flame)
and decorative inlay.

**Spoons and containers.** The natural curve of a horn
lends itself to spoon shapes (the tip, carved and
thinned) and small lidded containers (the wide end,
sealed with a wooden plug). A horn spoon is lighter
than a wooden one and does not split from wet-dry
cycling.

## Leather Working and Tanning

### The Full Process

Leather does not happen automatically. Skin rots. Turning
skin into leather is a multi-day process that stinks so
distinctly that tanneries are always downwind of
settlements.

**Skinning.** The hide is removed from the carcass in one
piece. The cut runs from throat to tail along the belly,
then down each leg. The skin is peeled away from the
flesh using fist-pressure and short knife cuts where
the membrane clings. Nicking the hide (cutting through
it accidentally) reduces its value — each hole is a weak
point. A skilled skinner produces a perfect hide in
thirty minutes from a deer-sized animal.

**Fleshing.** The inside of the hide (the flesh side) is
scraped to remove all remaining meat, fat, and membrane.
The hide is stretched over a smooth log or a fleshing
beam (a rounded wooden form at waist height). A fleshing
knife (a dull-edged blade — it scrapes, does not cut) is
pushed across the flesh side at a shallow angle. Every
scrap of tissue must come off. Remaining flesh rots and
contaminates the leather.

**Dehairing (for smooth leather).** The hair side is
treated to loosen the hair follicles. Methods: soak the
hide in a slurry of wood ash and water (lye — the alkali
loosens the hair) for one to two weeks, turning daily.
Or: fold the hide hair-side in and let it sit until the
hair loosens naturally (controlled rotting — the smell
is extraordinary). When the hair pulls free with a tug,
scrape the hair side clean with the fleshing knife.

**Tanning.** The cleaned hide is preserved. Methods used
in the Norse world:

- **Bark tanning.** The hide is soaked in a solution made
  from tree bark (oak, willow, birch — each producing
  different color and flexibility). Tannins in the bark
  bind to the collagen in the hide, converting it to
  leather. This takes weeks to months. The hide is
  moved through progressively stronger bark solutions.
  The result is firm, water-resistant leather (the
  "leather" smell of a new belt or boot is tannin).

- **Brain tanning.** The animal's own brain is mashed
  and worked into the hide. Brain contains lecithin
  (an emulsifying fat) that penetrates the collagen
  fibers and makes them supple. The hide is worked
  (stretched, pulled, twisted) continuously as it
  dries. If the hide dries without being worked, it
  stiffens to board-hardness. Properly brain-tanned
  leather is butter-soft, breathable, and comfortable
  against skin. Light-colored. Absorbs water (not
  water-resistant like bark-tanned leather). Used for
  clothing rather than hard goods.

- **Smoke tanning.** After brain tanning, the hide is
  hung in dense smoke for several hours. The smoke
  deposits compounds that add water resistance and
  prevent the leather from stiffening when wet (brain-
  tanned leather that gets thoroughly soaked must be
  re-worked as it dries or it goes stiff; smoke-tanned
  leather does not). The smoke also adds the
  distinctive gold-brown color.

**Conditioning.** Finished leather is rubbed with fat
(tallow, lard, or fish oil) to keep it supple. Leather
dried out by sun, wind, or heat cracks and loses
strength. Every leather item — boots, belts, scabbards,
armor, harness — must be oiled periodically. This is
maintenance, not luxury.

## Rope and Cordage

### Materials

**Bast fiber.** The inner bark of lime (linden), willow,
or elm. Stripped in spring when the sap is running (the
bark peels easily). The strips are retted (soaked in
water for a week or two until the outer bark rots away
and the inner fibers separate), dried, and processed.
The resulting fibers are strong, flexible, and make
excellent rope.

**Nettle fiber.** Stinging nettles. Harvested in autumn
when the stems are tall and dry. The stems are split, the
pith removed, and the outer fibers peeled off. Retted
and processed like bast fiber. Nettle cordage is fine and
strong — suitable for fishing lines and sewing thread as
well as rope.

**Rawhide.** A strip of untanned hide, twisted while wet
and allowed to dry under tension. Rawhide cordage is
immensely strong (stronger than plant fiber rope of the
same thickness) but stiffens when dry and softens when
wet. Used for lashing heavy loads, securing structural
joints (green rawhide lashed around a joint shrinks as it
dries, tightening to iron-hardness), and making snowshoe
webbing.

**Sinew.** Tendons from large animals (deer, elk, cattle),
dried and split into fine fibers. Sinew is the strongest
natural cordage material per unit width. Used for bowstrings,
sewing heavy leather, and binding arrow points to shafts.
Not used for general rope — too labor-intensive to produce
in quantity.

**Horsehair.** Tail and mane hair, braided or twisted.
Strong, smooth, water-resistant. Used for fishing lines,
snares, and fine lashing. Enough horsehair for a fishing
line comes from a single tail-dressing.

### Making Rope

**Twisting.** The basic technique. Fibers are divided
into two or three bundles. Each bundle is twisted
clockwise. Then the bundles are twisted together counter-
clockwise (the opposite direction). The opposing twists
lock against each other — each tries to unwind and in
doing so tightens the other. This is the principle
behind all rope.

**Two-ply.** Two bundles twisted together. Fast to make.
The simplest functional rope. Used for light duty —
tying bundles, securing gear, clothes lines.

**Three-ply.** Three bundles twisted together. Stronger,
rounder, handles better. The standard rope for hauling,
climbing, and structural work. Takes longer to lay (the
three-way relationship requires even tension throughout).

**Splicing.** The ends of rope are joined by unraveling
each end back into its component strands, then weaving
the strands of one end through the body of the other.
A properly spliced joint is as strong as the rope itself.
A knotted joint reduces rope strength by 30-50% (the
sharp bend in each knot concentrates stress). Where
strength matters, splice. Where convenience matters, knot.

## Woodcarving

### Utility Carving

Carving for function, not decoration.

**Spoons.** Every man can carve a spoon. A billet of
green wood (birch, alder, cherry) is split to rough
shape, the bowl is carved with a hook knife (a curved
blade that scoops), and the handle is shaped with a
straight knife. A serviceable spoon takes thirty minutes.
A good one takes an hour. A beautiful one takes an
afternoon, and the carver keeps that one.

**Bowls.** A section of log (or a burl — a swollen
growth on a tree trunk that produces wild, interlocked
grain that resists splitting) is hollowed by rotating
the piece and cutting with a curved knife or an adze
(a chisel-like blade set perpendicular to the handle,
swung like a small axe). Green wood hollows easily. The
bowl is left thick (thumb-thickness) because it shrinks
and may crack as it dries. Oiled with animal fat after
drying.

**Trenchers.** Flat wooden plates, carved or hewn from
a slab. The center is slightly dished. Trenchers
replace bowls when wood is plentiful — they are faster
to make and stack for packing.

**Cutting boards.** A flat slab of durable hardwood
(oak, maple). No carving needed — just a cleanly split
and smoothed surface.

### Decorative Carving

Norse decorative carving follows established styles —
interlocking animals, knotwork, vine scrolls. This is
skilled work. But simple decorative carving (chip-
carving — geometric patterns produced by removing small
triangular chips with a knife point) is practiced by
many men as a way to pass time and mark possessions.

A man's comb, spoon, and knife handle often carry chip-
carved patterns. These are not art for display — they
are identification. In a camp where every man owns a
wooden spoon, the carved pattern tells whose is whose.

## Basket Weaving

Baskets are containers. Where a Norse man uses barrels
and chests for permanent storage, baskets fill every role
that requires a light, expendable, or quickly-made
container.

**Materials.** Willow (the primary material — flexible,
strong, available near water), hazel, birch bark, roots,
and rush.

**The woven basket.** A frame of rigid stakes (thick
willow rods) set into a base, with thinner rods
(weavers) woven in and out between the stakes. The
weave pattern (over one, under one, or over two, under
two) determines the tightness and strength. A tight weave
makes a water-carrying basket (lined with pitch or wax).
A loose weave makes a fish trap or a foraging basket.

**Birch-bark containers.** The bark is cut from the
tree in large sheets (cut a ring around the trunk, a
vertical slit, and peel). Shaped into boxes, containers,
and lightweight packs by folding and sewing the seams
with root fibers or sinew. Birch-bark containers are
naturally water-resistant and lightweight. They are the
Tupperware of the Viking age — common, practical,
disposable.

## Pottery

### What a Band Carries

Ceramic pots are heavy and fragile — broken by a fall
from a mule or a kick in camp. A traveling band carries
as few as possible. But pottery does things that wood
and leather cannot: it holds boiling liquid directly over
a fire (wooden vessels cannot — the wood chars and burns
unless continuously full of water), and it resists acid
and alkali (important for fermenting, pickling, and
storing rendered fat).

### Field Pottery

In a long-term camp, a potter (or anyone with
knowledge) can make pottery from local clay.

**Finding clay.** Clay deposits are found at riverbanks,
in eroded hillsides, and wherever the soil has a smooth,
sticky, grey or red consistency. Dig below the topsoil.
Good pottery clay is smooth when wet, holds its shape
when formed, and does not crumble when partially dried.
Sandy clay works but produces weaker vessels. Pure clay
with no sand works but cracks during drying and firing.
Most natural clay is a workable mixture.

**Forming.** The coil method: long ropes of clay are
rolled, then coiled in a spiral to build the walls of
the vessel. Each coil is smoothed into the one below it
(inside and outside) to seal the joint. The base is a
flat disk of clay. The shape is built up coil by coil,
narrowing toward the rim. Smoothed inside and out with
a wet hand or a smooth stone.

**Drying.** The formed pot must dry slowly and completely
before firing. Too fast and it cracks (the outer surface
shrinks faster than the interior). Two to five days
in shade with good airflow. The pot changes color as it
dries — dark when wet, lighter when dry. When uniformly
light-colored and cool to the touch (no evaporative
cooling from internal moisture), it is ready to fire.

**Firing.** In a pit fire: the pot is placed in a shallow
pit, surrounded and covered with wood, and the fire is
burned for four to six hours, reaching 700-900°C. The
fired pot is left to cool slowly in the ash bed (pulling
it out while hot causes thermal shock and cracking). The
result is a porous, fragile but functional vessel. It
holds water (though it seeps slowly through the walls —
this is a feature for water storage, as the evaporation
cools the contents) and withstands direct fire heat.

## Stone Tools

### When Metal Is Unavailable

Metal tools break, are lost, and are sometimes impossible
to replace. A man who knows stone has a backup.

**Flint-knapping.** Flint (or obsidian, chert, or other
glassy stone) fractures in predictable curves when struck
correctly. The technique: hold the core (the piece of
stone being shaped) in one hand, strike the edge with a
hammerstone (a round, hard stone that fits the fist) at
a precise angle. The blow sends a shockwave through the
stone that detaches a flake — a thin, razor-sharp piece
that is either a finished tool (a flake knife) or requires
further refinement.

**Pressure-flaking.** Once the rough shape is struck, fine
edges are produced by pressing an antler tine against the
edge and levering off tiny chips. This produces a sharper,
more controlled edge than percussion.

**Stone tools in a metal-working culture.** Every man in
the Rimevegr owns metal tools. Stone tools are backup —
carried by nobody, improvised by anybody who needs one.
A broken knife means making a flint scraper from a
riverbed stone. A man who has done it once can do it
again in ten minutes. A man who has never done it takes
an hour and produces something crude but functional.

## Tar and Pitch Production

### What They Are

**Tar.** A thick black liquid produced by heating wood
(specifically resinous wood — pine) in the absence of
air (the same principle as charcoal-making, but the
volatile compounds are collected instead of burned off).

**Pitch.** Tar boiled down to a thicker consistency,
sometimes mixed with animal fat or beeswax for specific
properties.

### Uses

- **Waterproofing.** Boat seams, rope, leather, and
  cloth treated with tar or pitch resist water. Ships
  are tarred annually. A man's boots are pitched to
  extend their life in wet terrain.
- **Adhesive.** Pitch mixed with beeswax and charcoal
  dust creates a strong adhesive used to set arrowheads
  in shafts, seal joints, and repair cracked pottery.
- **Preservative.** Wooden structures (posts, pilings)
  coated with tar resist rot. Fence posts driven into
  the ground last years longer when the buried portion
  is tarred.
- **Medical.** Tar applied to wounds seals them against
  air and contamination. This is painful (the smell is
  intense, the tar is applied warm) but effective as an
  emergency sealant.

### Field Production

**The pit method.** A pit is dug. Resinous pine wood
(heartwood, especially from the base of old pines —
the most resin-saturated wood) is packed into the pit.
A fire is built on top and the pit is sealed with earth,
leaving a drain-hole at the bottom. As the fire heats the
wood below, the tar liquefies and drains out through the
hole into a collection vessel. A well-packed pit produces
a few liters of tar from a half-day burn. This is enough
to waterproof the seams of a small boat or treat several
pairs of boots.

## Soap Making

### The Problem It Solves

Grease, blood, and accumulated body soil do not wash off
with cold water alone. The fat bonds to the skin and
fabric. Soap breaks the bond — the lye in soap emulsifies
the grease, allowing water to carry it away.

### The Recipe

**Fat.** Any animal fat — rendered tallow (beef or
mutton fat melted down), lard (pig fat), or fish oil.
Rendered fat is filtered to remove tissue and impurities.

**Lye.** Wood ash (hardwood ash is strongest — oak, ash,
beech) is packed into a container with a drain. Water is
poured through the ash repeatedly. The water leaches
potassium hydroxide (lye) from the ash. The resulting
lye-water is caustic — it burns skin on prolonged
contact. The strength is tested by touch: if the lye-
water feels slippery between the fingers, it is strong
enough. Too strong and the resulting soap is harsh. Too
weak and the fat does not saponify.

**The process.** Rendered fat and lye-water are heated
together in a pot, stirring continuously, for several
hours. The fat and lye react (saponification), producing
soap and glycerin. The mixture thickens to a paste. Poured
into a mold (a wooden box lined with cloth) and left to
harden for days. The result is a block of soft soap —
not the hard white bars of later centuries, but a
yellowish, slightly gritty, powerfully effective
cleaning material.

**In the field.** Full soap-making is a settlement
activity. In the field, the expedient version: rub ash
directly on the hands and scrub. The lye in the ash
provides some emulsifying action. Not as effective as
soap, but better than water alone.

## Dye Making

### Plant Dyes

Clothing and cordage are dyed for identification (band
colors), status (certain colors reserved for leaders),
and preservation (some dyes contain tannins that protect
fiber from rot).

| Plant        | Color       | Process                         |
| ------------ | ----------- | ------------------------------- |
| Woad leaves  | Blue        | Ferment, reduce, dip repeatedly |
| Madder root  | Red         | Boil root, mordant first        |
| Weld plant   | Yellow      | Boil with alum mordant          |
| Walnut hulls | Brown-black | Soak cloth in hull water        |
| Birch bark   | Pink-tan    | Boil bark, soak cloth           |
| Elderberry   | Purple-grey | Crush berries, simmer           |
| Onion skins  | Orange-gold | Boil skins, mordant with alum   |

**Mordanting.** Most plant dyes do not bond permanently
to fiber without a mordant — a substance that fixes the
dye to the cloth. Common mordants: alum (a mineral salt —
available in some regions as a trade good), iron (rusty
water — produces darker, duller tones), and copper (green-
shifting). Without a mordant, the color fades with each
washing.

**Fixedness.** Woad blue and madder red are reasonably
fast (they resist fading). Most other plant dyes fade
over time, especially with sun exposure and washing.
This is why Norse clothing fades — fresh-dyed garments
are vivid; travel-worn garments are muted. The fade
pattern tells an observer how new the clothing is and
by implication how recently the wearer had access to
a settlement with dyeing facilities.

## Mead and Ale Brewing

### Mead

**Ingredients.** Honey and water. The ratio is roughly
one part honey to three parts water for a strong mead,
one to five for a lighter drink. Nothing else is
strictly necessary, though wild fruits, herbs, or
spices may be added for flavor.

**Fermentation.** Wild yeast — present on the honey, in
the air, on the vessel walls — begins the work. The
mixture is placed in a vessel (a wooden bucket, an
earthenware crock, a leather bag) and left in a warm
place. Within two to three days, it begins to bubble
as the yeast converts sugar to alcohol. The vessel is
not sealed — fermentation produces gas that must
escape, or the vessel bursts.

**Timing.** A young mead is drinkable in two to three
weeks. It is cloudy, sweet, and mildly alcoholic. Good
mead takes months. Exceptional mead takes a year or
more. The band does not make exceptional mead. They
make what they can drink before the next march.

**Failure modes.** Vinegar — the mead sours. This happens
when the wrong organisms colonize the batch (acetic
acid bacteria instead of or alongside yeast). Causes:
dirty vessel, too warm, too much air exposure. The sour
mead is not wasted — it becomes vinegar, which has its
own uses. Flat mead — insufficient yeast activity.
Causes: too cold, too little sugar, dead yeast.
Contaminated — off flavors, illness after drinking.
Causes: a dirty vessel, rotten fruit added, mold.

### Ale

**Ingredients.** Grain (barley preferred, but rye, oats,
or wheat work), water, and yeast. The grain must be
malted first: soaked in water until it begins to
sprout, then dried. The sprouting activates enzymes
that convert starch to sugar. Without malting, the
yeast has nothing to eat.

**The mash.** Malted grain is crushed (between stones,
with a pestle, or under a boot heel in the field) and
steeped in hot water. Not boiling — too hot kills the
enzymes. The water turns sweet. This sweet liquid
(wort) is drained off, cooled, and transferred to
the fermentation vessel.

**Fermentation and timing.** Similar to mead. Ale
ferments faster — drinkable in a week, sometimes less
in warm conditions. It does not keep well. Two weeks
to a month is the useful life. After that, it sours.

**Hops.** Not yet standard in the tenth century. Ale is
flavored and preserved with gruit — a mixture of herbs
(bog myrtle, yarrow, mugwort, juniper berries). The
specific gruit recipe varies by region and availability.
Without some form of flavoring and preservative, ale
tastes flat and spoils quickly.

## Vinegar

### Production

Leave ale or mead exposed to air. Acetic acid bacteria
(always present in the environment) convert the alcohol
to acetic acid. This takes days to weeks. The process
is faster in warm conditions. A "mother" — a gelatinous
mat of bacteria that forms on the surface — can be
transferred to fresh batches to speed the process.

### Vinegar Uses

**Preservation.** Meat, fish, and vegetables stored in
vinegar resist spoilage. Pickling is preservation
by acid — the low pH inhibits bacteria. A barrel of
vinegar in camp means vegetables that last months
instead of days.

**Cleaning.** Vinegar cuts grease, dissolves mineral
deposits, and disinfects surfaces. Chainmail rinsed
in vinegar after scouring loses its rust smell and
resists re-rusting longer.

**Medicine.** Diluted vinegar cleans wounds (painful
but effective at preventing infection). Vinegar-soaked
cloth applied to a feverish forehead is a folk remedy
that provides evaporative cooling.

**Descaling.** Lime scale in pots, on armor, in water
containers — vinegar dissolves it. A cooking pot scaled
from hard water is restored by boiling vinegar in it.

## Lye and Ash Water

### Lye Production

**Wood ash lye.** Hardwood ash (oak, ash, beech — not
softwood, which produces a weaker lye) is placed in a
bucket or trough. Water is poured through the ash
slowly. The water that drains out the bottom is lye —
potassium hydroxide in solution. Strength is tested by
touch: strong lye feels slippery (it is dissolving the
outer layer of skin). If it burns, it is concentrated
enough for soap.

**Concentration.** Weak lye is boiled down to increase
strength. The water evaporates; the lye remains. This
requires a dedicated pot — the lye corrodes most metals.
Earthenware or wooden vessels are used.

### Lye Uses

**Soap making.** Lye + fat = soap (see Soap Making
section in the existing document). This is lye's
primary use.

**Tanning.** Lye baths dehair hides. The hide is soaked
in a lye solution for several days. The hair loosens
and slips. The same bath softens the hide for further
processing. (See Leather Working section.)

**Cleaning.** Lye water cleans wood, cloth, and surfaces
more effectively than plain water. A floor scrubbed
with weak lye water is visibly cleaner. Clothing
boiled in lye water loses ground-in grime.

**Bleaching.** Lye water lightens cloth. Linen soaked in
ash water and spread in sunlight bleaches white over
days. This is how white linen is produced before
industrial chemistry.

**Burn risk.** Strong lye causes chemical burns. Skin
contact produces redness and pain similar to a heat
burn. Eye contact can cause blindness. Lye is handled
with respect and stored where it cannot be mistaken
for drinking water. The pot is marked. The children
are warned.

## Lime

### Lime Production

**Burning.** Limestone or chalk (calcium carbonate) is
heated in a kiln or a fire pit to extreme temperature.
The stone crumbles into quickite (calcium oxide) — a
white powder that reacts violently with water. This
requires sustained high heat for hours. A dedicated
lime kiln is a proper structure. A field expedient is
a deep fire pit with limestone stacked above a bed of
hardwood coals, burned for most of a day.

**Slaking.** Quickite plus water produces slaked lime
(calcium hydroxide). The reaction generates intense
heat — the water boils, the mixture spits. This is
done in the open, upwind, with no bare skin exposed.
Slaked lime is a paste or powder depending on the
amount of water added.

### Lime Uses

**Mortar.** Slaked lime mixed with sand and water
produces morite, which hardens as it dries. This is
the binding agent for stone construction. Every stone
building, wall, hearth, and chimney in the Rimevegr
uses lime mortar.

**Whitewash.** Slaked lime mixed with water produces a
white liquid that is painted onto walls. It seals,
brightens, and mildly disinfects surfaces. Interior
walls of any well-maintained building are whitewashed.

**Tanning.** Lime baths dehair hides (similar to lye
but a different chemistry). The hide swells and the
hair loosens.

**Disinfecting.** Lime spread in latrines, over graves,
and in areas of disease reduces smell and slows
decomposition. The Romans did this. The Norse do it
less systematically but know the principle.

**Agriculture.** Lime spread on acidic soil improves
fertility. A field that won't grow barley may grow it
after liming.

## Waterproofing

### Methods

**Lanolin.** The natural grease in sheep's wool. When
wool is washed, the lanolin floats in the wash water
and can be skimmed off. Applied to leather, cloth, or
seams, it provides a water-resistant coating. Lanolin
does not make a material waterproof — it makes it
water-resistant, shedding light rain and spray.
Heavy sustained rain eventually penetrates.

**Beeswax.** Melted and rubbed into leather or cloth.
More effective than lanolin. A boot treated with
beeswax sheds water better and longer than an
untreated boot. Thread rubbed with beeswax before
sewing becomes water-resistant — seams stop leaking.
The disadvantage is supply: beeswax requires bees
and is not always available.

**Pitch and tar.** The most waterproof coating available.
Birch-bark tar or pine pitch is heated until liquid and
applied to wood, leather, or cloth. Boat hulls, water
containers, and roofs are tarred. Pitch is heavy,
darkens the material, and stiffens leather. It is not
used on clothing — tarred clothing does not flex and
does not breathe. It is used on boats, containers,
and structural elements.

**Oil.** Linseed oil, fish oil, or animal fat. Rubbed
into leather and allowed to soak and dry. Multiple
coats build a waterproof surface. The disadvantage:
oil attracts dirt, can turn rancid (especially animal
fats), and may weaken some fibers over time.

**Combinations.** The most effective treatment combines
methods. A boat hull is tarred on the outside, oiled
on the inside. A boot is oiled and then waxed at the
seams. A tent is oiled overall and tarred at the seams.
Layered treatments last longer than single applications.

### Seam Sealing

The weakest point in any container or garment is the
seam. Water enters through needle holes and the gaps
between stitches. Seam sealing: wax or pitch drawn
along the seam with a heated tool, filling needle holes
and coating the thread. On boats, seams are caulked —
fibers (tar-soaked wool, animal hair, moss) are driven
into the gaps between planks, then sealed with pitch.

## Glue

### Types

**Hide glue.** Made by boiling scraps of hide, sinew,
and connective tissue in water for hours. The collagen
dissolves. The liquid, cooled, sets into a semi-solid
that liquefies again when heated. Applied hot, it bonds
wood, leather, bone, and paper. When dry, it is strong
but brittle. Moisture weakens it. Hide glue is the
universal adhesive of the pre-industrial world.

**Fish glue.** Made from fish skins and swim bladders,
boiled to extract collagen. Stronger and more flexible
than hide glue. More water-resistant. Used where a
better bond is needed — laminating bow staves, repairing
musical instruments, attaching fletching to arrows.
The disadvantage: it requires fish parts and time.

**Hoof glue.** Made by boiling hooves (horse, cattle).
Similar to hide glue but darker, smellier, and
slightly less strong. Used when hide scraps are not
available but hooves are — after butchering a horse,
for instance.

**Birch-bark tar.** Not a collagen glue but a resinous
adhesive. Strong, waterproof, and heat-resistant once
set. Used for hafting stone tools, sealing containers,
and waterproofing. Produced by destructive distillation
of birch bark. (See Tar and Pitch section in existing
document.)

### Application

All collagen glues are applied hot. The joint is fitted,
the glue is heated until liquid, applied to both
surfaces, and the surfaces are pressed together. The
joint must be clamped, bound, or weighted until the
glue sets (minutes to hours depending on temperature
and thickness). Reheating a glued joint releases it —
which means repairs are possible but moisture and heat
are enemies of glued assemblies.

## Poisons and Practical Toxicology

### What a Practical Man Knows

A warrior does not brew poisons — that is a thrall's
tale and a liar's boast. But a practical man knows what
kills, what sickens, and how to avoid both.

**Monkshood (wolfsbane).** A blue-flowered plant of
mountain meadows. Every part is toxic — root, stem,
leaf, flower. The root is the most potent. Handling
the plant with bare hands can cause numbness and
tingling. Ingestion causes burning in the mouth,
vomiting, paralysis, and death. The name "wolfsbane"
comes from its use as an arrow poison against wolves.
Whether it was applied to weapons against men is
debated and not proven. A man who recognizes monkshood
does not eat near it and does not let the cook
mistake the root for parsnip.

**Hemlock.** Tall, white-flowered, hollow-stemmed. Grows
near water. Often mistaken for wild carrot or parsley.
Ingestion causes ascending paralysis — the legs fail,
then the torso, then the lungs. The man dies conscious
and unable to breathe. Recognition: the stem has
purple-red blotches. Crush a leaf — it smells like
mice or musty grain, not like carrot.

**Yew.** Every part except the red berry flesh is toxic.
The seeds inside the berry are lethal if chewed. A
horse tethered under a yew tree and eating the fallen
leaves may be dead by morning. Men who carve yew bows
know not to put the wood shavings in the fire if
they are cooking over it — the smoke is irritating and
the residue on food is dangerous.

**Nightshade (Belladonna).** Sweet black berries that
look edible. A few berries can kill a child. More are
needed for an adult. Effects: dilated pupils, rapid
heartbeat, confusion, hallucinations, seizures, death.
The dilated-pupil effect is distinctive — a man
poisoned with nightshade has enormous black pupils even
in bright light.

**Water.** Standing water downstream of a battlefield
or a mass burial is contaminated. Water in which a dead
animal lies is contaminated. Water that smells or tastes
wrong is contaminated. The man who drinks it will know
in a day or two — cramps, flux, fever. Contaminated
water kills more men on campaign than weapons do.

**Rotten meat.** Meat that has spoiled beyond the point
of salvage produces toxins that survive cooking. The
smell is the warning. If the meat smells wrong, it is
wrong. "Cooking it longer" does not neutralize all
toxins. The Cook knows this. A hungry man who eats what
the Cook rejected is responsible for his own suffering.

## Medicinal Preparations

### The Three Forms

**Poultice.** Fresh or dried herbs, crushed, mixed with
a binder (water, honey, fat, clay), and applied
directly to a wound or swollen area. The herbs provide
their medicinal properties through skin contact. The
binder holds the material in place. A poultice is
covered with clean cloth and changed twice a day.

**Tincture.** Herbs steeped in alcohol (mead, spirits,
wine) for weeks. The alcohol extracts the active
compounds and preserves them. A tincture lasts months
in a sealed container. Administered by drops — a few
drops in water or directly on the tongue. Tinctures are
portable, concentrated, and the closest thing to
medicine the tenth century has.

**Salve.** Fat (lard, tallow, lanolin) melted with
crushed herbs, strained, and cooled to a semi-solid.
Applied to skin — wounds, rashes, burns, chapped
skin, cracked heels. A salve protects and delivers
herbs simultaneously. Dalla carries several salves in
small pots, each for a different purpose.

### What Works

**Honey.** Antibacterial. Applied directly to wounds, it
inhibits infection. This is not folk medicine — it is
chemistry. Honey's low water activity, acidity, and
hydrogen peroxide production create an environment
hostile to bacteria. Dalla uses it on every wound she
can.

**Yarrow.** Staunches bleeding. Applied as a crushed
poultice to a wound, it slows blood flow. Also taken
as a tea for fever.

**Willow bark.** Chewed or steeped as tea. Contains
compounds related to aspirin. Reduces fever, eases
pain, and reduces inflammation. Not as strong as
modern painkillers but measurably better than nothing.

**Garlic.** Antimicrobial. Eaten raw, applied crushed to
wounds, or steeped in oil. The active compound
(allicin) degrades with cooking — raw garlic is
medicinal, cooked garlic is food.

### What Does Not Work (But People Do Anyway)

**Amulets.** A rune carved on bone and worn around the
neck does not prevent illness. The man who believes it
does may feel better, which has measurable value but
no pharmaceutical mechanism.

**Bloodletting.** Cutting the patient to release "bad
blood." This weakens an already weakened person. It is
common practice in many cultures of the period. Dalla
does not practice it.

**Prayer alone.** To Eir, to Frigg, to any power — prayer
provides psychological comfort. Combined with actual
treatment, the comfort helps recovery. In place of
treatment, it is hope without substance.

## Ink and Writing Materials

### Gest's Domain

Gest keeps the band's records. He is one of the few
who write, and his tools are carried with the same
care as weapons.

**Iron gall ink.** The standard. Oak galls (the hard
round growths caused by wasp larvae on oak branches)
are crushed and soaked in water. Iron filings (or a
rusty nail) are added. Gum arabic (tree sap) is added
as a binder. The mixture reacts over days — the tannic
acid in the galls bonds with the iron to produce a
blue-black fluid that darkens with age and is nearly
permanent on parchment. This ink resists water once
dry, does not flake, and lasts centuries.

**Soot ink.** The field alternative. Lamp soot or
charcoal ground to a fine powder, mixed with water
and a binder (gum, egg white, or in desperation,
spit). Produces a black ink that writes well but
smears if wet and fades faster than iron gall.
Gest uses soot ink for daily notes and iron gall
for anything that must last.

**Charcoal.** The emergency writing tool. A stick of
charcoal writes on parchment, leather, wood, and
stone. It smears. It fades. It rubs off. But it
records a note, a count, a mark, in seconds and
without preparation. Gest's fingers are perpetually
black.

### Surfaces

**Parchment.** Prepared animal skin (sheep, goat, calf).
Expensive, durable, reusable (text can be scraped off
and the surface written on again — a palimpsest). Gest
carries a few sheets for important records.

**Wax tablets.** A wooden frame filled with a layer of
beeswax. Written on with a stylus (pointed bone or
metal). Erased by smoothing the wax with the flat end
of the stylus or by gentle warming. Reusable
indefinitely. Gest's daily working surface.

**Birch bark.** In the field, when parchment is not
available. Birch bark peeled in thin layers provides
a smooth writing surface. Charcoal or ink writes on
it. It curls, it tears, it is not permanent. But it
is free and everywhere.

## Light, Darkness, and Time

### Light Sources

| Source      | Duration   | Quality                               |
| ----------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Torch       | 15-45 min  | Bright, drips, visible at distance    |
| Tallow lamp | 2-4 hours  | Dim, smoky, stinks of rendered fat    |
| Oil lamp    | 4-8 hours  | Steadier, requires oil (fish or seal) |
| Rushlight   | 15-30 min  | Faint, cheap, a peeled rush in fat    |
| Candle      | 1-4 hours  | Steady, expensive, a settlement item  |
| Fire        | Continuous | Location-bound, attention-dependent   |

**Tallow lamps.** The most available portable light. A
shallow stone or clay dish with rendered animal fat and
a twisted fiber wick. The wick draws fat up by capillary
action and burns at the tip. The light is dim — enough
to see your hands and the face of the person across from
you, not enough to read by. The smell is rancid fat. In
camp, a tallow lamp beside Gest's ledger is how the
evening accounting gets done.

**Rushlights.** The cheapest light. A peeled rush (the
pithy core of a rush plant) soaked in waste fat and
clamped in a holder. Burns for fifteen to thirty minutes
with a guttering, unsteady flame. Peasant lighting.
Settlements use these. The band does not carry them.

### Telling Time

**No clocks.** Time is measured by sun position (quarter-
day system: dawn, midday, dusk, midnight), candle
consumption (a candle loses a marked line per hour —
settlement technique), and bodily rhythm. Experienced men
estimate time to within half an hour by sun angle.

**Watch system.** The night is divided into watches
(typically three or four). Each watch is timed by the
rotation of stars — a man on watch identifies a star near
the horizon at the start of his watch and marks its
position. When it has moved a fist's width at arm's
length (roughly 15 degrees = one hour), an hour has
passed. Two fist-widths in a three-watch system is close
enough. On overcast nights, watches are timed by gut
feeling and argued about in the morning.

## Weather Skills

### Reading Weather

**Wind shift.** A wind change from west to east in
northern latitudes usually means deteriorating weather
within hours. Backing wind (shifting counterclockwise)
means a weather system is approaching. Experienced men
feel the wind shift before they see the clouds change.

**Cloud reading.** High thin clouds (cirrus) moving fast
mean a front is approaching — weather changes in 12-24
hours. Low, grey, uniform overcast (stratus) means steady
light precipitation. Towering clouds building vertically
mean a storm within hours. Dark bases on tall clouds mean
heavy precipitation. Cloud reading is imprecise but better
than nothing.

**Atmospheric signs.** A ring around the moon (ice
crystals in high atmosphere) predicts precipitation within
24 hours. Unusually clear distant visibility often
precedes a storm (the air dries before the front arrives).
A sudden temperature drop after wind dies means a cold
front is arriving. Birds flying low indicate low pressure
and approaching bad weather. Spiders taking down their
webs indicates the same.

**Joint pain.** Old injuries and arthritic joints ache
before weather changes (barometric pressure sensitivity
is physiologically real). Snorri's bad knees are a weather
instrument. He does not know why they work. They work.

### Cold-Weather Survival

**Layering.** Air trapped between layers insulates. Three
thin layers are warmer than one thick layer. The inner
layer wicks sweat away from skin (linen). The middle layer
insulates (wool). The outer layer blocks wind and sheds
water (oiled wool, leather, or hide). Get the layers
wrong and you are wet, cold, and losing heat faster than
you can produce it.

**Extremities.** Fingers, toes, nose, and ears lose heat
first (blood retreats to the core in cold). Mittens are
warmer than gloves (fingers share heat in a mitten).
Wool-lined leather is standard. Toes are kept warm by
insulating from the ground (dried grass stuffed in boots)
and keeping boots dry. A man whose fingers are too numb
to grip a weapon has lost the fight before it starts.

**Hypothermia signs.** Shivering is the first stage (the
body generates heat through muscle contraction). When
shivering stops but the man is still cold, he is in
danger — the body has exhausted its heating capacity. He
becomes confused, clumsy, sleepy. He wants to stop and
rest. Letting him sit down kills him. The treatment is
warmth: fire, dry clothing, body heat from another
person, warm liquids. In the field, hypothermia is treated
aggressively or not at all. A cold man pulled from a
river and not warmed within minutes is dead regardless.

**Frostbite progression.** White patches on fingertips
or ears (blood has retreated, tissue is freezing). Then
numbness (the nerves have stopped working — the man does
not feel the damage happening). Then dark purple or black
discoloration (tissue death). Mild frostbite recovers —
the skin peels and is sensitive for weeks. Severe
frostbite requires amputation. Black fingers do not come
back. The amputation is done with a knife and a saw, and
it is done soon, because dead tissue rots on the living
body.

## March Discipline

### Pace

A band moves at the speed of its slowest element — usually
the mule, the wounded, or the terrain.

**Normal march.** Two and a half to three miles per hour
on flat ground, firm footing, good weather. This is a
walking pace — not forced, not dawdling. A man in
reasonable condition with a forty-pound load sustains
this indefinitely.

**Forced march.** Four miles per hour. This exhausts the
men and the mule within four to six hours. The mule
begins refusing to keep pace. The men with the heaviest
loads fall behind. Forced march is a command decision —
outrun a pursuit, reach a position before the enemy, make
a destination before nightfall. It costs a day's recovery
afterward.

**Terrain penalties.** Dense forest: halve the pace
(constant obstacle navigation, ducking, climbing over
deadfall). Uphill: halve again (legs and lungs work
double). Bog or marsh: a quarter of normal pace
(every step sinks, pulls, and exhausts). Snow above
the knee: a quarter pace or worse. Deep snow without
snowshoes: movement effectively stops.

**Distance per day.** Fifteen to twenty miles in good
conditions is a solid day's march. Twenty-five is
pushing. Thirty is exceptional and leaves the band
depleted. In mountain terrain or deep forest, eight to
twelve miles may be the maximum.

### March Order

**Column formation.** The standard travel formation.
Single file in narrow terrain. Two-abreast where the
path allows. The column has a structure:

- **Point.** One or two men ahead of the main body by
  fifty to a hundred yards. They read the terrain, find
  the route, and sight threats before the column walks
  into them. Point runs silently. They communicate by
  hand signal or by stopping (the column watches the
  point — when the point stops, the column stops).

- **Main body.** The bulk of the band. The mule and
  its handler travel in the center of the main body
  (protected position, where the column can close
  around it if attacked). The most experienced
  fighters are at the front and rear of the main body.
  New men and the least capable are in the center.

- **Rear guard.** One or two men trailing the main body
  by fifty to a hundred yards. They watch behind. In
  ambush country, the rear guard watches the high ground
  to the sides as well. Rear guard turns around every
  few minutes to check the back trail — walking forward
  while looking backward is how rear-guard men develop
  a distinctive gait.

- **Flankers.** In open terrain or ambush-likely country,
  one or two men walk parallel to the column on either
  side, fifty to a hundred yards out. They watch the
  flanks and give warning if hostiles approach from the
  side. In dense forest, flankers cannot keep pace with
  the column and are pulled in.

### Noise Discipline

Sound carries. A marching column makes noise — boots on
ground, equipment clinks and rattles, the mule's hooves,
men talking. In safe territory, noise does not matter.
In hostile territory, noise discipline tightens:

- Metal-on-metal contact is silenced: weapons are
  wrapped, buckles are padded, chainmail is covered
  with a cloth layer.
- Speech is forbidden. Communication is by hand signal.
- The mule is muzzled if it tends to bray.
- Pace slows (deliberate steps make less noise than
  hurried ones).
- Route selection favors soft ground (grass, moss, duff)
  over hard ground (rock, gravel, dry leaves).

Full noise discipline reduces the column's detection
range from half a mile to a hundred yards in forest.

### Rest Halts

**Every hour.** A ten-minute rest halt is called. Men
drop packs, drink water, adjust boots. The halt happens
in a defensible position if possible — not in the open,
not at the bottom of a defile. During the halt, point
and rear guard remain in position (they rotate at the
next halt).

**Noon.** A longer halt if conditions permit — thirty
minutes to an hour. Cold food is eaten (cooking takes
time and produces smoke — in hostile territory, no
fires). Boots are pulled off and feet examined. Blisters
are treated immediately (see medical section). A blister
left untreated becomes an open wound by evening and a
limping man by morning.

## Terrain Reading

### What the Ground Tells You

An experienced man reads terrain the way a literate man
reads a page — automatically, extracting meaning without
conscious effort.

**Ambush terrain.** Any place where an attacker can hide
close to the route and attack without warning. Dense
forest on both sides of a narrow path. High ground
overlooking a road. Stream crossings where the bank
provides cover. Defiles (narrow passages between rock
walls or steep hillsides) where the column is strung out
and cannot form a defense. In ambush terrain, the point
moves further ahead, flankers go out if possible, and
weapons come to hand.

**Escape routes.** When entering any area — a valley, a
village, a forest clearing — a man identifies how to
leave quickly. This is automatic for experienced
travelers and fighters. New men must be taught to think
this way. The question on arrival is not "how do we get
in" but "how do we get out."

**Water indicators.** Green vegetation in otherwise dry
terrain points to subsurface water. A line of trees
(particularly willows, alders, or cottonwoods) in
grassland marks a watercourse. Animal trails converge
on water sources — follow them downhill.

**Human presence.** Smoke (visible in daylight, smellable
at greater range). Stumps (trees cut, not fallen —
someone with an axe). Paths (worn ground means regular
passage). Dung (domestic animal dung means a farm or
camp nearby). Structures visible on a skyline. Dog
barking.

### Skyline Discipline

Never silhouette yourself against the sky. A man standing
on a ridgeline is visible for miles. A man ten yards
below the ridge is invisible. This applies to camps
(never on the crest), movement (cross ridges at the
lowest point and move through saddles), and observation
(look over the ridge while lying prone, with only the
head exposed — and that obscured by a rock or bush).

## Bog and Marsh Navigation

### Why Bogs Kill

A man walks onto what looks like solid meadow grass and
sinks to the thigh. The peat and water grip his legs.
His struggles force him deeper. If alone and up to the
waist in bog, he drowns — slowly, in daylight, within
sight of solid ground.

### How to Cross

**Testing.** A long staff (eight feet, cut on the spot)
is used to probe the ground ahead. Solid ground resists
the staff. Bog sinks it. Floating mat (a layer of
vegetation over liquid water) springs under the staff
and rebounds — this is the most dangerous because it
looks solid and supports a man's weight for one step
before giving way.

**Route finding.** Look for the game trails (animals know
the safe paths through bogs). Look for tussocks (raised
mounds of grass with root systems that support weight).
Step from tussock to tussock. Look for the color of the
water: dark brown stained water indicates stable peat
below. Green sphagnum moss floating in clear water
indicates deep bog. Bright green grass in a uniform flat
area often means a quaking bog — the worst kind.

**If trapped.** Do not thrash. Spread the body flat
(increase the surface area). Reach for solid vegetation
or tussocks. Pull horizontally, not vertically (pulling
straight up creates suction). Work each leg free with
a circular motion, breaking the suction seal. This is
slow, exhausting work. A companion throws a rope or
extends a pole.

**The mule.** Mules are better than horses in soft ground
(smaller hooves, more even weight distribution relative
to body size, more cautious by nature). But a mule in
deep bog is a crisis — the animal panics, thrashes, and
sinks faster. The mule's load is removed (if reachable),
the animal is calmed, and it is guided to solid ground
by leading from the front — never from behind (a mule
that feels trapped and sees a person behind it may try
to climb over them).

## Mountain and Ridge Travel

### The Pace Problem

Uphill movement is exhausting because the legs are
lifting the body's weight plus the pack weight with
every step. A grade of ten percent (rise of one foot in
ten feet of forward travel) reduces pace by a third. A
grade of thirty percent (steep hillside) reduces it by
half or more.

**The rest-step.** The mountaineer's technique: on each
uphill step, pause with the rear leg locked straight (the
skeleton supports the weight, not the muscles) for a
beat before transferring weight to the forward leg and
stepping again. This micro-rest extends endurance
enormously. A man using the rest-step climbs for hours.
A man powering up the slope on muscle alone stops every
fifteen minutes, panting.

**Traversing.** Walking diagonally across a slope rather
than straight up. Reduces the grade encountered (the path
is longer but less steep). Switchbacks: traversing in one
direction, turning, traversing in the other — a zigzag
path up the mountain. Every man who has traveled mountain
terrain knows switchbacks. Tired men try to shortcut
straight up and exhaust themselves.

### Hazards

**Rockfall.** Loose stone on a slope above is a threat.
Thermal cycling (freezing at night, thawing by day)
loosens rock. Wind loosens rock. A man walking above
loosens rock. The man below hears a clatter — the
warning is a shout: "ROCK!" and everyone presses against
the slope (presenting the smallest target). A head-sized
stone falling fifty feet hits with enough force to kill.
Helmets help. Looking up and moving quickly matters more.

**Scree slopes.** Loose small stones that slide under
the feet. Walking on scree is like walking on ball
bearings. Uphill: kick the toe into the scree to create
a step (two steps forward, one sliding back). Downhill:
lean back, dig the heels in, and ride the slide in
controlled half-steps. Running on scree is how ankles
break.

**Exposure.** Mountain ridgelines are exposed to weather
with no shelter. Wind increases with altitude. Temperature
drops three to five degrees per thousand feet of
elevation. A warm valley can have a ridge that is cold
enough to cause hypothermia — especially when the wind
is factored in (wind chill). Crossing high ground, the
band must be prepared for weather that is dramatically
worse than what they left in the valley.

**Lightning.** In mountain terrain during storms, the
highest point attracts lightning. Do not be the highest
point. On an exposed ridge during a storm: get off the
ridge. If impossble: crouch low (do not lie flat — ground
current from a nearby strike travels horizontally and
a prone body receives the full current across its length).
Crouch on the balls of the feet with feet together,
minimizing contact with the ground. Put metal objects
(weapons, armor, tools) twenty feet away.

## Snow and Ice Travel

### Snowshoes

When snow is deeper than mid-calf, walking without
snowshoes is a losing battle. The feet plunge through
the surface with each step, the energy cost triples,
and pace drops to a quarter of normal.

**Construction.** A frame of green wood (ash or birch),
steamed and bent into a teardrop or oval shape. Lashed
at the front where the tails cross. The interior is
webbed with rawhide or sinew strips, criss-crossing each
other to create a platform that distributes the wearer's
weight over a large surface area. The foot is bound to
the frame with leather ties — the toe is fixed, the heel
lifts free (so the foot can flex for walking, and excess
snow falls through the heel gap instead of accumulating
on the platform).

**Walking.** The stride widens to clear each snowshoe's
width. The gait is rolling — weight shifts from one foot
to the other with a slight waddle. In deep snow, the
trail-breaker works hardest (compacting the path for
those behind). The band rotates the lead position every
fifteen to twenty minutes.

### Ice Assessment

Not all ice is safe. The thickness, color, and sound
tell the story.

| Thickness | Can support        |
| --------- | ------------------ |
| 2 inches  | Nothing — stay off |
| 4 inches  | A man on foot      |
| 6 inches  | A group of men     |
| 8 inches  | A mule with load   |
| 12 inches | A heavy cart       |

**Color.** Clear blue or black ice is dense and strong.
White ice (snow-ice — formed when snow on top of ice is
compressed and frozen) is weaker — half the strength of
clear ice at the same thickness. Grey ice indicates melting
or flowing water beneath — do not trust it at any
thickness.

**Sound.** Walk slowly and listen. Solid ice gives a
deep resonant groan or no sound. Creaking and cracking
sounds mean the ice is flexing under weight — it may
hold, but retreat is wise. A sharp crack followed by
water appearing at the crack means leave immediately.

**Testing.** Strike the ice with an axe or a heavy staff.
Good ice resists or chips. Weak ice punches through. Test
every hundred yards when conditions are uncertain.

**If the ice breaks.** The man goes through. His clothing
soaks instantly, the weight pulling him down. The shock
of cold water causes involuntary gasping — if the head is
underwater when this happens, water is inhaled. The man
has one to two minutes of effective muscle function to
get himself out. Technique: kick to horizontal in the
water, get the chest and arms flat on the ice edge, and
kick/pull onto the surface. Roll away from the hole (do
not stand up near the break — the stressed ice may give
way again). Get to shore and get the wet clothing off
immediately — hypothermia follows within minutes in air
temperatures below freezing while wearing wet clothing.

### Skiing

Nordic in origin. A practical transportation method in
snow country.

**The skis.** Long (taller than the skier), narrow,
with a curved upturn at the front. The underside is
sometimes lined with animal fur (hair oriented toward
the rear) which grips the snow on uphill push-off
(the hair resists backward sliding) while allowing
forward glide on the downhill and flat. Without fur,
the skis are waxed with animal fat, pine tar, or a
mixture (providing similar directional grip).

**Technique.** The kick-and-glide: push off one foot
(the kick — snow compresses under the grip zone of the
ski), glide forward on the other. Poles (one or two,
though many Norse skiers used a single pole that doubled
as a balance staff and spear rest) plant ahead of the
body and push. Uphill: herringbone (skis angled outward
in a V, stepping uphill with the edges gripping) or
side-stepping. Downhill: crouch for balance, weight back,
one ski slightly ahead. Stopping: snowplow (tips together,
tails apart, pressing the inner edges into the snow).

## River Crossings

### With Improvised Raft

When no ford exists and the river must be crossed.

**The log raft.** Three to five logs of similar length
(eight to twelve feet) lashed side by side. The logs
must be buoyant (dry wood, not waterlogged — test by
pushing a log into the water; if it floats with a third
of its diameter above water, it carries weight). Cross-
pieces lashed on top bind the logs together. A load-
bearing raft for one man plus gear needs logs totaling
at least eighteen inches of combined diameter above water.

**Propulsion.** Poles (pushing off the bottom — works only
in water shallow enough to reach bottom, which limits
this to near-shore). Paddles (improvised: a flat board
lashed to a pole, or a split log carved flat on one
side). Rope-ferry: a rope stretched across the river
(this requires crossing once first to anchor the far
end), with the raft hauled across by pulling on the rope.

**Hazards.** A raft in current moves downstream while
crossing — the landing point on the far bank is
downstream of the launch point. The faster the current,
the further downstream. Plan for this. A raft that
misses the intended landing on a river with rapids
downstream has a serious problem.

### Swimming with Gear

Men could swim. Many cannot (swimming was not universal —
many inland people never learned). For those who can:

**Dry-bag method.** Gear is wrapped in an oiled or waxed
cloth, tied tight, and pushed ahead while swimming. The
air trapped in the bundle provides flotation. The swimmer
kicks and uses one arm while the other hand grips the
bundle.

**Inflated skin.** An animal skin (sheep or goat) is
sewn shut at all openings except the neck, inflated by
blowing, and the neck tied off. This bladder provides
flotation when gripped under one arm. Two inflated skins
support a man who cannot swim — he clings and kicks.

## Navigation and Wayfinding

### Without Compass

The magnetic compass did not reach Scandinavia until
after the Viking age (and its reliability was poor in
high latitudes anyway). Norse navigation was by
observation.

**Sun.** The sun rises roughly east and sets roughly west.
At noon in the Northern Hemisphere, the sun is due south.
A staff planted in the ground casts a shadow: the tip of
the shadow moves in an arc. The shortest shadow (noon)
points north. Connecting the shadow tips from morning and
afternoon gives an east-west line.

**Stars.** Polaris (the North Star) sits nearly above the
north pole of the sky. Finding it: locate the Big Dipper
(Ursa Major — seven bright stars in a ladle shape). The
two stars forming the outer edge of the ladle's cup
point toward Polaris. Polaris does not move — it stays
north while all other stars rotate around it. On a clear
night, north is found in seconds.

**Moss.** The popular claim that "moss grows on the north
side of trees" is unreliable. Moss grows on the shaded,
damp side — which is often north (less sun) but varies
with local conditions. In dense forest, moss grows
everywhere. Not a primary navigation aid.

**Wind.** Prevailing wind direction is known regionally.
A man who knows the prevailing wind of his region can
orient roughly by facing into the wind. This is fallback
navigation and works only when the wind is holding its
normal pattern.

**Terrain memory.** The primary method. A man traveling
through country he knows navigates by landmarks — that
mountain, this river, the pass shaped like a notch, the
stone shaped like a tooth. The entire Norse wayfinding
system is based on memorized terrain features passed
through oral tradition. "Three days upriver from the
river-fork where the banks are red clay, you reach the
falls. Half a day above the falls, the valley narrows..."

### Distance Estimation

**Counting paces.** A man's stride on flat ground is
roughly two and a half to three feet. One thousand
double-paces (counting every other footfall) equals
approximately one mile. Pace-counting is the standard
method for distance estimation on foot. Experienced
march-men count automatically and report distances: "The
village is three thousand paces from the ford."

**Visual estimation.** In clear conditions, objects at
known sizes provide range cues. A man-sized figure:
visible as a shape at 300 yards, visible as a person
at 200 yards, face features visible at 50 yards. A
cottage: visible as a shape at 1-2 miles, details
visible at half a mile. These estimates degrade with
rain, fog, and terrain.

**Time-distance.** At three miles per hour for six hours
of march (accounting for terrain and rest halts), a band
covers roughly eighteen miles. Track the time by sun
position and you know the approximate distance covered.

## Boat Handling

### Rowing

Every man in coastal Scandinavia rows. It is like
walking — learned young and never forgotten.

**The stroke.** Catch (blade enters the water, arms
extended), drive (pull the blade through the water using
the back muscles — the arms transmit force but the power
comes from the torso and legs), finish (blade exits the
water, hands come to the chest), recovery (arms extend
forward for the next catch). The rhythm is set by the
lead rower or by a drum/chant.

**Coordination.** A ship with twenty oars works only when
all twenty pull in unison. One out-of-rhythm oar tangles
with its neighbors, breaks the timing, and can injure the
rowers. Rowing practice is military drill.

**Endurance.** Sustained rowing at cruise pace: two to
three hours before fatigue degrades the stroke. At combat
pace (ramming, escape, pursuit): twenty minutes maximum
of all-out effort.

### Basic Sail Handling

The square sail — the standard Norse rig.

**Setting the sail.** The sail is stored furled (rolled
up) on the yard (the horizontal pole at the top of the
mast). To set: haul the yard to the top of the mast, then
release the brailing lines (ropes that hold the sail
furled to the yard). The sail drops and catches the wind.
Sheets (ropes attached to the lower corners of the sail)
control the angle — pulled tight to one side for sailing
across the wind, let out for sailing downwind.

**Reefing.** When the wind is too strong for full sail,
the lower portion of the sail is gathered up and tied:
the sail area is reduced without taking it down entirely.
In severe weather, the sail comes down completely and the
crew rows or drifts under bare mast.

**Tacking.** A square sail cannot sail efficiently into
the wind (within roughly 60 degrees of the wind
direction). To travel upwind, the ship sails at an angle
to the wind, then turns and sails at the opposite angle —
a zigzag course that makes forward progress against the
wind but at a fraction of the speed. Each turn (tack)
requires shifting the sheet and braces to the opposite
side as the wind crosses the bow. This is synchronized
crew work — a mistimed tack in heavy weather is
dangerous.

## Shield Wall

### Forming the Wall

A shield wall is not a decision. It is the default.
When men stand shoulder to shoulder with shields
overlapping, they are harder to kill than when they
stand alone. Every man in the band knows this from
the first day he holds a shield.

**The overlap.** Each man holds his shield on his left
arm. The right edge of his shield overlaps the left
edge of the man to his right. This means the man on
the far left of the line is the most exposed — his
left side has no neighbor's shield covering it. This
is where the best fighter stands, or the man with the
biggest shield.

**Stance.** Left foot forward, right foot back, weight
low. The shield is not held out — it is braced against
the left shoulder, the rim resting on the ground or
just above it. The boss takes impacts. The arm takes
the weight. After twenty minutes the arm shakes. After
an hour it is agony.

**Depth.** A single rank dies. Two ranks hold. Three
ranks push. The second rank braces the first — shield
pressed into the back of the man ahead, feet dug in,
pushing forward. The third rank replaces casualties and
adds mass. More than three ranks is wasted weight
unless the ground is sloped or the intent is to
steamroll through by mass alone.

### Holding the Wall

**The push.** When two walls meet, the fight becomes a
shoving match. Weight wins. The heavier, deeper, better-
braced line drives the lighter one back. Men in the
front rank stab over their shield rims — short jabbing
motions, targeting the face, throat, armpits, groin.
There is no room to swing. A man who raises his arm for
a full swing opens his side and dies.

**Rotation.** The front rank tires. Every two to five
minutes (it feels like an hour), fresh men push forward
from the second rank while exhausted men drop back. This
is done without orders — the man behind taps the man
ahead on the shoulder, steps into his place, and the
tired man slides sideways and back. A band that cannot
rotate smoothly loses to one that can, regardless of
individual skill.

**The man who falls.** When a man in the front rank goes
down — stabbed, tripped, shield split — the wall has a
gap. The man behind steps forward immediately. If he
hesitates, the enemy pours through the gap and the wall
is done. The fallen man is stepped over. If he is lucky,
hands from behind drag him back. If he is not lucky, he
is trampled by both sides.

### When the Wall Breaks

A shield wall does not break everywhere at once. It
breaks at a point — one man flinches, one section gives
ground, one gap opens and is not filled. The break
spreads fast. Once the enemy is through the wall and
behind it, the formation is useless and men fight as
individuals or die in a crush.

**The rout.** When a wall breaks, the losing side either
rallies behind a second line (if they have one) or runs.
Men who run with shields on their backs survive. Men who
drop shields and sprint sometimes survive. Men who stand
alone in a broken line die. The decision to run is not
cowardice — it is arithmetic. A man alone against three
is dead. A man who runs may fight tomorrow.

## Formation Fighting

### The Wedge (Svinfylking)

The swine-array. A triangular formation with the
strongest fighters at the point. Purpose: to punch
through a shield wall at a single point, splitting it
open so the men behind can exploit the gap.

**Shape.** One man at the point, two behind him, three
behind them, widening to the base. The point man is
armored, expendable, or both — he absorbs the first
impacts. The flanks of the wedge crash into the
separated halves of the enemy wall and roll them back.

**When it works.** Against a thin line or an exhausted
wall. The momentum of the charge and the concentrated
mass at the point splits the formation before it can
brace. The men inside the wedge are partially shielded
by the men at the edges.

**When it fails.** Against a deep wall that absorbs the
impact and wraps around the wedge's flanks. The wedge
becomes a surrounded pocket. Against archers who can
see the clear triangular target and concentrate fire
on the point.

### The Line

A single extended line. No tricks. The purpose is to
present the maximum number of weapons along the greatest
frontage, preventing the enemy from flanking. Used when
defending a position with anchored flanks (river on one
side, cliff on the other) or when numbers are roughly
equal and the fight will be decided by staying power.

### The Column

Men in file, narrow front. For moving through tight
terrain — forest paths, mountain passes, village
streets. Not a fighting formation. The danger is being
caught in column by an enemy in line — only the front
few can fight while the rest stand useless. Transition
from column to line is the critical drill.

### Refusing a Flank

When one end of the line is threatened — enemy appearing
on the side, or the line is shorter than the enemy's —
the end of the line bends back at an angle, forming an
L shape. The men at the bend fight facing two directions.
This is ugly, costly, and better than being rolled up
from the side.

## Ambush

### Setting One

**Site selection.** The ambush site needs three things:
concealment for the ambushers, a kill zone the enemy
must pass through, and an escape route for the
ambushers if things go wrong. A narrow trail between
dense forest. A ford where men are mid-stream. A
road that curves around a blind hillside.

**The kill zone.** The section of trail or road where
the ambushers' weapons can reach. Ideally less than
thirty paces long — concentrated fire, concentrated
chaos. The enemy is caught in a space where they
cannot spread out, cannot form up, cannot see where
the attack is coming from.

**The blocking force.** A small group positioned past
the kill zone to prevent the enemy from running
through the ambush and escaping forward. They do not
need to fight the whole enemy column — just hold for
thirty seconds while the main force does its work.

**The trigger.** The ambush is sprung when the maximum
number of enemy are inside the kill zone. Too early
and only the vanguard is caught. Too late and the
rear guard is not engaged. The trigger is usually the
leader's signal — a horn blast, a thrown spear, a
shout. Once triggered, everyone attacks simultaneously.
Hesitation destroys an ambush.

**Patience.** The hardest part of an ambush is waiting.
Men lie in concealment for hours, cold, stiff, needing
to piss. When the enemy appears, every instinct says
attack now. The leader who springs early catches three
men instead of thirty. An ambush is an exercise in
collective discipline, not individual courage.

### Surviving One

**Immediate action.** When an ambush is sprung on you,
the wrong response is to freeze. The wrong response is
to try to form a shield wall in the kill zone. The
correct response depends on position:

- **If in the kill zone:** Charge the ambush line.
  Directly into the attackers. This is counterintuitive
  and the only option that works. Staying in the kill
  zone means death. Retreating means death (the blocking
  force). Closing with the ambushers at least gives you
  a fight. The ambushers expect you to run — not to come
  crashing through the brush toward them.
- **If behind the kill zone:** Do not march forward into
  it. Pull back, find cover, form up. Support the men in
  the kill zone with missile weapons if possible. Flank
  the ambush position if the terrain allows.
- **If ahead of the kill zone:** You are past the
  blocking force or not yet engaged. Keep moving. Rally
  at a defensible point ahead. Send runners back only
  when you have a position of strength.

**Breaking contact.** If the ambush is too strong or too
well-sited, the surviving elements disengage — smoke,
noise, a sudden rush in an unexpected direction — and
scatter to a pre-designated rally point. A band that
has discussed rally points before the march survives
ambushes. A band that has not discussed rally points
becomes fugitives.

## Raiding

### The Approach

A raid is not a battle. A raid is theft with the option
of violence. The approach is quiet — by night, by fog,
by a route that avoids roads and settlements. The raiding
party is small. Ten men can raid. Fifty can sack. Five
hundred is an army, and armies are slow.

**Timing.** Before dawn is ideal. The defenders are asleep,
fires have burned low, dogs are their only alarm. Sentries
at the end of their watch are the most tired, the most
inattentive. The attackers have been awake and moving for
hours, blood up, eyes adjusted.

### The Breach

**Gates.** A gate is barred from inside. If there is no
gate, there is a gap in the fence or wall. The first men
through the gap secure it for those behind. If the gate
must be broken, an axe and two minutes of noise — which
means surprise is lost and speed must compensate.

**Fire.** Throwing fire onto thatch roofs drives defenders
out of buildings and into the open where they are
disorganized. Fire is the raider's best friend and worst
enemy — it spreads to the stores you came to take.
Controlled fire (one building, the one blocking your
route) is useful. Uncontrolled fire means you leave
with nothing and the smoke signals your position for
miles.

### Extraction

Take what you came for and leave. The mistake is staying
too long — looting one more building, chasing one more
fleeing defender, breaking into the locked strongroom.
Every minute in the target increases the chance of
organized resistance, of reinforcements arriving, of
your men scattering into individual looters who cannot
be rallied.

**Priority of looting.** Weapons and armor first (high
value, immediately useful). Silver and coin second
(compact, universally traded). Animals third (can carry
other loot, can be eaten). Grain and supplies fourth
(heavy, slow, but keeps the band alive). Luxuries last
(cloth, spices, goods that require a market to sell).

## Field Fortifications

### The Ditch and Bank

The most basic fortification. A trench dug in a circle
or arc, earth thrown up on the inner side to form a bank.
A man standing on the bank is three feet higher than a
man standing in the ditch — equivalent to fighting from
horseback. The ditch is three feet deep, four wide. The
bank is two feet high. Total height difference: five feet.
This is built in two hours by twenty men with entrenching
tools. In an emergency, axes and shields used as scoops.

### Staked Ground

Sharpened stakes driven into the ground at an angle,
points facing the enemy, about knee to waist height.
Purpose: to slow a charge and break a shield wall's
momentum before it reaches your position. A running man
impales himself. A cautious man stops to pick his way
through — and while he is picking, he is being shot.
Stakes work once. A determined enemy cuts them down, pulls
them up, or pushes through at cost. But "at cost" is the
point. Every casualty from stakes is one fewer in the
assault.

### The Wagon Laager

Circle the supply wagons into a ring. Fill the gaps
between wagons with whatever is available — barrels,
packs, timber, dead horses. Men fight from behind and
between the wagons. This is an emergency measure — it
works against a rush but not against a siege. The
advantage is speed: a wagon laager can be formed in
minutes. The disadvantage is immobility: you are
committed to that spot until the enemy leaves or you
break out.

### Hasty Palisade

Sharpened logs driven vertically into the ground, lashed
together. Chest height is sufficient — head height is
better. A full palisade around a camp requires hundreds
of logs and half a day's labor. A partial palisade
protecting one exposed side is more realistic for a
small band. Combined with the ditch-and-bank on other
sides, it provides genuine protection against anything
short of a determined assault with ladders.

### Abatis

Felled trees dragged into position with branches facing
the enemy. The tangle of branches creates an obstacle
that is extremely difficult to cross quickly. An abatis
line fifty yards in front of a position forces attackers
to climb, cut, or detour — all while under fire. Combined
with stakes in the gaps, it is as effective as a low wall.
The material cost is trees and labor, both available in
forested terrain.

## Siege Craft

### Attacking a Fortified Position

**Ladders.** The simplest and cheapest siege tool. Two
long poles with crosspieces lashed at intervals. Carried
by two men, set against the wall, climbed by one man at
a time. The man at the top of the ladder is the most
exposed human being on the battlefield. Defenders push
ladders sideways (they topple), pour boiling water or
heated sand, or simply stab the man as his head appears
above the parapet. Ladders work only through volume —
enough ladders on enough walls that the defender cannot
cover them all.

**Rams.** A heavy log carried by six to twenty men,
swung against a gate or a weak section of wall.
Protected by a roof of shields or a wheeled shed (the
"turtle"). The rhythm matters — the log must swing
together. An uncoordinated ram bounces off. A coordinated
ram will break any wooden gate in minutes. Stone walls
require sustained effort at a weak point — a corner, a
joint, a section where the mortar has crumbled.

**Fire.** Against wooden walls and gates, fire is faster
than rams. Bundles of brush and straw piled against the
base and lit. Oil or fat poured over the gate first.
The defenders pour water. The question is whose supply
runs out first. Against stone walls, fire is useless
except against the gate.

**Undermining.** Digging a tunnel under the wall,
supporting it with wooden props, then setting the props
on fire. When the props burn through, the tunnel
collapses and the wall above it falls. This takes days
to weeks, requires men who know digging (miners, not
soldiers), and can be countered by the defenders
digging counter-mines or listening for the sound of
digging underground (a bowl of water on the ground
ripples from the vibration).

**Starving out.** The slowest and most certain method.
Surround the fortification, cut supply lines, wait.
The question is: who is more patient, and who has
supplies? A well-stocked fort can hold for months. An
under-supplied siege camp starves faster than the
defenders. Most sieges are decided by politics, disease,
or treachery before starvation.

### Defending a Fortified Position

**Economy of force.** A defended wall multiplies a man's
effectiveness by five or ten. One man behind a parapet
can hold off five men on a ladder. This is why
fortifications exist — they are a machine for converting
a small number of defenders into an adequate number.

**Reserves.** The wall is the first line. The reserve is
the second — a group of men in the center of the
enclosure, ready to rush to whichever section is
threatened. If the reserve is committed too early to one
section, the enemy attacks another. If the reserve is
committed too late, the wall is already breached.

**Sorties.** A sudden attack out of the gate against the
besiegers. Purpose: to destroy siege equipment, to
disrupt preparations, to keep the enemy off-balance.
Timing is critical — the sortie force must be strong
enough to achieve its objective and fast enough to
withdraw before the full besieging force can respond.

### Terms and Parley

A siege that has not yet begun is an opportunity for
negotiation. Common terms: the garrison surrenders and
is allowed to leave with personal weapons. Or: the
garrison holds until a relief force arrives by a
specified date, and if no relief comes, surrenders.
Terms offered before an assault are generous. Terms
offered after a wall is breached are not offered.

## Pursuit and Retreat

### Pursuing a Beaten Enemy

**The decision to pursue.** When the enemy breaks, the
instinct is to chase. The risk is that the chase
scatters your formation, strings your men out along a
road, and leads them into a counterattack or an ambush
by the enemy's rearguard. Disciplined pursuit means:
one designated group pursues while the rest hold
position, reorganize, and secure the ground.

**Speed.** A retreating enemy drops equipment — shields,
packs, weapons. They move faster than the pursuers
until exhaustion catches them. Mounted pursuit catches
foot soldiers. Foot pursuit catches only the wounded
and the slowest. The return on pursuit is prisoners,
loot, and preventing the enemy from reforming.

**When to stop.** Pursuit into unknown terrain, into
forest, into the dark — all invitations to ambush. The
leader who calls off pursuit at the right moment saves
more men than the one who chases to the last fugitive.

### Retreating in Good Order

**The rearguard.** When the band must withdraw — the
fight is lost, the position is untenable, the orders
say fall back — the rearguard holds while the main body
moves. The rearguard is composed of the steadiest men.
Their job is to die slowly — to trade casualties for
time. When the main body has reached the next position,
the rearguard breaks contact and sprints to join them.
Then a new rearguard is designated and the process
repeats.

**Delay.** A retreating force makes the pursuit expensive.
Choke points are held briefly. Bridges are dropped.
Trails are blocked with felled trees. Caltrops (iron
stars that always land point-up, designed to lame
horses and cripple bare feet — rare but devastating)
are scattered on the road. Each obstacle buys ten
minutes. Ten minutes multiplied by ten obstacles is
the difference between annihilation and survival.

**Rally points.** Predetermined locations where scattered
men reassemble after a broken retreat. A distinctive
terrain feature known to everyone — the stone bridge,
the hilltop church, the ford with the dead oak. Without
rally points, a rout becomes permanent. With them, a
shattered band can reform in hours.

## After the Fight

### The Battlefield

**The wounded.** Yours first. A man down and screaming is
retrieved, carried to where Dalla can work. A man down
and silent is checked — if breathing, carried. If dead,
left for now. The enemy wounded are not a priority. If
they are in the way, they are moved. If they are
suffering and beyond help, mercy killing is an option
that no one discusses aloud and everyone understands.

**The dead.** Stripped of weapons, armor, and valuables.
This is not disrespect — it is logistics. A dead man's
sword keeps a living man alive. Identification of your
own dead: any personal item that proves who they were.
If there is time, burial or burning. If there is not
time, they are left. A man whose body was left on a
battlefield is mourned differently but not less.

**Looting protocol.** Who gets what is decided before the
fight, not after. Common systems: everything goes into a
common pool, divided by the leader. Or: each man keeps
what he takes, but weapons and armor go to whoever needs
them most. The system does not matter as long as it is
understood. Most fights within a mercenary band happen
over loot.

### Prisoners

**Ransom.** A prisoner worth money is kept alive, fed
enough to survive, and guarded. His value depends on his
family, his lord, and their willingness to pay. A soldier
is worth little. A lord is worth much. A relative of a
king is worth a fortune. Negotiations can take months.
The prisoner is currency that must be maintained.

**Interrogation.** A prisoner who knows the enemy's
numbers, position, and intentions is more valuable than
his ransom. Information is extracted by conversation
first — a man relieved to be alive talks freely. By
threat second. By force third. The reliability of
information obtained by force is low — a man in pain
says whatever stops the pain.

**Release.** A prisoner with no ransom value and no
information is a mouth to feed and a guard to assign.
He is released (unarmed, often stripped of boots to
slow pursuit), sold, or killed. The decision is
practical, not moral.

## Camp Defense Under Threat

### Standing To

When attack is possible — near enemy territory, after
contact with scouts, during retreat — the camp goes to
a heightened state. Every man knows his position. Weapons
are not stowed — they are beside the sleeping man. Armor
is not removed. The fire is banked low (enough for the
watch to see, dim enough not to silhouette the camp).
Half the men sleep while half watch. The sleeping half
can be on their feet with weapons in thirty seconds.

**The alarm.** A horn blast, a shout, a specific word.
The camp's response is drilled: each man goes to his
assigned position on the perimeter. This is not
improvised. The positions are assigned when the camp is
set. The man by the northern tree goes north. The man
by the supply pile goes to the gap in the earthwork.
Confusion in the dark kills more men than enemies do.

### Fire Discipline

In a night defense, fire is the enemy. A lit camp is
visible for miles. Fire silhouettes defenders and blinds
them to the dark beyond. The choice: fire for warmth and
cooking (peacetime camp), or darkness for survival
(operational camp). When under threat, fires are
extinguished or reduced to barely glowing coals covered
with earth. Men eat cold food. They curse the cold. They
survive.

### Fighting Withdrawal

When the camp is being overrun — the perimeter is
breached, the enemy is inside — the withdrawal follows a
simple plan: consolidate around the supply pile, grab what
you can carry, and move to the pre-designated withdrawal
route. The rearguard fights while the non-combatants and
wounded move first. The last men out set fire to anything
they cannot carry. Leave nothing the enemy can use.

## Battlefield Medicine — Field Triage

### The First Minutes

Not all wounded are equal. In the chaos after a fight,
Dalla (or whoever serves as healer) makes immediate
assessments:

**Walk and talk.** If the man can stand up and tell you
where he is hurt, he waits. He is alive and functional
enough to survive the next hour without help. Bandage
him roughly if he is bleeding and move to the next.

**Breathing but not talking.** Unconscious, stunned, or
in shock. Check for the kill wound — a stab to the gut,
a crushed chest, a head wound with brains showing.
If the wound is survivable, stop the bleeding and keep
him warm. If the wound is not survivable, make him
comfortable and move on.

**Not breathing.** Dead or nearly dead. There is no
battlefield resuscitation in the tenth century. Roll
him onto his back, check once for breathing. If
nothing, leave him. There are live men who need you.

### Mass Casualties

When the wounded outnumber the band's ability to treat
them, triage is brutal and practical:

1. **Treat men who can fight again soonest.** A man with
   a cut arm, bandaged and given a shield, fights in an
   hour. He is treated first.
2. **Treat men who will survive with help.** A man with
   a broken leg will live if the bone is set but cannot
   fight. He is treated second.
3. **Comfort men who will die regardless.** Water. A cloak.
   Quiet words. The mercy knife if they ask.

This is not taught. It is learned by doing it wrong
once and carrying the memory.

## Hypothermia

### What It Is

The body loses heat faster than it produces heat. Core
temperature drops. The brain slows. The heart stutters.
This is the primary killer in the Rimevegr — not blades,
not starvation, not disease. Cold.

### The Stages

**Shivering (mild — core 35-33°C).** The body's first
defense. Violent, uncontrollable shivering that burns
energy fast. The man is alert but clumsy. Fingers
fumble with buckles. Speech is clear but hands cannot
tie knots. This stage is recoverable with warmth, dry
clothing, and hot liquids. This is the stage where
action must be taken.

**Confusion (moderate — core 33-30°C).** Shivering
becomes intermittent, then stops. This is not
improvement — it is the body failing to generate heat.
The man stumbles. He makes bad decisions. He insists
he is fine. He may become combative when helped. He
cannot be trusted with navigation—he will walk in
circles. His speech slurs. He is slow to respond to
questions.

**Lethargy (severe — core 30-28°C).** The man stops
complaining. He wants to sit down. He wants to sleep.
If allowed to sit, he will not stand again without
help. Skin is blue-gray. Pulse is weak and slow.
Breathing is shallow. He may begin undressing —
paradoxical undressing, where the dying body's
vasodilation creates a sensation of burning heat.
A man stripping his clothes in a blizzard is minutes
from death.

**Death (core below 28°C).** Cardiac arrest. The heart,
cooled past function, stops. In extreme cold, death
is sometimes difficult to confirm — a deeply hypothermic
body can appear dead (no detectable pulse, no visible
breathing) and occasionally revive with rewarming.
The tenth-century rule: if the body is stiff and cold
to the core, he is dead. If the body is cold but still
pliable, try to warm him.

### Field Treatment

**Get him out of the wind.** Wind is the killer's
accelerant. A man in still air at freezing loses heat
slowly. A man in wind at freezing loses heat fast
enough to die in hours. Any shelter — a tent, a hollow,
a pile of branches — reduces wind and buys time.

**Get him dry.** Wet clothing conducts heat away from
the body twenty-five times faster than dry clothing.
Strip the wet clothing. Replace with dry if available.
If no dry clothing is available, wring the wet clothing
as dry as possible and insulate with whatever is at
hand — dry leaves, moss, grass, spare cloaks.

**Warm the core, not the extremities.** Warming cold
hands and feet first drives cold blood from the
extremities back to the heart, dropping core
temperature further. This kills. Warm the chest, the
belly, the armpits, the groin. Hot stones wrapped in
cloth. Heated water skins. Another man's body heat
against the torso. The extremities warm last, from
the inside out.

**Hot liquids.** Warm (not boiling) water, broth, or
tea. No alcohol. Alcohol dilates blood vessels in the
skin, creating a feeling of warmth while accelerating
heat loss from the core. A man given mead to warm him
will feel better and die faster.

**What not to do.** Do not rub the extremities. Frozen
tissue is damaged tissue — rubbing shreds it. Do not
put a severely hypothermic man near a roaring fire —
the sudden surface warming can trigger cardiac arrest.
Do not let him walk if he cannot think clearly — he
will fall and not get up. Carry him.

## Frostbite

### What Frostbite Is

Tissue freezes. Ice crystals form in the flesh,
destroying cells. Extremities go first — fingers, toes,
ears, nose, the exposed cheek facing the wind. The body,
protecting its core, constricts blood flow to the
extremities. The extremities, deprived of warm blood,
freeze.

### Recognition

**Frostnip (reversible).** Skin is pale, cold, and
numb. Tingling or stinging. Pressing the skin leaves
a white mark that does not immediately fill with color.
Rewarming produces pain, redness, and full recovery.
Every man in the Rimevegr knows frostnip. It is a
daily annoyance, not an injury.

**Superficial frostbite.** Skin is white and waxy. It
feels hard on the surface but soft underneath when
pressed. The man cannot feel the affected area.
Rewarming produces blisters filled with clear fluid.
Recovery takes days to weeks. Skin peels. Sensation
may or may not return fully.

**Deep frostbite.** Skin is white, blue-gray, or mottled.
Hard all the way through — like pressing frozen meat.
No sensation. Joints do not bend. Rewarming produces
blood-filled blisters and blackened tissue. The
blackened tissue is dead. Fingers, toes, and noses
lost to deep frostbite do not grow back. Amputation
is eventual. Infection is likely.

### Frostbite Field Treatment

**Do not rewarm if refreezing is possible.** A frostbitten
foot, thawed and then refrozen, suffers far worse damage
than a foot that stays frozen until proper shelter is
reached. If the man must walk to safety, leave the foot
frozen. It is numb — he can walk on it. Once thawed, he
cannot.

**Rewarming.** In warm water (body temperature — not
hot, never hot). The water is painfully warm to a
healthy hand. The frozen tissue is submerged. Pain
during rewarming is severe — the man will need to be
held or restrained. Rewarming takes thirty to sixty
minutes. Do not rub. Do not apply direct heat. Do not
break blisters.

**After rewarming.** Keep the tissue clean and protected.
Wrap loosely. Elevate if possible. The tissue will swell,
blister, and look horrifying. Some of it will blacken and
die. What survives is determined in the first forty-eight
hours, but the full extent of damage is not clear for
weeks.

### Prevention

**Buddy checks.** Every man checks the face of the man
next to him. A white patch on the nose or cheek that
the man himself cannot feel. The check takes two
seconds and saves noses. This is enforced as routine
on every cold march.

**Keep moving.** Wiggle toes inside boots. Clench and
release fists. Swing arms. Stamp feet at every halt.
Movement generates heat in the extremities and
maintains blood flow.

**Dry socks.** The single most important piece of
equipment in winter. Wet socks inside boots create
the perfect conditions for frostbite. Spare socks are
carried inside the tunic, against the body, where
body heat keeps them dry and warm. Socks are changed
at every extended halt. The wet pair goes against the
body to dry.

## Cold-Water Immersion

### The Timeline

**Cold shock (0-3 minutes).** The instant a man hits
cold water, he gasps. This is involuntary and cannot
be suppressed. If his head is underwater when the gasp
happens, he inhales water. The gasp is followed by
hyperventilation — rapid, uncontrollable breathing
that can last two to three minutes. During this phase,
a man cannot hold his breath, cannot swim effectively,
and drowns easily. The priority is keeping the head
above water and gripping something — wreckage, ice
edge, a thrown rope.

**Swim failure (3-30 minutes).** Cold muscles lose
function. Arms and legs become slow, weak, uncoordinated.
A strong swimmer who can cover a mile in calm water may
be unable to swim fifty paces in cold water. Fingers
cannot grip. Legs cannot kick effectively. The man
sinks if he stops moving, but moving becomes impossible.
In water near freezing, useful swimming ends in ten to
fifteen minutes.

**Hypothermia (30+ minutes).** If the man has not
drowned or been rescued, hypothermia sets in. Core
temperature drops. The stages are the same as on land
but compressed in time — water conducts heat away from
the body twenty-five times faster than air.

### Rescue

**Speed.** A man in cold water has minutes, not hours.
Get him out by any means — rope, pole, plank, reaching
hand, cutting a path through ice to shallower water
where he can stand.

**Horizontal extraction.** A deeply cooled man pulled
vertical out of water can die from cardiac arrest as
cold blood from the legs rushes to the heart. Pull him
out horizontal. Lay him flat. Do not stand him up until
he has warmed.

**After extraction.** Same protocol as hypothermia
treatment: shelter, dry clothing, core warming, hot
liquids, no alcohol. A man pulled from cold water who
seems fine may collapse minutes later as cold blood
from the extremities reaches the core. Watch him.

## Winter March Discipline

### Caloric Needs

A man marching in summer needs roughly three thousand
calories per day. The same man marching in deep cold
needs five thousand or more. The deficit is not
optional — the body burns fuel to maintain core
temperature, and if the fuel is not provided, the body
burns its own muscle. A band on short rations in winter
weakens faster than the same band on short rations in
summer. The arithmetic of supply is doubled.

**Fat.** In cold conditions, fat is the most valuable
food. It contains more than twice the calories per
weight of lean meat or grain. Tallow, butter, lard,
bone marrow, fatty meat — these are winter survival
foods. A lump of tallow the size of a fist provides
a day's caloric supplement. It tastes like a candle.
Nobody complains.

### Pace Adjustment

**Sweat kills.** The pace in cold conditions is set to
prevent sweating. Sweat soaks inner clothing. Wet inner
clothing against skin in freezing conditions leads to
rapid heat loss when the man stops moving. The march
pace is slower than summer — steady, sustainable, never
rushing. When a man begins to sweat, the pace is
reduced or a layer is removed.

**The ventilation principle.** Before marching, loosen
or remove one layer. The cold will be uncomfortable for
the first few minutes. Within ten minutes, exertion
generates enough heat to compensate. When the march
halts, the layer goes back on immediately. The man who
marches warm arrives at halt soaking. The man who
marches cool arrives dry.

**Rest halts.** Every hour, ten minutes. At the halt:
drink water (dehydration in cold is as deadly as in
heat — cold air is dry, breathing costs moisture,
thirst is suppressed but the need is real). Add the
removed layer. Check extremities. Check the man
next to you.

### Buddy System

**Nobody walks alone.** In cold conditions, a man who
falls behind and is not noticed dies. A man who
develops confusion from early hypothermia and wanders
off trail dies. A man whose boot is soaked and does not
change socks loses toes. The buddy system: every man
is paired. Each pair watches the other for signs of
cold injury — the white patch on the face, the stumble,
the slurred speech, the man who stops caring. If your
buddy says you need to stop and warm up, you stop and
warm up. Argument is not permitted.

## Sleeping in Extreme Cold

### The Danger

A man who falls asleep cold may not wake up. Core
temperature drops during sleep — the body's metabolic
rate decreases, heat production falls, shivering may
stop. A man who is borderline hypothermic while awake
can cross into severe hypothermia while sleeping. The
man who stops shivering in his sleep has stopped
generating heat.

### Techniques

**Body heat sharing.** Two or three men share a sleeping
arrangement. Back to back is good — chest to back is
better. The combined heat output of two men inside a
shared bedroll or under shared cloaks is significantly
more than either alone. There is no modesty in survival.

**Hot stones.** Stones heated in the fire and wrapped in
cloth. Placed at the feet, against the belly, in the
armpits. They hold heat for several hours. Too hot and
they burn — a man numb from cold does not feel the burn
until morning. The cloth wrapping matters.

**Insulation from the ground.** The ground steals more
heat than the air. A man sleeping on bare earth or snow
loses heat downward faster than upward. Boughs, bracken,
grass, straw, spare cloaks — anything between the body
and the ground. Six inches of compressed boughs under
the sleeper is more effective than an extra blanket
over him.

**Snow as insulation.** Counterintuitive but tested by
a thousand winters. A trench dug in deep snow, roofed
with branches and more snow, maintains a temperature
near freezing regardless of the air temperature above.
Freezing is not warm. But freezing is survivable. Minus
thirty is not. The snow cave is the emergency shelter
of the Rimevegr.

### Sleeping Shifts

In extreme cold, no one sleeps more than four hours at
a time. The fire must be maintained — its warmth is the
margin between discomfort and danger. The man on watch
feeds the fire, checks the sleepers (a nudge — if they
respond, they are alive and warm enough; if they do
not respond, wake them fully and assess). A sleeper
who is silent and still is checked more carefully than
a sleeper who is snoring and kicking.

## Snow Blindness

### Cause

Sunlight reflecting off snow. The UV radiation burns
the cornea. This happens on bright days but also on
overcast days when the light is diffused and the man
does not think to squint. It happens fastest at
altitude and on fresh snow. A full day's march across
open snow without eye protection produces symptoms by
evening.

### Symptoms

**Six to twelve hours after exposure.** Eyes feel gritty,
as if sand is under the lids. Redness. Tearing.
Sensitivity to light — the man flinches from firelight.

**Twelve to twenty-four hours.** Full pain. The man
cannot open his eyes without agony. Vision is blurred
or whited out. He is functionally blind for one to
three days. He cannot march, cannot fight, cannot
stand watch. He is a burden on the band.

### Snow Blindness Prevention

**Improvised slit-goggles.** A strip of birch bark, bone,
or leather with a narrow horizontal slit cut for each
eye. Tied around the head with cordage. The slit
restricts the amount of light reaching the eyes while
permitting forward vision. Peripheral vision is
sacrificed. Depth perception is reduced. But the man
can see and march for days without burning his eyes.

**Charcoal.** Soot or charcoal smeared in a band across
the cheekbones and around the eyes. Reduces reflected
glare from below. Not as effective as goggles but
better than nothing. Reapplied every few hours as
sweat washes it away.

**Discipline.** Snow blindness is preventable. A leader
who allows his men to march without eye protection
across open snow is responsible for the casualties.
Goggles are made in camp the night before a snow march.
Not suggested. Made.

## Drying and Warming

### The Wet Clothing Problem

Wet clothing kills. This is the refrain of every cold-
weather section because it is the truth that kills
more men than enemies do. A man who falls through ice,
fords a river, sweats through a march, or is caught in
freezing rain is wearing a heat-extraction system
against his skin.

**The protocol.** Strip the wet layer. Wring it as dry
as possible. Replace with a dry layer if available. If
no dry replacement, put the wrung-out clothing back on
and add insulation over it — a dry cloak, a blanket,
boughs, anything that traps air. Wet clothing with
insulation over it is survivable. Wet clothing alone
in wind is lethal.

### Fire-Drying

**Distance.** Wet clothing is dried near the fire, not
on it. Leather held too close to flame hardens, cracks,
and shrinks. Wool held too close scorches and weakens.
The correct distance is where the fabric is warm to the
hand but not too hot to touch. This means slow drying —
hours, not minutes. Impatient men who drape wet boots
on the fire stones wake to find them stiff, cracked,
and unwearable.

**Rotation.** Turn the garment frequently. The side
facing the fire dries while the side facing away stays
wet. Without rotation, one side is overdried and
damaged while the other is still damp.

**Boots.** The hardest item to dry properly. Stuff with
dry grass or cloth to hold shape. Set near (not on)
the fire. Rub with fat when dry to restore suppleness.
A pair of boots dried wrong is worse than no boots —
rigid leather causes blisters that lead to infection
that leads to lost feet.

### The Fatal Mistake

Sleeping wet. A man too exhausted to dry his clothing
who crawls into his bedroll wet will lose heat throughout
the night. If the night is cold enough, he will not wake
up. If it is not cold enough to kill outright, he will
wake stiff, chilled to the bone, and start the next day
already in heat deficit. Two nights of sleeping wet in
cold conditions breaks a man's health.

## Winter Camp

### Fuel Requirements

**Doubled at minimum.** A summer camp needs a fire for
cooking and comfort. A winter camp needs a fire for
survival. The fire burns all night, not just for the
evening meal. This means twice to three times the
firewood. A night fire for a band of ten requires a
stack of wood as long as a man and as high as his
waist. Gathering this wood is a daily labor that
consumes two to three hours. Failure to gather
sufficient wood before dark means a cold night, and
a cold night means a weaker band in the morning.

**Wood selection.** Hardwood (oak, ash, beech, birch)
burns slow and hot. Softwood (pine, spruce, fir)
catches fast, burns fast, throws sparks, pops, and
is consumed in half the time. A winter fire starts
with softwood for ignition and transitions to hardwood
for sustained heat. An all-softwood fire requires
three times the fuel and three times the tending.

### Windbreaks

**Natural.** Camp in the lee of a hill, a cliff, a
dense treeline. Wind is the force multiplier of cold.
A camp sheltered from wind at minus ten is more
survivable than an exposed camp at freezing.

**Constructed.** A wall of stacked logs, brush, or
snow blocks on the windward side of the camp. Chest
height is sufficient. The fire is placed between the
windbreak and the sleeping area so that the windbreak
reflects heat back toward the sleepers.

### Snow Walls

Where snow is deep and time permits, blocks of compacted
snow (cut with an axe or a flat blade) are stacked to
form a wall around the camp. Snow is an excellent
insulator — a three-foot snow wall cuts wind completely
and raises the effective temperature inside the
enclosure by several degrees. The labor of building
snow walls is repaid in fuel saved and sleep gained.

## Storms, Whiteout, and Blizzard Survival

### When to Move and When to Dig In

**The rule.** If you can see a landmark one hundred paces
ahead, you can march. If you cannot, you stop. Marching
in a whiteout — where snow, sky, and ground merge into
a featureless white void — leads men off cliffs, into
bogs, in circles, and to death by exhaustion.

**The decision point.** When a storm builds, the leader
assesses: can we reach shelter before the storm peaks?
If yes, push hard and fast. If no, stop where you are
and prepare. The worst outcome is being caught in the
open at the storm's full force, halfway between shelter
and the last defensible position.

### Digging In

**Snow trench.** If the snow is deep enough, dig down.
A trench six feet long, three feet wide, and three
feet deep. Roof it with packs, cloaks, and whatever
snow will hold across the top. Lay boughs on the
trench floor. Climb in. This is not comfortable. It
is alive.

**Huddled bivouac.** If the snow is not deep enough
for a trench, the group huddles together in the most
sheltered spot available — behind a rock, in a
depression, in a thicket. Back to back, cloaks over
all, packs as windbreaks. Body heat shared. The
outermost men rotate to the center at intervals. No
one sleeps — the man who sleeps in a blizzard huddle
is the man who freezes.

**Duration.** A blizzard in the Rimevegr can last one
day or five. The food carried and the fuel available
determine survival. A band caught in a five-day
blizzard with two days of food eats half rations and
endures. There are no other options.

### After the Storm

**Orientation.** After a blizzard, the landscape is
different. Trails are buried. Landmarks are obscured.
Drifts change the shape of terrain. The first task is
determining position. The second task is finding the
trail. The third task is moving before another storm
comes.

**Inventory.** Check men — who is injured, frostbitten,
snow-blind, or weakened beyond function. Check
supplies — what survived, what was buried, what was
consumed during the storm. Check animals — horses and
mules suffer in blizzards as badly as men. A horse
that has stood in a blizzard for two days without
shelter may be unfit to carry loads.

## Scavenging and Improvisation

### The Dead

A dead man's equipment transfer is immediate and
unsentimental. The procedure:

1. **Weapons first.** Sword, axe, knife, spear —
   anything combat-functional goes to whoever needs it
   most, by captain's decision or quartermaster's
   assignment.
2. **Armor.** Chainmail, leather, helm. Refitted if sizing
   is close. Chainmail is adjusted by adding or removing
   rows of links — a day's work with pliers.
3. **Boots.** Critical. Good boots on a dead man whose
   feet match a living man's feet are immediately claimed.
   Boot inheritance is one of the novel's recurring
   details (Haldr's boots on Lump).
4. **Clothing.** Patched, refitted, or cut for material.
   Nothing is wasted.
5. **Personal items.** Coins, dice, amulets, letters,
   keepsakes. These go into the purse if valuable, into
   the fire or a shallow grave if not. Sometimes someone
   keeps something — a bone die, a copper ring — but this
   is not remarked upon and not romanticized.

### Improvised Tools

**A knife and an axe can produce almost anything else.**
A splitting wedge from hardwood. A digging stick from a
sapling. A splint from a straight branch. A bowl from a
burl. A toggle-and-loop fastening from a carved bone and
a strip of leather. Fire-hardened stakes for a defensive
perimeter. Tent pegs. Cooking skewers. A stretcher from
two poles and a cloak. The ability to look at wood and
see tools is a survival skill that does not require
teaching — it requires necessity.

**Cordage from nature.** Inner bark of lime or willow,
twisted. Nettle stems, soaked and stripped to expose fibers,
then twisted. Rawhide cut into thin strips (the strongest
natural cordage available — a quarter-inch strip of
rawhide holds several hundred pounds). Long grass, braided.
The quality is not comparable to manufactured rope. It does
not need to be. It needs to hold for the night.

## Moving Unseen

### Terrain Use

**Dead ground.** Any fold in the terrain that conceals
a man from a specific observation point. A shallow
depression, a ditch, the reverse slope of a hill, a
stream bed. Dead ground does not need to be deep — a
man crawling in a ditch twelve inches deep is invisible
to an observer on level ground at fifty paces. The
skill is seeing the dead ground before moving into
the open—reading the terrain from above or from cover,
tracing the route that keeps the body below the sight
line.

**Shadow.** A man standing in shadow is harder to see
than a man in sunlight, even if the shadow is not
deep. The human eye is drawn to contrast and movement.
A still man in dappled forest shade is nearly invisible.
The same man crossing a sunlit clearing is obvious. The
rule: move through shade, cross open ground only when
necessary, and cross it fast.

**Vegetation.** Thick brush conceals but is noisy to
move through. Tall grass conceals a crawling man. A
man standing behind a single thin tree is not
concealed — but a man standing behind a tree with
brush around the base is, because the eye reads the
clump as a single mass. The skill is choosing the
route where vegetation screens the body without
requiring the man to push through it.

### The Fox-Walk

The silent movement technique. Used in forest, in dry
leaves, on gravel, on snow crust — any surface where
a normal step produces noise.

**Heel-toe-roll.** The foot is placed heel first, gently.
Weight transfers slowly through the ball of the foot.
If a stick or dry leaf is felt underfoot, weight is
held on the back foot and the forward foot adjusts.
The pace is agonizingly slow — one-tenth the speed of
a normal walk. A man fox-walking across one hundred
paces of dry forest floor takes ten minutes. A man
walking normally takes one minute and sounds like a
horse.

**Weight distribution.** The back leg carries the weight
until the forward foot is placed and tested. This means
the legs are always loaded — the thighs burn after
twenty minutes. Rest breaks are standing still, not
sitting.

**Arms.** Arms are held close to the body. A swinging
arm catches branches. An extended hand pushes aside
vegetation that springs back and moves, catching the
eye. Both hands control the space immediately in front
of the chest, gently moving obstructions rather than
pushing through them.

### Silhouette Discipline

A man on a ridgeline is visible against the sky from
miles away. A man standing in a doorway is framed by
the light behind him. A man moving along the top of
a wall is outlined against whatever is beyond. The
rule: never silhouette yourself. Cross ridges at the
lowest point. Stand beside the doorway, not in it.
Move along the base of a wall, not the top.

### Freeze and Move

The human eye detects movement before shape. A still
man in the open is often overlooked. A moving man in
good cover is often spotted. The technique: when the
observer's attention is elsewhere — looking in another
direction, bending to a task, speaking to another —
move. When the observer's attention sweeps toward
you, freeze. Total stillness. Not crouching, not
adjusting, not turning the head. Still. The urge to
duck or look away is overwhelming and must be
suppressed — both generate movement.

## Tracking

### Reading a Trail

**Species identification.** Bootprints are human.
Hoofprints are horse, mule, or deer (horses are shod
with iron — the crescent shape is distinct; unshod
hooves have a rounder print). Paw prints — wolves,
dogs, foxes, bears. Size, depth, and claw marks
distinguish them. A mercenary reads bootprints —
that is the track that matters.

**Number of men.** Count distinct boot patterns. If the
trail is a churned mess, estimate from trail width — a
single file produces a narrow track; a column of ten
produces a wider, more beaten path. Fresh horse
droppings count horses. Cook fire sites count the
group's ability to feed itself.

**Direction.** A boot pushed off the toe leaves a deeper
impression at the front. Grass and twigs are bent in
the direction of travel. Mud displaced by the boot
rolls forward. Stones kicked loose roll downhill from
the direction of travel.

**Speed.** A walking man leaves full footprints, evenly
spaced. A running man leaves deeper toe impressions,
wider spacing, and scuff marks. A man carrying a heavy
load leaves deeper prints, shorter stride, and
sometimes drag marks from the pack.

**Age of sign.** Fresh prints in wet ground have sharp
edges and water has not yet seeped in. Prints hours
old have softened edges and may have water pooling.
Prints a day old are weathered — edges rounded by rain,
dried by sun, filled by wind-blown debris. Broken
twigs: fresh breaks show white or green inner wood;
old breaks are dried and darkened. Spider webs across
a trail: if intact, no one has passed since the
spider built it (usually overnight).

### Tracking in Specific Conditions

**Snow.** The easiest terrain. Every step shows. Age
is estimated by snowfall — a print filling with new
snow is from before the current snowfall. A print with
crisp edges on undisturbed snow is recent. Depth tells
load. Stride tells pace. The disadvantage: the tracker
also leaves prints.

**Hard ground.** Rock, packed earth, gravel. Prints may
be invisible. The tracker looks for displaced stones
(a kicked stone shows a lighter patch where it lay;
the stone itself is lighter on the bottom), scrape
marks, crushed vegetation in cracks, moisture
transferred from a boot to dry rock (fades in minutes).

**Rain-washed trails.** Rain can obliterate soft-ground
tracks in hours. But rain also creates new sign —
puddles divert the trail, men step on higher ground,
boot treads fill with mud and leave marks on rock
they step on next.

## Counter-Tracking

### Principles

You cannot avoid leaving sign entirely. You can make
the sign harder to read, slower to follow, and
misleading in direction.

**Step on stone.** Where rock is available, walk on it.
A man crossing a rock outcrop leaves no footprints.
The tracker must search the far side of the outcrop
in all directions to pick up the trail — this costs
time.

**Use water.** Walk in streams. The current erases
footprints. Enter the water at a rocky point (not a
muddy bank) and exit at a rocky point. A tracker who
finds the entry point must check both banks upstream
and downstream until the exit is found.

**Walk in your own tracks.** On the return, step exactly
in your outbound prints. The trail looks like one
passage, not two. This requires discipline and good
light.

**Brush the trail.** A branch dragged over soft-ground
prints obscures them. This works briefly — a skilled
tracker recognizes brush marks and knows the trail was
deliberately hidden, which tells him someone is evading.
But an average pursuer loses the trail.

**Split the group.** Five men split in five directions.
The tracker must choose which trail to follow. Even if
the tracker picks correctly, the others escape. The
group reassembles at a pre-arranged point by a different
route.

**Hard surface transitions.** Walk on soft ground to an
area of hard ground (rock, road), change direction on
the hard ground, and continue. The tracker follows the
soft-ground trail to the hard surface and must then
search for where the trail resumes — which may be in
any direction.

## Camouflage

### Natural Materials

**Mud.** Applied to the face and hands (exposed skin
reflects light). Dark mud on light skin breaks the
recognizable pattern of a human face. The forehead,
cheekbones, nose, and chin are lightened; the eye
sockets, under the jaw, and temples are already
shadowed. Reverse the pattern — dark on high points,
nothing on low points — and the face becomes an
unreadable shape.

**Charcoal.** Same function as mud but drier, stays on
longer, and works on greasy skin where mud slides off.
Mixed with fat, it adheres well. The disadvantage:
charcoal smells of fire, and in fresh forest it is out
of place.

**Vegetation.** Branches, leaves, and grass tucked into
clothing, the helmet, the pack straps. The purpose is
to break the human silhouette — the distinctive
head-and-shoulders shape that the eye identifies
instantly. A man covered in loose branches does not
look like a bush. He looks like a vaguely bush-shaped
lump that the eye dismisses if it is not moving.
Movement destroys the concealment — the branches shake
in a way that vegetation does not.

### Breaking Outline

The human shape is betrayed by straight lines (the
rifle, the spear shaft, the shield edge), by smooth
curves (the helmet dome, the shoulder), and by
symmetry (two arms, two legs, evenly spaced). Natural
terrain has none of these. The goal is to disrupt the
recognizable shape:

- Wrap the spear shaft in cloth strips.
- Hang a cloak unevenly, not draped symmetrically.
- Tilt the head or crouch to change the shoulder line.
- Keep one arm tucked, not both extended.

### Concealing Fire and Smoke

A fire at night is visible for miles. Smoke by day
rises and marks a position. The solution is never
perfect — fire produces both light and smoke.

**Dakota fire hole.** A fire in a pit, with a separate
air-intake tunnel. The pit conceals the flame from
ground-level observation. Smoke is reduced (the
efficient burn produces less visible smoke). This
does not eliminate the smoke but reduces it
considerably. Best with dry hardwood, which produces
less smoke than green or resinous wood.

**Smoke dispersal.** Build the fire under a tree canopy.
Smoke passing through branches disperses rather than
rising as a telltale column. The canopy must be high
enough not to catch fire — a mistake made once and
learned permanently.

## Night Movement

### Dark Adaptation

**The physiology.** Human eyes take twenty to thirty
minutes to fully adapt to darkness. One bright light —
a lit match, a glance at a fire, a torch — resets the
adaptation to zero and the thirty minutes starts over.

**The one-eye rule.** When tending a fire or passing a
light source at night, close one eye. The closed eye
maintains its dark adaptation. When the light is gone,
open the adapted eye. This is an old trick that works.
Petra teaches it to every new man in the band.

**What you can see.** In full dark adaptation under
starlight, a man can see terrain, movement, and large
objects at twenty to thirty paces. He cannot see faces,
fine detail, or the ground surface beneath his feet.
Under moonlight, vision is considerably better — shapes
are clear to a hundred paces or more. Under overcast
with no moon, darkness is near-total and movement is
by feel and memory.

### Pace in Darkness

**Halved at best.** Even on known terrain, night
movement is half the speed of day movement. On unknown
terrain, it is slower — one-third or one-quarter.
Every step is tested. Hands are extended to feel for
obstacles. The man ahead is followed by sound (his
breathing, his footfalls) more than by sight.

**Noise.** At night, sound carries farther and the ear
is the primary sense. A man who trips in daylight makes
a noise lost in ambient sound. A man who trips at night
sounds like a landslide. Night movement discipline is
absolute: no talking, no metal clanking, no packs
jingling. Equipment is secured. Mail is padded.
Weapons are tied down.

**Terrain hazards.** Holes, roots, rocks, cliffs — all
invisible. The lead man moves slowest, probing with
feet and hands. A staff or spear used as a probe
extends the man's reach and detects drops and
obstacles before the body finds them.

### Navigation at Night

**Stars.** The pole star is the primary reference.
Other star patterns mark east and west as they rise
and set. A man who knows the sky can hold a bearing
through the night. A man who does not know the sky
follows the man who does.

**Wind.** If the wind direction was noted at dusk and
has not changed, it provides a bearing. Wind on the
right cheek at dusk means wind on the right cheek
should persist. A shift means either the wind changed
or the man is off course.

**Terrain memory.** The route was studied during daylight.
The shape of the ridge, the sound of the stream, the
feel of the ground underfoot. A man who paid attention
during scouting can navigate ground he cannot see.

## Reconnaissance

### Approaching a Settlement

**Never approach directly.** Circle wide. Observe from
high ground or dense cover at a distance. Approach from
downwind (smoke from the settlement drifts toward you;
dogs do not smell you). The first observation period is
at least an hour — long enough to see the routine, count
fires, estimate population, and note defensive features.

**What to look for:**

- **Fires.** Number of hearth fires estimates the number
  of households. A single large fire may indicate a
  gathering or a garrison. No fires at midday is unusual
  and worth noting.
- **Animals.** Livestock in pens — a settled, functional
  community. No livestock — abandoned, raided, or a trap.
  Saddled horses — armed men, possibly arriving or
  departing.
- **Routine.** When do people emerge? When do they eat?
  Is there a watch posted? Is there a pattern of patrols?
  A settlement with no visible watch is either safe
  (far from threats) or complacent (about to be raided).
- **Defensive features.** Walls, ditches, palisades.
  Their condition — a maintained wall versus a rotted
  palisade. Gates — open, barred, guarded.
- **Armed men.** Number, equipment, alertness. Armed men
  sitting in the sun playing dice versus armed men
  walking the perimeter with purpose.

### Reading a Camp from Distance

An enemy camp reveals itself:

- **Fire count** estimates strength (one fire per five
  to eight men, approximately).
- **Horse lines** estimate cavalry or mounted troops.
- **Latrines** indicate duration of stay (deep, well-dug
  latrines mean they plan to stay).
- **Noise level** indicates discipline (a quiet camp is
  well-led; a noisy camp is relaxed or leaderless).
- **Guard pattern** reveal when and where the perimeter
  is weakest.

### Estimating Strength

**The cook-fire method.** Count fires. Multiply by six
(the average number of men per fire). This gives a
rough estimate — plus or minus thirty percent. A camp
of twenty fires holds roughly a hundred to a hundred
and fifty men. This estimate breaks down with very
large or very disciplined forces (who build fewer fires
for security) or with very cold weather (where more
fires are built for warmth).

## False Trails and Deception

### Decoy Camps

Build fires in a position you have already left. The
fires must be fed — green wood or thick logs that burn
for hours. From a distance (and especially at night),
the decoy camp appears occupied. By the time a scout
approaches close enough to confirm it is empty, the
real group has several hours of distance. Works once
against a given enemy. A good scout recognizes the
trick from the absence of noise, the absence of
movement silhouettes against the firelight, and the
absence of sentries.

### False Trails

When evading pursuit, a detached group of two or three
men creates an obvious trail in a false direction while
the main body moves quietly in the true direction. The
false trail group walks normally, does not attempt to
hide sign, and leads the pursuit for several hours
before splitting apart and individually doubling back
by counter-tracking techniques. The pursuing force
follows the obvious trail and finds nothing at the end.

### Misdirection with Noise

Noise draws investigation. A man banging a shield with
a rock in the forest draws a patrol toward the sound
while the main body crosses open ground behind them.
Bird calls that are slightly wrong draw attention — a
hunter or a scout recognizes the fake and investigates.
The distraction needs to be plausible enough to draw
at least a few men and sustained enough to keep them
engaged for minutes, not seconds.

### The Feigned Retreat

A tactical deception as old as warfare. A force engages
the enemy, then withdraws in apparent disorder. The
enemy, sensing victory, pursues — and is led into an
ambush, a prepared position, or difficult terrain where
the retreating force turns and fights with advantage.
The difficulty: the retreat must look real. Men must
drop shields and packs. The enemy must believe the
retreat is genuine. And the men performing the feigned
retreat must trust the plan enough to turn their backs
on the enemy and know the ambush force is waiting
behind them.

## Signals and Communication

### Smoke Signals

**Column.** A sustained column of smoke (feed the fire
with green wood or damp grass) says: "Here. Come to
this position." Visible for miles on a clear day. The
disadvantage: everyone can see it, including the enemy.
A smoke signal is an announcement, not a secret message.

**Puffs.** Alternating covering and uncovering the fire
with a blanket produces distinct puffs. Pre-arranged
meanings: one puff for "all clear," two for "enemy
sighted," three for "come immediately." The system
must be agreed upon before the band separates, and
it must be simple — more than three signals are
forgotten under stress.

### Horn Calls

A horn carries farther than a shout and has distinct
tones that can be pre-arranged. Common signals:

- **One long blast:** rally to this position.
- **Two short blasts:** enemy contact.
- **Three short blasts:** withdraw.
- **Continuous blowing:** alarm — stand to arms.

The horn does not specify direction or distance. It
says only: this is happening, react as planned. Like
smoke, it is audible to everyone — the enemy knows
something has changed.

### Bird Calls

A cupped hand against the mouth, manipulated to
produce the call of a specific bird. The nightjar,
the owl, the cuckoo — birds whose calls are simple
two-note patterns easy to imitate. The recipient
hears the call and recognizes it as the signal. The
enemy, if they know the local bird sounds, recognizes
the call as false — wrong season, wrong time of day,
wrong location. A cuckoo calling at midnight is a
man calling to another man. Bird calls are for short-
range communication between scouts and the main body,
not for strategic signaling.

### Runner Relay

The most reliable method for complex messages over
distance. A man runs from the main body to the scout
position (or vice versa), delivers the message verbally,
and returns with the reply. The disadvantages: time (a
runner covering two miles each way takes thirty to
forty minutes round trip) and risk (the runner can be
intercepted). The advantages: the message can be
detailed, nuanced, and include instructions that no
horn blast or smoke puff can convey.

## Trade and Barter

### The Economy of Exchange

Coin exists in the Rimevegr, but most transactions are
barter. A man who cannot judge the value of what he holds
gets cheated. A man who knows values trades profitably.

**Standard values.** Everything is measured against
commonly understood benchmarks:

| Item               | Approximate trade value                     |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| A cow              | The universal unit of major value           |
| A good sword       | Half a cow to a full cow                    |
| An axe-head        | A tenth of a cow                            |
| A day's labor      | Food and shelter for the day                |
| A barrel of grain  | Seasonal — cheap at harvest, dear in spring |
| A silver coin      | A day's food in a market town               |
| An ounce of silver | Several days' provisions                    |
| A slave            | Three to twelve cows, depending             |

**Hack-silver.** Silver (and sometimes gold) is traded by
weight, not by coin denomination. A silver arm-ring is
snapped or cut into pieces of the needed weight. Payment
is weighed on a small balance (a folding scale that
travels in a pouch). A man who trades carries a scale
and a set of standardized weights. Disputes over weight
are serious — accusing a man of using false weights is
accusing him of theft.

### Haggling

**The protocol.** The seller states a price. The buyer
responds with a lower offer. This is expected — accepting
the first price signals either wealth (you do not need
to negotiate) or ignorance (you do not know the value).
The negotiation proceeds through counter-offers. Each
offer comes with justification: the quality is not as
claimed, the season favors the buyer, the distance to
the next market is far, the buyer's need is not urgent.

**Walking away.** The strongest tactic. A buyer who
walks away forces the seller to decide between the
offered price and no sale. This fails if other buyers
are present. It works best in private transactions.

**Information.** The man who knows the prevailing prices
at the nearest markets has the advantage. Dalla, who
tracks plant and herb values, knows what medicines sell
for. Viggo, who has traveled widely, knows the price of
iron at three different towns. This knowledge is power
in every transaction.

## Guest Law and Hospitality

### The Obligation

A traveler arriving at a household has a right to shelter,
food, and fire for three days. The host provides these
without asking payment. This is not generosity — it is
law and custom. Refusing hospitality to a peaceful
traveler is a social offense that shames the household.

**The exchange.** The guest, in return, brings news (the
most valuable currency in an isolated homestead — what
is happening in the wider world), offers labor if
staying more than one night, and does not abuse the
host's property or women. Violating guest-right —
stealing from a host, seducing the host's wife, or
harming a fellow guest — is among the most serious social
crimes. A man who breaks guest-right finds no welcome
anywhere the story spreads, and it spreads fast.

**The time limit.** Three days of free hospitality is
standard. On the fourth day, the guest is expected to
contribute or leave. An indefinite guest becomes a
burden — the host's provisions are not unlimited,
especially in winter. A guest who overstays is told,
and the telling is direct.

### The Host's Rights

The host can refuse entry to an armed band (hospitality
is for travelers, not armies). The host can ask a guest's
name and business (and the guest is expected to answer
truthfully — lying about your identity under a host's
roof is a form of guest-right violation). The host may
set conditions: weapons left at the door, behavior
standards while under the roof.

## Law and the Thing

### Common Legal Knowledge

Norse law is not written in books. It is memorized and
recited. The lawspeaker at the Thing (the district or
regional assembly) knows the law by heart and recites
it publicly. Every free man knows the broad principles:

**Weregild.** Every life has a price. If a man kills
another, the killer (or his kin) owes compensation to
the victim's kin. The amount depends on the victim's
status (a free farmer is worth more than a thrall, a
chieftain more than a farmer). Failure to pay weregild
invites blood-feud — the victim's kin has the right to
kill the killer. Weregild exists to prevent spiraling
cycles of revenge by converting violence into economic
transaction.

**Outlawry.** The most severe legal penalty. A man
declared outlaw at the Thing loses all legal protection.
He cannot buy or sell. No one may shelter him (doing so
is a crime). His property is forfeit. Anyone may kill
him without legal consequence. There are degrees: lesser
outlawry (three years of exile — survive that and return
with rights restored) and full outlawry (permanent —
the man is legally dead while biologically alive).

**The oath.** Legal proceedings depend on oaths. A man
swears on his honor and names oath-helpers — men of
standing who swear that his oath is trustworthy. The
weight of an oath depends on the standing of the
oath-helpers. A respected farmer's oath backed by three
substantial neighbors carries more weight than a
drifter's oath backed by his drinking companions. The
system is not about truth in the modern sense — it is
about social weight and reputation.

**Trial by combat (holmgang).** When oaths conflict and
the Thing cannot resolve the dispute, either party may
demand holmgang — a regulated duel. The rules vary by
region: the fighting space is marked out (often on a
cloak or a small island — "holm" means islet), the
weapons are prescribed, and the fight continues until
first blood, until one party cannot continue, or until
one party steps outside the boundary. Holmgang is legal
resolution, not murder. Refusing a challenge to holmgang
means forfeiting the case.

## Honor and Insult

### The Weight of Words

In a culture without police, a man's reputation is his
security. A good reputation means allies, trade,
hospitality, and marriage prospects. A damaged reputation
means isolation, suspicion, and vulnerability.

**Níðing.** The worst insult. It means coward, oath-
breaker, a man without honor. Being called níðing
demands a response — either forcing a retraction (by
threat or social pressure) or fighting. Accepting the
label without response confirms it in the eyes of the
community.

**Flyting.** Ritualized insult-exchange. Two men trade
composed insults publicly, each trying to outdoes the
other in wit, obscenity, and cutting observation. Flyting
is entertainment and social combat simultaneously. A man
who loses a flyting (whose insults are weaker, less
clever, or less well-received by the audience) loses
reputation. A man who wins gains it. This is not idle
banter — it is public performance with real stakes.

**What cannot be said.** Certain accusations, if spoken
publicly, require the speaker to back them up or face
legal consequences. Accusing a man of cowardice in battle,
of being the passive partner in a sexual act, or of
practicing seiðr (a form of magic associated with
unmanliness) — these are actionable insults that the
accused can take to the Thing as a matter of legal
challenge.

## Games and Recreation

### Board Games

**Hnefatafl (tafl).** The primary strategic board game of
the Norse world. Played on a checkered board (various
sizes — 9×9, 11×11, 13×13). Two sides: the king's side
(a small number of pieces including the king, starting
in the center) and the attacking side (a larger number
of pieces starting around the edges). The king's side
wins by getting the king to a corner or edge square. The
attacking side wins by surrounding the king on all four
sides.

Tafl is played in camp, in halls, and during every
waiting period longer than an hour. Proficiency at tafl
signals strategic thinking. A man who plays well is
presumed to think well. This may or may not be true, but
the presumption exists.

**Nine Men's Morris (myllna).** A simpler strategy game
played on a grid with three nested squares connected by
lines. Each player has nine pieces and tries to form
lines of three (mills). Forming a mill entitles the player
to remove one of the opponent's pieces. The game ends
when one player is reduced to two pieces.

### Dice

**Knucklebones (astragalus).** The ankle bones of sheep,
dried and used as four-sided dice — each face is a
different shape (flat, concave, convex, sinuous) and has
a different value. Used for gambling, divination, and
children's games. The randomness is genuine but the weighting
is not perfectly equal — certain faces land more often on
heavier bones.

**Cubic dice.** Six-sided, marked with drilled pips.
Made from bone, antler, or wood. Standard gambling
equipment. Every camp has at least one pair. The games
range from simple high-roll to complex betting games with
multiple throws and scoring rules.

**Cheating.** Loaded dice (weighted with lead on one side),
shaved dice (one face slightly flattened to favor its
opposite), and switching dice (palming a fair die and
replacing it with a rigged one). Detecting loaded dice:
drop them in water — a weighted die sinks with the heavy
side down consistently, while a fair die orients
randomly. A man caught cheating at dice faces beating,
theft charges, or worse — cheating is dishonor.

### Physical Contests

**Wrestling (glíma).** The traditional Norse wrestling
form. Opponents grip each other's belts and attempt to
throw the other to the ground. The feet must stay within
a marked area. Tripping is allowed. Strikes are not.
The match ends when one man's body (anything above the
knee) touches the ground. Glíma is practiced for fitness,
entertainment, and as a measure of physical worthiness.
A good glíma wrestler earns respect independent of his
weapon skills.

**Swimming.** A competitive sport as well as a survival
skill. Swimming contests in Norse culture included
distance swims, diving, and underwater wrestling (two
men grapple in deep water, each trying to hold the other
under). Swimming in cold Scandinavian water is an
endurance test as much as a physical one.

**Stone-lifting.** Heavy stones, usually at prominent
locations (farmsteads, harbors), are designated as lifting
stones. Men test their strength by lifting them to various
heights — waist, chest, shoulder, overhead. The weights
are known and the achievements remembered. A man who can
lift the heavy stone at a well-known farm has that fact
in his reputation for life.

**Archery.** Competitive shooting — distance, accuracy,
speed. Not the slow aim of a hunting stalk but rapid
shooting at a mark (a post, a shield, a bundle of straw).
Men wager on the outcome.

## Storytelling and Poetry

### The Skald's Role

Professional poets (skalds) compose and perform complex
verse — but every man knows enough poetry to quote it.
Verse is the culture's database. History, law, navigation
instructions, genealogy, and moral instruction are all
encoded in verse for memorization. A man who cannot recite
basic verses from the Edda and the sagas is culturally
illiterate.

**Kennings.** Poetic circumlocutions used in skaldic
verse: "wave-steed" for ship, "wound-sea" for blood,
"battle-sweat" for blood, "word-smith" for poet. Kennings
are not decoration — they are the convention. A skald
who uses plain words where a kenning is expected is
judged as incompetent.

**Performance.** Storytelling and verse recitation are
evening entertainment. A fire, a seated audience, and a
performer who tells a saga episode — complete with voices,
dramatic pauses, and audience interaction. The ability to
tell a story well is a social asset. A man who can hold
a fire-circle with a tale earns hospitality, favor, and
invitations.

### Work Songs

**Rowing chants.** Rhythmic songs that set and maintain
the rowing cadence. Simple, repetitive, with a strong
beat that matches the stroke cycle. The chanter leads;
the rowers answer or join on the chorus. The chant
eliminates the need for a drum and keeps twenty men
pulling in unison.

**Marching songs.** Less structured than rowing chants
but serving the same purpose — keeping a group moving at
a common pace and maintaining morale during tedious
travel. A good marching song shortens a long road (not
literally, but the perception of effort decreases when
the mind is engaged in rhythm and verse).

## Drinking Customs

### The Social Ritual

Drinking is formal in hall settings and informal in camp,
but both follow customs.

**The toast (skál).** The host raises the first cup and
drinks to the gods, to the dead, or to the guest of
honor. Each man drinks when toasted and is expected to
drain the vessel (the horn does not stand — it must be
emptied). Refusing a toast is an insult. Drinking more
than your share is boasting — acceptable if you can back
it up.

**Mead and ale.** Mead (fermented honey-water) is the
prestige drink — expensive, associated with poetry,
wisdom, and celebration. Ale (fermented grain) is the
everyday drink — cheaper, brewed regularly, consumed at
every meal. Water is for the desperate; milk is for
children and the sick. This is the cultural framework,
not medical advice.

**Drunkenness.** Expected at feasts. Tolerated at camp
(so long as the drunk does not endanger others). Not
tolerated on watch, on the march, or before a fight. A
man drunk on watch is punished severely — he endangers
everyone.

## Watch and Sentry Duty

### The Watch System

A band in hostile territory posts watch through the
night. The standard system:

**Shifts.** The night is divided into watches (typically
three or four shifts). Each watch is long enough to be
useful but short enough that the sentry stays alert.
Two hours is standard — after two hours of motionless
observation in the dark and cold, alertness degrades
sharply. A sentry who falls asleep on watch is a
catastrophic failure.

**Position.** The sentry positions himself where he can
see (or hear) the most likely approach routes. Not at
the fire (fire blinds night vision and silhouettes the
sentry). Not standing above the camp's skyline (visible
against the sky). In shadow, still, listening. The
sentry's eyes and ears are the band's early warning
system.

**Night vision.** Takes twenty to thirty minutes to
develop fully after leaving the fire. A sentry who
stares into the flames before his watch is blind for
the first half hour. Experienced men avoid looking at
the fire during the watch before their shift. When on
watch, the sentry looks to the side of objects rather
than directly at them — peripheral vision is more
sensitive to movement in low light than central vision.

**Challenges.** A man approaching the camp at night is
challenged by the sentry before being allowed to enter.
The challenge requires a pre-arranged response (a password,
a name, a phrase agreed before nightfall). A man who
does not give the correct response is not allowed in —
even if the sentry recognizes his voice. The discipline
of the challenge system protects against an enemy who
has captured a band member and sends him in first.

### Signals

**Horn calls.** Different calls mean different things:
one long blast for attention, two for assembly, three for
danger, a series of short blasts for attack. The specific
codes are agreed within the band and may change daily in
hostile territory to prevent the enemy from understanding
them.

**Fire signals.** A fire on a hilltop visible for miles.
One fire: "we are here." Two fires: "come to us." Three
fires: "danger." Fire-and-smoke signals (adding green
branches to produce smoke visible by day) extend the
range but are slow and imprecise.

**Runners.** The most reliable signal method over
distance. A fast man carries an exact message. The
downside: time (a runner covers three to five miles in
thirty minutes at speed, arriving exhausted) and risk
(a runner intercepted reveals the message to the enemy).
Important messages are sent with two runners on different
routes.

## Camp Life and Routine

### The Daily Rhythm

**Dawn.** The camp stirs with first light. The fire is
fed (if it was kept alive overnight) or restarted. Water
is fetched. Men urinate and defecate at the designated
latrine area (downhill and downwind of camp — see
sanitation). The cook starts the morning meal — typically
porridge (grain boiled in water) or yesterday's stew
reheated, with whatever bread or hard food is available.

**Morning.** Breaking camp (if moving) takes an hour for
an experienced band — shelters are struck, gear is packed,
the mule is loaded. In a fixed camp, the morning is work:
repairs, maintenance, foraging, training.

**Midday.** A brief meal if on the march (cold food, eaten
during a rest halt). In camp, the main meal may be at
midday — stew, meat if available, bread, and ale.

**Afternoon.** March or work continues. In camp, this is
the productive time — crafting, repairing, improving
defenses, hunting and trapping.

**Evening.** Camp is made (if on the move). The evening
meal is cooked. This is the social time — the fire is
the gathering point, stories are told, dice are thrown,
grievances are aired, and the captain briefs the band
on tomorrow's plans. Watch assignments are given.

**Night.** Men sleep. The watch keeps vigil. The cycle
repeats.

### Sleeping Arrangements

**Ground insulation.** A man sleeping on bare ground
loses heat through conduction — the ground draws warmth
from the body faster than the air. Insulation between
the sleeper and the ground is more important than
blankets above. A bed of spruce boughs (six inches thick),
dry grass, bracken, or a roll of furs solves this.

**Warmth sharing.** In cold conditions, men sleep close
together. Body heat is pooled. Two men under one cloak
are warmer than two men under two cloaks. This is not
intimacy — it is thermal survival.

**Bed-warming.** Stones heated in the fire (not river
stones — they can explode from trapped moisture) are
wrapped in cloth and placed in the sleeping area. A hot
stone retains useful heat for two to three hours. In
extreme cold, the process is repeated through the night
by the man on watch.

## Animal Husbandry

### What Every Man Knows About Livestock

A mercenary band does not herd cattle. But every man has
grown up around livestock and understands the basics.

**Cattle.** A cow produces milk (the dairy products —
butter, cheese, skyr — are staple foods), calves (the
herd grows), and eventually meat and leather. A small
farm's entire wealth might be measured in cattle. Knowing
a cow's age by its teeth, assessing health by coat and
gait, and understanding breeding cycles is basic farming
knowledge.

**Sheep.** Wool (the primary textile fiber — clothing,
blankets, sailcloth), milk (sheep cheese), and meat
(mutton). Icelandic and northern sheep are semi-feral —
rounded up seasonally from open grazing. Shearing is a
community event in late spring.

**Goats.** Milk (richer than cow's milk, tolerated by
some who cannot drink cow's milk), meat, and hides.
Goats are browsers, not grazers — they eat brush, bark,
and weeds that cattle and sheep ignore, making them
useful in rough terrain.

**Pigs.** Pork (the most calorie-dense common meat,
preserved beautifully by smoking and salting), lard
(cooking fat, leather conditioning, waterproofing), and
bristles (used for brushes). Pigs are efficient converters
of waste to food — they eat anything.

**Dogs.** Every farm has dogs. Guard dogs (alert to
intruders, including at night when the family sleeps),
herding dogs (essential for managing sheep and cattle on
open range), hunting dogs (finding, pursuing, and
retrieving game), and companionship (this is real and
acknowledged — a man's relationship with his dog is
practical and emotional).

## Burial Customs

### The Practical and the Sacred

When a man of the band dies, the body is dealt with.
The method depends on the situation:

**In camp (time available).**

- **Cremation.** The body is placed on a pyre (see
  digging section for fuel and time requirements). The
  dead man's personal items may be burned with him —
  a weapon, a personal tool, a drinking vessel. The
  idea: the man carries his tools into the afterlife.
  Valuable items (weapons, armor, silver) may or may
  not be burned — the captain decides, balancing honor
  against practical need. A dead man's excellent sword
  burned on his pyre is a waste that the living cannot
  afford. This tension is real and acknowledged.

- **Burial.** The body is placed in a grave (see digging
  section). Laid on its back or side, facing east or
  facing the direction of the homeland (practices
  vary). A stone marker or a mound of earth over the
  grave marks it. In Norse culture, the mound-burial
  is the prestige method — great men have ship-burials
  (the body placed in a ship or boat, surrounded by
  grave goods, the ship covered with a mound of earth).
  A mercenary in the field gets a simpler version.

**On the march (no time).**

- The body is cached if possible (covered with stones
  to protect from animals) for later recovery. If not
  possible, the body is left where it fell — the living
  cannot carry the dead at the cost of their own
  survival. The man is remembered by name at the
  next fire.

- Prayer or ritual words are spoken. The specifics vary
  — Christian, pagan, or an unsorted mixture of both
  that is common in the transitional period. Dalla may
  recite. Viggo may speak a verse. The captain says
  the man's name and one thing true about him. This is
  enough.

## Religion and Omens

### Practical Piety

Religion in the Rimevegr is not theology — it is practice.
Men sacrifice (small offerings — a portion of food, a
splash of ale poured on the ground) before risky
undertakings. Men wear symbols (Thor's hammer, a
Christian cross, sometimes both simultaneously). Men
invoke divine names in oaths ("by Odin's eye"), curses
("Hel take you"), and exclamations ("Thor!").

**What men believe.** The practical beliefs that affect
daily behavior:

- The gods favor the brave and punish the coward.
- An oath sworn on the gods is binding — breaking it
  brings divine punishment as well as social.
- The dead watch. Ancestors see what the living do.
  Shameful behavior dishonors not only the man but his
  lineage.
- Fate (wyrd) exists — each man has an appointed death.
  But within that fate, a man's choices determine whether
  he dies well or poorly. The moment is fixed; the manner
  is earned.

### Omens

**Raven behavior.** Ravens are Odin's birds. A raven
circling overhead before a battle is favorable (Odin
watches). Ravens avoiding an area may signal danger.
Two ravens (reflecting Odin's Huginn and Muninn, Thought
and Memory) are especially significant.

**Animal encounters.** A white animal (white horse, white
deer) is often divine or fey. Cross its path carefully.
A wolf seen before battle: ambiguous — wolves accompany
Odin but also presage death. An eagle overhead: favorable
(power, victory).

**Weather signs.** A red sky at evening is fair weather
coming. A red sky at morning is foul weather coming
(genuinely reliable — the rhyme exists because it works).
A ring around the moon: rain within a day. Swallows
flying low: rain coming (they follow insects, which fly
lower in falling barometric pressure — medieval men
observed the correlation without knowing the mechanism).

## Superstitions That Affect Behavior

### The Practical Ones

**Never whistle aboard a ship.** Whistling calls the wind
— which sounds superstitious, but on a ship that can be
swamped by a gale, any act believed to invite strong wind
is avoided. The enforcement: a man who whistles at sea is
told firmly to stop by every sailor within earshot.

**Never rename a ship.** The existing name holds the
vessel's luck. A renamed ship is unlucky. This is taken
seriously enough that ships are not renamed regardless of
new ownership.

**Never step over a sleeping man.** The sleeper's soul
may be wandering and the step may prevent its return.
Practically: a man who steps over sleepers in a dark
camp trips over them. The superstition prevents the
practical problem.

**Leave food for the landvættir.** The land spirits
(local supernatural entities associated with specific
features of the landscape — a particular hill, stream,
or rock formation) are appeased with small offerings.
A scrap of food placed at the base of an unusual rock
or at a crossroads. This costs almost nothing and is
observed widely. Not observing it makes the man who
doesn't an irritant to those who do.

## Children and Apprenticeship

### How Skills Transfer

Every skill described in this document was learned by
doing, not by reading. A boy begins learning at six or
seven — carrying wood, tending fire, holding tools for
adults. By ten, he handles a knife, rows a boat, and
manages animals. By fourteen, he knows the basics of
every household and traveling skill. By sixteen, he is a
functional adult member of a working band or farm —
capable but not expert.

**Mastery comes later.** A man of twenty has a decade
of practice in the skills of his role. A man of thirty
has two. The difference is visible — the older man's
economy of movement, his anticipation of problems, his
instinctive calibration of effort. The younger man works
harder for less result. The older man makes it look easy.

**Teaching.** Instruction is by demonstration and
correction. "Watch me. Now you do it. No, hold it this
way. Try again." Lengthy explanation is rare — the hands
learn faster than the mind. A man teaching another to
split wood does not describe the physics of fracture
propagation. He picks up the axe, splits a round, hands
the axe over, and watches the student make every mistake
that contact with real wood teaches — the twist, the
bounce, the stick, the off-center strike — and corrects
each one by showing the right way once more.

## Measuring and Weighing

### Body Measures

In the absence of standardized units, the body provides
measurement.

| Measure     | Definition                             | Approximate |
| ----------- | -------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| Thumb-width | Width of thumb at the nail             | 1 inch      |
| Palm        | Width of four fingers at base          | 3 inches    |
| Span        | Thumb-tip to little-finger-tip, spread | 9 inches    |
| Cubit       | Elbow to fingertip                     | 18 inches   |
| Fathom      | Fingertip to fingertip, arms spread    | 6 feet      |
| Pace        | One double-step                        | 5 feet      |

These measures are sufficient for construction, cloth
cutting, rope measuring, and distance estimation. They
vary between men — a tall man's fathom is longer than a
short man's. Where precision matters (in trade), the
measure of a specific known person (the chieftain, the
ship-builder, the lawspeaker) may be the standard.

### Weight

**The balance scale.** Two pans suspended from a beam.
The item to be weighed goes in one pan; standardized
weights (stones of known weight, calibrated metal pieces)
go in the other until the beam is level. Every trader
carries one. Accuracy depends on the quality of the
weights — forged weights are a form of fraud.

**Estimation by hand.** An experienced man judges weight
by hefting. A pound of silver feels distinct from two
pounds. This is not calibrated enough for trade, but it
catches gross discrepancies — if the merchant says a
piece weighs a pound and it feels like half a pound, the
scale comes out and the merchant's honesty is tested.

## Salt

### Value

Salt is essential for food preservation (meat cannot be
reliably kept through winter without salt or smoking, and
smoking requires a smokehouse). Salt is also essential
for leather tanning, medical treatment (wound rinsing,
gargling), and livestock management (animals need salt and
will travel to salt sources).

### Sources

**Evaporation.** Seawater boiled in pans until the water
evaporates and salt crystals remain. Enormous quantities
of fuel are needed — six hundred liters of seawater
produce roughly fifteen kilograms of salt. Coastal
communities produce salt; inland communities trade for it.

**Mining.** Rock salt deposits (rare in Scandinavia, more
common further south). Mined salt is purer and denser
than evaporated sea salt.

**Trade.** For most inland communities, salt is an import.
By the time salt reaches the interior, its value has
increased substantially. A small bag of good salt has
meaningful trade value. A man returning from the coast
with salt is bringing back currency as much as seasoning.

## Grain Knowledge

### What a Man Needs to Know

A mercenary does not farm — but he eats grain daily and
must judge its quality when trading, foraging, or raiding.

**Types.** Barley (the hardiest northern grain — grows
in poor soil and short seasons; used for ale, porridge,
and flatbread). Rye (darker flour, dense bread, tolerates
cold climate). Oats (porridge grain, horse feed, grows
in wet and cool conditions). Wheat (the prestige grain —
lighter bread, preferred but demanding of soil and climate,
not always available in the north).

**Quality.** Good grain is dry (a kernel bitten should
crack cleanly, not be soft or chewy), free of mold (smell
it — moldy grain has a musty sweetness), and free of
weevils (look for small holes in the kernels and fine
powder at the bottom of the container — this is insect
frass). Moldy grain causes hallucinations, gangrene, and
death (ergot poisoning from contaminated rye — the
condition called "Saint Anthony's Fire" in the medieval
period). A man who buys grain without checking for ergot
may be buying poison.

**Storage.** Grain must stay dry. A container of grain
that gets wet sprouts, molds, or both within days. The
container must also exclude rodents — mice eat grain and
contaminate what they do not eat with droppings and urine.
A sealed barrel, raised off the ground, in a ventilated
space, is ideal. In the field, a tightly closed sack
inside the mule's panniers, kept dry with an oiled cover.

## Swimming

### The Skill

Not universal. Inland men may never learn. Coastal men
and rivermen swim from childhood.

**Cold water.** Northern water is cold year-round. A man
entering cold water experiences cold shock — involuntary
gasping, hyperventilation, and a spike in heart rate.
This passes in one to two minutes. The danger: gasping
while underwater means inhaling water. Entry is controlled
— wade in, face the fact that it's cold, get the
shoulders under, and wait for the shock to pass before
swimming.

**Endurance.** In water below 15°C (common in northern
rivers and seas even in summer), a man's functional
swimming time is limited by hypothermia. Thirty minutes
in 10°C water is dangerous. Sixty minutes is often
fatal. A river crossing should be as short and direct as
possible.

**Drowning.** The actual process: the swimmer's muscles
cool and fail, the head drops below the surface, water
is inhaled, the lungs flood. Drowning is quiet — the
popular image of thrashing and screaming is the early
stage. By the time the man is actually drowning, he
cannot shout or wave. Watchful companions are the
difference between a rescue and a death.

## Pre-Battle Fear

### What It Feels Like

The body prepares to die. This is not metaphor — it is
physiology. Adrenaline floods the system. The heart rate
doubles. The mouth goes dry. The hands shake. The bowels
loosen — some men void, some merely feel the urge. Vision
narrows to what is directly ahead (tunnel vision). Hearing
becomes selective — the leader's voice may be inaudible
while the clink of enemy armor is deafening. Time distorts
— the minutes before contact feel like hours.

**The universal experience.** Every man feels this. The
man who claims he does not feel it is either lying or
too stupid to understand what is about to happen. The
difference between a veteran and a new man is not the
absence of fear but the ability to function inside it.
The veteran's hands shake while he checks his shield
straps. The new man's hands shake while he stares.

### What Experienced Men Do

**Routine.** Check equipment. Tighten straps. Test the
edge of the blade on a thumbnail. Adjust the helmet.
These actions are unnecessary — the equipment was
checked an hour ago. The actions are necessary — they
give the hands something to do and the mind something
to focus on. The ritual displaces the fear without
denying it.

**Humor.** Dark, crude, and directed at the enemy, the
situation, or each other. A man who makes his companions
laugh before a fight has done more for the band's
survival than a man who delivers a speech. Laughter
releases tension that silence stores.

**Quiet.** Some men go silent. They are not more
frightened — they are processing differently. The man
who falls quiet and checks his gear methodically is
often the steadiest once the fight begins. Do not
mistake silence for paralysis.

**The vomiter.** Some men vomit. Before every fight.
Reliably. This is not cowardice — it is a physiological
response as involuntary as blinking. The man vomits,
wipes his mouth, picks up his shield, and fights
competently. The band knows who its vomiters are and
does not remark on it.

## Exhaustion

### Stages of Exhaustion

**Irritability.** The first sign. The man who was even-
tempered yesterday snaps at questions, quarrels over
trivial things, is short with companions. He does not
recognize this in himself. His companions do. A leader
who recognizes irritability as an early sign of
exhaustion can intervene before the later stages set in.

**Clumsiness.** Stumbling on flat ground. Dropping tools.
Fumbling with knots that the hands have tied a thousand
times. The fine motor skills go first — threading a
needle, striking a fire, tying a secure load. The man
is not injured. He is running on empty.

**Poor judgment.** The man who decides to cross a river
at a bad ford. The man who picks a fight he cannot win.
The man who wants to push on for "just one more hour"
when the band should camp. Exhaustion mimics stupidity.
The decisions are not different in kind from the
decisions of a drunk man — inhibition is lowered,
risk assessment is impaired, and the short-term
impulse overrides the long-term plan.

**Hallucination.** After two to three days without sleep
or after prolonged physical exertion with inadequate
rest, the mind begins to produce things that are not
there. Shadows move. Sounds are misidentified. The
sentry who reports movement in the trees may be seeing
branches in the wind, or he may be hallucinating. The
distinction is critical and difficult to make from
the inside.

### A Leader's Response

**The push.** Sometimes the situation demands continued
effort — the enemy is behind, the shelter is ahead,
the storm is coming. The leader pushes. But the push
has a visible end: "We march to the treeline and camp."
An open-ended push — "We keep going until we find
something" — breaks men because the effort has no
limit.

**The halt.** When the situation permits, stop before the
later stages. A leader who halts his band an hour early
because he sees irritability and clumsiness has made a
better decision than a leader who pushes until a man
collapses and must be carried. The hour of march
sacrificed is repaid by the day of recovery avoided.

## Sleep Deprivation

### Effects

**After 24 hours without sleep.** Equivalent to a blood-
alcohol level of approximately 0.10 — legally drunk by
any modern standard. Reaction time is slowed. Judgment
is impaired. Mood is volatile. Memory is unreliable.
A man who has been awake for twenty-four hours should
not be making decisions, standing watch, or performing
tasks that require precision. He will, because the
situation demands it. The errors he makes are
predictable.

**After 48 hours.** Microsleeps — the brain forces
involuntary sleep episodes lasting seconds. The man
is standing, eyes open, and unconscious. When a
sentry falls asleep on watch, this is usually the
mechanism — not laziness, not disrespect, but a brain
that has seized the controls from the will.
Paranoia increases. Familiar faces look threatening.
Sounds are misinterpreted. Emotional control is
severely degraded.

**After 72 hours and beyond.** Full hallucinations.
Disorientation in time and place. Inability to perform
basic tasks. The man is functionally useless and may
be dangerous — a hallucinating man with a weapon
cannot distinguish friend from enemy.

### The Sentry Problem

A man on third watch (the hours before dawn) has been
asleep for three hours, woken to stand watch for two
hours, and is expected to remain alert during the body's
lowest metabolic ebb. He will nod off. This is not a
character flaw — it is biology.

**Countermeasures.** Two men on watch, not one — they
keep each other awake. Standing, not sitting. Movement
every fifteen minutes — walk the perimeter, check a
specific point, return. Cold water on the face. Chewing
something (a strip of dried meat, a twig). These help.
Nothing fully works after seventy-two hours.

**Punishment.** A sentry who sleeps on watch endangers
every man in the band. The traditional punishment is
severe — beating, expulsion, or death, depending on
the severity of the situation. A good leader
distinguishes between the man who slept because he
was careless and the man who slept because he was on
his third consecutive night of broken rest. The first
is punished. The second is given sleep.

## Cold and Hunger as Psychological Weapons

### Cold

Cold does not just kill the body. It kills the will
first. A man who is cold enough for long enough stops
caring about the mission, the band, his companions,
and his own survival. The progression: discomfort
(endurable with complaint) → misery (endurable in
silence) → apathy (the man stops complaining because
he has stopped caring) → passivity (the man does
what he is told but initiates nothing) → withdrawal
(the man stops responding to orders and sits).

The transition from misery to apathy is the critical
threshold. A complaining man is still fighting. A
silent man may have already surrendered internally.
The leader watches for the man who was loud yesterday
and is quiet today.

### Hunger

**Days one through three.** Hunger is a distraction.
The man is irritable, loses concentration, is
preoccupied with food. Performance degrades but
remains functional. Men quarrel over portions. The
fair division of food becomes the most important
social contract in the band.

**Days four through seven.** The body begins consuming
its own resources. Muscle weakness sets in. Stamina
drops sharply — a march that was manageable on a full
stomach becomes agonizing on an empty one. The mind
becomes food-obsessive: men talk about meals they have
eaten, meals they will eat, food they have seen. They
dream about food.

**Beyond a week.** Personality changes. The generous man
becomes selfish. The honest man steals. The loyal man
considers desertion if there is food elsewhere. These
are not moral failures — they are the survival instinct
overriding social bonds. A leader who does not understand
this loses his band. A leader who shares the hunger
equally retains his band longer.

**The breaking point.** Varies by man. Some men function
on starvation rations for weeks. Others break in days.
Physical constitution matters less than mental
constitution — a stubborn, angry man often outlasts
a stronger man who has given up hope.

## Grief and Loss

### After a Companion Dies

**The first hours.** Shock. Numbness. The man follows
orders mechanically. He does what must be done — fights,
marches, makes camp — without emotional presence. He
is functioning but not whole. This is normal and should
not be interrupted. The body protects the mind by
delaying the full impact.

**The days following.** The delayed response arrives in
different forms:

- **Rage.** The man wants to kill something. He is
  aggressive, reckless, volunteers for the dangerous
  task. This is not bravery — it is grief wearing
  the mask of anger. A leader channels this carefully
  or suppresses it. A raging man in a fight is
  dangerous to the enemy and to his own companions.
- **Silence.** The man withdraws. He eats when food is
  put in front of him. He marches when told. He does
  not speak. He is present in body. This can last days
  or weeks. Left alone, most men emerge from it. Forced
  out of it, some men break.
- **Recklessness.** The man no longer values his own
  life. He takes risks that are beyond courage — they
  are indifference. "Nothing to lose" is the most
  dangerous state a man can occupy, because it is
  unstable: it can resolve into renewed will to live,
  or it can end in a suicide assault dressed as bravery.
- **Drinking.** The man numbs with mead. In a mercenary
  band, this is tolerated for a night. On the second
  night, the leader intervenes. A man drunk with grief
  is still drunk — and a drunk man on watch, on march,
  or in the line is a liability.

### What Helps

**Tasks.** Give the grieving man work. Physical,
repetitive, useful work. Digging, firewood gathering,
equipment maintenance. The body's engagement pulls the
mind forward. The man who sits with his grief sinks
into it. The man who works alongside companions remains
connected to the living.

**Time.** There is no speech that fixes grief. The
acknowledgment — "He was a good man" — is sufficient.
Elaborate eulogies are for halls and hearths, not for
a march camp. Grief resolves in its own time. The
leader's job is to keep the man alive and functional
until it does.

## The Man Who Breaks

### What It Looks Like

**Shaking.** The whole body trembles. The man cannot hold
a weapon. He may be standing but cannot walk. He may
be sitting and cannot stand. The shaking is not cold
and is not fever — it is the nervous system overwhelmed
by more than it can process.

**Refusal.** The man will not move forward. He does not
argue — he simply does not move. He may not be able to
explain why. He may not hear the order.

**Weeping.** Uncontrollable. The man is beyond shame
and beyond self-awareness. He cries as a child cries —
total, physical, consuming.

**Catatonia.** The man goes still. Eyes open, body
unresponsive. He does not react to voice, to touch,
to pain. He is alive but unreachable. This is the
deepest level of psychological shutdown.

### What Helps a Broken Man

**A quiet voice.** Not a command. A name. "Bjorn. Bjorn,
look at me." The specific use of the man's name can
penetrate where an order cannot. The voice must be
steady, calm, and close — a pace away, not shouted
from a distance.

**A physical task.** "Hold this." Put something in his
hands — a rope, a pack strap, a water skin. The hands
engage before the mind does. The physical act of
gripping, carrying, or doing breaks the paralysis of
doing nothing.

**Getting him moving.** A man standing still stays
broken. A man walking — even led by the arm — begins
to function. The body's rhythm (step, step, step)
pulls the mind into a pattern. Walk him. It does not
matter where. Walk him.

### What Does Not Help

**Shouting.** A broken man does not respond to volume.
Screaming orders at a catatonic man produces nothing.
Screaming at a weeping man produces shame on top of
the break, which deepens it.

**Shame.** "Coward." "Weakling." These words do not
produce courage. They produce silence — the man
internalizes the break instead of processing it.
He may resume function externally while remaining
broken internally, which produces a man who no longer
trusts his own responses and hesitates at the critical
moment.

**Ignoring.** A man left broken and alone may recover.
Or he may walk into the woods and not come back. Or
he may sit where he stopped and die of exposure. A
broken man is a casualty and is treated as such.

## Panic and Rout

### How It Spreads

Panic is contagious. It bypasses thought and spreads
through the oldest part of the brain — the part that
reads the group and matches its state. One man runs.
The man next to him sees the running and his body
surges with the impulse to run. If that man holds, the
line holds. If he runs, the three men near him feel
the same surge. Within seconds, the line dissolves.

**The tipping point.** Roughly ten to fifteen percent
of a group breaking simultaneously triggers mass rout.
If fewer than one in ten break, the rest hold them.
If more, the herd instinct overwhelms individual
courage and the formation collapses from within.

### What Stops It

**A physical barrier.** A ditch, a wall, a river behind
the line. Men who cannot run do not run. They fight
because there is no alternative. This is why defenders
fight harder than attackers — the defender has nowhere
to go.

**A leader in the gap.** When the line breaks, a man
standing in the gap — visibly, loudly, fighting — can
stop the rout at that point. The men who see him stop
running because the presence of a standing fighter
invalidates the signal that caused them to run ("we
are beaten"). This is the most dangerous thing a leader
does. It is also the most important.

**A rally point.** Predetermined. Every man knows it. A
terrain feature that is defensible and recognizable.
When the line breaks, the survivors run — not in all
directions, but toward the rally point. A shattered
formation that reaches a rally point can reform within
minutes. A scattered mob cannot.

**Reserves.** A second line that has not been engaged.
The sight of uncommitted fresh troops steadies the
men ahead who are wavering. It also provides a physical
line behind which the retreating men can rally.

## Leadership vs Authority

### The Man Others Follow

He is not necessarily the strongest, the bravest, or
the smartest. He is the one who:

**Stays calm.** When the situation is bad, his voice
does not change. His hands do not shake visibly (they
may shake — he controls the visibility). His decisions
come at the same pace regardless of pressure. This
calm is not fearlessness — it is discipline. The men
around him draw their emotional state from his. If he
is calm, they are calmer. If he panics, they panic.

**Is competent.** He knows his craft. He makes the right
call more often than the wrong one. When he makes the
wrong call, he adjusts without pretending it was right.
Men forgive a leader's mistake faster than they forgive
a leader's refusal to admit one.

**Is fair.** He shares the hardship. He eats last, not
first. He takes the watches no one wants. When loot is
divided, his share is larger (his position earns it)
but not grotesquely so. Fairness buys more loyalty
than generosity — a fair leader with small gifts
outperforms a generous leader who plays favorites.

**Makes the hard decision.** Someone must be left behind.
Someone must hold the rearguard. Someone must die so
the rest live. The leader who makes this decision and
owns it is trusted. The leader who avoids it is despised.
The leader who makes it and weeps over it privately is
respected. The leader who makes it and seems indifferent
is feared, which is not the same as respected.

### Earning and Losing Trust

**Earning.** Trust accumulates slowly. It is built by
correct decisions, fair treatment, shared hardship,
and kept promises. A hundred small moments of
reliability produce trust. There is no shortcut.

**Losing.** Trust is lost fast. A single act of
cowardice, unfairness, or broken faith can destroy
what a year of good leadership built. The man who
fled once — even with good reason — carries the mark.
The man who took more than his share, once, is watched
forever. Men forgive mistakes. They do not forgive
betrayals.

## Routine as Psychological Anchor

### Why It Matters

A man in a predictable routine is a man with a
framework. He knows what comes next. He knows what
is expected. He knows that the small acts of living —
fire, food, shelter, rest — will happen in sequence.
The routine is not comfort. It is architecture. It
gives the day a shape that the mind can inhabit.

**When routine is maintained.** Men who make camp in the
same order, eat at the same time, take watches in the
same rotation, maintain their equipment on the same
schedule — these men function longer under stress than
men in chaos. The routine provides a series of small
accomplishments that maintain the sense of agency: I
made fire, I cooked food, I cleaned my weapon. I am
still capable. I still function.

**When routine breaks down.** No regular meals. No camp
discipline. Sleeping whenever exhaustion forces it.
Equipment neglected. The camp is filthy, the men are
filthy, the food is careless. This is the visible
sign that the band is losing cohesion. A leader who
sees routine breaking down is watching morale die.

### The Camp Ritual as Medicine

The evening camp routine — establishing shelter,
building fire, preparing food, posting watch, bedding
down — is the single most important morale tool
available. It is more effective than speeches. More
effective than extra rations. More effective than
promises. It says without words: we are still a band.
We still function. Tomorrow is another day, and we
will meet it in order.

## Mercy Killing

### When It Comes

A man with a wound that will kill him but has not yet
killed him. The gut wound that will take three days.
The crushed legs that cannot be saved, in terrain
where carrying is impossible. The gangrene that has
reached the torso. The burns over most of the body.
Dalla has done what she can. What she can do is not
enough. The man is dying, and the dying is slow.

### The Request

Sometimes the man asks. "Do it." Or words to that
effect — a look, a nod, a hand gripping the arm of
the man who can end it. The request simplifies the
decision without making it easy. A man who asks for
death has made a choice about his own life. The man
who honors the request is an instrument of that choice.

Sometimes the man does not ask. He may be beyond
speech, beyond consciousness, or simply unable to
form the words. The decision then falls on the healer
(Dalla) or the leader (Voss). The assessment is
medical: will this man live? If the answer is no,
and the dying is prolonged, the question becomes:
how much suffering is enough?

### The Act

A knife to the throat. Fast. The man who does it has
done it before or has watched it done. The blade is
very sharp. The cut is deep and decisive — hesitation
causes suffering that the act exists to prevent. The
dying man is held. If conscious, he is spoken to.
His name is used. There is no ceremony. There is
nothing to say that improves the moment.

### Carrying It After

The man who performs the mercy killing carries it.
Not as guilt — guilt implies wrongdoing, and there is
no wrong in ending suffering that has no cure. He
carries it as weight. The memory of the man's face.
The feel of the knife. The sound. This weight is
not spoken of. It is not confessed. It is not
worked through in conversation. It is carried quietly
and permanently, and it changes the carrier in ways
others can see but not name.

The band does not romanticize mercy killing. It does
not condemn it. It acknowledges it with silence —
the same silence that acknowledges the dead. The man
who did it is not thanked. He is not blamed. He is
treated as he was before, which is the kindest
response available.

## Sound, Smell, and the Senses

### What a Camp Sounds Like

- Whetstone on steel (rhythmic scraping, pitch rising as
  the edge takes)
- Fire cracking (sharp pops from resinous wood, hissing
  from damp fuel)
- Mail shifting in a pack (metallic rustling)
- Bones in a dice cup (hollow clatter)
- A mule tearing grass (rhythmic ripping)
- Wind in tent fabric (low flapping, thrumming in gusts)
- A man coughing in the dark (the sound that wakes the
  man next to him)
- Gest's charcoal on parchment (faint scratching, barely
  audible)
- Morning: ice cracking on puddles underfoot

### What Things Smell Like

| Thing                    | Smell                                  |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| A leather camp           | Smoke, animal fat, unwashed wool, mud  |
| Wet wool drying by fire  | Acrid, sour, dense — fills a tent      |
| Tallow lamp              | Rancid fat, like old bacon left out    |
| A fresh-killed deer      | Blood (copper-metallic), wet grass     |
| Pine resin heating       | Sharp, clean, turpentine-sweet         |
| Chainmail after scouring | Vinegar and metal, faintly gritty      |
| A latrine trench         | Ammonia and earth. Distance helps      |
| A festering wound        | Sweet, rotten — distinct and alarming  |
| Birch bark catching fire | Brief clean sweetness before the smoke |
| Stew on the third day    | Deep, savory, slightly gamey           |
| A man after three days   | Sour sweat, smoke, leather, dirt       |

These are the sensory anchors that make a scene real.
The Cook Catalogue technique applied to smell converts
a generic "the camp" into a specific, inhabited place.

## Rendering Rules

### Integration with Prose

These skills do not appear as exposition. They appear as
gesture, as incidental detail, as the ambient competence
of people who have done these things ten thousand times.

**Wrong:** "Voss struck the fire-steel against the flint,
which produced sparks that landed on the prepared tinder
of char-cloth, which caught an ember that he then
transferred to a nest of dried grass."

**Right:** "Voss struck a light. The tinder caught on the
second try. He blew the nest to flame and set it in the
fire-lay, feeding sticks over it until it held."

The knowledge is in the background — in the precision of
the gesture, in the absence of fumbling, in the detail
that appears only when something goes wrong. When a man
who has lit ten thousand fires does it again, the prose
does not notice. When the tinder is wet and the fire
fails on the twentieth try, the prose notices — because
the failure is the story.

### The Competence Tax

Every skill in this document has failure modes. The
failures are where the narrative lives:

- The fire that will not catch in the rain.
- The boot that splits on the third day of a forced march.
- The wound that festers despite Dalla's best work.
- The mule that develops saddle sores because someone
  loaded it wrong.
- The bread that breaks a tooth.
- The latrine that was dug uphill and fouls the water.

Competence is the baseline. Failure is the story. The
skills in this document define what competence looks like
so that failure — when it comes — is specific, physical,
and consequential.
