## The Legal Mechanics of Outlawry

### Declaration at the Thing

**Outlawry was pronounced through the public judicial machinery
of the Thing — the assembly of free men.** The process was
adversarial. A plaintiff (the injured party or their kin) brought
the case. Witnesses were called. Oaths were sworn. Oath-helpers
vouched for the credibility of the accuser. The prosecution had
to follow exact procedural forms — a case could be thrown out on
technicality if the wording of the charge was wrong, if the
summons was delivered improperly, or if the plaintiff brought
the suit at the wrong assembly.

In Iceland, the Althing met for two weeks each summer at
Þingvellir. Local spring assemblies (várþing) handled regional
cases. A man could be outlawed at either level, but full outlawry
for serious crimes was typically passed at the Althing. The
Lawspeaker recited the relevant law from memory. Chieftains
(goðar) nominated judges for the courts. The accused could defend
himself or have allies speak for him, but if no one would stand
as his oath-helper, his position was dire.

Once judgment was passed, the sentence was proclaimed publicly.
The full outlaw could not be killed at the Thing itself — he had
until the assembly closed to leave. After that, he was fair game.

Grágás specifies that the outlawry sentence was proclaimed in
formulaic terms. The condemned man was declared to have lost all
legal protection — unable to plead at any assembly, unable to be
named as defendant. He was, in the legal phrase, _outside all
law_ (útlagi).

### Fjörbaugsgarðr — Lesser Outlawry (Three-Year Exile)

**Fjörbaugsgarðr (literally "life-ring enclosure") was the lesser
sentence, a three-year exile with a path back to normal life.**
Its provisions, as recorded in Grágás:

- **Duration:** Three years of exile from Iceland.
- **Property:** Not confiscated. The man's farm, livestock, and
  goods were held intact, making return to normal life possible.
- **Three places of safety.** The lesser outlaw retained three
  designated safe havens. He was immune from attack at those
  three locations, within a bowshot of them, and on the roads
  connecting them — provided he moved a spear-length off the
  road when other travelers passed.
- **Passage requirement.** Each summer, he had to seek passage
  out of Iceland on at least three ships. If none would take
  him, he retained his immunity for another year. If he failed
  to seek passage or refused available berths, he was elevated
  to full outlawry.
- **The fjörbaugr itself.** The convicted man paid one ounce of
  silver (an eyrir) — the symbolic "life-ring" — at a
  confiscation court held at his home. If he failed to arrange
  this pledge (the alaðsfestr), he was sentenced to full
  outlawry instead.

The critical danger of lesser outlawry was procedural escalation.
In Víga-Glúms saga, Glúmr was convicted of lesser outlawry but
could not stay at his father's home because it was the site of a
temple to Freyr — and outlaws were forbidden in holy places. He
stayed regardless, his father sheltered him secretly, and when
discovered, he was declared a full outlaw. The system was designed
so that one mistake — one failure to observe a rule — upgraded
your sentence permanently.

### Skóggangr — Full Outlawry (Permanent "Forest-Going")

**Skóggangr literally means "going into the forest." The full
outlaw was called a skógarmaðr — "forest-man," "man of the
woods." The sentence was effectively a privatized death penalty:**

- **All property confiscated.** A confiscation court
  (féránsdómr) was convened a bowshot from the outlaw's home, a
  fortnight after sentencing. A goði was nominated to oversee
  it and received a fee. The confiscated property was split:
  half to the prosecutor, half to the men of the local
  assembly. Grágás specifies that the assembly's share was to
  be used first for supporting the outlaw's dependents (wife,
  children, elderly parents) — if he had any — and only then
  for other needs.
- **No shelter, no food, no aid.** It was a crime to feed,
  shelter, or assist a full outlaw. Anyone who did so faced
  outlawry charges themselves. The only exception: groups
  assisting the outlaw to leave Iceland entirely.
- **Anyone could kill him.** Wherever a full outlaw was found,
  anyone could kill him without legal consequence. There was no
  wergild owed for an outlaw's death. He was not a legal
  person. Killing him was, in legal terms, no different from
  killing a wolf.
- **No time limit.** Full outlawry was permanent. In Grettis
  saga, after nineteen years of outlawry, Grettir's friends
  argued at the Althing that no man could be outlaw for more
  than twenty years. The saga notes explicitly that no such law
  existed. The assembly agreed to lift the sentence after
  twenty years, but Grettir was killed before the term expired.
- **The formulaic sentence.** According to the Grettis saga
  translation: "He shall be barred from churches and the
  company of Christians, from heathen farmers, and from house,
  cave, and every world except from Hell."

### Vargr í Véum — Wolf in Holy Places

**The term _vargr_ had a double meaning in Old Norse. It meant
"wolf" and it meant "criminal, outcast, accursed being."** A man
who committed violence in a _vé_ (a sanctified space — a temple,
a Thing-ground, or any place where holy peace prevailed) was
designated a _vargr_ — the worst category of outlaw.

In Old English, the cognate _wearg_ meant almost exclusively
"criminal" or "accursed." The convergence of wolf and criminal
in one word tells us what the Norse understood outlawry to be:
the man who broke sacred peace was reclassified as a predatory
animal.

Specific recorded instances:

- **Landnámabók:** A man accompanying King Harald Fairhair was
  outlawed for killing someone in a vé. The phrase: _hann vá
  víg í véum ok varð útlægr_ — "he committed killing in holy
  places and became an outlaw."
- **Egils saga:** Queen Gunnhild's brother Eyvind stabbed a man
  during a fight at a feast. None was armed in the hall because
  _hofshelgi_ (temple-holiness) prevailed. Eyvind was outlawed
  and called a _vargr_.
- **Vatnsdœla saga:** Hrafn entered a temple armed. Ingimund
  warned him the gods would be angered and that the only way
  to make amends was to surrender his sword.
- **The Frostating Law:** Anyone who violated the _grið_
  (truce) at an assembly forfeited their rights to land,
  personal property, and peace among men, and was outlawed.

The vé was where law itself was made. Violence there was not just
murder — it was an attack on the social order. The _vargr í véum_
was therefore the most dangerous category: a man who had struck
at the root of civilized life.

## What Happened to Outlaws Physically

### Where They Went

**Iceland's interior is a volcanic desert — lava fields, glaciers,
sandur plains, mountain ridges.** No trees to speak of (Iceland
was largely deforested by the settlement era). The term
skóggangr ("forest-going") was borrowed from Scandinavian law
where actual forests existed. In Iceland, outlaws fled to:

- **Highland valleys.** Remote, uninhabited valleys in the
  interior. Grettir spent a winter in Þórisdalur behind
  Prestahnjúkur — "only a giant or an outlaw would choose to
  live there."
- **Islands.** Grettir's final refuge was Drangey, an island
  off the northern coast with sheer cliffs accessible only by a
  ladder that could be withdrawn. Hord's band occupied
  Geirshólmr in Hvalfjörður.
- **Lava caves and rock shelters.** The Icelandic interior has
  lava tubes and natural shelters — minimal protection from
  wind but nearly invisible.
- **Remote fjords.** Gísli spent years at Geirþjófsfjörður, an
  isolated Westfjords location, where his wife Auð maintained
  a farm as cover.
- **Abroad.** Some outlaws fled Iceland entirely — to Norway,
  the Orkneys, Greenland. Erik the Red's outlawry drove him
  to explore and settle Greenland. This was the best outcome.
  Most could not afford passage.

In mainland Scandinavia, the forest was real. The vast boreal
forests of Norway and Sweden swallowed men. But forest life was
marginally more survivable — there was game, timber, and flowing
water.

### Survival in the Wilderness

**The physical reality of outlawry was slow starvation punctuated
by violence.**

**Food.** An outlaw could not buy, trade, or receive food:

- **Hunting and fishing.** Arctic fox, ptarmigan, waterfowl,
  seal, freshwater fish. An outlaw without tools improvised —
  snares from sinew, spears from sharpened sticks, fishhooks
  from bone. On the mainland: deer, hare, grouse — but
  movement attracted attention.
- **Scrounging.** Seabird eggs (seasonal). Wild angelica root.
  Berries in late summer. Seaweed. Lichen. Moss. None sustain
  a man through winter.
- **Raiding.** The most reliable source was theft from isolated
  farms. This meant violence or the threat of it. Too visible
  and you drew organized pursuit.
- **Secret aid.** Despite the law, sympathetic kin sometimes
  left food at prearranged locations. Criminal but widespread.
  Gísli survived thirteen years largely because Auð and a few
  loyal friends supplied him.

**Shelter.** Without help: piled-stone windbreaks, turf-roofed
hollows, cave camps. Iceland's winters bring temperatures well
below freezing, gale winds, and weeks of near-total darkness. A
man without a proper turf house, without fuel, without stored
food, was in mortal danger from exposure alone.

**Fire.** The outlaw's dilemma: fire meant warmth and cooked food,
but smoke was visible for miles in Iceland's open landscape. A
fire at night drew anyone looking for you. Grettir burned fires
on Drangey because the island was hard to reach — most outlaws on
the mainland did not have that luxury. Many likely survived on raw
food for extended periods.

### First-Winter Mortality

**Most outlaws who did not flee the country or receive secret aid
died in their first winter.** The three great outlaw sagas
celebrate men who survived — Gísli for thirteen years, Grettir
for nineteen — precisely because survival was extraordinary.
For every Grettir who made it through two decades, scores of
nameless men froze, starved, or were hunted down within months.

Grettir himself barely survived multiple winters. By the end, he
was weakened, sick from a cursed wound, and dependent on his
brother Illugi and a slave named Glaumr to keep him alive. Even
the strongest outlaw in Icelandic history was, by the end, a
diminished man.

## Outlaw Bands and Companies

### The Solitary Outlaw vs. the Outlaw Company

**Most Icelandic outlaws were solitary.** The sagas emphasize
isolation as the norm — a single man in the waste, dependent on
luck, skill, and whatever secret help he could extract from
sympathizers. Gísli is the archetype: alone or sheltered by his
wife, moving between hiding places, composing poetry about his
despair.

But some outlaws formed bands. The most significant example
is Hord Grimkelsson and the Hólmsmenn.

### Hord and the Hólmsmenn — The Model Outlaw Band

**Hord was outlawed after his servant killed a farmhand, and
Hord — as the servant's master — bore legal responsibility.**
His brother-in-law Indriði refused to support him. Rather than
flee alone, Hord burned his own farm (to prevent his enemy Torfi
from seizing it), gathered his family and followers, and joined
his foster-brother Geir on the island of Geirshólmr in
Hvalfjörður. Other fugitives joined them, forming the
Hólmsmenn — "Island-Dwellers."

**How the band operated:**

- The island was a natural fortress. Steep-sided, accessible
  only by boat. The Hólmsmenn controlled access.
- They raided farms along Hvalfjörður for food and supplies.
  Local farmers could not dislodge them because the island was
  impregnable.
- The band had an internal hierarchy mirroring legitimate
  society — Hord as chieftain, Geir as lieutenant, other men
  in ranked positions. Hord's wife Helga and their children
  lived on the island.
- Hord is portrayed as trying to restrain his men from excess.
  But his men were "morally inferior" and he "unsuccessfully
  tried to restrain [them] from excess and ill-advised
  actions."
- The band raided even Hord's own brother-in-law Indriði. In
  one episode, Hord attempted to set fire to Indriði's home —
  a kinsman. The escalation shows how outlawry corroded even
  family bonds.

**The end.** The Hólmsmenn were lured from their island by
treachery. Kjartan, posing as a friendly trader, convinced
outlaws to come ashore group by group — each was killed on land.
When Hord finally came ashore and saw foster-brother Geir's body
in the water, it was too late. He was captured. Hord broke free
once — twisted out of his bonds, seized an axe from Indriði's
hand, and leapt over a ring of men three deep — but was
recaptured and executed. Vengeance for his death was carried
out by his sister, his wife, and his two young sons.

### Other Outlaw Groupings

**Grettir's Drangey.** Not a raiding band but a survival unit —
Grettir, his fifteen-year-old brother Illugi, and a slave named
Glaumr. The dynamic was grim. Glaumr was unreliable — he forgot
to pull up the ladder on the night Grettir's enemies climbed the
cliffs. Illugi fought beside his brother to the death.

**The pattern across sources:** organized outlaw bands were rare
and unsustainable. They drew too much attention, consumed
resources too fast, and generated such intense local hostility
that coordinated action against them was inevitable. Hord's
Hólmsmenn lasted several years only because of their island
fortress. On open ground, outlaw bands were hunted down quickly.

## The Social Death

### The Concept

**Norse scholar Stefan Brink described outlawry as "social
death."** The phrase _vár lög_ ("our law") was used synonymously
with "our society." To be outside the law was to be outside
society itself. The outlaw was not merely a criminal — he was a
non-person.

### Family Consequences

**The wife.** A full outlaw's wife was not automatically divorced,
but her position was desperate. Husband's property confiscated.
She could not legally harbor or help him. A woman with powerful
natal kin might retain some standing. A woman without connections
was destitute.

Grágás specified that confiscated property earmarked for the
assembly was to be used first for "the assistance of dependents
of the man who is outlawed." But this provision was administered
by the same assembly that convicted her husband.

**The children.** Children of outlaws could inherit from their
mother's side but lost access to their father's confiscated
property. They bore the social stain. "Son of an outlaw" marked
a family for a generation. In Harðar saga, Hord's two young sons
eventually exacted vengeance — the saga accepts that the sons
inherited the revenge obligation even though their father died a
condemned outlaw.

**The name.** An outlaw's name became toxic — associated with
shame, danger, and failure. Outlaws who survived sometimes earned
displacing epithets: Grettir became simply "the Strong" (inn
sterki), an epithet that both honored and categorized him.

### The Crime of Helping

**Helping a full outlaw was itself punishable by outlawry.** This
was one of the most devastating features of the system. Every act
of kindness was a legal risk. Every farmer who left food at a
hiding place, every wife who harbored her husband for a night,
every friend who passed along news — all risked losing their own
legal protections, property, and lives.

The effect was total isolation. Even those who wanted to help
were terrified of being caught. Gísli's thirteen-year survival
depended on the extraordinary courage of his wife Auð. When the
bounty-hunter Eyjólfur offered Auð three hundred pieces of
silver plus help with remarriage to betray Gísli's location, she
asked to examine the silver — then hurled the bag of coins into
Eyjólfur's face and called him a villain. Her foster-daughter,
thinking Auð was accepting the bribe, ran to warn Gísli. He told
her to return: "I know my wife."

But Auð was exceptional. Most people complied with the law.

## Atrocities and Violence

### What Outlaws Did to Settlements

**The sagas record outlaws and outlaw bands engaging in
escalating predation:**

- **Livestock raiding.** The most common form. Stealing sheep,
  cattle, or horses. For isolated homesteads, this could mean
  starvation — a family's entire winter food supply on the
  hoof.
- **Burning.** Hall-burning — setting fire to a longhouse with
  people inside — was one of the most extreme acts in saga
  literature. Grettir's outlawry itself resulted from an
  accidental hall-burning that killed Icelanders. The act was
  so monstrous that the dead men's sons pushed through
  outlawry even though Grettir had not intended the fire.
- **Extortion.** Road-tolling and farm-shakedowns by armed men.
  The word _rán_ (highway robbery) appears in Norse law as a
  crime warranting outlawry — common enough to require specific
  legal prohibition.
- **Killing.** Outlaws had nothing to lose. A man already
  outside the law could kill without additional legal penalty.
  They fought without restraint because capture meant death.

### Níðstöng — The Shame-Pole

**Not exclusive to outlaws but a weapon of psychological warfare
available to anyone with sufficient rage.**

In Egils saga (ch. 59), Egil Skallagrímsson erected a
_níðstöng_. The ritual: he set up a horse's head on a pole,
carved runes into it, and proclaimed: _"Here I set up a
shame-pole and I turn this invective against King Eiríkr and
Queen Gunnhild"_ — then turned the horse's skull to face
inland — _"I turn this invective on the land-spirits who
inhabit this land, so that they all go astray, so that none
finds or reaches his home until they drive King Eiríkr and
Gunnhild from the land."_

The níðstöng was a curse aimed at both the human target and the
_landvættir_ (land-spirits). It was spiritual terrorism — not
merely insulting a man but turning the invisible forces of the
land against him.

### The Blood Eagle — The Scholarly Debate

**The blood eagle (_blóðörn_) appears in only two or three
instances across four or five texts.** The most prominent:

- **Orkneyinga saga:** Torf-Einar carved an eagle on Hálfdan
  Longlegs' back "with a sword, and cut the ribs all from the
  backbone, and drew the lungs there out, and gave him to Odin
  for the victory he had won."
- **Heimskringla:** The same event: "He cut an eagle on his
  back after this fashion, that he thrust a sword deep into his
  back and cut all the ribs right down to his rump, drawing out
  his lungs through the wounds."

Roberta Frank (1984) argued the "blood eagle" was a literary
misinterpretation of the "beast of battle" motif — describing
eagles feeding on battlefield corpses, not deliberate ritual.
Rory McTurk and others defended the accounts as cultural memory
of ritualized execution/sacrifice.

**For fiction:** whether the blood eagle was historically
performed matters less than the fact that the Norse _imagined_
it, _wrote about it_, and attached it to specific named men. The
capacity to conceive such a thing — and to frame it as an
offering to Odin — tells us about the psychological landscape.

### Mutilation as Punishment and Terror

**Saga literature records specific mutilations used as punishment,
terror, and shame-making:**

- **Castration.** An extreme insult-punishment. Associated with
  the _argr/ergi_ complex — rendering a man physically what
  he was accused of being socially.
- **Blinding.** Several saga accounts of eyes put out — leaving
  the victim alive but dependent, humiliated, unable to
  maintain himself.
- **Maiming of hands and feet.** Rendered a man unable to
  fight, work, or travel — a slower death sentence in a
  subsistence society.
- **Hanging by the heels.** In Hrafnkels saga, Hrafnkel
  Freysgoði and his men were strung up by their Achilles
  tendons through slits cut in the skin behind their heels.
  They hung until they agreed to terms. Full body weight on
  torn tendon and skin.

### What the Sagas Record Without Moral Commentary

**The distinctive feature of saga violence is its flat affect.**
The sagas describe dismemberment, burning, blinding, enslavement,
and sexual violence in the same tone as weather or livestock
transactions. There is no moralizing. Things happened. The saga
recorded them. The absence of moral commentary is itself a
statement about the world the sagas describe.

## The Psychology of Outlawry

### Legal Wolfhood and What It Does to a Man

**The outlaw was _vargr_ — wolf. Not metaphorically. Legally.
He was reclassified from human to animal.** Anyone could kill him
as they would kill a wolf, because that is what he was.

Consider what this means over years. At first, perhaps, defiance.
Grettir's early outlawry is marked by continued heroic action —
killing monsters, protecting farmers, fighting off attackers. But
the saga watches him erode. By the end, he is afraid of the dark.
A man who fought draugar and trolls, afraid to be alone in
darkness. The saga does not explain this fear. It simply records
it. Scholars have read it as a depiction of psychological
breakdown under prolonged isolation and threat.

### The Despair in the Sources

**Gísli Súrsson's dreams.** Gísli was haunted by two dream-women
throughout his outlawry — one benevolent, one malevolent. The good
dream-woman offered comfort. The evil one poured blood on him,
washed him in gore, promised his death. As outlawry continued,
the evil dream-woman appeared more frequently. The dreams became
so disturbing Gísli could not sleep. The modern reader recognizes
this: a man under unrelenting stress whose unconscious mind has
turned against him. PTSD expressed through the only cultural
framework available.

**Grettir's fear of darkness.** After wrestling the revenant
Glámr, Grettir was cursed: "This I lay upon you: that you will
always see these eyes of mine before your own." But the fear
intensified through years of outlawry, suggesting the curse was
a narrative device for what isolation and constant threat had
actually done to him.

**Hord's attempt at restraint.** Harðar saga portrays Hord not as
a man descending into brutality but as one desperately trying to
maintain moral standards while surrounded by men who have
abandoned them. His failure is the tragedy — he could not keep his
men from raiding kin, burning farms, and provoking the coalition
that destroyed them. The outlaw-leader's psychology is distinct:
he carries not only his own survival but responsibility for
others, and watches them become what the law already says they
are.

### The Brutality Escalation Pattern

**The logic of outlawry pushed men toward increasing violence
through a consistent four-phase arc:**

1. **Survival.** Hiding, scrounging, depending on secret help.
   The outlaw tries to maintain his self-image as a man
   wrongfully condemned.
2. **Predation.** When help dries up or hiding places are
   discovered, the outlaw begins raiding. Each raid makes him
   more visible, more hated. Community response escalates.
3. **Entrenchment.** The outlaw accepts his status. He becomes
   what the law says he is. Violence is no longer desperate —
   it becomes habitual. The distinction between survival theft
   and gratuitous cruelty blurs.
4. **Terminal.** Killed in a final confrontation — usually
   through treachery, exhaustion, or overwhelming numbers — or
   escapes the country entirely.

Not all followed this arc. Some were killed immediately. Some
fled. Some died quietly of exposure. But for those who survived
long enough, the pattern is consistent: the law declared them
wolves, and over time, they became wolves.

### The Norse Soul-Model Applied to Outlawry

> See also: `psychological-trauma.md` § Norse Soul-Model

- The outlaw's _hugr_ (mind-soul) was under constant siege. No
  respite, no communal fire, no saga-telling by allies to
  reframe horror as narrative. The things that kept normal
  warriors functional — companionship, ritual, purpose — were
  stripped away.
- The outlaw's _fylgja_ (guardian spirit) was understood to
  have turned its face. He was _unlucky_, and bad luck was
  contagious. This belief reinforced isolation — even those who
  wished to help feared proximity to an unlucky man would draw
  misfortune.
- The outlaw's _hamr_ (social mask, composure) had no audience.
  No community to perform normalcy for. Without social pressure
  to maintain his hamr, internal damage surfaced unchecked.

## The Plunder Economy

### How Outlaw Bands Sustained Themselves

**The Hólmsmenn provide the clearest model of outlaw-band
economics:**

- **Raiding for livestock and food.** The primary activity.
  Sheep and cattle from farms along their fjord. Dried fish.
  Stored grain. Whatever could be seized quickly.
- **Extortion.** Isolated farms that could not resist an armed
  band paid tribute — food, tools, clothing — in exchange for
  being left unburnt.
- **Fishing.** On an island, fish were accessible. Grettir's
  survival on Drangey depended partly on fishing and egg
  collection from seabird colonies.
- **Scavenging from the dead.** When outlaws killed attackers,
  they stripped bodies of weapons, clothing, food, and silver.
- **Secret trade with corrupt farmers.** Trading with outlaws
  was criminal, but some farmers exchanged food for stolen
  goods or the promise that their farm would be spared. This
  black market is implied throughout Harðar saga.

### Why Outlaw Economies Failed

**Outlaw bands were economically parasitic and unsustainable:**

- **Consumption without production.** They ate neighboring
  farms' livestock without raising their own. Once surrounding
  farms were stripped, the band starved or had to range farther,
  increasing exposure.
- **Escalating hostility.** Each raid turned more people into
  enemies. The Hólmsmenn's raids united the farmers of
  Hvalfjörður against them.
- **Internal discipline collapse.** Without legitimate social
  structures — law, assembly, public opinion — outlaw bands
  had no mechanism for resolving disputes except violence. Men
  with nothing to lose and no social consequences for
  misconduct became increasingly ungovernable.
- **Bounty hunting.** Full outlaws had a price on their heads.
  In Gísla saga, Eyjólfur the Grey operated as a professional
  bounty hunter, funded by Bork. Persistence eventually found
  the outlaw.

## Saga Case Studies

### Gísli Súrsson — Gísla saga Súrssonar

**Written:** c. 1230. **Setting:** Westfjords, c. 962-978.

**The crime.** Gísli killed his brother-in-law Þorgrímr the Goði
in a vengeance killing. The killing was discovered through a
verse Gísli composed — his sister Þordís identified the
confession.

**Thirteen years of survival.** Gísli and wife Auð retreated to
Geirþjófsfjörður. Gísli hid in the wilderness whenever visitors
appeared. Auð maintained the farm as cover. Several chieftains
refused to shelter him — "every door seemed to be closed."

**Key scenes.**
Eyjólfur offered Auð silver and help remarrying. She struck him
with the bag of coins. — Increasingly dark dreams plagued Gísli;
the evil dream-woman washed him in blood; he could not sleep. —
Discovered at last, Gísli fought on the cliffs. Auð fought beside
him with a wooden club. His final blow was "as strong as his
first." He died surrounded. — Aftermath: Gísli was "honoured
and respected" even in death. His killers went home
"without honor."

### Grettir Ásmundarson — Grettis saga Ásmundarsonar

**Written:** c. 1320. **Setting:** Across Iceland, c. 1011-1031.

**The crime.** Grettir accidentally burned down a hall in Norway,
killing two Icelanders. Outlawed _in absentia_.

**Nineteen years.** The longest-surviving outlaw in Icelandic
history. Moved constantly. At one point thirty farmers combined
to capture him and barely held him.

**Key scenes.**
The curse of Glámr: after wrestling the revenant, cursed to see
dead man's eyes in every darkness — fear grew through outlawry,
symbolizing psychological disintegration. — Drangey: final
refuge. Island cliffs as natural fortress. — The death: cursed
driftwood gashed his leg, wound festered. Weakened, overwhelmed
when enemies climbed the cliffs (Glaumr left the ladder down).
Illugi died defending his brother. — The twenty-year argument:
friends argued at the Althing that outlawry should be limited.
No such law existed. Grettir was killed before the term expired.

### Hord Grimkelsson — Harðar saga ok Hólmverja

**Written:** Late 14th-15th c. **Setting:** SW Iceland, c. 950-989.

**The crime.** Hord's servant killed a farmhand. Hord bore legal
responsibility. Brother-in-law refused support. Outlawed.

**The band.** Hord formed the Hólmsmenn on Geirshólmr. They
raided Hvalfjörður. An outlaw society with its own hierarchy.

**Key scenes.**
Burned his own homestead rather than let enemies seize it. —
Raided even his brother-in-law's livestock. Attempted to burn
Indriði's home. — Kjartan's treachery: posing as friend, lured
outlaws ashore group by group, each killed on land. Hord saw
Geir's body in the water. — Captured, broke free — twisted out
of bonds, seized an axe, leapt over a ring of men three deep.
Recaptured. Executed. — Vengeance: carried out by his sister,
his wife, and his two young sons.

### Egill Skallagrímsson — Egils saga

**Not primarily an outlaw saga, but Egil's outlaw-adjacent
violence illuminates the system.** Repeatedly nearly outlawed for
killings in Norwegian royal feuds. The níðstöng episode — ritual
curse-attack against King Eiríkr and Queen Gunnhild. Bjorn, son
of Ketil Flat-Nose, was declared outlaw by King Harald's Thing —
among the earliest references to outlawry as a political tool
wielded by royal power rather than community consensus.

### Eyrbyggja saga

**Relevant for the blurring of outlaw and revenant.** Depictions
of outlaws haunting the same landscapes as ghosts. The saga
blurs the line between the living outlaw and the restless dead.
Both haunt the margins. Both threaten the farm. Both must be
dealt with. — Bjorn, outlawed by King Harald's Thing, fled to
Iceland — the outlaw-as-settler pattern that populated the island.
