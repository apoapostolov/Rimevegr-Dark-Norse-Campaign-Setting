## Norse Soul-Model

### Hugr — The Mind-Soul

Norse psychology centered on _hugr_ — the active, conscious
mind-soul that governed will, courage, and intent. A man's hugr
could be:

- **Strong (hugsterkr):** Steady under pressure, calm in blood.
  The ideal warrior state.
- **Restless (hugrunnr):** Agitated, unable to settle. The mind
  circles. Sleep is poor. The man is watchful beyond reason.
- **Sick (hugsjúkr):** The hugr is wounded. Decision-making
  fails. The man withdraws, lashes out, or freezes. He is no
  longer reliable under pressure.
- **Broken (hugbrotinn):** The hugr has cracked. Functionally
  incapacitated by internal damage that no leech can reach.
  He may seem whole but cannot function as he was.

The hugr was understood as something that could be exhausted, just
like the body. A man could spend his hugr on too many battles, too
many winters, too many dead companions — and find that one day
there was nothing left to spend.

### Fylgja — The Following Spirit

Every person had a _fylgja_ — a personal spirit or fetch, often
appearing in animal form, that accompanied them through life:

- A man whose fylgja appeared to others in dreams was about to
  die.
- A man who could no longer sense his fylgja was considered cut
  off from his own luck — a dangerous state that affected the
  whole band.
- Trauma was sometimes understood as the fylgja pulling away —
  the protective spirit withdrawing from a man who had seen
  too much.

This gave trauma a supernatural dimension. A broken man was not
just weak; he was _unlucky_. His fylgja had turned its face.
Being near him was dangerous for everyone.

### Hamr — The Shape-Self

The _hamr_ was a person's outward presentation — composure,
bearing, and social mask. A man who was _hamrammr_ (shape-strong)
could maintain his outward form under any pressure. A man whose
hamr slipped showed his internal damage:

- Trembling hands where there were none before.
- A flinch at sudden sounds — doors, iron on iron, shouting.
- Changed gait — moving as if expecting a blow.
- Loss of the social mask: saying things that should stay inside,
  laughing at wrong moments, going silent when speech was
  expected.

The band watched each other's hamr constantly. It was the primary
diagnostic tool. When a man's hamr slipped, every man noticed —
and began calculating what it meant for them.

## Norse Sources of Damage

These extend the generic sources with Norse-specific frameworks:

### Bardagi-Sótt (Battle-Sickness)

The Norse term for battle-shock. Specifically:

- The man's hugr has fled his body momentarily. It will return.
  If it does not, he is _hugsjúkr_.
- Shield-brothers shake, shout, or strike the frozen man. If he
  comes back, it happened. No one mentions it unless it happens
  again.

### Vig-Byrðr (Killing-Weight)

The cumulative spiritual cost of ending lives, rendered through
Norse legal and spiritual frameworks:

- Every killing carried a _manngjöld_ (man-price) — a spiritual
  and legal debt. Even justified battle-killings left a residue.
- The blood-guilt concept was not purely legal. Some men felt
  physically heavier after prolonged campaigns.
- Berserkers were thought immune to killing-weight during their
  fury, but the price came after. The _berserksgangr_ hangover
  was the weight arriving all at once.

### Ógnar-Sjón (Horror-Sight)

The Norsemen had no concept of "processing" horror. The
expectation was endurance: you saw it, you carry it, you do not
speak of it. The man who could not carry it had suffered a
wound-of-the-eyes — something that cut him and did not heal.

### Svikar-Sár (Betrayal-Wound)

In a culture built entirely on oath and reciprocal obligation,
betrayal cut deeper than any blade. A man with unresolved
svikar-sár could never lean on mutual dependence again. He
hoarded food, slept light, watched every hand.

## Norse Coping Mechanisms

### The Fire and the Mead

The primary coping mechanism was the communal fire. Not therapy —
proximity. The warmth, the noise of others eating and talking, the
sight of living faces in firelight after a day of killing.

Mead and ale loosened the hamr. A man who would never speak of
what he saw sober might, two horns deep, say something that
released pressure. The band tolerated this. A man could weep into
his cup and it happened only in firelight. By morning it was
unsaid.

The limit was functional. A man who drank so much that he could
not march, fight, or do his share of work had crossed from coping
to liability.

### Saga-Telling

Recounting battles in stylized, crafted form served a dual purpose:
processing experience through narrative, and reframing horror as
story. A battle that was terrifying became, in the telling,
something with structure — beginning, turning point, end.

The sagas never mentioned fear except as a setup for its conquest.
This was cultural technology for converting raw experience into
bearable form. The cost was that true suffering went unspoken.
The saga preserved the heroic version. The real version rotted
inside.

### Ritual and Sacrifice

Making offerings — particularly to Odin (who knew suffering) and
Tyr (who knew sacrifice) — was understood as transferring some of
the weight to powers who could bear it. A man who sacrificed after
battle was not asking for forgiveness; he was paying a debt,
balancing the spiritual ledger.

_Seiðr_ practice — reading wyrd, interpreting dreams, burning
herbs and singing — was the closest thing to psychological
intervention. Whether it worked clinically was irrelevant. The act
of someone attending to the invisible damage was itself
therapeutic. The men would not call it that. They called it the
healer's business.

### Physical Hardship as Medicine

The Norse instinctively treated psychological damage with physical
hardship — not punishment, but distraction and proof of continued
function. A man whose mind was sick was made to march, carry, chop
wood, stand watch. The body's exhaustion overrode the mind's
circling.

This worked up to a threshold. Below it, hard work kept the man
in his body, in the present, in a world of immediate physical
demands that left no room for the images in his head. Above that
threshold, exhaustion compounded the damage.

### The Shield-Bond (Skjaldvinr)

The single most effective protection against psychological
collapse was the shield-bond — the one-to-one relationship between
men who fought side by side.

The shield-bond worked because it provided witness. Someone else
had seen what you saw. You did not need to explain or speak. You
just needed to know they were there, at the fire, alive.

When a shield-brother died, the surviving partner was at highest
risk of collapse. Assigning a new partner was not replacing the
dead man — it was preventing a second loss.

## The Healer's Approach in Norse Context

### Seiðr Framing

When a man is breaking, the healer may frame it in supernatural
terms: "Your fylgja is restless. We must make an offering." This
gives the man a narrative that does not require admitting weakness.
He is not broken — his spirit is unsettled. The distinction
matters enormously in a culture where admitting fear is social
death.

### What the Norse Culture Cannot Say

- **"I am afraid."** A Norse warrior cannot say this and remain
  in the band. Fear is acknowledged only through ritual —
  offerings, wyrd-reading — never through direct speech.
- **"I need help."** A man may seek herbs to sleep. He will
  never say "my mind is broken."
- **"I cannot do this anymore."** Desertion is the only exit.
  There is no concept of respite within the Norse warband.

### What the Norse Culture Permits

- Weeping at the fire, under cover of drink. Acknowledged by no
  one. Remembered by everyone.
- Silence that lasts days. "He is carrying something" — not
  "he is weakening."
- Reckless courage that is actually death-seeking. Called
  heroism by those who do not look closely.
- Ritual mourning: three days of acknowledged grief, then the
  man is expected to function.

| Flashback              | mara-ríðr (mare-riding), ógnar-minni | Relived experience, waking or sleeping |
| Hypervigilance         | vök-ástand (watch-state)             | Constant alertness beyond need         |
| Emotional numbing      | kjölnun (cooling/freeze)             | Flattened affect, no emotional range   |
| Moral injury           | vig-byrðr (killing-weight)           | Guilt/shame from acts committed        |
| Dissociation           | hamr-slit (shape-slip)               | Detachment from self or reality        |

| Complicated grief      | harmr-bani (grief that kills)        | Grief that does not resolve            |
