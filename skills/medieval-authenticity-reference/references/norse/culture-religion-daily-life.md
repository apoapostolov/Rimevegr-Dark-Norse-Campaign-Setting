## Games and Recreation

### Board Games

**Hnefatafl (tafl).** The primary strategic board game of
the Norse world. Played on a checkered board (various
sizes — 9×9, 11×11, 13×13). Two sides: the king's side
(a small number of pieces including the king, starting
in the center) and the attacking side (a larger number
of pieces starting around the edges). The king's side
wins by getting the king to a corner or edge square. The
attacking side wins by surrounding the king on all four
sides.

Tafl is played in camp, in halls, and during every
waiting period longer than an hour. Proficiency at tafl
signals strategic thinking. A man who plays well is
presumed to think well. This may or may not be true, but
the presumption exists.

**Nine Men's Morris (myllna).** A simpler strategy game
played on a grid with three nested squares connected by
lines. Each player has nine pieces and tries to form
lines of three (mills). Forming a mill entitles the player
to remove one of the opponent's pieces. The game ends
when one player is reduced to two pieces.

### Dice

**Knucklebones (astragalus).** The ankle bones of sheep,
dried and used as four-sided dice — each face is a
different shape (flat, concave, convex, sinuous) and has
a different value. Used for gambling, divination, and
children's games. The randomness is genuine but the weighting
is not perfectly equal — certain faces land more often on
heavier bones.

**Cubic dice.** Six-sided, marked with drilled pips.
Made from bone, antler, or wood. Standard gambling
equipment. Every camp has at least one pair. The games
range from simple high-roll to complex betting games with
multiple throws and scoring rules.

**Cheating.** Loaded dice (weighted with lead on one side),
shaved dice (one face slightly flattened to favor its
opposite), and switching dice (palming a fair die and
replacing it with a rigged one). Detecting loaded dice:
drop them in water — a weighted die sinks with the heavy
side down consistently, while a fair die orients
randomly. A man caught cheating at dice faces beating,
theft charges, or worse — cheating is dishonor.

### Physical Contests

**Wrestling (glíma).** The traditional Norse wrestling
form. Opponents grip each other's belts and attempt to
throw the other to the ground. The feet must stay within
a marked area. Tripping is allowed. Strikes are not.
The match ends when one man's body (anything above the
knee) touches the ground. Glíma is practiced for fitness,
entertainment, and as a measure of physical worthiness.
A good glíma wrestler earns respect independent of his
weapon skills.

**Swimming.** A competitive sport as well as a survival
skill. Swimming contests in Norse culture included
distance swims, diving, and underwater wrestling (two
men grapple in deep water, each trying to hold the other
under). Swimming in cold Scandinavian water is an
endurance test as much as a physical one.

**Stone-lifting.** Heavy stones, usually at prominent
locations (farmsteads, harbors), are designated as lifting
stones. Men test their strength by lifting them to various
heights — waist, chest, shoulder, overhead. The weights
are known and the achievements remembered. A man who can
lift the heavy stone at a well-known farm has that fact
in his reputation for life.

**Archery.** Competitive shooting — distance, accuracy,
speed. Not the slow aim of a hunting stalk but rapid
shooting at a mark (a post, a shield, a bundle of straw).
Men wager on the outcome.

## Storytelling and Poetry

### The Skald's Role

Professional poets (skalds) compose and perform complex
verse — but every man knows enough poetry to quote it.
Verse is the culture's database. History, law, navigation
instructions, genealogy, and moral instruction are all
encoded in verse for memorization. A man who cannot recite
basic verses from the Edda and the sagas is culturally
illiterate.

**Kennings.** Poetic circumlocutions used in skaldic
verse: "wave-steed" for ship, "wound-sea" for blood,
"battle-sweat" for blood, "word-smith" for poet. Kennings
are not decoration — they are the convention. A skald
who uses plain words where a kenning is expected is
judged as incompetent.

**Performance.** Storytelling and verse recitation are
evening entertainment. A fire, a seated audience, and a
performer who tells a saga episode — complete with voices,
dramatic pauses, and audience interaction. The ability to
tell a story well is a social asset. A man who can hold
a fire-circle with a tale earns hospitality, favor, and
invitations.

### Work Songs

**Rowing chants.** Rhythmic songs that set and maintain
the rowing cadence. Simple, repetitive, with a strong
beat that matches the stroke cycle. The chanter leads;
the rowers answer or join on the chorus. The chant
eliminates the need for a drum and keeps twenty men
pulling in unison.

**Marching songs.** Less structured than rowing chants
but serving the same purpose — keeping a group moving at
a common pace and maintaining morale during tedious
travel. A good marching song shortens a long road (not
literally, but the perception of effort decreases when
the mind is engaged in rhythm and verse).

## Watch and Sentry Duty

### The Watch System

A band in hostile territory posts watch through the
night. The standard system:

**Shifts.** The night is divided into watches (typically
three or four shifts). Each watch is long enough to be
useful but short enough that the sentry stays alert.
Two hours is standard — after two hours of motionless
observation in the dark and cold, alertness degrades
sharply. A sentry who falls asleep on watch is a
catastrophic failure.

**Position.** The sentry positions himself where he can
see (or hear) the most likely approach routes. Not at
the fire (fire blinds night vision and silhouettes the
sentry). Not standing above the camp's skyline (visible
against the sky). In shadow, still, listening. The
sentry's eyes and ears are the band's early warning
system.

**Night vision.** Takes twenty to thirty minutes to
develop fully after leaving the fire. A sentry who
stares into the flames before his watch is blind for
the first half hour. Experienced men avoid looking at
the fire during the watch before their shift. When on
watch, the sentry looks to the side of objects rather
than directly at them — peripheral vision is more
sensitive to movement in low light than central vision.

**Challenges.** A man approaching the camp at night is
challenged by the sentry before being allowed to enter.
The challenge requires a pre-arranged response (a password,
a name, a phrase agreed before nightfall). A man who
does not give the correct response is not allowed in —
even if the sentry recognizes his voice. The discipline
of the challenge system protects against an enemy who
has captured a band member and sends him in first.

### Signals

**Horn calls.** Different calls mean different things:
one long blast for attention, two for assembly, three for
danger, a series of short blasts for attack. The specific
codes are agreed within the band and may change daily in
hostile territory to prevent the enemy from understanding
them.

**Fire signals.** A fire on a hilltop visible for miles.
One fire: "we are here." Two fires: "come to us." Three
fires: "danger." Fire-and-smoke signals (adding green
branches to produce smoke visible by day) extend the
range but are slow and imprecise.

**Runners.** The most reliable signal method over
distance. A fast man carries an exact message. The
downside: time (a runner covers three to five miles in
thirty minutes at speed, arriving exhausted) and risk
(a runner intercepted reveals the message to the enemy).
Important messages are sent with two runners on different
routes.

## Camp Life and Routine

### The Daily Rhythm

**Dawn.** The camp stirs with first light. The fire is
fed (if it was kept alive overnight) or restarted. Water
is fetched. Men urinate and defecate at the designated
latrine area (downhill and downwind of camp — see
sanitation). The cook starts the morning meal — typically
porridge (grain boiled in water) or yesterday's stew
reheated, with whatever bread or hard food is available.

**Morning.** Breaking camp (if moving) takes an hour for
an experienced band — shelters are struck, gear is packed,
the mule is loaded. In a fixed camp, the morning is work:
repairs, maintenance, foraging, training.

**Midday.** A brief meal if on the march (cold food, eaten
during a rest halt). In camp, the main meal may be at
midday — stew, meat if available, bread, and ale.

**Afternoon.** March or work continues. In camp, this is
the productive time — crafting, repairing, improving
defenses, hunting and trapping.

**Evening.** Camp is made (if on the move). The evening
meal is cooked. This is the social time — the fire is
the gathering point, stories are told, dice are thrown,
grievances are aired, and the captain briefs the band
on tomorrow's plans. Watch assignments are given.

**Night.** Men sleep. The watch keeps vigil. The cycle
repeats.

### Sleeping Arrangements

**Ground insulation.** A man sleeping on bare ground
loses heat through conduction — the ground draws warmth
from the body faster than the air. Insulation between
the sleeper and the ground is more important than
blankets above. A bed of spruce boughs (six inches thick),
dry grass, bracken, or a roll of furs solves this.

**Warmth sharing.** In cold conditions, men sleep close
together. Body heat is pooled. Two men under one cloak
are warmer than two men under two cloaks. This is not
intimacy — it is thermal survival.

**Bed-warming.** Stones heated in the fire (not river
stones — they can explode from trapped moisture) are
wrapped in cloth and placed in the sleeping area. A hot
stone retains useful heat for two to three hours. In
extreme cold, the process is repeated through the night
by the man on watch.

## Animal Husbandry

### What Every Man Knows About Livestock

A mercenary band does not herd cattle. But every man has
grown up around livestock and understands the basics.

**Cattle.** A cow produces milk (the dairy products —
butter, cheese, skyr — are staple foods), calves (the
herd grows), and eventually meat and leather. A small
farm's entire wealth might be measured in cattle. Knowing
a cow's age by its teeth, assessing health by coat and
gait, and understanding breeding cycles is basic farming
knowledge.

**Sheep.** Wool (the primary textile fiber — clothing,
blankets, sailcloth), milk (sheep cheese), and meat
(mutton). Icelandic and northern sheep are semi-feral —
rounded up seasonally from open grazing. Shearing is a
community event in late spring.

**Goats.** Milk (richer than cow's milk, tolerated by
some who cannot drink cow's milk), meat, and hides.
Goats are browsers, not grazers — they eat brush, bark,
and weeds that cattle and sheep ignore, making them
useful in rough terrain.

**Pigs.** Pork (the most calorie-dense common meat,
preserved beautifully by smoking and salting), lard
(cooking fat, leather conditioning, waterproofing), and
bristles (used for brushes). Pigs are efficient converters
of waste to food — they eat anything.

**Dogs.** Every farm has dogs. Guard dogs (alert to
intruders, including at night when the family sleeps),
herding dogs (essential for managing sheep and cattle on
open range), hunting dogs (finding, pursuing, and
retrieving game), and companionship (this is real and
acknowledged — a man's relationship with his dog is
practical and emotional).

## Burial Customs

### The Practical and the Sacred

When a man of the band dies, the body is dealt with.
The method depends on the situation:

**In camp (time available).**

- **Cremation.** The body is placed on a pyre (see
  digging section for fuel and time requirements). The
  dead man's personal items may be burned with him —
  a weapon, a personal tool, a drinking vessel. The
  idea: the man carries his tools into the afterlife.
  Valuable items (weapons, armor, silver) may or may
  not be burned — the captain decides, balancing honor
  against practical need. A dead man's excellent sword
  burned on his pyre is a waste that the living cannot
  afford. This tension is real and acknowledged.

- **Burial.** The body is placed in a grave (see digging
  section). Laid on its back or side, facing east or
  facing the direction of the homeland (practices
  vary). A stone marker or a mound of earth over the
  grave marks it. In Norse culture, the mound-burial
  is the prestige method — great men have ship-burials
  (the body placed in a ship or boat, surrounded by
  grave goods, the ship covered with a mound of earth).
  A mercenary in the field gets a simpler version.

**On the march (no time).**

- The body is cached if possible (covered with stones
  to protect from animals) for later recovery. If not
  possible, the body is left where it fell — the living
  cannot carry the dead at the cost of their own
  survival. The man is remembered by name at the
  next fire.

- Prayer or ritual words are spoken. The specifics vary
  — Christian, pagan, or an unsorted mixture of both
  that is common in the transitional period. The healer may
  recite. Viggo may speak a verse. The captain says
  the man's name and one thing true about him. This is
  enough.

## Religion and Omens

### Practical Piety

Religion in the north is not theology — it is practice.
Men sacrifice (small offerings — a portion of food, a
splash of ale poured on the ground) before risky
undertakings. Men wear symbols (Thor's hammer, a
Christian cross, sometimes both simultaneously). Men
invoke divine names in oaths ("by Odin's eye"), curses
("Hel take you"), and exclamations ("Thor!").

**What men believe.** The practical beliefs that affect
daily behavior:

- The gods favor the brave and punish the coward.
- An oath sworn on the gods is binding — breaking it
  brings divine punishment as well as social.
- The dead watch. Ancestors see what the living do.
  Shameful behavior dishonors not only the man but his
  lineage.
- Fate (wyrd) exists — each man has an appointed death.
  But within that fate, a man's choices determine whether
  he dies well or poorly. The moment is fixed; the manner
  is earned.

### Omens

**Raven behavior.** Ravens are Odin's birds. A raven
circling overhead before a battle is favorable (Odin
watches). Ravens avoiding an area may signal danger.
Two ravens (reflecting Odin's Huginn and Muninn, Thought
and Memory) are especially significant.

**Animal encounters.** A white animal (white horse, white
deer) is often divine or fey. Cross its path carefully.
A wolf seen before battle: ambiguous — wolves accompany
Odin but also presage death. An eagle overhead: favorable
(power, victory).

**Weather signs.** A red sky at evening is fair weather
coming. A red sky at morning is foul weather coming
(genuinely reliable — the rhyme exists because it works).
A ring around the moon: rain within a day. Swallows
flying low: rain coming (they follow insects, which fly
lower in falling barometric pressure — medieval men
observed the correlation without knowing the mechanism).

## Superstitions That Affect Behavior

### The Practical Ones

**Never whistle aboard a ship.** Whistling calls the wind
— which sounds superstitious, but on a ship that can be
swamped by a gale, any act believed to invite strong wind
is avoided. The enforcement: a man who whistles at sea is
told firmly to stop by every sailor within earshot.

**Never rename a ship.** The existing name holds the
vessel's luck. A renamed ship is unlucky. This is taken
seriously enough that ships are not renamed regardless of
new ownership.

**Never step over a sleeping man.** The sleeper's soul
may be wandering and the step may prevent its return.
Practically: a man who steps over sleepers in a dark
camp trips over them. The superstition prevents the
practical problem.

**Leave food for the landvættir.** The land spirits
(local supernatural entities associated with specific
features of the landscape — a particular hill, stream,
or rock formation) are appeased with small offerings.
A scrap of food placed at the base of an unusual rock
or at a crossroads. This costs almost nothing and is
observed widely. Not observing it makes the man who
doesn't an irritant to those who do.
