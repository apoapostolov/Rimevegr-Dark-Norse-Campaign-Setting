# Trades — Generic Skills (Muddle-Through)

**Category:** Generic Medieval
**Part of:** `medieval-authenticity-reference` skill

The things any competent person in a pre-industrial settlement could
attempt without formal training — badly, slowly, and with ugly results,
but well enough to survive. These are not trade skills. These are
desperation skills, camp skills, the knowledge that comes from watching
others work and having no choice but to try it yourself.

A man who patches a roof is not a thatcher. A man who whittles a peg
is not a carpenter. But when the rain comes through and the door hangs
crooked, the distinction matters less than the result.

## Rough Carpentry

**Any man with an axe and a knife can shape wood — after a fashion.**
The result will not be square. The joints will not be tight. The
surface will be rough and splintery. But a crude shelf holds jars,
a rough-hewn plank bridges a gap, and a whittled peg holds a broken
frame together until someone who knows what they are doing arrives.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Splitting logs.** Drive a wedge into the end grain and hammer it
through. Green wood splits easier than dry. Follow the grain — do
not fight it. The result is a rough plank, uneven in thickness,
with one flat face and one ragged face. Good enough for a bench
top, a door, a temporary bridge plank.

**Pegging joints.** Bore a hole with a knife-point or heated iron
rod. Whittle a peg from dry hardwood. Hammer it in. The peg
swells as it absorbs moisture and tightens. The joint is crude
but functional. This is how every field-repair works — broken
handles re-hafted, split frames pulled together, doors hung on
wooden pivot-pegs instead of iron hinges.

**Notch joints.** Cut a notch in one timber and set another into
it. A lap joint, a bird's-mouth, a simple saddle notch. The
axe does the work. The fit is loose. But gravity and weight hold
the joint where precision does not. Every camp shelter uses notch
joints. Every cattle pen. Every temporary bridge made of felled
trees.

**Rough shaping.** An axe takes a log from round to roughly flat.
A knife cleans up the surface. The result is not smooth — tool
marks are visible, the thickness varies, the edges are
uneven. For structural work that will not be seen, this is fine.
For anything that must fit against another surface, the gaps
show.

### What Goes Wrong

**Splitting across the grain.** The wood cracks the wrong way
and the workpiece is ruined. Green wood is more forgiving.
Dry oak and ash split unpredictably along knots and reaction
wood.

**Pegs that split the timber.** A hole too close to the edge,
or a peg hammered too hard, and the wood around the hole
cracks open. The joint is now weaker than before the repair.

**Joints that work loose.** Without a carpenter's understanding
of wood movement — how timber shrinks across the grain as it
dries, how the shoulder of a joint must seat tight — field
joints loosen within weeks. The shelf sags. The pen gate
drops. The bridge plank shifts underfoot.

**Timber selection.** The untrained man grabs what is closest.
The carpenter knows that green elm bends, dry oak holds, ash
takes shock, pine rots in the wet. The wrong wood in the wrong
place fails — sometimes dramatically, when a load-bearing
beam cracks in frost.

## Cart and Wagon Repair

**A broken cart on the road stops everything.** The cargo
sits in the mud, the ox stands idle, and every hour costs
daylight. Any man who has traveled with a baggage train has
been pressed into cart repair.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Lashing a cracked spoke.** Wrap wet rawhide or rope tight
around the crack. As rawhide dries, it shrinks and
compresses the wood. The spoke holds — for a while. Miles,
not months. Enough to reach the next settlement.

**Re-setting a wheel.** When the iron tyre works loose and
the wheel wobbles, soak the wheel in water. The wood
swells, tightens against the tyre. If there is no pond,
pour water over it repeatedly. This buys time. The proper
fix is the wheelwright's fire-shrink, which nobody attempts
without training unless they want to set the wheel on fire.

**Replacing a broken axle pin.** The linchpin holds the
wheel on the axle. A lost linchpin drops the wheel. Whittle
a replacement from the hardest green wood available — oak,
ash, hornbeam. Shape it thick enough to hold under the
sideways wobble of a loaded wheel. The real pin was iron.
The wooden one is temporary.

**Patching a cracked bed.** The cart bed — the flat platform
that holds cargo — cracks under overloading or rough
terrain. Lash a plank across the crack from underneath
with rope. Redistribute the load. It will creak and flex,
but it holds if the lashing is tight.

### What Goes Wrong

**Wheels that collapse.** A spoke repair holds for straight
road. The first rut or sharp turn puts lateral stress on
the patched spoke, and it snaps. The wheel folds, the
cart tips, and the cargo spills.

**Wrong wood for axle pins.** Soft wood (willow, poplar,
alder) crushes under load. The pin compresses, the wheel
wobbles loose, and you are back where you started — minus
a wheel that rolled into the ditch.

**Failing to check the other wheels.** The break that you
see is rarely the only damage. A hard jolt that cracked
one spoke has probably loosened two others and started a
split in the hub. The repaired wheel holds; the one on
the other side fails an hour later.

## Barrel and Bucket Patching

**Coopered vessels leak.** Wood dries, staves shrink, hoops
work loose, and the water (or ale, or salted fish) runs
out. A trained cooper builds a new barrel in a day. An
untrained man can sometimes stop the leak.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Soaking a dry barrel.** A barrel that has dried out has
gaps between the staves. Fill it with water and let it
sit. The wood swells, the gaps close, and the barrel
seals — sometimes. This only works if the hoops are
still tight and the staves have not warped beyond
recovery.

**Driving hoops tight.** The iron or willow hoops that
hold the staves together can be hammered downward
(toward the belly of the barrel) to tighten. Use a
blunt piece of wood as a drift and hammer it against
the hoop, working around the circumference evenly.
Uneven tightening cocks one stave inward and makes
the leak worse.

**Caulking joints.** Oakum (tarred fiber), pine pitch,
beeswax, or even tallow pressed into the seam between
staves can stop a small leak. The real cooper uses
rush pith and mallet to drive caulking into the
croze (the grove that holds the barrel head). The
field repair uses whatever is at hand, pushed in with
a knife blade.

**Patching a cracked stave.** A stave cracked along
the grain can be bound with wet rawhide or waxed
cord. If the crack is small and the barrel is not
under pressure, this holds. For a bucket, this is
often enough.

### What Goes Wrong

**Over-tightening hoops.** The stave cracks. Now you
have a broken stave instead of a loose hoop, and the
repair is beyond field work.

**Pitch that does not seal.** Pine pitch must be hot
and liquid when applied. Cold pitch sits on the surface
and peels off. But a man who has never heated pitch
underestimates how fast it goes from liquid to on-fire.
Burning pitch dripping into a barrel full of ale is
a memorable mistake.

**Warped staves.** A barrel that has sat empty and dry
for weeks warps. The staves cup, twist, and no amount
of soaking returns them to shape. The barrel becomes
a planter, a fire barrel, scrap wood. The only fix is
new staves — which is the cooper's job.

## Emergency Roof Patching

**A leaking roof is not a matter of comfort. It is a matter
of rot.** Water dripping onto stored grain destroys it.
Water dripping onto a sleeping man in winter can kill him
through hypothermia during the night. Roof repair is
urgent, and thatchers are not always available.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Layering fresh material over the leak.** If the existing
thatch is still largely intact, bundle straw, reeds,
bracken, or long grass into thick handfuls and push
them into the damaged area, working from the bottom of
the hole upward so each new layer overlaps the one below.
Water runs downhill. Overlap prevents it from running
inward.

**Tar or pitch coating.** For a flat or gentle roof
(boards, bark, turf), smear hot pitch or birch-tar
over the leaking area. The waterproof layer seals
the gap. On steeply pitched thatch this does not work
— the pitch runs off before it sets.

**Turf patching.** Cut a slab of turf (grass and roots
and soil together, about a hand-span thick) and lay
it root-side-up over the damaged area. Turf is
naturally waterproof when fresh and thick. Scandinavian
roofs are often entirely turf over birch bark. A
turf patch on a thatch roof is ugly but functional.

**Bark sheeting.** Large pieces of birch bark peeled
in spring (when the sap runs and the bark lifts freely)
can be laid over damage in overlapping layers like
scales. Weight them with stones or peg them to the
roof timbers. Bark rots within a season, but it
stops the rain now.

### What Goes Wrong

**Working from the top down.** The untrained man starts
patching at the top of the hole. Water follows the new
material downward and flows behind the existing thatch
below. The leak moves, or gets worse. Always patch from
the eave (bottom) upward.

**Insufficient overlap.** Thatch works by overlap —
each layer covering the one below by at least two-thirds.
The amateur lays material too thin, with too little
overlap. It looks covered. The first heavy rain proves
otherwise.

**Ignoring the structure.** The thatch rests on rafters
and laths. If the rafter is rotten or the lath has
snapped, piling new thatch on top hides the problem.
The next heavy snow load collapses the weakened
section — and now the hole is bigger.

**Falling off.** Roofs are steep and slippery,
especially when wet. Thatch provides poor footing.
A man unfamiliar with roof work puts his weight in the
wrong place — on the thatch between rafters instead
of on the rafters themselves — and goes through the
roof into the house.

## Wall Repair — Daub and Infill

**Wattle-and-daub is the simplest wall construction in
the north.** A framework of woven sticks (wattle) is
plastered over with a mixture of clay, straw, and dung
(daub). Almost anyone can patch a damaged section. Almost
no one does it well enough to last more than a season.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Mixing daub.** The recipe is forgiving. Clay-rich
subsoil, chopped straw (or grass, or animal hair), and
fresh dung (cow or horse). Mix by treading — stamp it
with bare feet in a shallow pit until it is smooth and
cohesive, with no dry lumps. The straw provides tensile
strength, the dung provides binding proteins and
slow-drying properties. The proportions do not need
to be exact. Too much straw makes it crumbly. Too little
makes it crack. Too much dung makes it stink for weeks.

**Patching a hole.** Wet the edges of the existing daub
around the hole. This helps the new daub bond to the old.
If the wattle underneath is intact, press new daub into
the framework, working from both sides simultaneously.
Build up thin layers — each about a thumb's thickness —
and let each layer firm up before adding the next.

**Replacing wattle.** If the woven sticks are broken,
cut flexible green wood (hazel, willow, young ash) and
weave it through the remaining uprights. The weave is
simple — over one upright, under the next. Pack tightly.
Then daub over it once the weave is complete.

**Limewash.** A coat of slaked lime (lime putty mixed
with water to the consistency of thin cream) over
finished daub provides a hard, waterproof, insect-
repelling surface. Anyone can slap on limewash with a
rag or brush. Getting an even coat is harder.

### What Goes Wrong

**Drying cracks.** The biggest problem. As daub dries,
it shrinks. Thick applications crack deeply — sometimes
all the way through the wall. Thin layers dry without
cracking but take many coats to build thickness. The
untrained man applies one thick coat and leaves. A week
later, the wall is cracked open.

**Poor bonding.** New daub on dry old daub does not stick.
It falls off in chunks once dry. The surface must be
dampened and roughened (scratch the old daub with a
knife or stone to create a key) before new material
goes on.

**Wrong soil.** Sandy soil does not bind. The daub
crumbles once dry. Clay is the essential ingredient.
The test: roll wet soil into a worm shape. If it holds
together and bends without cracking, it has enough clay.
If it falls apart, add more clay — or find a different
soil source.

**Rain before curing.** Fresh daub that gets rained on
before it firms up washes off the wall. The work is
wasted. Daub walls in the north are built (or repaired)
in dry weather, and sheltered until cured. A man who
patches a wall in autumn and does not shelter it has
donated his afternoon's labour to the gutter.

## Rough Masonry

**Even a man with no training can lay a crude wall.** Stack stones, jam
smaller stones into gaps, and pack mud or clay around the lot. The wall
stays up — for a season or two. A mason does the same work, but the wall
lasts two hundred years.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Gathering and breaking stone.** Find stones (from the ground, from a
riverbed, from the remains of an old wall) and shape them with a hammer
into roughly cuboid pieces. This is not precision work — it is hitting
rocks until they break the way you want. Heavy, destructive labor that
any man can perform.

**Laying a crude wall.** Stack the stones in rough courses (horizontal
layers). Fill the gaps with smaller stones. Pack the joints with mud
mortar (clay and straw). The wall is thick, the face is irregular, and
the inside is hollow — just rocks stacked with mud. It stands under its
own weight.

**Building with turf blocks.** In treeless areas, cut sods (grass and
roots and soil together, about a foot thick) and stack them like stone.
The grass prevents the block from disintegrating. Turf walls are soft,
not permanent, but they insulate and they are free.

**Pointing a wall.** After a wall has settled, rakes out the soft mortar
from the joints and pack new mortar (clay, mud, sometimes with lime if
available). The work is slow and rough, but anyone can do it.

### What Goes Wrong

**Walls that settle and shift.** Without a proper foundation (on
bedrock or on undisturbed soil), the wall settles unevenly. One section
sinks, the wall cracks and leans, and eventually a section fails
catastrophically.

**Frost damage.** Water in the mortar freezes and expands, breaking the
bond between stones. A wall repaired in autumn with fresh mortar often
has loose stones by spring.

**Rising damp.** Water wicks up from the ground into the wall. The
mortar dissolves, the stones shift, and the wall becomes unstable. Amateur
walls without raised foundations or lime mortar fail from the bottom up.

## Basic Brickmaking

**A brick is the same as stone — just shaped by human hands instead of
geology.** The basic process is forgiving: dig clay, mix it, form it,
dry it, fire it. Any man can make rough bricks that will not fall apart.
The specialist makes bricks that are uniform, durable, and strong.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Digging clay.** Find clay (usually in riverbanks or below the topsoil).
Dig it out. This is labor.

**Mixing clay.** Tread the clay with bare feet in a shallow pit, mixing
in straw or chaff for fibre. The straw prevents cracking as the brick
dries. Keep mixing until the clay is homogeneous.

**Forming rough bricks.** Pack the clay into a wooden frame (a mould)
and strike off the excess level with the top of the frame. Turn the frame
over and eject the brick onto the ground. The brick is crude — uneven
thickness, rough texture, but recognizably a brick.

**Stacking for drying.** Stand the bricks on edge, stacked in a loose
pattern that allows air to flow between them. Cover the top to prevent
rain from washing them apart. Wait weeks for them to dry.

**Firing in a clamp.** Stack the dried bricks into a large pile with
fuel (wood, coal, dried manure) interspersed through it. Set the fuel on
fire and let it burn through the stack. The heat bakes the bricks hard. It
is crude, unpredictable, and loses about half the bricks to warping or
underfire, but it works.

### What Goes Wrong

**Bricks that crumble.** Under-fired bricks (not hot enough during
firing) never fully harden. They crumble under load or dissolve in wet
weather.

**Warping.** Bricks fired unevenly expand unevenly and warp into
unusable shapes. A wall built from warped bricks has gaps that let water
through.

**Cracking in the kiln.** Bricks that dry too fast (exposed to sun and
wind) develop internal stress. When they are fired, they crack. The amateur
does not have the covered drying yard that prevents this.

## Lime Burning (Basic)

**The untrained man can heat limestone in a fire.** The limestone breaks
down into quicklime — caustic, hot, dangerous. The specialist controls
the burn, quenches the product, slakes it into putty, and stores it. The
amateur gets something vaguely lime-like and learns why limeburners are
scarred.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Building a clamp.** Stack limestone lumps into a dome or mound shape
with wood fuel underneath and between the stones. Leave air gaps so fire
can draw through it.

**Burning.** Light the fire and maintain it. The burn lasts from several
days to several weeks. The limestone in the flame zone melts and breaks
down. The smoke and heat are intense and the surface is untouchable.

**Rough quenching.** Once the lime stops changing color (bright white to
yellowish), douse it with water. The quicklime reacts violently with
water — steam, heat, chemical reaction. This is where most amateurs get
burned. The lime cools into soft lumps, some unburned limestone mixed in,
some reburned into brittleness.

**Spreading on fields.** Burnt lime spread on acidity soil neutralizes it.
Anyone can haul and scatter lime. The specialist knows pH and soil type.
The amateur throws lime and hopes.

### What Goes Wrong

**Under-burning.** The kiln temperature never gets high enough. The
limestone does not fully decompose. The product is weak, crumbly, less
caustic. The wall built with it fails to harden.

**Over-burning.** The heat is too intense and the quicklime begins to
reburn into a brittle, less reactive form. The product is useless.

**Uncontrolled quenching.** Dumping water on a hot lime clamp can cause
a violent explosion as steam escapes. The explosion throws hot lime and
stones in all directions. Limeburners lose eyes, hands, faces this way.

## Basic Quarrying

**Stone quarrying is heavy labor divided into two tasks: extracting the
stone and breaking it to size.** The specialist knows geology and has
tools. The amateur has a hammer and spite.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Finding stone that splits easily.** Some stone (slate, schist,
sedimentary layers) splits naturally along grain lines. Strike the right
spot and it splits clean. Other stone (granite, basalt) does not split —
it breaks unpredictably.

**Hitting rocks.** Strike a chisel or wedge driven into a crack and the
stone breaks. Heavy, repetitive work that any man can learn in minutes.
Results are crude — stones of irregular size and shape.

**Hauling.** Drag the broken stone to the site. This is labor, not skill.

**Piling for transport.** Stack the stones so they do not roll off the
cart. Anyone can do this.

### What Goes Wrong

**Bad stone selection.** Granite with internal flaws cracks unpredictably.
Sedimentary stone that looks good on top is rotten underneath.

**Breaking yourself.** A hammer strike that misses the intended target
hits your leg. Quarrying accidents require amputation or are fatal.

**Over-reaching and dropping stone.** A stone that weighs more than your
frame can handle, lifted too high, crushes the foot when it falls. The
specialist lifts with technique and leverage. The amateur uses brute force
and pays for it.

## Basic Well-Digging

**Digging a well is simple: dig down until water.** The specialist
understands depth, water tables, and structural integrity. The amateur
digs a hole that fills with water and crushes in.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Digging in soft soil.** With a spade and pick, a man can dig straight
down. The deeper he goes, the slower the work — and the more dangerous.

**Stoning a shaft.** Once the hole is deep enough to tap groundwater, line
it with stones to prevent cave-in. Throw stones into the hole so they pile
up around the inside, creating a crude wall. Mud and clay pack between the
stones.

**Building a windlass.** A simple mechanical lifter — a log lashed
horizontally above the hole, wrapped with rope, turned by two men to haul
water in a bucket. Any man can build this.

### What Goes Wrong

**The well collapses.** A shaft that is not properly shored caves in,
burying the digger. Wet soil is worst — it flows like mud once disturbed.

**Striking bedrock and stopping.** The digger hits impermeable rock and
stops digging. The hole is dry. The work was wasted.

**Tapping contaminated water.** The water is close to the surface and is
contaminated with latrine seepage or dead animals upstream. The well is
poisoned.

**Going blind from the water.** Some well water carries parasites that
infect the eyes. Over weeks, the person drinking it goes progressively
blind. The risk is unknown until months later.

## Basic Weaving

**Any person can tie threads together to make cloth.** The process is ancient
and simple: warp threads (the long threads stretched on a frame) and weft
threads (passed over and under in alternating pattern) lock together into
fabric. An amateur produces cloth. A specialist produces cloth that is even,
dense, and durable.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Setting up warp.** Tie thread to a frame (two stakes in the ground, two
sticks tied between them) and wrap the threads back and forth to create the
warp. Not perfectly spaced (distance varies between threads), but recognizable
as a loom.

**Weaving simple cloth.** Pass the weft (held in a shuttle, a simple wooden
bar, or just the ball) over and under the warp threads. Return with the
opposite pattern (under, over). Keep weaving. The threads lock together slowly
and form cloth — rough, inconsistent, but cloth.

**Finishing cloth edges.** Tie off the warp threads at the end so they do not
fray. Wash the cloth (in water and possibly with ash-lye) to remove dirt and
the natural oils and sizing that holds the threads stiff.

**Mending cloth.** Patch a torn area with careful hand-sewing (threading a
needle, pulling stitches tight) or weaving new thread through the damaged area
to recreate the weave pattern. Invisible mending requires skill. Visible mending
anyone can do.

### What Goes Wrong

**Uneven weave tension.** The threads are pulled too tight on some shots (passes
of the weft) and too loose on others. The cloth has dense and loose spots. Once
wet, the loose spots stretch and the densespots do not — the cloth warps.

**Dropped stitches or broken threads.** A poorly maintained warp breaks. The
weaver does not notice quickly enough and weaves several inches without tension
on one thread. Patching is visible and weak.

**Shrinking in the wash.** Undyed cloth shrinks 10-20% when washed for the first
time. Cloth that seemed the right size before washing fits a child after. Knowing
to account for this comes from experience.

## Basic Fulling

**Fulling is the finishing of cloth after weaving.** The raw cloth from the
loom is loose and rough. Fuller's work — washing, beating, and sometimes
carding — compresses the fibers and creates a surface that is felted, smooth,
and weather-resistant.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Soaking cloth.** Submerge the cloth in hot water (heated over a fire) with
ash-lye (wood ash mixed with water, caustic and degreasing) or with stale
urine (ammonia is the active ingredient). Soak for hours.

**Beating fibers together.** Put the cloth on a flat surface (a smooth stone,
a wooden table) and beat it with a wooden mallet or by hand, folding and
beating repeatedly. This compresses the fibers against each other. The cloth
becomes denser.

**Rinsing and wringing.** Wash out the lye or urine-smell. Wring the cloth to
remove excess water. Hang to dry.

**Walking cloth (treading).** In later medieval practice, cloth is placed in a
trough and walked on (treaded, as if trampling grapes) for hours. The friction
and pressure full the cloth more aggressively than hand-beating.

### What Goes Wrong

**Over-fulling.** The fibers compress so much that the cloth becomes stiff and
board-like. Continue further and the cloth felts so much that the original weave
disappears entirely — now it is felt, not cloth.

**Uneven fulling.** The thicker areas of cloth take longer to full. If not
monitored, some parts are over-fulled and others are under-fulled. The cloth is
patchy and ugly.

**Shrinking dramatically.** An under-fulled cloth shrinks 20-30% during fulling.
The specialist accounts for this — cuts the cloth oversized. The amateur fails to
recover 30% of the cloth's value to shrinkage.

## Basic Dyeing

**Cloth is dyed after fulling.** The untrained person throws cloth into a pot
with a source of color and boils it. The color often washes out or fades. The
specialist uses mordants and fixatives to make the color last.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Gathering dye sources.** Madder root (red), woad (blue), weld (yellow), walnut
hulls (brown), oak gall (black/brown). Find the plants or purchase them. Dry or
process them into usable form.

**Dyeing cloth.** Place the cloth in water, add the dye source, heat it. Boil
for hours. Remove the cloth, rinse, and lay to dry. The cloth has acquired color.

**Rinsingand drying.** Multiple rinses remove excess dye and reduce (but do not
prevent) subsequent color loss from wear or washing.

### What Goes Wrong

**Color that fades.** Without a mordant (a chemical that bonds the dye to the
fiber), the color slowly washes out or fades in sunlight. A year of wearing and
the color is half gone.

**Uneven dye.** The cloth that was at the bottom of the pot for the first hour
is a different shade than the cloth on top during the last hour. Layering,
temperature changes, and circulation all affect color uniformity.

**Color that stinks.** Some dyes (especially urine-based processes for some colors)
require urine or stale fish as a mordant. The cloth stinks until it has been worn
and washed many times. The specialist knows which colors are worth wearing the
stink for and which are not.

## Basic Tailoring

**The tailor cuts cloth and joins it into fitted garments.** A man can cut cloth
with a knife and sew it with a needle — but the garment will be loose and wrong.
The tailor fits the garment to the body.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Sewing straight seams.** A needle threaded with a long strand of thread,
stitches right through both pieces of cloth, regular passes at regular spacing.
The seam holds the two pieces together.

**Hemming edges.** Fold the edge of the cloth double and sew the fold down. This
prevents fraying. Anyone who has sewn can do this.

**Attaching fasteners.** Sew buttons through the cloth with a needle and thread,
winding the thread around the button shank several times to create tension. Sew
on a tab of leather as a grip point. Basic labor that does not require skill.

**Simple garment assembly.** Cut cloth into a rough shape, sew the pieces
together, attach sleeves (if any), hem, add fasteners. The result is a garment
that covers the body and stays together.

### What Goes Wrong

**Seams that stress and tear.** A seam that is stitched once is stronger than no
seam, but weak. Stress points (underarm, crotch, collar) tear if the seam is
only single-stitched.

**Garments that hang wrong.** Without fitting the body, the garment binds at
the shoulder, gaps at the chest, hangs off-center. The wearer is uncomfortable
and looks poor.

**Poor edge finishing.** A garment with raw (unseamed) edges and loose thread
looks makeshift. The edges fray in the wash.

## Basic Sail-Making

**A sail is cloth stitched to be strong — under extreme stress from wind.** The
untrained person cuts square cloth and sews it with regular seams. The sail rips
the first time the wind hits it hard. The sailmaker's cloth is reinforced and
stitched to withstand the strain.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Cutting cloth to shape.** Measure and cut the sail to the desired size and
shape. Some curves and angles, but essentially cutting rectangles from a bolt.

**Sewing seams.** Stitch pieces of sailcloth together end-to-end to create a
larger surface. Regular stitches in straight lines. The seam holds under normal
tension.

**Adding grommets.** Tie rope through holes in the sail's edges to attach it to
the boom and yard (the wooden spars that control the sail). Anyone can punch a
hole and tie a rope.

### What Goes Wrong

**Seams that tear under load.** Sailcloth is under enormous tension. A single
line of stitches is insufficient. The stitch spacing is too wide and fibers pull
through the stitches.

**Patching that does not hold.** A ripped sail is patched with a square of
cloth stitched over the hole. If the patch is stitched only around its edges,
the patch pulls away from the edges under load. The rip opens again.

**Rope attachments that pull out.** Grommets and rope eyes must be backed with
multiple layers of cloth and heavily stitched. A single layer stitched at one
point fails under load. The sail detaches from the boom mid-sail.

## Basic Metalworking

**Every man knows that fire softens metal.** Heat iron enough and it bends. The
specialist knows the exact colors, temperatures, and timing that control the
metal's behavior — hardness, flexibility, brittleness. The amateur heats metal
until it is soft and beats it.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Using existing metalwork.** A broken tool can be straightened by heating and
bending. A bent nail can be straightened. A simple repair can be done by anyone
who has watched it done.

**Riveting.** Joining two pieces of metal (or metal to other material) with a
rivet — a short rod hammered flat on both ends. The technique is basic: align
holes, insert the rivet, hammer the ends. This is done everywhere -— repairs to
armor, shields, tools, equipment.

**Shaping simple pieces.** Flatten a lump of metal with repeated hammer blows.
Bend a rod by heating and hammering. The results are crude but functional.

**forge fire management.** Keeping a fire hot and controllable is partly craft
and partly brute force. The amateur blows air (with a bellows) to intensify the
heat and manages the fuel.

### What Goes Wrong

**Cracking under load.** Metal cooled too quickly after forging is brittle. It
cracks under stress instead of bending. The amateur does not understand tempering
(slow cooling) or does not have patience for it.

**Tool failure.** An axe head that was hammered wrong cracks and splits. The wood
handle splinters and the tool becomes dangerous. A shovel that was bent instead
of properly forged snaps under weight.

**Rust.** Iron oxidizes in water and wet air. The amateur leaves metal wet and
it rusts. Prevention (keeping it dry, oiling it) is simple but must be done.

## Basic Hide Preparation

**Any man can scrape a skin, but the result will be stiff and brittle.**
Soak the hide in water, scrape off hair and fat with a stone or bone scraper,
and hang it in the sun. The hide dries into something vaguely leather-like —
useful for water bags or drum heads, but prone to cracking and rotting.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Scraping.** Use a smooth stone or bone edge to push away hair and fat. The
work is repetitive and physical. Scrape until the skin is bare.

**Soaking and drying.** Soak hides in water or urine (which softens them).
Hang them in the sun. The smell is terrible but unavoidable.

**Basic leather smell and decay prevention.** Hang leather away from damp.
Oil it occasionally to keep it supple. Wrap it to keep vermin and insects
out of it.

### What Goes Wrong

**Hide rots.** Leather stored in damp rots, becoming slick and useless.
The rot spreads to anything touching it.

**Cracking under stress.** Leather that was dried too quickly becomes
brittle. The edges crack. Folds split.

**Stiffness.** Leather that was not worked (stretched and bent) while drying
becomes inflexible — hard, cracked, and painful to use.

## Basic Saddlemaking and Harness

**A crude saddle can be lashed together from wood and leather scraps.**
Strap a wooden frame to a horse with leather straps and cord. The result will
slip, chafe the horse, and come loose if the horse bucks, but for short trips
on calm animals it holds.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Lashing leather to wood.** Poke holes in leather, thread cord or thin
strips of leather through, and tie tight. Soak leather first to make it
easier to work.

**Adjusting straps.** Loosen or tighten straps by moving the attachment
point or adding extra knots. A saddle that fits a calm horse may need
adjustment for a new horse.

**Basic saddle sore prevention.** Check the horse beneath the saddle.
If the hair is rubbed raw, something is wrong — the saddle is too tight,
too loose, or sitting wrong.

### What Goes Wrong

**Saddle slipping under load.** A loosely-lashed saddle rotates under the
rider's weight. The rider ends up on the ground.

**Saddle sores.** A saddle that sits wrong or has rough edges rubs the
horse raw. An infected sore can make the horse permanently unusable.

**Leather straps breaking.** Leather that is thin or damaged breaks under
the strain of holding a rider. The saddle comes apart mid-march.

## Basic Leatherworking — Patches and Repairs

**Any man can stitch leather with an awl and cord.** The result will not be
flat. The seams will leak. But it holds together long enough to matter.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Poking holes.** Use an awl (a pointed tool) to punch holes through leather
for stitching. Wet the leather first to make it easier to work.

**Stitching.** Thread cord (sinew, plant fiber, or thin strips of leather)
through holes and tie knots to hold it. Over-under-over-under, tight enough
to hold but not so tight the leather tears.

**Sealing seams against water.** Rub oil or fat into the stitched seam.
This does not make it waterproof, but it slows water infiltration.

### What Goes Wrong

**Seams split under stress.** Your stitching does not hold. The leather
tears or the cord breaks.

**Water leaks through seams.** Oil slows it but does not stop it. Leather
goods will leak in heavy rain.

**Holes punched too close together.** The leather between holes tears away.
The whole seam comes apart.

## Basic Shoemaking

**Any man can wrap leather around a foot and tie it on.** The result will be
uncomfortable, will rub blisters, and will fall apart after a week of hard
marching. But it is better than barefoot.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Wrapping leather around a foot to fit.** Measure by eye and instep. Wrap
leather strips around the foot and tie them tight enough to hold but loose
enough to walk.

**Stitching sole to upper.** Poke holes around the edge where sole meets upper.
Stitch with sinew or cord. The seam will not be watertight but it holds together.

**Basic sole reinforcement.** Glue or stitch a wooden heel peg to the bottom
sole. This gives the shoe a bit of extra life.

### What Goes Wrong

**Blisters and raw feet.** Shoes that do not fit cause blisters. Blisters
become infected. An infected foot can slow a man to a crawl or stop him entirely.

**Sole separates.** Your stitching fails or the leather rots. The sole flaps
loose on your foot.

**Shoes fall apart.** After a week of hard wear, the seams give out and the
whole shoe disintegrates.

## Basic Belt and Strap Work

**Any man can strap a buckle to a piece of leather.** The result might last
a season if the leather does not rot and the buckle does not break off.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Punching attachment holes.** Use an awl to punch holes where the buckle or
strap will attach.

**Stitching buckle or attachment.** Stitch the buckle to the belt with sinew
or cord. Wrap the stitching around several times to spread the load.

**Adjusting for fit.** Punch new holes along the length of the belt. Adjust
the buckle position to your waist size.

### What Goes Wrong

**Buckle pulls free.** Your stitching was not strong enough or you did not
have enough attachment points. The buckle comes off at an inconvenient moment.

**Strap tears under load.** Leather that is thin or brittle tears when you
carry weight on it.

**Holes enlarge under stress.** Stitching through weak leather tears enlarged
holes. The buckle eventually pulls free again.

## Basic Pottery and Clay Work

**Any man can dig clay, shape it, and bake it.** The result will not be round.
It will crack. It might contain rocks that will explode when heated. But a crude pot
holds water (sort of) and a crude roof tile covers a gap (barely).

### What Anyone Can Do

**Finding and preparing clay.** Clay is found in river banks and low places.
Dig it, break it apart, mix it with water, and knead it like bread. Bits of
stone will be in it.

**Shaping by hand.** Squeeze and shape clay into a rough bowl or brick shape.
The result will be lumpy and asymmetrical, but it holds together while drying.

**Building a firing pit.** Stack wood, place clay objects on top, and light the
fire. Heat the clay until it hardens. The result is fragile pottery.

**Understanding basic drying and firing.** Clay must dry before firing (too fast
and it cracks). It must be fired hot enough to harden but not so hot it warps.
The amateur learns these lessons through trial and breakage.

### What Goes Wrong

**Cracking during drying.** Clay dries unevenly. Thick areas crack as they dry
faster than the interior.

**Shattering during firing.** Water trapped in the clay turns to steam and explodes.
Or the clay was not fully hardened before firing.

**Pots that do not hold water.** Porous clay allows water to leak through. Some
sealing is necessary.

**Fragility.** Pottery that was not fired hot enough is fragile and breaks easily.

## Basic Charcoal Making

**Any man can burn wood in a controlled way and collect the charcoal.** The result
is crude and impure, but it burns and produces intense heat.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Stacking wood for burning.** Cut wood into pieces, stand them in a pile or in a
basic pit or oven structure.

**Controlling oxygen.** Partially cover the wood so it burns slowly instead of
flaming. Too much oxygen and you get ash, not charcoal. Too little and it goes out.

**Harvesting charcoal.** Let the fire burn down. Cool the charcoal. Collect the
chunks.

**Using charcoal.** Charcoal burns hotter and longer than wood. The amateur learns
this and builds fires accordingly.

### What Goes Wrong

**Complete consumption to ash.** Burning too hot leaves only ash.

**Incomplete burning.** The wood only partially converts to charcoal and is still
essentially wood.

**Explosion and fire loss.** If trapped moisture or wood orientation changes oxygen
flow, the charcoal catches fire and is consumed.

**Dust and fines.** Most of your product is fragmented charcoal dust, not large
usable pieces.

## Basic Candle and Tallow Work

**Any man who kills an animal can save the tallow (fat) and render it (melt it
down) into candles or lamp fuel.** The result smells terrible and produces thin
light, but it is better than darkness.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Collecting fat.** Save animal fat from kills and butchering.

**Rendering tallow.** Melt the fat over heat, break up solid bits, and strain
liquid tallow into a container.

**Dipping candles.** Tie string to a stick, dip the string repeatedly in melted
tallow, let it cool between dips. Build up layers of tallow around the string core.

**Using tallow.** Tallow burns in open lamps or in candles. It smells. It produces
dim light.

### What Goes Wrong

**Rancid tallow.** Fat that was not properly saved or stored goes rancid and
produces evil smoke and smell.

**Candles that will not stay lit.** Improperly-dipped candles have an inconsistent
wick and burn unevenly.

**Candles that burn down too fast.** Thin tallow builds yield candles that burn
fast and produce little light per candle.

**Grease and stain.** Tallow is oily and stains everything.

## Basic Soap Making

**Traditional soap is made from lye (wood ash filtered with water) and fat (animal
or vegetable).** The mixture is boiled until the fat reacts with the lye and turns
into soap. The amateur's result is harsh and crude but it cleans.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Making lye from wood ash.** Collect wood ash. Add water. The water dissolves
lye from the ash. Strain off the liquid — this is lye water.

**Collecting and rendering fat.** Save animal fat or use plant oils.

**Boiling lye and fat together.** Heat the mixture for hours. Watch for the moment
when the soap "traces" — thickens and becomes opaque. Pour into molds.

**Basic soap use.** Soap cleans skin and clothing. The amateur's soap is harsh
and drying, but it works.

### What Goes Wrong

**Lye that is too weak.** Weak lye does not convert fat to soap. You get a greasy
mess.

**Soap that does not trace.** Improper temperature or ratio means the reaction never
happens. The mixture stays liquid or separates.

**Soap that is caustic (chemically dangerous).** Leftover lye in the soap burns skin.
The amateur does not know how to test for this.

**Soap that dissolves in use.** Improperly-made soap is soft and dissolves in the
first week.

## Basic Salt Making

**Salt can be collected by boiling down saltwater (from ocean or from salt springs).**
The work is repetitive and the fuel cost is high, but the result is essential.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Collecting saltwater.** From the ocean (evaporate seawater) or from salt springs
(water that naturally contains dissolved salt).

**Building evaporation ponds.** Dig shallow ponds. Direct tidal flow or spring flow
into the ponds. Let the sun evaporate the water.

**Boiling down saltwater.** Heat saltwater in large pans. As the water evaporates,
salt crystals remain. Continue until all water is gone.

**Harvesting salt.** Scrape up the salt crystals and pile them for storage.

### What Goes Wrong

**Slow evaporation.** In cool climates, sun evaporation is slow or impossible.
Boiling becomes necessary, which consumes huge quantities of fuel.

**Marine organisms in the salt.** Pond evaporation collects seaweed, fish, and
other contamination.

**Mineral contamination.** Saltwater contains other dissolved minerals that
crystallize along with the salt, making the product bitter or granular.

**Fuel cost.** Boiling large quantities of water to extract salt consumes massive
amounts of wood.

## Basic Grain Milling

**Any man can pound grain between two rocks to crush it into flour or meal.**
The result will be coarse and inconsistent, but it is food.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Crushing grain by pestle and mortar.** Pound whole grain in a mortar with a
pestle. The result is a mixture of whole grains, crushed fragments, and flour.

**Hand-grinding with stones.** Place grain between a flat stone and a grinding
stone. Grind back and forth. This produces flour, but it is slow.

**Understanding basic sifting.** Some of the crushed material is flour, some is
larger fragments. Shake it through cloth to separate coarse and fine.

**Drying ground grain.** Moisture in flour causes spoilage. Dry the flour by
spreading it and letting sun and air do the work.

### What Goes Wrong

**Inconsistent grind.** Some grain stays whole, some turns to fine powder.

**Contamination with stone.** Grinding stone against stone produces fragments
of stone mixed with the flour.

**Spoilage of damp flour.** Flour that retains moisture spoils within days.

**Huge time investment.** Hand-grinding large quantities of grain is backbreaking
and takes days.

## Basic Baking

**Any man can mix grain, water, and salt, and bake it in an oven.** The result
will be dense and poorly-risen, but it is bread.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Mixing dough.** Flour, water, salt, and (if available) yeast or sourdough starter.
Mix until combined.

**Shaping.** Form the dough into loaves or flat cakes.

**Baking.** Put the dough in a hot oven (wood-fired, by fire, or in hot stones).
Heat until the outside hardens and the inside is no longer raw.

**Understanding yeast.** Some doughs rise (air bubbles forming). Some do not.
The amateur does not understand why.

### What Goes Wrong

**Dense, hard bread.** Bread that did not rise is dense and difficult to eat.

**Over-baking.** Bread that stayed in the oven too long is hard and burnt outside,
sometimes raw inside.

**Under-baking.** Bread that was not hot enough is damp and will go moldy quickly.

**Spoilage.** Bread that was not dried enough after baking stays soft and spoils
in days.

## Basic Butchering

**Any man who kills an animal must break it down into usable pieces and
salvage as much as possible.** The work is bloody and the skill is crude, but
the result is meat.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Breaking down the carcass.** Use knives and saws to separate legs, remove meat
from bones. You will waste meat.

**Understanding muscle and joint structure.** Following the natural seams is easier
than cutting across them.

**Preserving meat by smoke.** Hang meat in smoke to dry it. Smoke preserves meat
for weeks or months.

**Preserving meat by salt.** Rub salt into meat and pile it. Salt draws out moisture
and preserves the meat.

### What Goes Wrong

**Wasted meat.** Cuts that do not follow muscle and joint structure tear and waste
good meat.

**Spoilage.** Meat that was not properly salted or smoked spoils quickly.

**Contamination.** Meat that was near internal organs can be contaminated by
spilled contents.

**Bone and organ waste.** The amateur throws away bones, organs, and scrap that
could have been used for broth, lard, or tools.

## Basic Brewing

**Any man can ferment grain and water into a drinkable (barely) beverage.**
The result will be sour and weak, but alcohol prevents most spoilage and infection.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Grain preparation and soaking.** Soak grain in water to begin the process.

**Basic fermentation knowledge.** A living process happens — bubbles, temperature
changes. The amateur does not understand why.

**Understanding temperature.** Fermentation happens at certain temperatures. Too cold
and it stops. Too hot and it produces bad flavors or spoils.

**Separating liquid from solids.** After fermentation, tip the liquid away from
the grain solids (this is straining).

**Drinking and hoping.** If the drink has alcohol and does not taste like poison,
it is probably safe to drink.

### What Goes Wrong

**Spoilage instead of fermentation.** The mixture goes rotten instead of fermenting.

**Weak or non-alcoholic result.** The process did not work and the result is not
safely preserved.

**Sour and undrinkable.** The result is so sour or has bad flavors that it is barely
drinkable.

**Contaminated batches.** One bad batch ruins everything else if storage containers
are cross-contaminated.

## Basic Beekeeping

**Any man can offer bees shelter, leave them alone, and (if lucky) harvest honey.**
The work is minimal; the bees do most of the work.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Building hives.** Stack straw, wood, or wattle to create a shelter for bees.
Bees will move in or can be moved in.

**Leaving bees alone.** The primary skill is not interfering. Bees will produce
honey and reproduce if left undisturbed.

**Smoke to manage bees.** Smoke calms bees. The amateur uses smoke to get close
without being stung too much.

**Harvesting comb.** When honey is visible in the hive, break off honeycomb and
strain honey out. You will crush some bees and get stung.

**Understanding seasons.** Bees produce honey in season. Outside of season, they
produce bee bread and survive on stores.

### What Goes Wrong

**Angry bees.** Bees that are mismanaged or handled roughly attack with intensity.

**Destruction of the hive.** Poor harvesting can destroy the hive structure or
damage the queen, causing the bees to abandon the hive.

**Low production.** Hives that are crowded or unhealthy produce little honey.

**Winter loss.** Hives without adequate food stores die in winter.

## Basic Fishing

**Any man with patience and basic materials can catch fish.** The result is unreliable
and inefficient, but fish is food.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Hook and line.** Tie a hook to a cord. Cast into water. Wait. If fish bite the hook,
pull hard.

**Understanding bait.** Worms, insects, small fish — fish eat them. The amateur knows
fish must be attracted.

**Basic net casting.** Throw a weighted net into shallow water. Pull it closed. Collect
whatever is trapped. Most of your effort is wasted casting.

**Weir and trap construction.** Stack rocks or branches to direct fish into narrow spaces.
Most fish escape.

### What Goes Wrong

**No fish despite effort.** The amateur wastes hours catching nothing because he doesn't
know where fish feed or what attracts them.

**Lines breaking.** Cord that is too thin breaks when fish pull. Knots that are poorly
tied fail.

**Nets with holes.** Nets that are crudely made have gaps. Fish escape through them.

**Spoilage of catch.** Fish must be eaten or preserved quickly or spoilage ruins them.

## Basic Net-Making

**Any man can tie knots in cord or twine to build a net.** The result will have irregular
mesh and gaps, but it is a net.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Tying mesh knots.** Create a repeating knot that holds one loop of cord to another. Repeat
many times. Eventually you have a net.

**Understanding cord thickness.** Thick cord creates large mesh (good for large fish). Thin
cord creates small mesh (catches more but is slower to make).

**Basic net shape.** Nets are roughly rectangular or bag-shaped. The amateur ties knots at
random and hopes the shape holds.

### What Goes Wrong

**Irregular mesh.** Knots tied inconsistently create nets with some large holes and some
small ones.

**Asymmetrical shape.** Nets that are crudely made are lopsided and tangle easily.

**Knots untying.** Poorly-tied knots slip apart during use.

**Weak net structure.** Nets that are crudely made tear easily or lose shape.

## Basic Rope-Making

**Any man can twist cord together to make rope.** The result will be weak and uneven,
but it is rope.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Twisting fibers into cord.** Take plant fibers (flax, hemp, straw) or hair and twist them
together. The twist creates strength. Repeat with multiple twisted strands for thicker rope.

**Understanding twist direction.** Twist in one direction, then twist multiple strands
in the opposite direction. This creates rope that does not unwind.

**Basic rope thickness.** More strands and more material create thicker rope. The amateur
guesses at adequate thickness.

### What Goes Wrong

**Weak rope.** Rope that is poorly twisted breaks under load.

**Uneven rope.** Inconsistent twisting creates rope with thin spots that break first.

**Rope unwinding.** If the final twist is wrong, rope unravels.

**Poor fiber quality.** Wet or damaged fibers create weak rope.

## Basic Boat Construction

**Any man can lash logs or planks together to create a floating vessel.** The result
will leak, be slow, and dangerous, but it floats.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Selecting logs.** Find logs that are straight and relatively dry. Tie them together with
rope. The result floats.

**Building a crude frame.** Tie additional logs cross-wise to create a frame that holds
the floating logs together.

**Adding a shelter.** Lash branches or cloth over the frame for protection from weather.

**Understanding water entry.** All crude boats leak. Bailing water is continuous work.

### What Goes Wrong

**Boats that sink.** Boats loaded too heavily or with insufficient buoyancy sink.

**Boats that split.** Logs that crack or rope that breaks can cause sudden failure.

**Boats that are slow.** Crude boats have no shape for speed and fight the water.

**Boats that capsize.** Boats with poor balance or narrow beam tip over easily in waves.

## Basic Caulking

**Any man can jam material into cracks to stop leaks.** The result is temporary and
will fail, but it slows the leak.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Selecting caulk material.** Straw, cloth, moss, anything that can be crammed into gaps.

**Ramming material into cracks.** Tightly pack material into the gap between planks or
joints.

**Sealing with pitch or grease.** If available, seal the material with sticky substance to
stop water.

**Maintaining the seal.** As the boat works, caulk loosens. Constant maintenance required.

### What Goes Wrong

**Caulk that fails.** Material that is not tightly packed or is damaged by water pressure
falls out.

**Leaks persist.** Caulking slows leaks but does not stop them. Water always finds a way through.

**Poor seal material.** Pitch or grease that is wrong quality or not properly applied fails.

**Structural damage underneath.** If the plank is cracked or rotted, caulking the surface
does not fix the problem.

## Basic Jewelry Work

**Any man can hammer and shape metal into basic ornaments.** The result will be crude and
poorly finished, but it is ornament.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Hammering metal flat.** Take a piece of metal and hammer it against a hard surface. The
metal flattens and can be shaped with repeated blows.

**Basic bending and shaping.** Hot metal is soft. Hammer it into rough shapes — loops, bands,
basic forms.

**Drilling holes.** Use a pointed tool and repeated strikes to bore a hole through metal.

**Attaching pieces.** Solder (if available) or rivets can attach parts together. The amateur
tries to make it hold.

### What Goes Wrong

**Metal that is too brittle to shape.** Cold metal cracks instead of bending. The amateur
doesn't know to heat it.

**Weak attachments.** Solder that is poorly applied or rivets that are driven wrong fail when
the ornament is worn.

**Uneven surfaces.** Crude hammering leaves the surface irregular and dented.

**Poor design.** The amateur creates ornaments that are thick, clumsy, and visibly amateurish.

## Basic Glass-Bead Making

**Any man can heat glass in fire and shape it before it cools.** The result will be crude
beads, but they are beads.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Selecting glass.** Scrap glass from vessels or broken items can be melted and reformed.

**Heating glass in fire.** Glass must be very hot to shape. Hold it in fire until it softens.

**Shaping on a rod.** Wind soft glass around a rod and roll it in your hands to make a crude
bead shape.

**Cooling.** Allow the glass to cool slowly or it will crack.

### What Goes Wrong

**Glass that cracks during cooling.** Cooling too fast causes cracking that ruins the bead.

**Glass that is not hot enough.** Glass that is not soft enough cracks when shaped.

**Beads that are lopsided.** Crude shaping creates uneven, poorly-proportioned beads.

**Colors that bled or changed.** Glass colors can change if improperly heated or mixed.

## Basic Bone-Carving

**Any man can shape bone with knife and scraper into simple objects.** The result will be
crude and poorly-finished, but it is carved bone.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Selecting bone.** Animal bone (from meals, hunting) can be carved. Thicker bones are
easier than thin ones.

**Roughing out shapes.** Use a knife to chip away and shape the bone into rough forms.

**Scraping and smoothing.** Scrape the surface with a sharp tool to create a smoother finish.

**Drilling holes.** Use a pointed tool and fire-hardening to create holes for attachment.

### What Goes Wrong

**Bone that breaks during carving.** Brittle areas or carving against the grain cause breaks.

**Uneven surfaces.** Crude carving leaves deep tool marks and irregular surfaces.

**Poor proportions.** The amateur creates items that are lopsided or visibly amateurish.

**Structural weakness.** Items carved too thin or with thin connecting parts break during use.

## Basic Antler-Working

**Any man can shape antler (shed or hunted) into basic implements and ornaments.** The result
will be crude but functional.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Selecting antler.** Shed antler or antler from killed game can be worked. Some areas of
antler are softer than others.

**Sawing and splitting.** Use a saw or repeated knife blows to divide antler into workable
pieces.

**Basic shaping.** Carve the antler into rough shapes for buttons, points, handles, or
decorative objects.

**Drilling holes.** Create attachment points by drilling or abrading holes.

### What Goes Wrong

**Antler that splits unexpectedly.** Antler is dense and can split along grain lines that
are not visible.

**Crude finishing.** Amateur carving leaves visible tool marks and irregular surfaces.

**Poor structural design.** Thin connecting parts break during use.

**Discoloration and rot.** Antler that is not properly prepared deteriorates over time.

## Basic Comb-Making

**Any man can carve a comb from bone or antler.** The result will work, though poorly.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Selecting material.** Bone or antler thick enough to support teeth is needed.

**Carving the body.** Shape the basic comb form with knife and scraper.

**Cutting teeth.** Use a thin blade or fine saw to cut narrow slots for teeth. Alternatively,
drill and split or carve individual tooth shapes.

**Smoothing.** Scrape the surface until it is reasonably smooth.

### What Goes Wrong

**Teeth that are uneven.** Amateur cutting creates teeth of different sizes and spacing.

**Teeth that are too weak.** Teeth carved too thin break during use.

**Uneven handle.** The handle is uncomfortable or poorly finished.

**Comb that breaks during use.** Structural weakness causes sudden failure.

## Basic Healing

**Any man with knowledge of plants and a willingness to try can attempt to heal the sick
or hurt.** The results are inconsistent and sometimes deadly.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Gathering and preparing medicinal plants.** Many plants have healing properties (or seem to).
Boil them, crush them, make poultices.

**Treating wounds.** Wash with water, stop bleeding with cloth or pressure, cover with cloth
to keep dirt out.

**Treating fevers.** Apply cold water or heat, depending on the theory being used. Give the
patient water or broth.

**Comfort measures.** Keep the patient warm, give them food and rest when possible.

### What Goes Wrong

**Infections and blood poisoning.** Wounds that are not properly cleaned become infected.
Infection can be fatal.

**Ineffective treatments.** Many plants do nothing. The amateur tries many things hoping
something works.

**Poisoning instead of healing.** Some plants are toxic. Misidentifying plants can poison
the patient.

**Diagnosis errors.** The amateur does not know what is actually wrong, so treatment is
random.

## Basic Midwifery

**Any woman present at a birth can attend it with no special training.** Many births succeed
despite the attendant's ignorance.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Supporting the mother.** Hold her hand, let her grip your arm, encourage her to push.

**Catching the baby.** When the baby comes, catch it so it does not fall.

**Delivering the afterbirth.** Wait for the sac of blood and tissue to come out after the
baby. This must happen or the mother can die.

**Basic care of the newborn.** Clean the baby, wrap it to stay warm.

### What Goes Wrong

**Breach presentations and stuck babies.** Some babies come wrong and get stuck. The amateur
has no idea how to handle this.

**Hemorrhage after delivery.** Some mothers bleed uncontrollably after birth. Speed and knowledge
separate getting help quickly from too-late intervention.

**Infections of the mother.** Unclean hands, dirty cloth, or slow delivery can cause infections
that kill the mother days after the birth.

**Dying infants.** Some infants are born dead or die shortly after. The amateur is helpless.

## Basic Bloodletting

**Any man with a sharp blade can open a vein and let blood flow out.** The result is often
the patient's death.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Opening a vein.** Cut the skin over a visible vein or artery and open it so blood flows free.

**Controlling bleeding.** Apply cloth pressure, tie a cloth, burn with hot iron if necessary.

**Leeches.** If available, leeches naturally draw blood. Apply them and let them feed.

### What Goes Wrong

**Opening the wrong structure.** Cutting an artery instead of a vein causes rapid death from
blood loss.

**Uncontrollable bleeding.** Opening a large vessel and having no way to stop the bleeding
causes death.

**Infection from the wound.** The opening becomes infected, causing systemic infection.

**Death from blood loss.** Removing too much blood (the intended result of bloodletting) weakens
or kills the patient.

## Basic Tooth Extraction

**Any man with pliers or wrench can pull a tooth.** The result is the tooth being gone and
often severe complications.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Gripping the tooth.** Use pliers or wrench to firmly grip the tooth.

**Quick pulls.** Pull forcefully and suddenly to remove the tooth fast.

**Managing bleeding.** Stuff cloth in the hole and pressure the patient to bite down.

### What Goes Wrong

**Breaking the tooth and leaving roots.** Incomplete extraction leaves broken pieces that
fester.

**Excessive bleeding.** Some tooth extractions bleed for days or weeks.

**Infections that kill the patient.** Tooth extractions often lead to blood poisoning that kills
weeks later.

**Extreme pain and shock.** The patient may lose consciousness or go into shock from the pain
and bleeding.

## Basic Bone-Setting

**Any man can splint a broken bone and hope for the best.** Some bone injuries will heal
despite crude treatment.

### What Anyone Can Do

**Assessing the break.** Look at the limb. If it is bent wrong, it might be broken.

**Straightening the limb.** Pull on it in opposite directions to straighten it, then immobilize
it.

**Building a splint.** Use cloth, wood, or straw to immobilize the limb.

**Managing pain.** Pain can be reduced with alcohol or plant medicines (results vary).

### What Goes Wrong

**Breaks that remain crooked.** Improper setting results in limbs that heal twisted, causing
permanent disability.

**Compound fractures and infection.** If the bone breaks the skin, infection often follows,
potentially fatal.

**Nerve damage from improper setting.** Improper setting can damage nerves, causing permanent
deadness or pain.

**Limbs that are too immobilized.** Immobility too long results in stiff, unusable joints.
