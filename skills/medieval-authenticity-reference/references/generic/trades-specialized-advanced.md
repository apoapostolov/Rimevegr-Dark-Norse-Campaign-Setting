## Weaving

**The loom is the foundation of civilization.** Complex weaves — twills, damasks,
decorative patterns — require understanding of thread structure, pattern
repetition, and the mathematics of the loom. Every culture's clothing, tentage,
and functional textiles depend on the specialist weaver.

### Loom Types and Setup

**The warp-weighted loom.** Vertical warp threads hang from a beam, weighted at
the bottom to keep tension. Common in northern Europe. The specialist sets the
warp with perfect parallel spacing and tension — the foundation of even cloth.

**The frame loom.** Horizontal warp stretched between two bars. Simpler to set
up but less suitable for wide cloth or complex patterns.

**The treadle loom.** A sophisticated later-medieval development with foot
pedals that raise and lower parts of the warp. These looms enable patterns and
speed that hand manipulation cannot achieve.

### Core Knowledge

**Tension management.** The warp threads must all be under identical tension.
Uneven tension produces uneven cloth. The specialist adjusts continuously as the
cloth advances.

**Pattern notation and mental calculation.** Complex weaves (twills, damasks,
decorative bands) require the weaver to hold the pattern in mind and execute the
correct shed (raising and lowering the correct threads) for each shot. A mistake
early is only visible many rows later — and fixing it requires unraveling.

**Fiber properties.** Flax and hemp are narrow and strong — good for warp. Wool
takes the warp too but is less common. Cotton (rare in medieval Europe) is smooth
and beautiful but weak in long fibers. Silk is precious and requires different
techniques. The specialist chooses fiber for purpose and knows how each behaves
when wet, when tensioned, and in the dye vat.

**Density and cover.** A loose weave produces cloth that is airy but weak. A
dense weave produces cloth that is water-resistant but heavy. The specialist
adjusts thread spacing and shot density to achieve the desired properties.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Weave complex patterns.** A twill (diagonal ribs) requires raising different
threads on each shot in a pattern. A mistake is visible and throwing off the whole
pattern. Years of practice are required to weave complex patterns quickly without
mistakes.

**Maintain consistent cloth width.** As the cloth advances on the loom, the warp
tension changes unless constantly adjusted. Cloth that is narrower on one edge
than the other is unsaleable. The specialist maintains width within a finger's
breadth over many meters of cloth.

**Weave multiple colors in pattern.** Decorative bands or checks require keeping
multiple colors in circulation on the loom. Mistakes are visible and costly.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Broken warp threads.**Warp threads break during weaving. The specialist ties
off the broken thread immediately and continues. The knot is invisible under the
weft. After weaving, the specialized repair work (resplicing the break) is done
on the finished cloth. An amateur's broken warp runs for tens of rows before being
noticed, marring the whole section.

**Tension loss.** As cloth advances on the loom, the warp loses tension unless
wound forward correctly. Tension loss produces loose cloth. The specialist winds
the beam with deliberate care.

**Twisted thread.** Thread that has been twisted or kinked will create a weak
spot in the cloth. The specialist untwists the supply thread as it feeds through
the loom.

## Fulling

**Fulling is a skilled trade with specific knowledge about fiber, temperature,
chemistry, and aging.** The specialist's cloth has properties that the amateur's
cloth does not — weather resistance, durability, and comfort.

### Fiber Knowledge

**Different fibers full differently.** Coarse wool fulls easily and dramatically.
Fine wool fulls gently and can be over-fulled easily. Linen does not full well
and requires different techniques. The specialist handles each differently.

**Preparation before fulling.** The cloth should be thoroughly clean before
fulling — any grease or residue prevents even fulling. Some cloth is pre-combed
(carded) to stand up the fibers before fulling, making them more receptive to
the process. The specialist prepares carefully.

### Fulling Agents and Chemistry

**Alkali sources.** Wood ash (weak), stale urine (medium, contains ammonia and
urea), and plant-derived alkali (strong, used in later periods). Each has
different strength and effectiveness. Some are made fresh, some are aged for
strength. The specialist chooses and prepares the right agent for the cloth.

**Temperature and timing.** Hot water (hot, not boiling) activates the fulling
process but extreme temperature damages the fibers. The specialist judges
temperature by hand and maintains it through the process.

**Repetitive cycles.** Full cloth is cycled through soak, beat or tread, rinse,
and dry multiple times. Each cycle takes hours. The specialist does 2-4 cycles
depending on the desired final properties.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Judge the state of the fulling.** Is the cloth fully fulled? Over-fulled?
Just right? The specialist feels the cloth's weight, its drape, its flexibility,
and knows. The amateur guesses.

**Fix over-fulled cloth.** Once over-fulled, it cannot be reversed. The specialist
avoids this by careful observation. The amateur's mistake is permanent.

**Achieve consistent results.** Every batch of cloth is different (different fibers
, different dyes, different starting conditions). The specialist adjusts the
process. The amateur follows a recipe and gets inconsistent results.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Felting instead of fulling.** Over-fulled cloth loses its original weave
structure and becomes felt — matted together fibers with a random structure. This
is sometimes desired (felt) but not when the goal is fulled cloth with retained
weave.

**Shrinkage loss.** If the cloth is cut to final size before fulling, the 20-30%
shrinkage takes it below the specified size. The specialist cuts cloth oversized
before fulling and trims after. The amateur wastes material.

**Smell and stains.** Urine-based solutions leave residual ammonia smell that
takes weeks and repeated wearing to dissipate. Some stains from the fulling
agent are permanent. The specialist manages these.

## Dyeing

**The dyer's trade is chemistry mediated through plant sourcing, material science,
and the patience to repeat the same process consistently.** A proper dyed cloth
maintains color through years of wearing and washing. An improperly dyed cloth
fades within months.

### Mordants

**Understanding mordants.** A mordant is a chemical that bonds the dye molecules
to the fiber. Without it, the dye sits on the surface and washes off. The
specialist understands which mordants work with which dyes and which fibers.

**Alum.** The most common mordant. Creates different colors with different dyes:
red with madder, yellow with weld, orange with weld+madder. The specialist
calculates proportions carefully.

**Tannin.** Often used after alum to deepen color and improve fastness. Creates
different shades depending on concentration and fiber type.

**Iron (ferrous sulfate).** Used in small amounts to deepen colors and affect
hue. Too much iron makes the cloth brittle and weakens fibers.

### Dye Sources and Processes

**Madder (red/orange/brown).** A root dye requiring careful temperature control
(never boiling, which destroys the color) and alum mordanting. The extraction
is slow — the dye pot must be built up over days. The color is permanent and
highly valued.

**Woad (blue).** A leaf dye requiring fermentation and pH control. The proper
woad vat is maintained at a specific pH range and temperature. The process is
complex and the specialist's vat is treated with care (not allowed to cool,
not exposed to sunlight, regularly tested). A well-maintained woad vat lasts
for months or years.

**Indigo (blue, from imported plants).** Similar to woad but even more
sophisticated. The vat chemistry is critical. In medieval Europe, indigo is
expensive and usually reserved for valuable cloth or expensive customers.

**Plant dyes (weld for yellow, madder-based oranges, etc.).** Each has specific
temperature, pH, mordant, and timing requirements. The specialist remembers
dozens of recipes or maintains notes. Consistency matters — the same recipe
should produce the same color batch after batch.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Achieve permanent color.** Without mordants, the color is temporary. The
specialist knows which combination of mordant, temperature, and dye produces a
permanent bond.

**Reproduce the same color.** Dye extraction varies with the plant source, the
season collected, the age, and storage conditions. The specialist tastes or tests
the dye strength and adjusts the proportions. The amateur dyes the same way every
time and gets inconsistent color.

**Troubleshoot a failed dye.** The cloth came out the wrong color (too pale, too
orange when it should be red, too muddy, too harsh). The specialist analyzes
what failed — temperature, timing, dye strength, mordant, iron content — and
corrects it. The amateur rejects the batch or wastes it at a discount.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Crock (color rubs off).** Under-thorough mordanting or improper dye bonding
leaves loose dye on the surface. The color rubs off onto skin or other cloth.
The specialist rinses thoroughly and tests wear before completing the job.

**Fading in sunlight.** Some colors fade faster than others. The specialist
chooses dyes and mordants that are known to be light-fast, at the cost of more
complex processing or more expensive materials.

**Uneven dyeing.** The dye vat requires constant circulation and careful
temperature and pH management. Uneven heat creates uneven color. Uneven pH
creates uneven color. The specialist manages all of these.

## Tailoring

**The tailor's art is fitting cloth to body.** A tailor produces garments that
fit, that move correctly, and that last through years of wear. The specialist
combines knowledge of cloth behavior, human anatomy, and technique.

### Pattern and Fitting

**Measurement and proportion.** The tailor measures the customer (shoulder width,
arm length, torso length, circumferences) and knows the proportions required for
proper fit. A sleeve that is one finger-width too short or too long is wrong.

**Pattern layout.** The tailor cuts the cloth pieces with understanding of how
the cloth will distort and move. Bias cuts are used for stretch (like neck
facings). Lengthwise cuts are used for panels that should not stretch.

**Grain direction.** Cutting with the grain gives one set of properties. Cutting
across the grain (bias) gives different properties. The specialist uses this
deliberately.

### Joinery and Reinforcement

**Seam types.** Simple running seams for hidden areas. Reinforced stitching for
stress points (shoulders, crotch, underarm). Some seams are flat-fell (folded and
stitched twice) for durability and a clean appearance.

**Interfacing and reinforcement.** Collar points and facing require interfacing
(a stiff layer) to hold shape. Cuffs require a double layer. Buttonholes require
reinforcement or they rip. The specialist uses these techniques selectively for
durability.

**Hem quality.** Fine hems are almost invisible — small stitches, thread color
matching, angled cuts at corners to reduce bulk. Rough hems are visible and ugly,
but functional.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Fit to the body.** Trial and error fitting, adjusted after sewing, shows as
loose or tight areas. The specialist fits before final sewing, making adjustments
on paper and cloth (with basting stitches) before permanent stitching.

**Manage cloth behavior.** Some cloth stretches on the bias, some shrinks uneven
ly under tension, some pills from wear. The specialist chooses cloth properties
deliberately and adjusts technique to handle them. The amateur ignores these
properties and gets bad results.

**Repair professionally after wear.** A garment worn for years likely develops
rips, loose seams, and worn edges. The specialist repairs invisibly or near-
invisibly. The amateur patches visibly or replaces the whole garment.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Seam failure under stress.** A seam cut too close to the edge (not enough seam
allowance) or stitched with insufficient thread tension fails when the garment
is worn. The tear is often in a visible location.

**Button failure.** Buttons exposed to stress pull away from the cloth, taking
fibers with them. Proper button attachment (reinforced, multiple layers of
thread) prevents this. Poor attachment leads to lost buttons and torn cloth.

**Zipper or fastener failure.** Medieval tailors use buttons, toggles, and
laces — all of which can fail. Modern zippers (centuries later) also fail at
the seams. The specialist designs the attachment to survive the stress.

## Sail-Making

**The sailmaker's craft is cloth engineering under extreme stress.** A sail that
fails catastrophically can kill the crew. The specialist understands forces,
reinforcement, and the properties of sailcloth under wind load.

### Sailcloth Selection and Preparation

**Linen for cold climates.** Linen sails breathe in sun and dry quickly. They
don't rot as easily as wool in wet conditions. But they require more tensioning.

**Wool for northern waters.** Thick wool sails are water-resistant and relatively
light (for their thickness). They stretch under load but are more forgiving of
imperfect construction.

**Thread selection.** The sailmaker uses heavy linen thread (not wool) for
stitching. The thread must be fine (for strength) and tightly twisted (for
durability under abrasion from the spars).

### Seam Design and Stitches

**Width and angle.** Sail seams are stitched at a shallow angle (not 90 degrees)
to the load direction. This distributes stress across a wider area of cloth.

**Cross-stitching.** Multiple lines of stitches running in different directions
reinforce the seam. A single line of stitches will tear under load. Two or three
lines give redundancy — if one line tears, the others hold.

**Rope reinforcement.** Along high-stress edges (luff, leech, foot), the
sailmaker sews a rope into the edge of the cloth. This rope absorbs the tension
instead of the cloth. Without it, the edge of the sail tears and rips progressively
inward.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Calculate necessary reinforcement.** A tall sail with a long foot (bottom) and
long leech (trailing edge) has different stress distributions than a short,
square sail. The specialist accounts for wind loads and designs reinforce
accordingly.

**Adjust for cloth properties.** Sailcloth can be loose (light and breathable
but weak) or dense (heavy and strong). The specialist chooses cloth for the
application and adjusts seam design accordingly.

**Repair a ripped sail at sea.** A small rip is patched with heavy stitching on
both sides of the cloth. A large rip requires taking the sail down, removing it
from the spars, and extensive repair — sometimes impossible at sea. The
specialist designs sails to resist ripping under normal conditions.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Seam failure in a gale.** A sail that is stitched with insufficient thread
density or weak stitching tears at the seams when wind pressure is extreme. The
sail is lost and the crew is endangered.

**Edge fraying and progressive tearing.** Without rope reinforcement, the edge of
a sail frays in the wind. The fraying turns into rips that propagate inward. The
sail fails progressively.

**Distortion under load.** A sail cut with insufficient bias compensation
stretches unevenly under load. The sail loses its shape and does not present the
proper angle to the wind. Wind efficiency drops and the sail may luff (flutter).

## Smithing and Metalwork

**The smith is the technological foundation of medieval civilization.** Every
tool, every nail, every piece of metal equipment comes from the smith. The quality
of swords, tools, and hardware depends on the smith's knowledge of metallurgy and
technique — understanding of carbon content, temperature, and the infinitesimal
timing that separates a good blade from a failure.

### Forge and Equipment

**The forge.** A pit of earth or stone hearth containing a wood or charcoal fire
fed by a bellows. The bellows is worked by foot or hand, pushing air through the
fire to achieve and maintain high temperature. A good forge reaches 1100°C or
higher — bright orange heat.

**Anvil.** A heavy block of iron (often with an attached or separate hardy — a
spike extending upward). The smith strikes the heated metal against the anvil
face, shaping it with each blow. The anvil's shape and quality affect the work.

**Hardy and other tools.** A hardy (a cutting tool inserted in a hole in the anvil
top), a set, a punch, tongs, swages (shaped tools that shape the metal), and
various hammers of different sizes and faces. Each tool has a purpose and the
smith chooses the right tool for the task.

### Core Knowledge

**Reading color.** As iron heats, the color changes — from dark iron through
cherry red, to orange, to yellow, to white. Each color corresponds to a specific
temperature. The specialist knows by sight and experience the color needed for
different operations:

- cherry red (~730°C): for bending and initial shaping
- orange (~900°C): for welding and heavy forging
- yellow-white (~1100°C+): for drawing out (elongating) and final shaping

**Carbon and hardness.** Iron with different carbon content behaves differently
— pure iron is soft and malleable. Steel (iron with 0.5-2% carbon) is stronger
and can hold an edge. Cast iron (over 2% carbon) is brittle and cracks. The smith
sources iron with the right carbon content for the work. A sword requires higher
carbon steel. A nail requires lower carbon iron.

**Quenching and tempering.** Hot metal plunged into water hardens (quenching).
Full hardness is brittle — an edge so hard it shatters when it hits bone. The
smith reheats the quenched blade to a lower temperature and lets it cool slowly
(tempering). The temper color (straw, bronze, blue, purple) indicates the
hardness. A sword edge is straw-colored (slightly softer but tougher). A chisel
is blue-colored (harder, for resistance to denting).

**Welding.** Two pieces of hot iron (at orange heat and above) can be hammered
together until they fuse. The joint must be perfectly clean, perfectly hot, and
perfectly timed. An imperfect weld fails under stress. This is the smith's most
difficult skill and the hardest to learn.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Achieve correct hardness and temper.** A sword that is too hard shatters. A
sword that is too soft bends and will not hold an edge. The specialist judges
by color and by testing the metal's response to a light hammer blow.

**Weld iron cleanly.** Joints must be seamless and strong. The specialist has the
skill and timing to heat, align, strike, and cool in perfect sequence. The
amateur's welds separate under stress.

**Shape metal efficiently.** Each hammer blow must move the metal toward the
desired shape. The specialist sees the final form in the rough lump and works
toward it with minimal waste. The amateur hammers inefficiently and uses extra
metal or wastes material.

**Repair broken tools correctly.** A broken tool requires breaking it at the
damage, shaping new metal onto the break, and welding or riveting the pieces
together. The repair must be stronger than the original or it fails again on the
first use.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Cold iron cracking.** Metal hammered when it is too cool cracks before the
smith realizes the metal is not hot enough. The workpiece is ruined and must be
reheated and reshaped — all extra work.

**Overheating.** Metal that is too hot (white heat and above) becomes brittle and
weak. The grain structure breaks down. The workpiece is ruined.

**Underfired metal in a sword or tool.** If the energy and knowledge for proper
heat treatment is skipped, the tool or weapon fails in use. An axe head that was
not properly tempered cracks at the edge and becomes unsafe.

**Inclusions and impurities.** Iron ore contains impurities. Some are removed in
the smelting process, but carbon-monoxides created during smelting can be trapped
in the iron (blowholes). These are weak spots that fail under stress.

## Armouring

**The armourer combines knowledge of metallurgy with understanding of how armor
behaves under impact and blade.** Every piece must fit, must move freely, and must
protect without binding the wearer or becoming a weakness.

### Armor Types and Construction

**Mail.** Interlocking iron rings, each linked to four neighbors, sewn or riveted
together onto a backing textile. Mail is flexible but heavy (10-15 kg for a shirt).
The armourer understands how rings should overlap (1/4" to 1/2" depending on ring
size), how they should be linked (4-in-1 is standard, with solid rings linking
four loose rings), and how the backing textile keeps the rings from being driven
into the skin by impact.

**Lamellar.** Overlapping small rectangular plates of iron sewn onto a backing.
Lighter than mail for the same protection (4-8 kg) but less flexible. The plates
must overlap correctly or an upward blade stroke can get past the protection.

**Plate.** Full rigid steel plates, articulated at joints. The earliest plate
armor is heavy and clumsy. Later medieval plate armor is elegant and properly
fitted, with movement at hips and shoulders. Full plate requires years of smith
and armourer work per suit — expensive, rare, and worth far more than the metal's
weight in trade goods.

### Fitting

**Measurement and adjustment.** Armor must move with the body. Ill-fitting armor
binds at the armpit, preventing the arm from rising. Armor that gaps at the
sides leaves ribs exposed. The armourer custom-fits each piece to the individual.

**Joints and articulation.** Mail at joints (elbow, knee) must move freely but
still protect. Plates at joints must articulate without binding the limbs at full
extension. The armourer designs plates and strapping to achieve this.

**Weight distribution.** Heavy armor that is not balanced correctly pulls hard at
the shoulders and neck, exhausting the wearer. Well-designed armor uses harnesses
and fastenings to distribute weight to the hips and legs. The armourer understands
human anatomy and mechanical distribution.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Rivet mail tight enough to hold.** Mail rivets must be tight enough to prevent
rings from being driven inward but not so tight that the rings become brittle. Bad
riveting is obvious when a blow dents the ring without breaking the link — the
ring is tight-riveted enough to dent but the links slipped. The armourer's mail
resists this.

**Fit plate armor to a specific body.** Off-the-shelf armor is heavy and awkward.
Custom-fitted armor moves like a second skin. The armourer takes days of
measurement and fitting to achieve this. The result is vastly more effective and
more expensive.

**Design articulation that holds.** Armor at the elbow or knee must allow full
movement while protecting the vulnerable joint. This requires careful design of
moving plates and flexible connection points. Improvised armor either binds or
leaves the joint exposed.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Mail failure in combat.** If the backing textile rots or tears, the rings are
no longer supported and can be driven inward. Arrows or blows can exploit this.
The armourer maintains the backing when repairing mail.

**Plate cracking under repeated blows.** Plate armor meant to withstand blades
can crack under repeated heavy strikes. The crack spreads and the plate becomes
useless. Repairs involve replacing the plate entirely.

**Rust and corrosion.** Iron armor surfaces rust easily. Maintenance (oiling,
polishing) extends the life. Neglected armor becomes brittle and weak.

## Blade-Smithing

**The bladesmith is the apex of the metalworking trades.** A sword or axe head
is not just forged metal — it is a finely tuned blade with specific qualities:
an edge that is hard and sharp, a spine that is flexible and resilient, and the
balance between these that makes the blade effective.

### Steel and Carbon Control

**Different steels for different purposes.** A sword edge needs carbon (hardness),
a spine needs flexibility. The bladesmith often pattern-welds: combining different
carbon steels to create a blade with hard edges and a flexible spine. This requires
sourcing multiple steel types and the skill to weld them at exactly the right
temperature and strike them correctly.

**Reading the metal.** The bladesmith judges carbon content and quality by the
fracture pattern when a test piece is broken. High carbon steel has a crystalline
appearance with specific characteristics that the bladesmith recognizes
immediately.

**Controlling hardness across the blade.** A blade that is uniformly hard is
brittle. A blade that is uniformly soft will not hold an edge. The bladesmith
quenches by plunging the spine first (which cools fastest and hardens) while
partially withdrawing the edge (which cools slower and remains flexible). This
differential hardening is invisible to look at but critical to performance.

### Shaping and Grinding

**The shape of the blade** determines where force concentrates. A flat spine is
flexible. A hollow-ground or beveled edge is sharper and cuts more easily but is
more fragile at the cutting edge. The bladesmith grinds the blade shape as
carefully as it is forged.

**The edge.** The angle of the edge determines cutting ability versus fragility.
A 15-degree angle (per side) is sharp and cuts well but is fragile. A 30-degree
angle is less sharp but more durable. The bladesmith chooses the angle based on
the blade's purpose — a hunting knife has a sharper edge than a battle axe.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Pattern weld correctly.** Welding two different steels without voids or
discontinuities is extraordinarily difficult. The joints must be perfect. A flaw
means the blade shatters catastrophically in use.

**quench a blade properly.** The movement of the blade into the water, the speed,
the angle — all affect how the blade hardens. Improper quenching distributes
hardness unevenly. The blade fails in use — perhaps splitting lengthwise, or
losing its edge despite being hard.

**Grind a blade straight and even.** Blade grinding is tedious and repetitive.
The bladesmith grinds at a consistent angle for hours, achieving a blade that is
straight within a fraction of a millimeter and symmetrical edge to edge. An
amateur's ground blade is wavy or uneven and does not cut as well.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Pattern-weld separation.** If the weld between the edge steel and spine steel is
incomplete, the layers can separate during use. The blade shatters or the edge
peels away. This is catastrophic failure.

**improper hardness distribution.** A blade that is too hard everywhere is brittle
and shatters. A blade that is too soft everywhere will not hold an edge. An
improperly quenched blade might have both problems — hard in some spots, soft in
others, and completely unreliable.

**Hidden cracks from improper forging.** A small crack in the spine of a blade is
invisible. Under impact, the crack propagates and the blade breaks. This is why
blades are tested before delivery — a test blow on a target wood and the blade's
response tells the bladesmith if it is sound.

## Lock-Smithing

**The locksmith combines metalwork with mechanical understanding.** A lock must
open with the correct key and close in a way that cannot be easily forced. Every
lock is a puzzle of pins, springs, and internal geometry.

### Lock Mechanisms

**Pin tumbler locks.** A series of vertical pins at different heights. The correct
key aligns the pins at the right height, allowing the lock cylinder to turn. The
wrong key does not align the pins (or aligns them wrong), preventing turning. This
is the standard medieval lock design.

**Spring considerations.** The pins must be held in the raised position by
springs (leaf springs, coil springs) that must have the right tension — strong
enough to hold the pins up, not so strong that the key cannot operate them.

**Key profile.** Each lock has a unique key with a unique cut pattern. The cuts
must match the pin heights exactly. A key cut in the wrong place does not work.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Design an effective lock.** The pin arrangement is the secret. If all the pins
are at the same height, anyone can open it with a straightened stick. If the pins
are arranged in a good sequence, only the correct key works. The locksmith
designs this carefully.

**Cut a key accurately.** The key's cuts must align with the lock's pins exactly.
Too high and the pins do not rise enough. Too low and the key hits the bottom of
the lock. The locksmith uses files and careful measurement to achieve the correct
cuts.

**Manufacture without visible wear.** A lock that is filed too aggressively
shows tool marks that are visible and ugly. The locksmith files smoothly and
finishes with finer and finer tools until the lock is smooth and finished like
fine jewelry.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Worn locks losing security.** As a lock is used, the pins and internal surfaces
wear. A worn lock can be opened by similar keys or even with a shim (a thin metal
wedge). The locksmith either maintains the lock (replacing worn parts) or replaces it entirely.

**Keys breaking.** A key under stress (if someone pulls hard on a stuck lock)
can break. The locksmith manufactures replacement keys. This is simple for a basic key,
complex if the original is lost and the lock cannot be opened to extract the pin pattern.

**Key sticking or jamming.** Rust, dirt, or wear can make a lock stick or jam.
The locksmith opens it by disassembly, cleans it, and checks that the pins move
freely before reassembly.

## Farriery

**The farrier combines blacksmithing with veterinary knowledge.** A horse that
lames is a catastrophe — ten soldiers can march, but without horses, a company
cannot fight. The farrier keeps cavalry mounted.

### Horseshoe Manufacture and Fitting

**The horseshoe shape.** An iron bar bent into a U shape, with nail holes
punched or forged around the outside edge. The shoe must follow the curve of the
horse's hoof closely — not too tight (which causes lameness) and not too loose
(which causes the shoe to come off).

**Fitting the shoe.** The farrier measures the hoof, shapes the shoe to fit, and
nails it on. The nails are driven through the shoe from the outside, through the
hoof wall (the insensitive outer part), and clinched (the sharp ends bent flat)
on the inside. Done correctly, the horse feels nothing and the shoe is secure.

**Hoof care.** The farrier cleans out packed dirt and manure, trims the hoof to
proper length and angle, and evaluates the hoof structure. A hoof that is split,
infected, or asymmetrical gets special attention — special shoes, nail placement
changes, or poulticing.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Recognize hoof problems.** A horse limping slightly might have a stone, a
bruise, a separation, or infection. The farrier feels the hoof, watches the
horse's gait, and diagnoses the problem. Wrong diagnosis means wrong treatment and
a lame horse that should be sound.

**Fit a shoe that does not cause lameness.** A shoe that is too tight restricts
blood flow and causes soreness after hours of work. A shoe fitted with nails in
the wrong place can hit sensitive tissue. The farrier knows the anatomy and
nails carefully where the hoof wall can tolerate it.

**Repair a severely damaged hoof.** A hoof with deep rot or major damage cannot
bear weight. The farrier might apply a bar shoe (which spreads the load) or keep
the horse off the damaged hoof for weeks. Some damage is permanent and the horse
is lamed forever — but proper treatment can sometimes recover a horse that would
otherwise be lost.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Nail hitting the sensitive tissue.** If a nail is driven too deeply or in the
wrong place, it hits the sensitive tissue (quick) inside the hoof. The pain is
immediate and severe. The horse cannot walk. The nail must be removed and the
hoof treated for infection — a long recovery.

**Shoes falling off.** A loose clinch (the bent nails on the inside of the hoof)
and the shoe can come loose and fall off. This usually happens mid-march or mid-
operation, leaving the horse with an unshod hoof — which wears quickly and becomes
sore.

**Hoof splitting or cracking under wear.** A hoof that is not properly trimmed or
a shoe that distributes load unevenly can cause the hoof to crack. A longitudinal
(lengthwise) crack is often permanent and causes lameness. Transverse (across the
hoof) cracks sometimes heal if kept trimmed and maintained.
