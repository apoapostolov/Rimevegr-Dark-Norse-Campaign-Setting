## March Discipline

### Pace

A band moves at the speed of its slowest element — usually
the mule, the wounded, or the terrain.

**Normal march.** Two and a half to three miles per hour
on flat ground, firm footing, good weather. This is a
walking pace — not forced, not dawdling. A man in
reasonable condition with a forty-pound load sustains
this indefinitely.

**Forced march.** Four miles per hour. This exhausts the
men and the mule within four to six hours. The mule
begins refusing to keep pace. The men with the heaviest
loads fall behind. Forced march is a command decision —
outrun a pursuit, reach a position before the enemy, make
a destination before nightfall. It costs a day's recovery
afterward.

**Terrain penalties.** Dense forest: halve the pace
(constant obstacle navigation, ducking, climbing over
deadfall). Uphill: halve again (legs and lungs work
double). Bog or marsh: a quarter of normal pace
(every step sinks, pulls, and exhausts). Snow above
the knee: a quarter pace or worse. Deep snow without
snowshoes: movement effectively stops.

**Distance per day.** Fifteen to twenty miles in good
conditions is a solid day's march. Twenty-five is
pushing. Thirty is exceptional and leaves the band
depleted. In mountain terrain or deep forest, eight to
twelve miles may be the maximum.

### March Order

**Column formation.** The standard travel formation.
Single file in narrow terrain. Two-abreast where the
path allows. The column has a structure:

- **Point.** One or two men ahead of the main body by
  fifty to a hundred yards. They read the terrain, find
  the route, and sight threats before the column walks
  into them. Point runs silently. They communicate by
  hand signal or by stopping (the column watches the
  point — when the point stops, the column stops).

- **Main body.** The bulk of the band. The mule and
  its handler travel in the center of the main body
  (protected position, where the column can close
  around it if attacked). The most experienced
  fighters are at the front and rear of the main body.
  New men and the least capable are in the center.

- **Rear guard.** One or two men trailing the main body
  by fifty to a hundred yards. They watch behind. In
  ambush country, the rear guard watches the high ground
  to the sides as well. Rear guard turns around every
  few minutes to check the back trail — walking forward
  while looking backward is how rear-guard men develop
  a distinctive gait.

- **Flankers.** In open terrain or ambush-likely country,
  one or two men walk parallel to the column on either
  side, fifty to a hundred yards out. They watch the
  flanks and give warning if hostiles approach from the
  side. In dense forest, flankers cannot keep pace with
  the column and are pulled in.

### Noise Discipline

Sound carries. A marching column makes noise — boots on
ground, equipment clinks and rattles, the mule's hooves,
men talking. In safe territory, noise does not matter.
In hostile territory, noise discipline tightens:

- Metal-on-metal contact is silenced: weapons are
  wrapped, buckles are padded, chainmail is covered
  with a cloth layer.
- Speech is forbidden. Communication is by hand signal.
- The mule is muzzled if it tends to bray.
- Pace slows (deliberate steps make less noise than
  hurried ones).
- Route selection favors soft ground (grass, moss, duff)
  over hard ground (rock, gravel, dry leaves).

Full noise discipline reduces the column's detection
range from half a mile to a hundred yards in forest.

### Rest Halts

**Every hour.** A ten-minute rest halt is called. Men
drop packs, drink water, adjust boots. The halt happens
in a defensible position if possible — not in the open,
not at the bottom of a defile. During the halt, point
and rear guard remain in position (they rotate at the
next halt).

**Noon.** A longer halt if conditions permit — thirty
minutes to an hour. Cold food is eaten (cooking takes
time and produces smoke — in hostile territory, no
fires). Boots are pulled off and feet examined. Blisters
are treated immediately (see medical section). A blister
left untreated becomes an open wound by evening and a
limping man by morning.

## Terrain Reading

### What the Ground Tells You

An experienced man reads terrain the way a literate man
reads a page — automatically, extracting meaning without
conscious effort.

**Ambush terrain.** Any place where an attacker can hide
close to the route and attack without warning. Dense
forest on both sides of a narrow path. High ground
overlooking a road. Stream crossings where the bank
provides cover. Defiles (narrow passages between rock
walls or steep hillsides) where the column is strung out
and cannot form a defense. In ambush terrain, the point
moves further ahead, flankers go out if possible, and
weapons come to hand.

**Escape routes.** When entering any area — a valley, a
village, a forest clearing — a man identifies how to
leave quickly. This is automatic for experienced
travelers and fighters. New men must be taught to think
this way. The question on arrival is not "how do we get
in" but "how do we get out."

**Water indicators.** Green vegetation in otherwise dry
terrain points to subsurface water. A line of trees
(particularly willows, alders, or cottonwoods) in
grassland marks a watercourse. Animal trails converge
on water sources — follow them downhill.

**Human presence.** Smoke (visible in daylight, smellable
at greater range). Stumps (trees cut, not fallen —
someone with an axe). Paths (worn ground means regular
passage). Dung (domestic animal dung means a farm or
camp nearby). Structures visible on a skyline. Dog
barking.

### Skyline Discipline

Never silhouette yourself against the sky. A man standing
on a ridgeline is visible for miles. A man ten yards
below the ridge is invisible. This applies to camps
(never on the crest), movement (cross ridges at the
lowest point and move through saddles), and observation
(look over the ridge while lying prone, with only the
head exposed — and that obscured by a rock or bush).

## Bog and Marsh Navigation

### Why Bogs Kill

A man walks onto what looks like solid meadow grass and
sinks to the thigh. The peat and water grip his legs.
His struggles force him deeper. If alone and up to the
waist in bog, he drowns — slowly, in daylight, within
sight of solid ground.

### How to Cross

**Testing.** A long staff (eight feet, cut on the spot)
is used to probe the ground ahead. Solid ground resists
the staff. Bog sinks it. Floating mat (a layer of
vegetation over liquid water) springs under the staff
and rebounds — this is the most dangerous because it
looks solid and supports a man's weight for one step
before giving way.

**Route finding.** Look for the game trails (animals know
the safe paths through bogs). Look for tussocks (raised
mounds of grass with root systems that support weight).
Step from tussock to tussock. Look for the color of the
water: dark brown stained water indicates stable peat
below. Green sphagnum moss floating in clear water
indicates deep bog. Bright green grass in a uniform flat
area often means a quaking bog — the worst kind.

**If trapped.** Do not thrash. Spread the body flat
(increase the surface area). Reach for solid vegetation
or tussocks. Pull horizontally, not vertically (pulling
straight up creates suction). Work each leg free with
a circular motion, breaking the suction seal. This is
slow, exhausting work. A companion throws a rope or
extends a pole.

**The mule.** Mules are better than horses in soft ground
(smaller hooves, more even weight distribution relative
to body size, more cautious by nature). But a mule in
deep bog is a crisis — the animal panics, thrashes, and
sinks faster. The mule's load is removed (if reachable),
the animal is calmed, and it is guided to solid ground
by leading from the front — never from behind (a mule
that feels trapped and sees a person behind it may try
to climb over them).

## Mountain and Ridge Travel

### The Pace Problem

Uphill movement is exhausting because the legs are
lifting the body's weight plus the pack weight with
every step. A grade of ten percent (rise of one foot in
ten feet of forward travel) reduces pace by a third. A
grade of thirty percent (steep hillside) reduces it by
half or more.

**The rest-step.** The mountaineer's technique: on each
uphill step, pause with the rear leg locked straight (the
skeleton supports the weight, not the muscles) for a
beat before transferring weight to the forward leg and
stepping again. This micro-rest extends endurance
enormously. A man using the rest-step climbs for hours.
A man powering up the slope on muscle alone stops every
fifteen minutes, panting.

**Traversing.** Walking diagonally across a slope rather
than straight up. Reduces the grade encountered (the path
is longer but less steep). Switchbacks: traversing in one
direction, turning, traversing in the other — a zigzag
path up the mountain. Every man who has traveled mountain
terrain knows switchbacks. Tired men try to shortcut
straight up and exhaust themselves.

### Hazards

**Rockfall.** Loose stone on a slope above is a threat.
Thermal cycling (freezing at night, thawing by day)
loosens rock. Wind loosens rock. A man walking above
loosens rock. The man below hears a clatter — the
warning is a shout: "ROCK!" and everyone presses against
the slope (presenting the smallest target). A head-sized
stone falling fifty feet hits with enough force to kill.
Helmets help. Looking up and moving quickly matters more.

**Scree slopes.** Loose small stones that slide under
the feet. Walking on scree is like walking on ball
bearings. Uphill: kick the toe into the scree to create
a step (two steps forward, one sliding back). Downhill:
lean back, dig the heels in, and ride the slide in
controlled half-steps. Running on scree is how ankles
break.

**Exposure.** Mountain ridgelines are exposed to weather
with no shelter. Wind increases with altitude. Temperature
drops three to five degrees per thousand feet of
elevation. A warm valley can have a ridge that is cold
enough to cause hypothermia — especially when the wind
is factored in (wind chill). Crossing high ground, the
band must be prepared for weather that is dramatically
worse than what they left in the valley.

**Lightning.** In mountain terrain during storms, the
highest point attracts lightning. Do not be the highest
point. On an exposed ridge during a storm: get off the
ridge. If impossble: crouch low (do not lie flat — ground
current from a nearby strike travels horizontally and
a prone body receives the full current across its length).
Crouch on the balls of the feet with feet together,
minimizing contact with the ground. Put metal objects
(weapons, armor, tools) twenty feet away.

## Snow and Ice Travel

### Snowshoes

When snow is deeper than mid-calf, walking without
snowshoes is a losing battle. The feet plunge through
the surface with each step, the energy cost triples,
and pace drops to a quarter of normal.

**Construction.** A frame of green wood (ash or birch),
steamed and bent into a teardrop or oval shape. Lashed
at the front where the tails cross. The interior is
webbed with rawhide or sinew strips, criss-crossing each
other to create a platform that distributes the wearer's
weight over a large surface area. The foot is bound to
the frame with leather ties — the toe is fixed, the heel
lifts free (so the foot can flex for walking, and excess
snow falls through the heel gap instead of accumulating
on the platform).

**Walking.** The stride widens to clear each snowshoe's
width. The gait is rolling — weight shifts from one foot
to the other with a slight waddle. In deep snow, the
trail-breaker works hardest (compacting the path for
those behind). The band rotates the lead position every
fifteen to twenty minutes.

### Ice Assessment

Not all ice is safe. The thickness, color, and sound
tell the story.

| Thickness | Can support        |
| --------- | ------------------ |
| 2 inches  | Nothing — stay off |
| 4 inches  | A man on foot      |
| 6 inches  | A group of men     |
| 8 inches  | A mule with load   |
| 12 inches | A heavy cart       |

**Color.** Clear blue or black ice is dense and strong.
White ice (snow-ice — formed when snow on top of ice is
compressed and frozen) is weaker — half the strength of
clear ice at the same thickness. Grey ice indicates melting
or flowing water beneath — do not trust it at any
thickness.

**Sound.** Walk slowly and listen. Solid ice gives a
deep resonant groan or no sound. Creaking and cracking
sounds mean the ice is flexing under weight — it may
hold, but retreat is wise. A sharp crack followed by
water appearing at the crack means leave immediately.

**Testing.** Strike the ice with an axe or a heavy staff.
Good ice resists or chips. Weak ice punches through. Test
every hundred yards when conditions are uncertain.

**If the ice breaks.** The man goes through. His clothing
soaks instantly, the weight pulling him down. The shock
of cold water causes involuntary gasping — if the head is
underwater when this happens, water is inhaled. The man
has one to two minutes of effective muscle function to
get himself out. Technique: kick to horizontal in the
water, get the chest and arms flat on the ice edge, and
kick/pull onto the surface. Roll away from the hole (do
not stand up near the break — the stressed ice may give
way again). Get to shore and get the wet clothing off
immediately — hypothermia follows within minutes in air
temperatures below freezing while wearing wet clothing.

### Skiing

Nordic in origin. A practical transportation method in
snow country.

**The skis.** Long (taller than the skier), narrow,
with a curved upturn at the front. The underside is
sometimes lined with animal fur (hair oriented toward
the rear) which grips the snow on uphill push-off
(the hair resists backward sliding) while allowing
forward glide on the downhill and flat. Without fur,
the skis are waxed with animal fat, pine tar, or a
mixture (providing similar directional grip).

**Technique.** The kick-and-glide: push off one foot
(the kick — snow compresses under the grip zone of the
ski), glide forward on the other. Poles (one or two,
though many Norse skiers used a single pole that doubled
as a balance staff and spear rest) plant ahead of the
body and push. Uphill: herringbone (skis angled outward
in a V, stepping uphill with the edges gripping) or
side-stepping. Downhill: crouch for balance, weight back,
one ski slightly ahead. Stopping: snowplow (tips together,
tails apart, pressing the inner edges into the snow).

## River Crossings

### With Improvised Raft

When no ford exists and the river must be crossed.

**The log raft.** Three to five logs of similar length
(eight to twelve feet) lashed side by side. The logs
must be buoyant (dry wood, not waterlogged — test by
pushing a log into the water; if it floats with a third
of its diameter above water, it carries weight). Cross-
pieces lashed on top bind the logs together. A load-
bearing raft for one man plus gear needs logs totaling
at least eighteen inches of combined diameter above water.

**Propulsion.** Poles (pushing off the bottom — works only
in water shallow enough to reach bottom, which limits
this to near-shore). Paddles (improvised: a flat board
lashed to a pole, or a split log carved flat on one
side). Rope-ferry: a rope stretched across the river
(this requires crossing once first to anchor the far
end), with the raft hauled across by pulling on the rope.

**Hazards.** A raft in current moves downstream while
crossing — the landing point on the far bank is
downstream of the launch point. The faster the current,
the further downstream. Plan for this. A raft that
misses the intended landing on a river with rapids
downstream has a serious problem.

### Swimming with Gear

Men could swim. Many cannot (swimming was not universal —
many inland people never learned). For those who can:

**Dry-bag method.** Gear is wrapped in an oiled or waxed
cloth, tied tight, and pushed ahead while swimming. The
air trapped in the bundle provides flotation. The swimmer
kicks and uses one arm while the other hand grips the
bundle.

**Inflated skin.** An animal skin (sheep or goat) is
sewn shut at all openings except the neck, inflated by
blowing, and the neck tied off. This bladder provides
flotation when gripped under one arm. Two inflated skins
support a man who cannot swim — he clings and kicks.

## Navigation and Wayfinding

### Without Compass

The magnetic compass did not reach Scandinavia until
after the Viking age (and its reliability was poor in
high latitudes anyway). Norse navigation was by
observation.

**Sun.** The sun rises roughly east and sets roughly west.
At noon in the Northern Hemisphere, the sun is due south.
A staff planted in the ground casts a shadow: the tip of
the shadow moves in an arc. The shortest shadow (noon)
points north. Connecting the shadow tips from morning and
afternoon gives an east-west line.

**Stars.** Polaris (the North Star) sits nearly above the
north pole of the sky. Finding it: locate the Big Dipper
(Ursa Major — seven bright stars in a ladle shape). The
two stars forming the outer edge of the ladle's cup
point toward Polaris. Polaris does not move — it stays
north while all other stars rotate around it. On a clear
night, north is found in seconds.

**Moss.** The popular claim that "moss grows on the north
side of trees" is unreliable. Moss grows on the shaded,
damp side — which is often north (less sun) but varies
with local conditions. In dense forest, moss grows
everywhere. Not a primary navigation aid.

**Wind.** Prevailing wind direction is known regionally.
A man who knows the prevailing wind of his region can
orient roughly by facing into the wind. This is fallback
navigation and works only when the wind is holding its
normal pattern.

**Terrain memory.** The primary method. A man traveling
through country he knows navigates by landmarks — that
mountain, this river, the pass shaped like a notch, the
stone shaped like a tooth. The entire Norse wayfinding
system is based on memorized terrain features passed
through oral tradition. "Three days upriver from the
river-fork where the banks are red clay, you reach the
falls. Half a day above the falls, the valley narrows..."

### Distance Estimation

**Counting paces.** A man's stride on flat ground is
roughly two and a half to three feet. One thousand
double-paces (counting every other footfall) equals
approximately one mile. Pace-counting is the standard
method for distance estimation on foot. Experienced
march-men count automatically and report distances: "The
village is three thousand paces from the ford."

**Visual estimation.** In clear conditions, objects at
known sizes provide range cues. A man-sized figure:
visible as a shape at 300 yards, visible as a person
at 200 yards, face features visible at 50 yards. A
cottage: visible as a shape at 1-2 miles, details
visible at half a mile. These estimates degrade with
rain, fog, and terrain.

**Time-distance.** At three miles per hour for six hours
of march (accounting for terrain and rest halts), a band
covers roughly eighteen miles. Track the time by sun
position and you know the approximate distance covered.

## Boat Handling

### Rowing

Every man in coastal Scandinavia rows. It is like
walking — learned young and never forgotten.

**The stroke.** Catch (blade enters the water, arms
extended), drive (pull the blade through the water using
the back muscles — the arms transmit force but the power
comes from the torso and legs), finish (blade exits the
water, hands come to the chest), recovery (arms extend
forward for the next catch). The rhythm is set by the
lead rower or by a drum/chant.

**Coordination.** A ship with twenty oars works only when
all twenty pull in unison. One out-of-rhythm oar tangles
with its neighbors, breaks the timing, and can injure the
rowers. Rowing practice is military drill.

**Endurance.** Sustained rowing at cruise pace: two to
three hours before fatigue degrades the stroke. At combat
pace (ramming, escape, pursuit): twenty minutes maximum
of all-out effort.

### Basic Sail Handling

The square sail — the standard Norse rig.

**Setting the sail.** The sail is stored furled (rolled
up) on the yard (the horizontal pole at the top of the
mast). To set: haul the yard to the top of the mast, then
release the brailing lines (ropes that hold the sail
furled to the yard). The sail drops and catches the wind.
Sheets (ropes attached to the lower corners of the sail)
control the angle — pulled tight to one side for sailing
across the wind, let out for sailing downwind.

**Reefing.** When the wind is too strong for full sail,
the lower portion of the sail is gathered up and tied:
the sail area is reduced without taking it down entirely.
In severe weather, the sail comes down completely and the
crew rows or drifts under bare mast.

**Tacking.** A square sail cannot sail efficiently into
the wind (within roughly 60 degrees of the wind
direction). To travel upwind, the ship sails at an angle
to the wind, then turns and sails at the opposite angle —
a zigzag course that makes forward progress against the
wind but at a fraction of the speed. Each turn (tack)
requires shifting the sheet and braces to the opposite
side as the wind crosses the bow. This is synchronized
crew work — a mistimed tack in heavy weather is
dangerous.
