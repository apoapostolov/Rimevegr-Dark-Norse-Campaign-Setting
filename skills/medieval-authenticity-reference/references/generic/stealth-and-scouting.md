## Moving Unseen

### Terrain Use

**Dead ground.** Any fold in the terrain that conceals
a man from a specific observation point. A shallow
depression, a ditch, the reverse slope of a hill, a
stream bed. Dead ground does not need to be deep — a
man crawling in a ditch twelve inches deep is invisible
to an observer on level ground at fifty paces. The
skill is seeing the dead ground before moving into
the open—reading the terrain from above or from cover,
tracing the route that keeps the body below the sight
line.

**Shadow.** A man standing in shadow is harder to see
than a man in sunlight, even if the shadow is not
deep. The human eye is drawn to contrast and movement.
A still man in dappled forest shade is nearly invisible.
The same man crossing a sunlit clearing is obvious. The
rule: move through shade, cross open ground only when
necessary, and cross it fast.

**Vegetation.** Thick brush conceals but is noisy to
move through. Tall grass conceals a crawling man. A
man standing behind a single thin tree is not
concealed — but a man standing behind a tree with
brush around the base is, because the eye reads the
clump as a single mass. The skill is choosing the
route where vegetation screens the body without
requiring the man to push through it.

### The Fox-Walk

The silent movement technique. Used in forest, in dry
leaves, on gravel, on snow crust — any surface where
a normal step produces noise.

**Heel-toe-roll.** The foot is placed heel first, gently.
Weight transfers slowly through the ball of the foot.
If a stick or dry leaf is felt underfoot, weight is
held on the back foot and the forward foot adjusts.
The pace is agonizingly slow — one-tenth the speed of
a normal walk. A man fox-walking across one hundred
paces of dry forest floor takes ten minutes. A man
walking normally takes one minute and sounds like a
horse.

**Weight distribution.** The back leg carries the weight
until the forward foot is placed and tested. This means
the legs are always loaded — the thighs burn after
twenty minutes. Rest breaks are standing still, not
sitting.

**Arms.** Arms are held close to the body. A swinging
arm catches branches. An extended hand pushes aside
vegetation that springs back and moves, catching the
eye. Both hands control the space immediately in front
of the chest, gently moving obstructions rather than
pushing through them.

### Silhouette Discipline

A man on a ridgeline is visible against the sky from
miles away. A man standing in a doorway is framed by
the light behind him. A man moving along the top of
a wall is outlined against whatever is beyond. The
rule: never silhouette yourself. Cross ridges at the
lowest point. Stand beside the doorway, not in it.
Move along the base of a wall, not the top.

### Freeze and Move

The human eye detects movement before shape. A still
man in the open is often overlooked. A moving man in
good cover is often spotted. The technique: when the
observer's attention is elsewhere — looking in another
direction, bending to a task, speaking to another —
move. When the observer's attention sweeps toward
you, freeze. Total stillness. Not crouching, not
adjusting, not turning the head. Still. The urge to
duck or look away is overwhelming and must be
suppressed — both generate movement.

## Tracking

### Reading a Trail

**Species identification.** Bootprints are human.
Hoofprints are horse, mule, or deer (horses are shod
with iron — the crescent shape is distinct; unshod
hooves have a rounder print). Paw prints — wolves,
dogs, foxes, bears. Size, depth, and claw marks
distinguish them. A mercenary reads bootprints —
that is the track that matters.

**Number of men.** Count distinct boot patterns. If the
trail is a churned mess, estimate from trail width — a
single file produces a narrow track; a column of ten
produces a wider, more beaten path. Fresh horse
droppings count horses. Cook fire sites count the
group's ability to feed itself.

**Direction.** A boot pushed off the toe leaves a deeper
impression at the front. Grass and twigs are bent in
the direction of travel. Mud displaced by the boot
rolls forward. Stones kicked loose roll downhill from
the direction of travel.

**Speed.** A walking man leaves full footprints, evenly
spaced. A running man leaves deeper toe impressions,
wider spacing, and scuff marks. A man carrying a heavy
load leaves deeper prints, shorter stride, and
sometimes drag marks from the pack.

**Age of sign.** Fresh prints in wet ground have sharp
edges and water has not yet seeped in. Prints hours
old have softened edges and may have water pooling.
Prints a day old are weathered — edges rounded by rain,
dried by sun, filled by wind-blown debris. Broken
twigs: fresh breaks show white or green inner wood;
old breaks are dried and darkened. Spider webs across
a trail: if intact, no one has passed since the
spider built it (usually overnight).

### Tracking in Specific Conditions

**Snow.** The easiest terrain. Every step shows. Age
is estimated by snowfall — a print filling with new
snow is from before the current snowfall. A print with
crisp edges on undisturbed snow is recent. Depth tells
load. Stride tells pace. The disadvantage: the tracker
also leaves prints.

**Hard ground.** Rock, packed earth, gravel. Prints may
be invisible. The tracker looks for displaced stones
(a kicked stone shows a lighter patch where it lay;
the stone itself is lighter on the bottom), scrape
marks, crushed vegetation in cracks, moisture
transferred from a boot to dry rock (fades in minutes).

**Rain-washed trails.** Rain can obliterate soft-ground
tracks in hours. But rain also creates new sign —
puddles divert the trail, men step on higher ground,
boot treads fill with mud and leave marks on rock
they step on next.

## Counter-Tracking

### Principles

You cannot avoid leaving sign entirely. You can make
the sign harder to read, slower to follow, and
misleading in direction.

**Step on stone.** Where rock is available, walk on it.
A man crossing a rock outcrop leaves no footprints.
The tracker must search the far side of the outcrop
in all directions to pick up the trail — this costs
time.

**Use water.** Walk in streams. The current erases
footprints. Enter the water at a rocky point (not a
muddy bank) and exit at a rocky point. A tracker who
finds the entry point must check both banks upstream
and downstream until the exit is found.

**Walk in your own tracks.** On the return, step exactly
in your outbound prints. The trail looks like one
passage, not two. This requires discipline and good
light.

**Brush the trail.** A branch dragged over soft-ground
prints obscures them. This works briefly — a skilled
tracker recognizes brush marks and knows the trail was
deliberately hidden, which tells him someone is evading.
But an average pursuer loses the trail.

**Split the group.** Five men split in five directions.
The tracker must choose which trail to follow. Even if
the tracker picks correctly, the others escape. The
group reassembles at a pre-arranged point by a different
route.

**Hard surface transitions.** Walk on soft ground to an
area of hard ground (rock, road), change direction on
the hard ground, and continue. The tracker follows the
soft-ground trail to the hard surface and must then
search for where the trail resumes — which may be in
any direction.

## Camouflage

### Natural Materials

**Mud.** Applied to the face and hands (exposed skin
reflects light). Dark mud on light skin breaks the
recognizable pattern of a human face. The forehead,
cheekbones, nose, and chin are lightened; the eye
sockets, under the jaw, and temples are already
shadowed. Reverse the pattern — dark on high points,
nothing on low points — and the face becomes an
unreadable shape.

**Charcoal.** Same function as mud but drier, stays on
longer, and works on greasy skin where mud slides off.
Mixed with fat, it adheres well. The disadvantage:
charcoal smells of fire, and in fresh forest it is out
of place.

**Vegetation.** Branches, leaves, and grass tucked into
clothing, the helmet, the pack straps. The purpose is
to break the human silhouette — the distinctive
head-and-shoulders shape that the eye identifies
instantly. A man covered in loose branches does not
look like a bush. He looks like a vaguely bush-shaped
lump that the eye dismisses if it is not moving.
Movement destroys the concealment — the branches shake
in a way that vegetation does not.

### Breaking Outline

The human shape is betrayed by straight lines (the
rifle, the spear shaft, the shield edge), by smooth
curves (the helmet dome, the shoulder), and by
symmetry (two arms, two legs, evenly spaced). Natural
terrain has none of these. The goal is to disrupt the
recognizable shape:

- Wrap the spear shaft in cloth strips.
- Hang a cloak unevenly, not draped symmetrically.
- Tilt the head or crouch to change the shoulder line.
- Keep one arm tucked, not both extended.

### Concealing Fire and Smoke

A fire at night is visible for miles. Smoke by day
rises and marks a position. The solution is never
perfect — fire produces both light and smoke.

**Dakota fire hole.** A fire in a pit, with a separate
air-intake tunnel. The pit conceals the flame from
ground-level observation. Smoke is reduced (the
efficient burn produces less visible smoke). This
does not eliminate the smoke but reduces it
considerably. Best with dry hardwood, which produces
less smoke than green or resinous wood.

**Smoke dispersal.** Build the fire under a tree canopy.
Smoke passing through branches disperses rather than
rising as a telltale column. The canopy must be high
enough not to catch fire — a mistake made once and
learned permanently.

## Night Movement

### Dark Adaptation

**The physiology.** Human eyes take twenty to thirty
minutes to fully adapt to darkness. One bright light —
a lit match, a glance at a fire, a torch — resets the
adaptation to zero and the thirty minutes starts over.

**The one-eye rule.** When tending a fire or passing a
light source at night, close one eye. The closed eye
maintains its dark adaptation. When the light is gone,
open the adapted eye. This is an old trick that works.
The hunter teaches it to every new man in the band.

**What you can see.** In full dark adaptation under
starlight, a man can see terrain, movement, and large
objects at twenty to thirty paces. He cannot see faces,
fine detail, or the ground surface beneath his feet.
Under moonlight, vision is considerably better — shapes
are clear to a hundred paces or more. Under overcast
with no moon, darkness is near-total and movement is
by feel and memory.

### Pace in Darkness

**Halved at best.** Even on known terrain, night
movement is half the speed of day movement. On unknown
terrain, it is slower — one-third or one-quarter.
Every step is tested. Hands are extended to feel for
obstacles. The man ahead is followed by sound (his
breathing, his footfalls) more than by sight.

**Noise.** At night, sound carries farther and the ear
is the primary sense. A man who trips in daylight makes
a noise lost in ambient sound. A man who trips at night
sounds like a landslide. Night movement discipline is
absolute: no talking, no metal clanking, no packs
jingling. Equipment is secured. Mail is padded.
Weapons are tied down.

**Terrain hazards.** Holes, roots, rocks, cliffs — all
invisible. The lead man moves slowest, probing with
feet and hands. A staff or spear used as a probe
extends the man's reach and detects drops and
obstacles before the body finds them.

### Navigation at Night

**Stars.** The pole star is the primary reference.
Other star patterns mark east and west as they rise
and set. A man who knows the sky can hold a bearing
through the night. A man who does not know the sky
follows the man who does.

**Wind.** If the wind direction was noted at dusk and
has not changed, it provides a bearing. Wind on the
right cheek at dusk means wind on the right cheek
should persist. A shift means either the wind changed
or the man is off course.

**Terrain memory.** The route was studied during daylight.
The shape of the ridge, the sound of the stream, the
feel of the ground underfoot. A man who paid attention
during scouting can navigate ground he cannot see.

## Reconnaissance

### Approaching a Settlement

**Never approach directly.** Circle wide. Observe from
high ground or dense cover at a distance. Approach from
downwind (smoke from the settlement drifts toward you;
dogs do not smell you). The first observation period is
at least an hour — long enough to see the routine, count
fires, estimate population, and note defensive features.

**What to look for:**

- **Fires.** Number of hearth fires estimates the number
  of households. A single large fire may indicate a
  gathering or a garrison. No fires at midday is unusual
  and worth noting.
- **Animals.** Livestock in pens — a settled, functional
  community. No livestock — abandoned, raided, or a trap.
  Saddled horses — armed men, possibly arriving or
  departing.
- **Routine.** When do people emerge? When do they eat?
  Is there a watch posted? Is there a pattern of patrols?
  A settlement with no visible watch is either safe
  (far from threats) or complacent (about to be raided).
- **Defensive features.** Walls, ditches, palisades.
  Their condition — a maintained wall versus a rotted
  palisade. Gates — open, barred, guarded.
- **Armed men.** Number, equipment, alertness. Armed men
  sitting in the sun playing dice versus armed men
  walking the perimeter with purpose.

### Reading a Camp from Distance

An enemy camp reveals itself:

- **Fire count** estimates strength (one fire per five
  to eight men, approximately).
- **Horse lines** estimate cavalry or mounted troops.
- **Latrines** indicate duration of stay (deep, well-dug
  latrines mean they plan to stay).
- **Noise level** indicates discipline (a quiet camp is
  well-led; a noisy camp is relaxed or leaderless).
- **Guard pattern** reveal when and where the perimeter
  is weakest.

### Estimating Strength

**The cook-fire method.** Count fires. Multiply by six
(the average number of men per fire). This gives a
rough estimate — plus or minus thirty percent. A camp
of twenty fires holds roughly a hundred to a hundred
and fifty men. This estimate breaks down with very
large or very disciplined forces (who build fewer fires
for security) or with very cold weather (where more
fires are built for warmth).

## False Trails and Deception

### Decoy Camps

Build fires in a position you have already left. The
fires must be fed — green wood or thick logs that burn
for hours. From a distance (and especially at night),
the decoy camp appears occupied. By the time a scout
approaches close enough to confirm it is empty, the
real group has several hours of distance. Works once
against a given enemy. A good scout recognizes the
trick from the absence of noise, the absence of
movement silhouettes against the firelight, and the
absence of sentries.

### False Trails

When evading pursuit, a detached group of two or three
men creates an obvious trail in a false direction while
the main body moves quietly in the true direction. The
false trail group walks normally, does not attempt to
hide sign, and leads the pursuit for several hours
before splitting apart and individually doubling back
by counter-tracking techniques. The pursuing force
follows the obvious trail and finds nothing at the end.

### Misdirection with Noise

Noise draws investigation. A man banging a shield with
a rock in the forest draws a patrol toward the sound
while the main body crosses open ground behind them.
Bird calls that are slightly wrong draw attention — a
hunter or a scout recognizes the fake and investigates.
The distraction needs to be plausible enough to draw
at least a few men and sustained enough to keep them
engaged for minutes, not seconds.

### The Feigned Retreat

A tactical deception as old as warfare. A force engages
the enemy, then withdraws in apparent disorder. The
enemy, sensing victory, pursues — and is led into an
ambush, a prepared position, or difficult terrain where
the retreating force turns and fights with advantage.
The difficulty: the retreat must look real. Men must
drop shields and packs. The enemy must believe the
retreat is genuine. And the men performing the feigned
retreat must trust the plan enough to turn their backs
on the enemy and know the ambush force is waiting
behind them.

## Signals and Communication

### Smoke Signals

**Column.** A sustained column of smoke (feed the fire
with green wood or damp grass) says: "Here. Come to
this position." Visible for miles on a clear day. The
disadvantage: everyone can see it, including the enemy.
A smoke signal is an announcement, not a secret message.

**Puffs.** Alternating covering and uncovering the fire
with a blanket produces distinct puffs. Pre-arranged
meanings: one puff for "all clear," two for "enemy
sighted," three for "come immediately." The system
must be agreed upon before the band separates, and
it must be simple — more than three signals are
forgotten under stress.

### Horn Calls

A horn carries farther than a shout and has distinct
tones that can be pre-arranged. Common signals:

- **One long blast:** rally to this position.
- **Two short blasts:** enemy contact.
- **Three short blasts:** withdraw.
- **Continuous blowing:** alarm — stand to arms.

The horn does not specify direction or distance. It
says only: this is happening, react as planned. Like
smoke, it is audible to everyone — the enemy knows
something has changed.

### Bird Calls

A cupped hand against the mouth, manipulated to
produce the call of a specific bird. The nightjar,
the owl, the cuckoo — birds whose calls are simple
two-note patterns easy to imitate. The recipient
hears the call and recognizes it as the signal. The
enemy, if they know the local bird sounds, recognizes
the call as false — wrong season, wrong time of day,
wrong location. A cuckoo calling at midnight is a
man calling to another man. Bird calls are for short-
range communication between scouts and the main body,
not for strategic signaling.

### Runner Relay

The most reliable method for complex messages over
distance. A man runs from the main body to the scout
position (or vice versa), delivers the message verbally,
and returns with the reply. The disadvantages: time (a
runner covering two miles each way takes thirty to
forty minutes round trip) and risk (the runner can be
intercepted). The advantages: the message can be
detailed, nuanced, and include instructions that no
horn blast or smoke puff can convey.
