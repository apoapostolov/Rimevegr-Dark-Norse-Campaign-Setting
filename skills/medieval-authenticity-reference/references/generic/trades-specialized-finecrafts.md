These include fine leather work, fire management trades (pottery, charcoal-burning,
soap and salt production), food production and preservation trades, and maritime
work (fishing, net-making, rope work, shipwright, caulking).

## Tanning

**The tanner transforms raw hide into stable leather through chemistry and
time.** Hide is protein. Without tanning, it rots, hardens, and cracks. Tanning
binds the protein structure, making it stable, flexible, and resistant to decay.
The tanner controls this transformation through choice of tanning agent, water
temperature, and timing.

### Tools of the Trade

**Vats.** Large wooden containers filled with tanning liquor. The tanner must
be able to move hides in and out repeatedly, and access the contents easily.

**Scraping and fleshing tools.** Blunt and sharp edges for removing hair and
fat. A fleshing beam (a log set at an angle) hold the hide while working.

**Lime pit.** A pit containing slaked lime and water, used to loosen the hair
before scraping.

**Drying racks and storage.** Leather must dry with good air flow and be stored
where moisture and temperature are stable.

### Core Knowledge

**Hide selection and preparation.** Different animals yield different leathers.
Cattle hides produce thick leather for armor and tool handles. Sheep and goat
produce fine, supple leather for clothing. Pig hides are tough and durable.
The tanner sources the right hide for the intended use.

**Hair removal with lime.** Slaked lime swells the hide and loosens the hair.
After soaking for days in the lime pit, the hair pulls away easily. The tanner
rinses away all lime before moving to tanning — residual lime prevents the
tanning agent from working properly.

**Oak gall tannin liquor.** Oak galls (wasp-induced swellings on oak trees) are
crushed and soaked in water. The resulting liquid is rich in tannic acid. Hides
soaked in this liquor gradually transform. The longer the soak, the deeper the
tanning penetrates. A light tan takes weeks. A heavy tan takes months.

**Alum tanning.** A faster method using alum (mineral salt) and oil. The tanner
dissolves alum in water, adds white oak or barley bran (which adds oils), and
soaks the hide. Alum tanning works faster than oak gall but produces leather
that is more vulnerable to moisture.

**Vat temperature and timing.** Warm vats work faster than cold ones, but
excessive heat damages leather. The specialist knows by touch and experience
when the liquor is warm enough but not too hot. The specialist also knows by
testing (folding the hide, checking color) when tanning is complete.

**Fat-tanning (chamois).** Some leathers are tanned with fish oil or animal fat
instead of plant tannins. The oil replaces the water in the hide, preventing
rot and keeping the leather supple. This is slow work — repeated soaking,
wringing, and re-soaking to work the oil deep into the structure.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Achieve consistent tanning depth.** The amateur soaks hides haphazardly,
pulls them out too early, or leaves them too long. The result is patchy —
some parts soft, some still stiff. A tanned hide must be uniformly tanned
throughout.

**Know when leather is ready.** The specialist judges by color, by flexibility,
by how the leather bends and holds a fold. The amateur cannot distinguish between
"not ready," "ready," and "overtanned."

**Manage tanning vats over time.** Vats grow weak with use. The specialist adds
fresh liquor, maintains temperature, and knows when to pull a vat out of service.
The amateur lets vats degrade and produces progressively worse leather.

**Work with different hide types.** Beef, mutton, goat, horse, pig — each behaves
differently in the vat. Different ages and conditions require different timing.
The amateur treats all hides the same and gets poor results.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Leather remains stiff and sticky.** Over-tanning or incompletely rinsed lime
produces leather that is hard and does not soften. This leather cannot be worked
into shoes or garments.

**Rot despite tanning.** If lime was not fully rinsed or if the leather is stored
in damp, it rots from the inside out. The tanning agent cannot prevent rot if the
hide contains residual lime or if moisture returns after tanning.

**Color bleeding.** Leather that was tanned with weak liquor can bleed tannin
dye into anything it touches when wet.

**Uneven tanning causing structural failure.** Patchy tanning means patchy strength.
Seams follow the weak lines and rip. A boot sole that is unevenly tanned wears
unevenly.

## Saddlemaking

**The saddler builds the seat between horse and rider — a structure that must
distribute weight, absorb shock, and endure years of hard use.** A good saddle
is the difference between a comfortable campaign and one where every man limps.

### Tools of the Trade

**Saddle tree (wooden frame).** The backbone of the saddle — two wooden arches
connected by side rails. The tree must be strong enough to support a man's weight
without flexing, but light enough to carry. The saddler either carves the tree
or negotiates with a wood carver.

**Padding and materials.** Hair, wool, linen, and leather for building up the
seat and ensuring comfort and shock absorption.

**Stitching tools and materials.** Awl, needles, thread, and sinew for attaching
leather to the frame and stitching panels together.

**Form for fitting leather.** A dummy horse-back (leather-covered wood form) to
drape and shape leather panels while building the saddle.

### Core Knowledge

**Tree design and weight distribution.** The saddle tree must support the rider's
weight on the horse's back without crushing the spine or ribs. The arches must be
shaped to fit the horse's back — too narrow and the tree digs in, too wide and it
shifts. Some horses are fresh and strong, some are sway-backed, some are built high.
The saddler either builds trees for average horses or negotiates custom trees for
difficult mounts.

**Leather selection and panel design.** The leather covering the tree must be thick
and durable but supple enough to mold to the tree and the horse. The panels that
contact the horse's back must be stuffed with hair to distribute pressure and absorb
shock. The saddler designs panels that are thicker under the rider's weight, thinner
at the edges to avoid pressure points.

**Attachment and structural integrity.** Every seam, every attachment point, every
transition must be strong enough to hold under a rider's shifting weight, under the
stress of climbing hills, under combat movement in the saddle. The saddler uses
multiple passes of stitching, wraps attachment points with cord, and tests every
joint before declaring the saddle finished.

**Custom fitting.** A saddle built for an average horse will not fit every horse.
The saddler can build generic saddles or can take a commission and build a custom
tree for a specific horse. Custom fitting requires the saddler to see the horse,
measure the back, and understand the horse's build.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Build a tree that distributes weight correctly.** Weight that is not distributed
causes sores, spinal damage, and lameness. The specialist understands the geometry
and physics. The amateur does not.

**Stuff panels for correct shock absorption.** Too little stuffing and the tree
digs in. Too much and the saddle is unwieldy and unbalanced. The specialist knows
the right density and shape. The amateur guesses.

**Stitch strongly enough to survive campaign.** Saddles in use take enormous stress.
Every seam must survive thousands of hours of riding, jumping, combat, river
crossings. The specialist knows how to bridge stress and reinforce pressure points.
The amateur stitches once and the saddle falls apart.

**Fit a saddle to an unusual horse.** Most horses are average. Some are unusually
built — short-backed, high-withers, sway-backed. The specialist can measure, modify,
or build custom. The amateur cannot.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Saddle sores.** Pressure points cause sores. A wounded horse cannot march and may
be permanently withdrawn from service.

**Saddle shifts under load.** An underfitted or poorly-stitched saddle rotates as
the rider shifts. Falling from a sliding saddle in armor (with a spear in hand) often
ends in death.

**Tree cracks or breaks.** Weak wood or poor joinery means the tree fails under
stress. A broken tree is dangerous and cannot be repaired — it must be replaced
entirely.

**Leather cracks or rips.** Leather that was poorly selected or improperly finished
cracks under saddle sweat and movement. Rips spread and eventually the saddle comes
apart.

## Cobbling

**The cobbler builds shoes — structures that protect the feet through years of
marching, riding, standing, climbing.** A good shoe is invisible; the wearer walks
without thinking. A bad shoe ends expeditions.

### Tools of the Trade

**Lasts (wooden foot-forms).** The cobbler maintains a range of lasts in different
sizes and shapes. The shoe is built on the last and takes its shape from the last.
A last with the wrong shape produces a shoe that does not fit and causes blisters.

**Stitching awls and needles.** Fine work requires fine tools. The cobbler uses
awls that can punch through leather and sole material without dulling.

**Sole-shaping knives and rasps.** To shape and smooth the sole.

**Heel and sole materials.** Various thicknesses of leather and wood for soles.
Wood pegs for heel attachment. Welt material (reinforced strip of leather that
connects sole to upper) for formal construction.

### Core Knowledge

**Leather selection for different purposes.** Thick hide for soles that must
withstand long marching. Supple leather for uppers that must flex with the foot.
Waterproofing materials for sealing uppers and seams.

**Last fitting and selection.** Different feet have different shapes. The cobbler
maintains a range of lasts and can judge which fits a particular customer. The shoe
will be built on that last and will fit only that person (or someone with identical
feet).

**Welt construction.** A welt is a strip of leather stitched between the upper and
the sole. The welt absorbs the shear stress that occurs where upper meets sole,
preventing the sole from ripping away. Welt construction is the mark of good
cobblers; the result is more flexible, more durable, and lasts longer.

**Sole attachment.** The sole can be stitched (cheap, fast, but less durable) or
welted (better, but takes more time and material). The specialist chooses based on
the customer's needs and budget.

**Waterproofing.** Oil, wax, and pitch can seal leather against water. The cobbler
coats the sole and applies sealant to allow seams. Complete waterproofing is
difficult, but good effort keeps feet dry on long marches.

**Heel construction and repair.** Heels take enormous stress. The cobbler attaches
heels with wooden pegs and leather stitching, and designs them to absorb repeated
impact. Worn heels can be replaced without replacing the entire sole.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Fit the shoe to the foot.** A shoe that is too tight causes blisters and blood
problems. Too loose and it shifts and rubs. The specialist judges fit by eye and by
the customer's walk.

**Build a sole that lasts.** A crude sole wears through quickly or delaminate. The
specialist selects the right thickness and grain alignment, prepares the leather
properly, and attaches it so strongly that it outlasts the upper.

**Maintain waterproofing during wear.** Initial waterproofing wears away. The
specialist teaches customers how to treat shoes (oil, wax, rest between wearings)
to maintain waterproofing. The amateur thinks one coat of oil is enough.

**Repair worn shoes without destroying them.** The specialist can replace heels,
resole, reinforce seams. The amateur's repairs are visible and crude.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Blister-inducing pressure points.** A shoe that fits poorly causes blisters.
Marching stops. Days are lost to healing.

**Sole failure.** A sole that separates mid-march leaves you barefoot. Marching
barefoot on hard ground ruins feet.

**Waterproofing failure.** Wet feet lead to trench foot (tissue death from constant
cold and dampness). A man with trench foot cannot march.

**Heel destruction.** Heels wear down or come off completely. Shoes without heels
are awkward to walk in and increase pressure on the ball of the foot.

## Gloving

**The glover creates fitted hand coverings — a trade requiring extreme precision
and understanding of hand anatomy.** Each finger must move freely. Each seam must
be strong but invisible. Each glove is essentially a miniature form-fitting garment.

### Tools of the Trade

**Pattern papers and cutting tools.** Templates for different hand sizes.

**Fine needles and thread.** Smaller than shoe-making tools; gloves require
delicate stitching.

**Leather suitable for gloves.** Kid (young goat), sheep, or deerskin — leather
that is soft, supple, and takes a fine finish.

**Blocking forms.** Wooden or straw-stuffed hand-forms to shape gloves during
construction.

### Core Knowledge

**Hand measurement and fitting.** Every person's hands are slightly different —
different finger lengths, different palm widths, different knuckle circumference.
The glover measures and selects or modifies patterns to fit.

**Seam placement to avoid bulk.** Gloves are tight garments. Seams must follow
the hand's natural lines and must be stitched so finely that they do not create
pressure points.

**Thumb construction.** The thumb is the hardest part. Cut sewn wrong, the thumb
either crowds the fingers or creates a gap. The glover understands the thumb's
angle and movement.

**Finger fitting and tapering.** Each finger must fit snugly in the center and
taper at the tip. Too loose and the glove slips. Too tight and circulation stops.

**Leather preparation and finishing.** Leather for gloves must be soft and supple
from the moment its finished. The glover oils, stretches, and blocks the glove to
ensure flexibility.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Fit gloves to individual hands.** Generic gloves do not fit all hands. Proper
fitting requires measurement and adjustment. The amateur makes one-size-fits-all
gloves that fit nobody well.

**Construct seamless or invisible seams.** Glove seams are highly visible. The
specialist stitches so finely and places seams so carefully that they blend into
the hand's natural folds. The amateur's seams are obvious and uncomfortable.

**Keep gloves supple.** Leather that is not properly finished stiffens quickly.
The specialist understands conditioning and blocking that keep gloves wearable for
years. The amateur's gloves stiffen after a few weeks.

**Repair individual fingers.** Worn finger tips can be replaced without replacing
the whole glove. The specialist can open a seam, remove a worn finger, sew in a new
one, and close cleanly. The amateur cannot.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Gloves that do not fit.** Poorly-fitted gloves cause blisters, circulation
problems, and loss of dexterity. A warrior in bad gloves cannot hold his weapon
properly.

**Seams that limit finger movement.** Bulky or placed seams restrict movement.
The glove feels wrong and becomes hot and uncomfortable.

**Rapid stiffening.** Gloves that stiffened quickly become uncomfortable and then
unusable.

**Seams separating under use.** Glove seams take enormous stress from hand bending
and movement. Weak stitching fails within days.

## Belt and Strap Work

**The belt-maker produces load-bearing straps — leather bands that hold clothing,
tools, and weapons.** A belt is not decorative; it is part of the support structure
for a marching man.

### Tools of the Trade

**Leather for belts.** Thick, strong leather that resists stretching and holds its
shape.

**Buckles, fittings, and decorative hardware.** Metal or bone buckles and rings.

**Edge tools and bevels.** To shape and finish belt edges so they neither dig in
nor separate.

**Burnishing tools.** To seal and polish edges against wear.

### Core Knowledge

**Leather selection for strength.** Belt leather must support weight without stretching.
The belt-maker selects or requests leather that is thick enough and has straight grain
lines (which resist stretching better than wavy grain).

**Width and thickness balance.** A belt that is too narrow digs into the hips under
load. Too wide and it restricts movement. The belt-maker selects width based on the
intended use — a belt for holding armor is wider than a belt for holding a tunic.

**Edge sealing and burnishing.** Exposed leather edges absorb water and fray. The
specialist seals edges with wax, oil, or by burnishing (heating and pressuring the
edge until it hardens shiny and smooth). Sealed edges last for years. Unsealed
edges degrade in weeks.

**Hardware selection and attachment.** Buckles must be strong enough for the load.
Attachment must be secure and protected against catching clothing. The specialist
stitches hardware with multiple passes and sometimes wraps the attachment point
with cord for extra strength.

**Repair and adjustment.** A belt can stretch or wear. Worn edges can be reworked.
Stretched belts can be reinforced. The specialist maintains customer belts. The
amateur makes belts; the specialist keeps them functional.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Select leather that will hold its shape.** Weak leather or leather with weak
grain stretches under load. The specialist knows which leather to choose.

**Seal edges against water and wear.** Unsealed edges fray quickly. The specialist
seals all edges.

**Attach hardware strongly.** Hardware that is stitched once pulls off under load.
The specialist uses multiple passes and reinforcement.

**Maintain a belt for years of wear.** Belts wear and stretch. The specialist can
refresh the edge, replace the buckle, reinforce weak areas. The amateur treats a
worn belt as garbage.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Belt stretching under load.** Weak leather or weak stitching means the belt
stretches. A stretched belt no longer holds your weight.

**Buckle pulling free.** Hardware that is not stitched strongly enough pulls off.

**Edge fraying and rotting.** Unsealed edges absorb water, start to rot, and separate.

**Leather tearing under load.** Poor quality leather tears before ripping free from
stitching.

## Pottery

**The potter transforms clay into functional and decorative ware through
understanding of material properties, kiln management, and design.** Pottery is
fragile but durable — it lasts centuries once fired properly.

### Tools of the Trade

**Potter's wheel.** A rotating platform (powered by foot or by an assistant turning
a shaft) on which clay is centered and shaped. The wheel allows symmetric, precise
shapes that hand-shaping cannot achieve.

**Throwing tools and trimming tools.** Wooden and bone tools for shaping, cutting,
and refining clay on the wheel.

**Kilns.** Large structures where pots are fired at high temperature. Kiln design
affects firing evenness and fuel efficiency.

**Clay preparation tools.** Wedging boards for conditioning clay. Slattern pits for
storing clay long-term.

### Core Knowledge

**Clay selection and preparation.** Different clays have different firing
temperatures, shrinkage rates, and working properties. The potter explores local
clays and blends them to achieve desired properties. Clay must be wedged (worked
repeatedly like bread dough) to remove air pockets, which would cause explosions
during firing.

**Wheel throwing and centering.** Centering wet clay on the spinning wheel is the
hardest skill. Once centered, the clay can be opened (a hole pushed into the center)
and raised (the sides pushed up and thinned). The potter feels the clay and the
wheel, adjusting hand position and pressure constantly.

**Slip and surface treatment.** Slip is liquid clay (clay mixed with water to a
creamy consistency). The potter uses slip to smooth surfaces, to attach handles and
feet, and to apply color or pattern (slip in a different clay type can be painted
on to create contrasting decoration).

**Drying and leather-hard staging.** Pots must dry slowly to avoid cracking.
"Leather-hard" is the stage when the clay is still damp but firm enough to hold
shape. At this stage, the potter can trim the base, attach handles, and refine
details. Leaving drying clay too long makes it too dry to work.

**Kiln design and firing temperature.** Different clay bodies mature at different
temperatures. Firing too cool leaves pottery fragile and porous. Firing too hot can
melt the ware. The potter knows the temperature and time needed for each clay and
has a kiln capable of achieving and maintaining that heat.

**Glaze and color.** Glaze is a glass-like surface that seals pottery and adds
color. Glazes are made from clay, minerals, and fluxes (materials that lower
melting point). Raw glazes must be suspended in water (slip consistency) and painted
or dipped onto leather-hard or bone-dry pots. The glaze melts during firing and
fuses to the pot surface.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Center clay on the wheel.** Centering requires feeling and practice. An uncentered
pot throws wildly and falls apart.

**Throw consistent forms.** Each pot should be the same height, same wall thickness,
same proportions as the others. The specialist produces dozens of identical pots.
The amateur produces lumpy variants.

**Achieve proper wall thickness.** Too thick and the pot is heavy and ugly at the
base. Too thin and it collapses or cracks during drying. The specialist judges by
touch.

**Manage drying to prevent cracks.** Fast drying causes cracks. Uneven drying causes
warping. The specialist controls moisture and temperature.

**Fire consistently.** Each kiln load produces variations based on kiln design, fuel,
and stacking. The specialist knows how to stack a kiln and manage heat flow to
achieve consistent results.

**Work with glazes and color.** Glazes are chemistry. Raw glazes must be ground,
mixed, suspended correctly. Glazes can interact during firing, creating unexpected
colors or running off the ware. The specialist has formulas, tests, and adjusts.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Separation and lamination.** Trapped air or weak bonding between clay layers
causes layers to separate during firing.

**Warping and slagging.** Uneven heat or too-high firing temperature causes pots to
warp or soften.

**Glaze running and melting.** Glazes that are too stiff run down the pot or melt
into pools. Glazes that stick to the kiln shelf ruin the piece.

**Crazing and dunting.** Glazed ware that cools too fast or that has incompatible
glaze-body combinations develops fine cracks (crazing) or shatters (dunting).

**Color instability.** Glazes that looked one color in the raw state change color or
separate during firing.

## Charcoal-burning

**The charcoal-burner converts wood into charcoal through controlled incomplete
combustion.** High-quality charcoal burns longer and hotter than wood, and is
essential for metallurgy and other industries.

### Tools of the Trade

**Charcoal kiln or pit.** A structure where wood is burned with limited oxygen.
Early designs are earth-covered pits. Later designs use permanent brick or stone
kilns.

**Wood preparation tools.** Axes, adzes, and saws for processing wood to size.

**Temperature sensing.** The burner judges temperature by color of smoke, by feel,
by experience.

**Covering and sealing materials.** Earth, mud, or metal plates to control oxygen
flow into the kiln.

### Core Knowledge

**Wood selection and sizing.** Different woods yield different charcoal. Oak and
beech produce high-quality charcoal. Softwoods produce lower-grade charcoal but are
available in some regions. Wood must be cut to roughly uniform size so it burns
evenly.

**Stacking and air flow.** The wood must be stacked so that some oxygen can enter
but most of the wood is partially deprived of oxygen. Air flow must be controlled
so the fire burns down slowly, converting wood to charcoal rather than to ash.

**Temperature and timing.** Initial high heat drives off volatile compounds and
partly combusts the wood. As temperature drops (and oxygen limits), carbonization
occurs. The burner judges by smoke color and smell when the process is complete.

**Cooling and collection.** Once complete, the charcoal must cool. If exposed to
oxygen during cooling, it can re-ignite. The burner carefully manages cooling and
collects the charcoal.

**Yield and grading.** High-quality charcoal is large chunks with few fines (dust).
Lower grades are smaller pieces or dust. The burner sorts and grades charcoal for
different uses.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Control oxygen to prevent ash formation.** Without proper oxygen management, wood
turns to ash instead of charcoal.

**Maintain consistent heat and timing.** Different wood types and sizes require
different burn times. The specialist adjusts the fire continuously.

**Avoid self-ignition during cooling.** Improperly cooled charcoal can reignite,
losing the entire batch.

**Produce large, high-quality pieces.** The specialist produces mostly large chunks
(good yield). The amateur produces mostly dust.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Complete combustion to ash.** Excessive oxygen or high heat turns the wood to ash
rather than charcoal.

**Incomplete carbonization.** Insufficient heat or time leaves charred wood rather
than true charcoal.

**Fire loss.** Self-ignition during cooling consumes the charcoal.

**Dust and loss of yield.** Most of the batch becomes fragmented fines rather than
large usable pieces.

## Chandlery

**The chandler produces candles and lamp fuel that provide consistent, reliable
light.** Good chandlery requires selecting and preparing tallow or wax, managing
wick quality, and testing burn characteristics.

### Tools of the Trade

**Tallow rendering equipment.** Large kettles for melting fat, strainers for
removing impurities, cooling molds.

**Dipping frames.** Rows of hooks holding multiple candles for batch dipping.

**Wick materials.** Plant fiber (rush, reed), twisted plant materials, or prepared
prepared cord.

**Wax if available (beeswax or plant wax).** Expensive, but produces superior
candles.

**Molds and pans.** For casting candles.

### Core Knowledge

**Tallow selection and quality.** Different animals produce tallow with different
burning characteristics. Cattle tallow is common but smoky. Sheep tallow is cleaner.
Tallow must be clean and free of water and impurities.

**Wick selection and thickness.** Wick thickness controls burn rate and light level.
Too thin and the candle drowns in melted tallow and goes out. Too thick and the
candle burns unevenly and produces excess smoke.

**Burn characteristics and flame steadiness.** The chandler Test-burns candles,
judges light output and smoke, and adjusts wick or tallow recipe.

**Wax blending if available.** Beeswax produces superior candles (bright, clean) but
is expensive. A small percentage of beeswax added to tallow improves quality
significantly.

**Mold design and casting.** Dipped candles are built up in layers. Molded candles
are cast in molds. Each method has tradeoffs in cost and quality.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Select high-quality tallow.** The specialist sources and tests tallow, rejects low
quality.

**Match wick to tallow.** Wick that is too thick produces smoke. Too thin produces
guttering (burning down unevenly). The specialist judges and chooses correctly.

**Produce consistent candles.** Each candle should burn the same, produce the same
light, and last the same time. The specialist controls these variables.

**Test and refine formulation.** The specialist tests candles, measures burn time,
judges light and smoke, and adjusts tallow recipe or wick selection.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Rancid tallow.** Tallow that was not properly stored goes rancid and produces
noxious smoke.

**Wick problems.** Wick that is too thin or too thick produces guttering, smoking,
or drowning out.

**Candles that burn too fast.** Poor tallow or wick selection means candles consume
fuel too quickly.

**Uneven burning.** Some portions of the candle burn at different rates, producing
an irregular burn.

## Soap-making

**The soap-maker transforms animal fat and lye (extracted from wood ash) into a
stable product that cleans and preserves itself indefinitely.** The chemistry is
precise and small changes dramatically affect the product.

### Tools of the Trade

**Lye-soaking equipment.** Barrels and containers for percolating water through
wood ash.

**Boiling kettles.** Large enough to hold multiple pounds of fat and lye mixture.

**Wooden paddles and stirring tools.**

**Molds and cooling surfaces.** For poured soap to harden.

**Testing equipment.** Means of testing lye strength and pH.

### Core Knowledge

**Ash selection and lye strength.** Different ashes produce different lye
concentrations. Hardwood ash produces stronger lye than softwood. The specialist
tests lye strength and adjusts boiling time accordingly.

**Fat selection and quality.** Different fats behave differently. Animal fat
(tallow) produces a firm soap. Plant oils (olive, coconut if available) produce
softer soap. Blending fats produces soap with intermediate properties.

**Temperature and timing.** Boiling too hot can weaken the soap. Too cool and the
reaction does not complete. The specialist maintains temperature and judges
completion by appearance and consistency (trace).

**Cure time and stability.** Fresh soap is caustic (contains excess lye) and soft.
Over weeks of storage, excess lye reacts with CO2 from air and neutralizes. The
soap hardens and becomes safe to use.

**Lye removal and safety.** Properly-made soap has no excess lye. The specialist
tests the finished soap to ensure safety before use.

**Additives and scent.** Herbs, oils, and other materials can be added for color
or fragrance, but must not interfere with the chemical process.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Test lye strength and adjust recipe.** Weak lye requires long boiling. Strong lye
requires careful watching. The specialist knows the difference.

**Judge when soap has traced.** The moment when the mixture thickens and becomes
opaque is crucial. Too early and it does not fully convert fat to soap. Too late and
the soap over-hardens. The specialist judges by appearance and consistency.

**Remove excess lye safely.** Soap with excess lye burns skin. The specialist uses
testing or curing methods to ensure complete reaction.

**Blend fats for desired properties.** Different fats produce soaps with different
hardness, lather, and performance. The specialist blends intentionally.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Lye that is too weak.** The soap reaction does not complete. You get a greasy,
non-cleaning result.

**Lye that is too strong.** Excess caustic lye burns skin.

**Over- or under-boiling.** Careless timing produces soap that does not set or that
is excessively hard and brittle.

**Soap that does not lather.** Soap made from high-tallow, no-additives can feel
slimy and not produce good lather.

**Storage instability.** Soap without proper curing goes soft or rancid.

## Salt-making

**The salt-maker collects and concentrates dissolved salt from seawater or salt
springs to produce a stable, storable product essential for food preservation.**

### Tools of the Trade

**Evaporation ponds or pans.** For concentrating saltwater.

**Boiling kettles (for large-scale operations).** For complete evaporation if ponds
are slow.

**Scraping and collection tools.** For harvesting crystallized salt.

**Simple scales (if available) for testing density.**

### Core Knowledge

**Salt sources and quality.** Seawater contains dissolved salt. Some regions have
salt springs (natural saltwater sources). Seawater is cleaner than most springs but
salt evaporation is slow in cool climates. Springs are faster but may contain
dissolved minerals that affect quality.

**Evaporation control.** In warm, dry climates, ponds concentrate salt slowly but
without fuel cost. In cool climates, boiling is necessary, which consumes fuel but
is predictable and fast.

**Contamination control.** Seawater and springs contain dissolved minerals and
organisms. Settling and filtration reduce (but do not eliminate) contamination.

**Crystal formation and sizing.** As brine concentrates, salt crystallizes. Crystal
size depends on evaporation rate. Slow evaporation produces larger, cleaner
crystals. Fast boiling produces smaller salt with more impurities.

**Mineral identification and removal.** Some dissolved minerals crystallize along
with salt. Others remain dissolved. The salt-maker learns local sources and knows
what impurities to expect and how to minimize them.

**Iodine content (if considered).** Salt from coastal areas naturally contains
iodine. Inland salt does not. This affects health in populations that lack iodine
from other sources.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Set up efficient evaporation systems.** Pond design and sealing affects
evaporation rate. The specialist optimizes for local climate and water source.

**Achieve salt purity.** Mineral content affects salt color and taste. The
specialist selects sources and methods that minimize contamination.

**Manage large-scale boiling.** Boiling large volumes requires fuel, kiln design,
and continuous management. The specialist does this efficiently.

**Test concentration and judge readiness.** The specialist can judge by observation
when brine has reached the right concentration for crystallization.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Slow product in cool climates.** Ponds evaporate slowly. High fuel cost if
boiling is necessary.

**Mineral contamination.** Salt from mineral-rich sources is bitter and off-color.

**Loss to over-crystalization.** If concentration is too high before cooling, salt
crystallizes in the container and is lost.

**Contamination with organisms or dirt.** Seawater ponds accumulate seaweed, fish,
and debris. These must be removed before crystallization but some settling is
required.

## Milling

**The miller converts grain into flour and meal with consistency and efficiency,
maintaining grinding machinery and managing grain quality.** A good mill is the
foundation of a settlement.

### Tools of the Trade

**Water wheel or treadle mechanism.** To provide rotating power for grinding stones.

**Grinding stones (millstones).** A pair of matched circular stones with serrated
surfaces. The bottom stone (bed stone) is fixed. The top stone (runner stone)
rotates.

**Stone-dressing tools.** To cut and maintain the serrated surface of the stones.

**Grain cleaning equipment.** To remove chaff, dirt, and damaged grain before
grinding.

**Sifting equipment.** To separate flour by fineness.

### Core Knowledge

**Grain types and grinding characteristics.** Wheat produces flour (fine, free-flowing).
Barley produces barley meal (coarser). Rye, oats, and other grains have different
characteristics. The miller knows which grain to grind into which product.

**Mill design and efficiency.** Water-powered mills are fastest (using falling water
to turn a wheel). Treadle mills are slower but work anywhere. The miller understands
water flow, wheel design, and power transmission.

**Millstone dressing and maintenance.** Grinding stones dull with use. The miller
periodically removes a stone, uses specialized tools to cut new furrows into the
surface, and reinstalls. Proper dressing is critical to grinding quality.

**Grinding process and flour quality.** Grinding speed and pressure affect flour
quality. Too slow and the flour is coarse. Too fast and it heats and damages the
grain. The miller adjusts the gap between stones for desired fineness.

**Grain moisture and storage.** Grain must be dry to prevent spoilage, but not so
dry that grinding produces dust. The miller understands moisture content and storage.

**Flour quality and grading.** Fine flour (white flour) is more valuable and more
difficult to produce. Coarser meal is cheaper and faster. The miller can produce
either on request.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Maintain millstones.** Stone dressing is a skilled task. Improperly-dressed
stones produce poor flour and can seize or kick.

**Achieve consistent flour fineness.** Miller stones that are improperly-dressed or
run at wrong speed produce inconsistent flour.

**Optimize mill efficiency.** Water mills only work with appropriate water flow.
The miller can assess site, design sluices, adjust wheel size.

**Manage grain quality.** Spoiled or contaminated grain produces bad flour. The
miller can judge grain quality and know which to accept and which to reject.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Stones seizing or kicking.** If gap is too small or stones are misaligned, they
seize. The resulting kick can injure the miller or break equipment.

**Flour contamination with stone dust.** Improperly-dressed stones shed particles
into the flour.

**Poor grinding efficiency.** Inefficient design or poor maintenance means slow
grinding and waste.

**Grain spoilage.** Grain stored in damp conditions spoils and must be discarded.

## Baking

**The baker produces bread that is consistent, flavorful, with good structure and
keeping quality.** Bread is the foundation of nutrition; the baker's skill determines
the settlement's basic food security.

### Tools of the Trade

**Oven.** Large, enclosed, capable of sustaining high heat for extended time.
Design affects heat distribution, fuel cost, and bread quality.

**Dough-mixing and preparation tools.** Large vessels for mixing. Kneading surface.

**Sourdough culture.** Living culture of wild yeast and bacteria, maintained
indefinitely by regular feeding (mixing with flour and water).

**Scales if available.** For precision in mixing ratios.

### Core Knowledge

**Grain types and flour quality.** Wheat flour has gluten (protein) that develops
structure and traps gas, producing rise and crumb. Rye flour has less gluten.
The specialist understands how flours behave and blends for desired results.

**Fermentation chemistry and yeast.** Yeasts consume sugar and produce alcohol and
CO2 gas. The gas causes rise. Bacteria in sourdough culture contribute flavor and
acid, which affects texture and keeping quality. The baker understands fermentation
timing and temperature.

**Sourdough culture maintenance.** A living culture must be fed regularly (flour
and water). The culture develops character over years — some cultures produce
sour flavor, some are more neutral. The baker maintains the culture carefully.

**Autolyse and fermentation timing.** Gluten develops during mixing and during
fermentation. The baker controls timing to develop desired structure. Long, cool
fermentation produces better flavor and keeping quality.

**Dough shaping and scores.** The baker shapes the final dough and cuts scores
(slashes) in the surface. Scores control where the bread expands and can affect
crust color and final shape.

**Oven management and crust development.** Bread baked on a hot stone or in a
wooden-fired oven develops a thick, flavorful crust. Oven humidity affects crust
color and thickness. The specialist manages oven temperature and steam.

**Understanding hydration and crumb structure.** Wetter doughs produce more open
crumb (larger holes). Drier doughs produce tighter crumb. The baker chooses based
on desired bread type.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Maintain a healthy sourdough culture.** The culture can be poisoned or allowed to
die. Maintaining it requires consistent care.

**Achieve consistent rise and shape.** Fermentation timing depends on temperature
and flour type. The specialist judges readiness by appearance and feel.

**Develop proper gluten structure.** Gluten development requires mixing and time.
The specialist understands when gluten is properly developed.

**Get crust color and crispness right.** Oven management and steam control affect
final crust. The specialist produces consistent results.

**Produce bread with good keeping quality.** Bread made without proper fermentation
goes stale quickly or grows moldy. Properly-made bread keeps for days.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Starter dying or becoming contaminated.** A neglected starter can die or be
poisoned by bad bacteria.

**Over-fermentation causing collapse.** If fermentation goes too far, the dough
loses strength and the bread falls flat.

**Under-fermentation leading to dense bread.** Insufficient fermentation produces
dense, gummy bread.

**Over-mixing producing tough bread.** Too much mixing over-develops gluten,
making tough, unpleasantly dense bread.

**Oven problems causing uneven baking.** Hot spots in the oven produce burnt bread.
Cold spots produce underbaked bread.

## Butchering

**The butcher breaks down animals into prime cuts and salvages every usable part,
minimizing waste and managing meat quality.** A skilled butcher supplies the
settlement with variety and maximizes nutrition from each animal.

### Tools of the Trade

**Knives of various shapes and sizes.** Long slicing knives, small boning knives,
cleavers.

**Saws for bone.** Bow saw or hand saw for cutting through bone.

**Hanging equipment.** Hooks, rope, and frameworks for hanging carcasses.

**Rendering and storage equipment.** For processing organs and fat.

### Core Knowledge

**Animal anatomy and muscle groups.** Different animals have different useful cuts.
The specialist knows where the valuable cuts are and how to remove them cleanly.

**Cut understanding and retail value.** Some cuts are prime (tender, flavorful,
expensive). Some are utility (tougher, useful for broth or stew, cheaper). The
specialist maximizes yield of prime cuts.

**Meat preservation.** Fresh meat is perishable. Smoking, salting, and drying
extend preservation. The specialist knows which preservation method for which meat.

**Offal use and organ processing.** Organs are valuable — liver, kidney, heart,
tongue. Organs require quick processing to prevent spoilage. The specialist knows
how to prepare each organ and which customers want them.

**Fat rendering and lard production.** Fat is valuable for cooking and candle-making.
The specialist renders fat carefully to produce lard that stores well.

**Bone use.** Bones are used for broth, for tools, for armor work. The specialist
saves bones by type.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Identify prime cuts.** The specialist can look at a carcass and see the prime cuts,
where they are, and how to remove them cleanly. The amateur cuts randomly and wastes
meat.

**Minimize spoilage of organs.** Organs must be processed quickly and properly.
The specialist does this efficiently. The amateur loses them to spoilage.

**Preserve meat correctly.** Different meats need different preservation. The
specialist chooses the right method.

**Remove meat cleanly following muscle seams.** Following the natural seams between
muscles is faster and cleaner. Cutting across muscles wastes meat. The specialist
knows the anatomy.

**Render lard properly.** Lard that is rendered badly spoils quickly or is
contaminated. The specialist produces clean, stable lard.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Wasted meat.** Cuts that do not follow natural seams tear and waste good meat.

**Organ spoilage.** Organs are high-value but spoil quickly if not processed
immediately.

**Meat spoilage.** Meat that is not preserved quickly or properly spoils.

**Cross-contamination.** Organs touching meat product can cause spoilage if
contents spill.

**Poor lard quality.** Lard contaminated with blood or bone fragments spoils
quickly.

## Brewing

**The brewer controls fermentation to produce consistent, flavorful beer that is
safe, keeps well, and provides reliable alcohol content for preservation and
consumption.**

### Tools of the Trade

**Brewing vessels.** Large pots and fermenting vessels (barrels, vats).

**Grain-roasting equipment.** For preparing grain before brewing.

**Mashing equipment.** For soaking grain and allowing enzyme breakdown.

**Cooling surfaces.** For cooling hot wort (liquid grain extract) before
fermentation.

**Sampling and testing equipment.** To measure gravity (sugar content) and alcohol.

### Core Knowledge

**Grain types and preparation.** Barley is the primary grain for beer. Different
roasts of barley produce different colors and flavors. The brewer selects grains
for desired final beer.

**Mashing and enzyme conversion.** Soaking cracked grain in hot water allows natural
enzymes to convert starches to sugars. Temperature control is critical — too cool
and enzyme activity is slow, too hot and enzymes are killed. The specialist
understands temperature and timing.

**Boiling and hop additions.** Boiling the grain liquid (wort) drives off water and
concentrates it. Hops (if available) provide bitterness and preservation. The
specialist controls boiling time and hop additions for desired flavor and bitterness.

**Fermentation management and yeast.** Adding yeast to cooled wort starts fermentation.
Yeast consumes sugars and produces alcohol and CO2. Temperature control is critical.
The specialist monitors fermentation, judges when it is complete, and maintains
consistent yeast cultures for batch-to-batch consistency.

**Gravity and alcohol content.** The original gravity (OG) is the sugar content
before fermentation. The final gravity (FG) is the sugar remaining after fermentation.
The difference indicates alcohol content. The specialist measures gravity and knows
desired FG for different beer styles.

**Preservation and stability.** Alcohol is a preservative. Higher alcohol beers keep
longer. Contamination risks are lower with proper fermentation. The specialist
produces beer that is stable and safe to store long-term.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Control fermentation temperature.** Fermentation produces heat. Too warm and the
yeast produces bad flavors. Too cool and fermentation stalls. The specialist
manages temperature.

**Judge fermentation progress.** The specialist knows by gravity testing and by
observation when fermentation is complete.

**Maintain healthy yeast cultures.** Yeast cultures can be contaminated or stressed.
The specialist maintains healthy cultures.

**Achieve consistent alcohol content.** The specialist measures gravity and knows
what alcohol content will result.

**Prevent spoilage and contamination.** Proper fermentation, proper handling, and
proper storage prevent beer from spoiling.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Fermentation stalling.** Temperature drops, stuck yeast, or contamination can
prevent fermentation from completing.

**Infected or spoiled batch.** Contamination produces vinegar or bad flavors.

**Over-attenuation (too much fermentation).** If fermentation goes too far, all
sugars are consumed and the beer is dry and harsh.

**Inconsistent products.** Batch-to-batch variation in alcohol, flavor, color.

## Beekeeping

**The beekeeper maintains healthy colonies, ensures adequate food, manages swarming,
and harvests honey sustainably — producing consistent, high-quality honey while
keeping the bees thriving.**

### Tools of the Trade

**Hives.** Long-term structures specifically designed for bees (not just shelters).
Fixed combs or removable frames depending on design.

**Smoke equipment.** For calming bees during handling.

**Honey harvesting tools.** Uncapping knives, strainers, storage containers.

**Queen excluders (if available).** Barriers that allow workers through but not
the queen.

### Core Knowledge

**Hive structure and bee behavior.** Bees build wax comb in hexagonal cells,
organizing the hive in distinct regions — brood (where larvae develop), honey
storage, pollen storage. The beekeeper understands this structure.

**Swarm management and prevention.** When a hive becomes too crowded, bees prepare
to swarm (send out a new colony). The beekeeper can prevent swarming (better for
the beekeeper) or allow swarming and hive the swarm (increases colonies). The
specialist manages this consciously.

**Feeding and nutrition.** Bees need adequate honey stores for winter. The specialist
judges when to harvest (leaving adequate stores for bees) and may feed bees sugar
water if stores are too low.

**Disease and pest prevention.** Various pests and diseases can kill colonies.
Mites, fungal diseases, and bacterial problems are managed by breeding for disease
resistance and monitoring hive health.

**Honey grading and quality.** Different nectar sources produce different honey —
different colors, different flavors. The specialist can judge quality and flavor
profile by color and taste.

**Wax recovery and use.** Beeswax can be rendered (melted and cleaned) for candles
or other uses. The specialist recovers wax from old comb.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Recognize and manage swarm pressure.** The specialist judges when a colony is
thinking about swarming and can either prevent it or capture the swarm.

**Judge adequate food stores.** The specialist lifts hives to judge weight and knows
if bees have enough food for winter.

**Recognize and treat bee diseases.** Various diseases can kill colonies. The
specialist can recognize early signs and take action.

**Produce consistent, high-quality honey.** Honey quality depends on which nectar
was visited and hive health. The specialist manages this.

**Develop and maintain good bee temperament.** Some colonies are naturally less
defensive. The specialist selects bees for good temperament.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Colony dying in winter.** Starvation or disease kills colonies that were not
properly managed.

**Loss to swarms.** Colonies that swarm reduce population and honey production.

**Pest and mite infestations.** Unmanaged infestations can kill colonies.

**Mold and fungal disease.** Damp hives develop fungal problems that kill colonies.

**Low honey production.** Stressed or poorly-managed colonies produce less honey.

## Fishing

**The specialist fisherman reads water and seasons, uses multiple methods, and reliably
produces catch.** A good fishing spot reliably feeds a settlement.

### Tools of the Trade

**Hooks of various sizes.** Bronze or iron hooks for different fish and methods.

**Lines and nets.** Multiple nets with different mesh sizes for different fish. Lines of
different weights and lengths.

**Fishing spears and leisters (multi-pronged spears).** For spearing fish in shallow water.

**Weirs and traps.** Permanent or semi-permanent structures that direct fish into enclosed
spaces where they cannot escape.

### Core Knowledge

**Fish behavior and seasons.** Different fish feed at different times of day and year.
Fish move with seasons. The specialist knows when and where fish congregate.

**Water reading.** Ripples, color, current patterns indicate where fish hide. The specialist
reads these signs.

**Lure and bait selection.** Different fish prefer different baits. The specialist matches
bait to fish and season.

**Multiple simultaneous methods.** While nets work, the specialist may also set traps and
lines to maximize catch.

**Preservation and transport.** Fresh fish spoils fast. The specialist knows how to salt,
smoke, and pack fish to preserve them.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Predict fish movement.** The specialist knows when and where fish will be without trial
and error.

**Select the right method and bait.** Multiple approaches work. The specialist chooses what
works best for the situation.

**Maximize catch rate.** The specialist uses multiple methods simultaneously and maintains
consistent production.

**Preserve large catches.** Large catches must be preserved quickly and correctly or spoil.
The specialist does this efficiently.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Seasonal fish absence.** Some seasons have no fish in a location. Moving or alternative
food is necessary.

**Overfishing a location.** Taking too many fish too fast depletes the population.

**Equipment failure.** Nets tear, hooks break, traps fail. Repair and replacement is constant work.

**Spoilage of catch.** More fish than can be preserved spoil and are wasted.

## Net-Making

**The net-maker creates nets with consistent mesh, proper strength, and shapes suited to
different fishing methods.** Quality nets are valuable and long-lived.

### Tools of the Trade

**Cord of consistent thickness.** Twisted fiber in various diameters.

**Net gauge (wooden frame with notches).** To maintain consistent mesh size while tying knots.

**Needle (wooden or bone).** For threading cord through the net to repair damage.

### Core Knowledge

**Mesh size and fish type.** Different fish sizes require different mesh. The specialist knows
which mesh for which fish.

**Knot strength and consistency.** The specialist ties knots that are uniform and do not slip
or tighten unpredictably.

**Net shape and purpose.** Casting nets have different shapes than drag nets or bag traps.
The specialist designs nets for their intended use.

**Cord selection and joining.** When a length of cord runs out, the specialist joins a new
length invisibly without weakening the net.

**Maintenance and repair.** Nets with small tears can be repaired. The specialist identifies
damage and fixes it before the tear spreads.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Create uniform mesh.** Irregular knots create irregular mesh that fishes inconsistently.

**Maintain consistent knot tightness.** Loose knots slip, tight knots bind. The specialist
controls both.

**Join cord invisibly.** Joined cord must be as strong as the original. The specialist
achieves this.

**Shape nets properly.** Nets must hang and cast correctly. Poor shape tangles and catches
air.

**Repair nets efficiently.** The specialist can repair small tears so skilfully that the
repair is stronger than the surrounding net.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Nets with weak knots.** Nets that unravel during use are worthless.

**Mesh that fishes inconsistently.** Irregular mesh catches some fish and lets others through.

**Knots that slip under load.** Improperly-tied knots loosen or tighten unpredictably, causing
mesh to collapse.

**Joining points that fail.** Joined cord that breaks under load causes net failure at exactly
the wrong moment.

## Rope-Making

**The rope-maker produces rope of consistent strength, thickness, and handling qualities suited
to different uses.** A sailing ship or settlement depends on reliable rope.

### Tools of the Trade

**Fiber preparation tools.** For preparing flax, hemp, or hair.

**Rope-walk or frame.** Long space for twisting cord into rope.

**Measuring device.** To control rope thickness and length.

### Core Knowledge

**Fiber selection and preparation.** Different fibers have different properties. Hemp is
strong and weather-resistant. Flax is supple. Hair is short-lived but strong. The specialist
selects fiber for intended use.

**Twist consistency and direction.** The specialist maintains consistent tension and twist
during cord and rope production.

**Rope thickness and strength.** Thicker rope is stronger. The specialist produces rope of
exact thickness for different loads.

**Joining long rope.** When a twist of cord runs out, the specialist joins a new supply so
the join is as strong as the rope.

**Rope handling and coiling.** Rope that is stored improperly deteriorates. The specialist
knows how to store rope to preserve it.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Achieve consistent twist.** Inconsistently-twisted rope has weak spots that fail unpredictably.

**Control rope thickness.** Rope that is too thin for its intended use breaks. Rope that is
thicker than necessary is wasteful.

**Join rope invisibly.** Joined rope must be as strong as the original or knots just move
the failure point.

**Match fiber type to use.** Different fibers have different properties. The specialist
chooses correctly.

**Maximize fiber yield.** Proper preparation and technique waste less fiber.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Rope that breaks under load.** Weak spots from inconsistent twisting cause catastrophic
failure.

**Rope that stretches or shrinks.** Rope made from wet fiber or improperly dried rope changes
length after use.

**Rope rot from storage failure.** Rope stored damp rots. The specialist prevents this.

**Joining points that fail.** Poorly-joined rope fails at the join during critical load.

## Shipwright

**The shipwright designs and builds seaworthy ships capable of carrying cargo, crew, and
weathering rough water.** A good shipwright is wealth and power to a settlement.

### Tools of the Trade

**Woodworking tools.** Axes, adzes, saws, chisels.

**Measuring and marking tools.** For laying out ship design.

**Large timber.** Planks, keels, frames of substantial size.

**Rope and fastenings.** For attaching components.

### Core Knowledge

**Ship design and geometry.** The shape of the ship determines how it handles water, wind,
and cargo. The specialist understands hull shape, beam, depth, and how these affect handling.

**Timber selection and seasoning.** Different woods have different properties. Oak is strong
but heavy. Ash is flexible. Spruce is light. The specialist knows which wood for which purpose.

**Joinery and fastenings.** Parts of the ship are joined with wooden joints, iron fastening,
and rope. The specialist creates joints that are strong and weather the working of the ship.

**Buoyancy and stability.** A ship must float without heeling excessively. The specialist
calculates weight distribution and buoyancy.

**Water-fitness and seaworthiness.** A seaworthy ship can survive rough water, heavy weather,
and the continuous working of wind and waves. The specialist builds with this in mind.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Design a ship that sails well.** Poor hull shape makes ships slow, unhandled, and dangerous.

**Select timber correctly.** Wrong timber choice creates ships that rot, weigh too much, or
break.

**Create proper joinery.** Loose joints fail under the constant working of a ship.

**Calculate loading and stability.** Overloaded or improperly loaded ships capsize.

**Build for the sea.** Ships built for rivers or calm water fail in rough conditions.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Ships that sink.** Improper design, weak joinery, or poor loading cause sinking.

**Ships that are unseaworthy.** Ships that cannot handle weather are dangerous.

**Ships that rot.** Wrong timber choice or improper construction allows rot to penetrate.

**Ships that work loose.** Constant motion causes loose joinery to fail over time.

**Ships that are slow or unmanageable.** Poor hull design creates ships that cannot sail
properly.

## Caulking

**The caulker seals ships and boats against water, knowing when joints need caulking and
which materials and techniques create lasting seals.** A good caulk seal keeps a ship floating.

### Tools of the Trade

**Caulk hammer and chisel (caulking iron).** Specialized tools for driving caulk tight.

**Caulk material.** Oakum (tarred fiber), wool, linen, hair.

**Pitch, tar, or other sealant.** For sealing caulk.

**Measuring tools.** To assess gap width and caulk consistency.

### Core Knowledge

**Gap assessment and joint reading.** The caulker examines joints to determine how wide
the gap is and whether it needs caulking or other repair.

**Caulk material selection.** Different gaps require different materials. Thick gaps need thick
caulk. Thin gaps need thin caulk.

**Driving technique.** Caulk must be driven tight without splitting the wood or breaking the
caulking iron. The specialist knows the right force and angle.

**Sealant application.** Once caulk is driven tight, sealant is applied to prevent water from
washing the caulk out.

**Seasonal movement and the working ship.** Ships work and move with water and weather. Caulk
must be checked and replaced regularly.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Assess which joints need caulking.** Some joints naturally tighten. Some need caulking to
seal. The specialist knows the difference.

**Choose the right material.** Wrong caulk material fails under water pressure or washing.

**Drive caulk properly.** Caulk that is not driven tight works water. Caulk that is driven too hard
splits the wood.

**Apply sealant effectively.** Sealant that is applied wrong washes out or cracks.

**Maintain caulked ships over time.** Caulk must be checked and replaced regularly.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Caulk that fails.** Caulk not driven tight, wrong material, or sealant failure causes water
to persist.

**Wood damage from caulking.** Driving too hard splits planks or frames.

**Caulk that washes out.** Sealant failure or wave action removes caulk.

**Leaks after caulking.** If the underlying joint is damaged or rotted, caulking the surface
does not solve the problem.
