## Jeweler

**The jeweler creates objects of beauty in precious metal and stone, working at scales from
fingernail-size to hand-sized decorated objects.** A skilled jeweler creates heirlooms and
status symbols.

### Tools of the Trade

**Anvil and hammer.** Small anvil for precision work. Multiple hammers for different strikes.

**Crucible and furnace.** For melting metal at high temperature.

**Molds and casting equipment.** For casting components.

**Files and gravers.** For detail work and engraving.

**Soldering tools and materials.** Solder, flux, and heat control.

**Magnification option.** If available, a magnifying glass for fine detail work.

### Core Knowledge

**Metal properties and behavior.** Different metals have different melting points, hardness,
and behavior. Gold is soft and workable. Silver is harder. Bronze requires high temperature.
The specialist knows the limits of each metal.

**Casting techniques and mold-making.** Clay molds can hold metal patterns. The specialist
can cast precise components from molds.

**Soldering and joining.** Metal joints must be strong and invisible. The specialist creates
joints that are perfect.

**Engraving and detail work.** Fine lines, patterns, and images can be cut into metal surface.
The specialist has a steady hand and deep knowledge of detail.

**Stone setting and inlay.** Precious stones can be set by wrapping metal around them or
setting in carved hollows. The specialist creates settings that are secure and beautiful.

**Color work and patination.** Surface color can be achieved through oxidation, enameling,
or inlay. The specialist controls color and finish.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Control metal temperature and flow.** Casting requires precise temperature. Too cool and
metal is thick. Too hot and it flows wrong.

**Create invisible joints.** Visible solder lines ruin the appearance of jewelry.

**Do fine detail work.** Engraving requires steady hands and years of practice.

**Set stones securely.** A loosely-set stone falls out. The specialist's stones stay forever.

**Achieve consistent beauty.** Each piece shows the maker's skill. The specialist creates
pieces that are uniformly beautiful.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Casting failures.** Molds that crack, metal that does not flow, inclusions in the casting.

**Solder that is visible.** Poor solder work is obvious and ugly.

**Joints that fail.** Weak solder or rivets cause pieces to fall apart.

**Stones that fall out.** Insecure setting loses stones.

**Breakage during wear.** Jewelry that breaks during normal use was poorly made.

## Glass-Bead Maker

**The glass-bead maker creates beads of various colors, shapes, and sizes through careful
control of temperature and material.** Beads are valuable trade goods.

### Tools of the Trade

**Furnace or fire pit.** For heating glass to workability.

**Metal rods and forms.** For shaping molten glass.

**Molds (stone or ceramic).** For creating consistent shapes.

**Colored glass sources.** Or techniques to color clear glass.

**Annealing oven or slow-cooling chamber.** To prevent cracking during cooling.

### Core Knowledge

**Glass properties and behavior.** Different glasses have different melting points and colors.
Some glasses are harder than others. The specialist knows glass material.

**Color sources and mixing.** Metals and minerals create glass colors. Copper creates blue/green.
Cobalt creates deep blue. Gold creates red/purple. The specialist knows how to create desired
colors.

**Temperature control.** Too hot and glass discolors or becomes brittle. Too cool and it won't
shape. The specialist maintains precise temperature.

**Shaping while hot.** Molten glass must be shaped before it cools. The specialist has speed
and precision.

**Annealing and cooling protocols.** Glass that cools too fast cracks. The specialist cools glass
slowly and carefully to avoid internal stress.

**Bead consistency and quality control.** All beads should be uniform. The specialist maintains
consistency.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Control glass color.** Colors are unpredictable and often wrong.

**Maintain consistent beads.** Each bead is different — different size, color, shape.

**Prevent cracking.** Beads crack during cooling unpredictably.

**Create complex shapes.** Complex bead shapes require very precise temperature and technique.

**Achieve professional quality.** Fine-quality beads are consistent and beautiful.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Color that is wrong.** Too much or too little colorant changes the result.

**Cracking during cooling.** Internal stress from improper cooling causes cracking.

**Inconsistent results.** Variation in temperature, glass source, or technique causes batch
variation.

**Cloudy or dull glass.** Over-heating or impurity causes glass to become dull.

**Beads that are too brittle.** Glass that is not properly annealed is brittle and breaks
during stringing or use.

## Bone-Carver

**The bone-carver creates objects of utility and beauty in bone and antler, from small toggle
buttons to large decorated combs and handles.** Fine bone-carving is valued across cultures.

### Tools of the Trade

**Saws (bone saw, fine saw).** For cutting bone.

**Knives and gravers.** For carving and detail work.

**Drills and augers.** For creating holes and channels.

**Abrading tools (sandstone, sharkskin).** For smoothing and finishing.

**Magnification option.** If available, for fine detail work.

### Core Knowledge

**Bone structure and grain.** Bone has grain direction and stress lines. The specialist carves
along the grain to avoid breakage.

**Tool sharpness and cutting technique.** Sharp tools cut cleanly. Dull tools tear bone.
The specialist maintains sharp tools and uses proper technique.

**Material selection and quality control.** Different bones are suitable for different uses.
The specialist knows which bone for which purpose.

**Design and proportion.** The specialist designs objects with proper proportions and design.

**Joining and attachment.** Pieces can be joined with pegs or glue. The specialist creates
strong, invisible joints.

**Antiquing and finishing.** Brown staining or patina can create aged appearance. The specialist
knows finishing techniques.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Prevent breakage.** Carving against grain causes stress fractures.

**Achieve smooth surfaces.** Crude carving leaves deep tool marks.

**Create fine detail.** Details require steady hands and years of practice.

**Design attractive items.** Good design enhances the beauty of bone.

**Create joints that are invisible.** Visible joints ruin the appearance.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Breakage during carving.** Grain lines and stress cause unexpected breaks.

**Over-carving and weakening.** Carving too aggressively leaves thin, weak sections.

**Poor surface finish.** Surface marks and irregularities are obvious.

**Design that is clumsy.** Poor proportions or ugly design make items look amateurish.

**Color loss or staining.** Bone that is not properly finished discolors or stains over time.

## Antler-Worker

**The antler-worker creates functional and decorative objects from antler material,
exploiting the natural properties and curves of antler to create items of strength and beauty.**

### Tools of the Trade

**Saws (bone and antler saws).** For cutting antler along grain and across it.

**Planes and scrapers.** For smoothing curved surfaces.

**Drills and augers.** For creating holes and dowels.

**Lathe (if available).** For creating round objects like buttons and decorative elements.

**Abrading and polishing materials.** For fine finishing.

### Core Knowledge

**Antler structure and properties.** Antler has different properties in different directions.
The specialist understands antler grain, density, and stress.

**Wood-working technique applied to antler.** Many woodworking techniques work on antler.
The specialist applies these modifications for antler's properties.

**Cutting plans and minimizing waste.** The specialist plans cuts to maximize yield from each
piece of antler.

**Decoration and carving.** Antler can be engraved or carved. The specialist creates decorative
elements.

**Drying and sealing.** Fresh antler must be dried properly and sealed to prevent rot.

**Achieving transparent and translucent effects.** Thin antler becomes slightly translucent
when finished well.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Predict crack lines.** Hidden stress in antler causes unexpected cracks.

**Achieve smooth curves.** Curved antler surfaces require special technique to smooth.

**Design efficient cutting plans.** Poor planning wastes usable material.

**Create fine detail.** Engraving and carving require steady hands and practice.

**Finish antler to highlight its properties.** Proper finishing enhances the natural beauty
of antler.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Unexpected cracking.** Stress lines in antler cause cracks that ruin pieces.

**Rot and discoloration.** Improperly dried or sealed antler rots or stains.

**Rough surfaces that cannot be smoothed.** Some cuts and techniques cannot be smoothed
no matter what.

**Over-carving and weakening.** Carving too much leaves weak, thin sections.

**Items that are structurally weak.** Poor cutting plan or design creates items that break
under use.

## Comb-Maker

**The comb-maker creates combs and decorative objects using bone, antler, and wood as material,
producing items that are strong, beautiful, and perfectly functional.** A fine comb is a valued
object.

### Tools of the Trade

**Saws (fine saws for cutting teeth).** Precision necessary for spacing and consistency.

**Planes and gravers.** For shaping and detail work.

**Drills and augers.** For holes and channels.

**Abrading materials.** For smoothing teeth and surfaces.

**Handles and decorative elements.** For embellishment.

### Core Knowledge

**Tooth design and spacing.** Different combs have different tooth spacing for different hair
types and uses. The specialist knows proper proportions.

**Cutting and sawing technique.** Teeth must be consistent and evenly spaced. The specialist
maintains precision.

**Handle design and balance.** The handle must be comfortable and properly balanced. The
specialist designs with this in mind.

**Material selection for different uses.** Fine-toothed combs need stronger material. Wide-toothed
combs can be softer. The specialist matches material to use.

**Joining handle and teeth.** The join must be invisible and strong. The specialist creates
perfect joins.

**Decoration and finishing.** Combs can be decorated with carving or inlay. The specialist
creates beautiful finished pieces.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Cut consistent teeth.** Uneven spacing or variable tooth size is obvious.

**Create acceptable tooth proportions.** Wrong proportions make combs unusable or ugly.

**Design comfortable handles.** Poor handle design makes combs unpleasant to use.

**Join handle and teeth invisibly.** Visible joints are ugly and suggest poor quality.

**Create perfectly smooth teeth.** Rough teeth catch hair instead of combing.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Teeth that break during cutting.** Too-thin teeth or teeth cut against grain break easily.

**Teeth that break during use.** Teeth that are too thin or poorly designed break when combing.

**Inconsistent spacing.** Uneven tooth spacing makes combing unpleasant.

**Poor handle design.** Uncomfortable handles make combs undesirable.

**Joint failure.** Handle that separates or loosens removes the primary use value.

## Healer / Herbalist

**The healer uses plant medicine, touch, and study of the patient to diagnose and treat disease,
injury, and chronic conditions.** A good healer can extend life, reduce suffering, and save lives.

### Tools of the Trade

**Medicinal plants and plant preparations.** Extensive knowledge of local plants and how to
prepare them — dried, infused, ground, fermented.

**Mortar and pestle.** For crushing and preparing medicines.

**Vessels and storage.** For preparing and storing medicines.

**Bandages and cloth.** For dressing wounds and applying poultices.

**Burning materials.** For burning wounds or cauterization if needed.

### Core Knowledge

**Plant identification and properties.** The healer knows which plants treat which conditions.
Which plants are local, which must be traded for, seasonal availability.

**Plant preservation and dosing.** Medicines must be stored properly and dosed correctly.
Too little is ineffective, too much is poison.

**Wound treatment and infection prevention.** Infection is the greatest killer of wounded.
The healer knows how to clean wounds, which plants prevent infection, when wounds are healing
versus festering.

**Pain management.** Various plants reduce pain. The healer knows which, when, and how to measure
dose.

**Diagnosis and patient assessment.** The healer examines the patient — color, temperature, smell
of breath and bodily fluids, swelling, appetite. Diagnosis guides treatment.

**Chronic condition management.** Some conditions recur or persist. The healer finds plants and
practices that relieve symptoms.

**Knowing limits.** The specialist knows when a patient is untreatable and when to refuse or prepare
the patient for death.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Diagnose correctly.** Most amateurs treat symptoms, not causes. The specialist diagnoses the
underlying problem.

**Dose medicines precisely.** Dosing requires knowledge of plant strength, patient weight and age,
and conditions.

**Prevent infection.** The specialist treats wounds so that infection is prevented.

**Recognize poison versus medicine.** Same plants at different doses can heal or kill. The specialist
knows the line.

**Manage chronic pain or conditions.** Long-term treatment requires monitoring and adjustment.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Incorrect identification of plants.** Misidentified plants can poison instead of heal.

**Over-dosing the patient.** Too much medicine causes poisoning or death.

**Ineffective treatment.** Some conditions are untreatable. The healer must recognize when
treatment is failing.

**Infection becoming systemic.** Despite treatment, some infections overcome the body and kill.

**Patient compliance.** Healing requires the patient to follow instructions. Noncompliance
causes treatment failure.

## Midwife

**The midwife attends births, knows how to interpret labor and complications, and acts to keep
mother and baby alive through the dangerous process of childbirth.** A skilled midwife saves lives.

### Tools of the Trade

**Clean cloth and bandages.** For blood and afterbirth.

**Warm water.** For cleaning and as needed during labor.

**Plant medicines.** To accelerate labor, reduce bleeding, manage pain.

**Instruments if available.** Simple tools to check presentation and help extraction.

### Core Knowledge

**Labor stages and timeline.** Normal labor has distinct stages. The specialist recognizes when
labor is proceeding normally and when something is wrong.

**Fetal position and presentation.** The baby should come head-first. The specialist examines
the laboring mother to determine position.

**Managing abnormal presentations.** Breech (backwards), sideways, or other presentations require
special technique.

**Hemorrhage management.** Some bleeding is normal. Abnormal bleeding requires fast action.
The specialist knows which plants accelerate uterine contraction to stop bleeding.

**Afterbirth management.** The placenta must come out. If it doesn't, the mother can die.
The specialist knows how to encourage this.

**Newborn care.** Some newborns breathe, some do not. Some are alive at first but die in the
first days. The specialist assesses and acts.

**Maternal complications.** Fever, convulsions, emotional distress, or milk failure can
follow birth. The specialist treats these.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Recognize abnormal presentations.** Breech births require special handling or they fail.

**Act in hemorrhage emergencies.** Uncontrolled bleeding requires fast, correct action.

**Manage afterbirth failure.** If the placenta does not come, the mother can bleed to death.
The specialist accelerates uterine contraction.

**Recognize neonatal distress.** Some infants are born alive but will not survive. The specialist
recognizes this early.

**Manage maternal complications.** Fever, infection, or emotional problems after birth require
treatment.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Inability to turn breech babies.** Some breech presentations cannot be turned and require
extraction technique beyond the amateur's knowledge.

**Hemorrhage that cannot be controlled.** Despite all efforts, some mothers bleed to death.

**Placental retention.** If the placenta does not come and cannot be forced, the mother dies.

**Infection of the mother.** Despite cleanliness, some births lead to infection.

**Newborn death from unknown cause.** Some babies are born alive and healthy-seeming but die
in the first days.

## Barber-Surgeon

**The barber-surgeon performs bloodletting, tooth extraction, wound care, and minor surgery.
A skilled practitioner combines knowledge of anatomy with practical speed and precision.**

### Tools of the Trade

**Lancet and fleam.** Sharp blades for opening veins for bloodletting.

**Tooth extractors (forceps, pelican).**Specialized tools for pulling teeth without breakage.

**Surgical knives and saws.** For surgical procedures including amputation.

**Sutures and needle.** For closing wounds.

**Cautery iron.** For burning to stop bleeding or burn away infected tissue.

**Bandages and cloth.** For dressing wounds.

### Core Knowledge

**Venous and arterial anatomy.** The specialist knows where veins and arteries are located,
their size, and how to open them safely.

**Tooth anatomy and extraction technique.** Each tooth has different roots. Extraction angle
and technique matter for clean extraction and complete root removal.

**Wound healing and infection prevention.** Clean wounds heal. Infected wounds kill. The specialist
knows how to keep wounds clean and recognize when they become infected.

**Pain management for procedures.** Alcohol, various plant medicines, and ice can reduce pain
and shock.

**Hemorrhage control.** Tourniquet technique, cautery, and suturing all control bleeding.
The specialist knows when to apply which.

**Amputation if necessary.** In cases of severe injury or gangrene, amputation saves life.
The specialist can perform this quickly and correctly.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Bloodlet safely.** Opening wrong structures in bloodletting causes death.

**Extract teeth completely and cleanly.** Amateur extraction breaks teeth and leaves roots
that fester.

**Prevent or manage wound infection.** Infection is the leading cause of death from wounds.
The specialist keeps wounds from becoming infected.

**Manage hemorrhage properly.** Uncontrolled bleeding causes death. The specialist knows
multiple methods to stop bleeding.

**Amputate safely if needed.** Amputation requires speed and knowledge of anatomy or the
patient dies from blood loss or shock.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Opening wrong vessel in bloodletting.** Opening an artery instead of a vein causes death.

**Incomplete tooth extraction.** Leaving roots causes much worse infection than the original tooth.

**Infection of wounds.** Despite treatment, wounds can become infected and prove fatal.

**Hemorrhage from wounds or procedures.** Bleeding that cannot be controlled causes death.

**Surgical shock or complications.** Major procedures like amputation can trigger shock that
kills the patient.

## Tooth-Puller

**The tooth-puller specializes in extracting teeth cleanly, managing pain and bleeding, and
recognizing when extraction is necessary versus when the tooth should be left.** A skilled
tooth-puller prevents more damage than the original tooth.

### Tools of the Trade

**Dental forceps and elevators.** Specialized tools of various sizes for different teeth.

**Lancet and cautery.** For opening gums if necessary and managing bleeding.

**Medicinal plants.** For pain reduction and infection prevention.

**Cloth and bandages.** For securing the patient and dressing after extraction.

### Core Knowledge

**Tooth anatomy.** Different teeth have different root shapes and numbers of roots.
The specialist knows tooth-by-tooth anatomy.

**Extraction angles and leverage.** Applying force at the right angle with the right tool
extracts the tooth cleanly and minimizes trauma.

**Gum management.** Sometimes gums must be opened to access the tooth. The specialist does
this cleanly.

**Pain management.** Various plant medicines and pressure techniques reduce pain and shock.
The specialist knows many approaches.

**Hemorrhage control.** Sockets can bleed heavily. The specialist uses multiple techniques
to manage bleeding.

**Post-extraction care.** Infection prevention after extraction is critical.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Extract teeth without breaking them.** Broken teeth leave roots that fester.

**Extract roots completely.** Root fragments left in the socket cause infection.

**Minimize trauma to surrounding teeth and gums.** Poor technique damages neighboring teeth.

**Manage pain effectively.** Pain can trigger shock. The specialist minimizes pain through
technique and medicine.

**Prevent post-extraction complications.** Infection or excessive bleeding can kill the patient.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Broken teeth during extraction.** Roots left behind necrotize and abscess.

**Excessive trauma to adjacent teeth.** Aggressive extraction damages surrounding teeth.

**Hemorrhage that cannot be controlled.** Sockets can bleed for days or weeks.

**Facial swelling and infection** despite treatment.

**Osteomyelitis.** Bone infection can set in despite treatment.

## Bonesetter

**The bonesetter repairs broken and dislocated bones, knowing the anatomy of each bone,
the proper alignment, and the patience and timing of healing.** A skilled bonesetter restores
function that would otherwise be lost to disability.

### Tools of the Trade

**Splinting material.** Cloth, wood, straw for creating splints.

**Measurements and gauges.** To ensure bones heal in proper alignment.

**Medicinal plants.** For pain reduction and inflammation management.

**Bandages and wrappings.** For securing splints and immobilization.

**Manual reduction equipment if available.** Leverage and counter-leverage to set bones.

### Core Knowledge

**Bone anatomy.** The specialist knows every major bone's shape, where common breaks occur,
and proper alignment for each break.

**Fracture assessment.** Examining the limb (and if available, palpating beneath the skin),
the specialist determines if the bone is broken, where, and how badly.

**Reduction technique.** Pulling bones into alignment requires leverage and counter-leverage.
The specialist does this quickly and correctly, minimizing operator-induced injury.

**Immobilization and healing timeline.** Bones must be held perfectly still during healing.
Healing timeline varies by bone and age.

**Recognizing and managing complications.** Infections, nerve damage, and vascular injury
can occur. The specialist recognizes these and acts.

**Rehabilitation.** After immobilization, joints must be carefully re-mobilized or they
stiffen permanently. The specialist knows the proper timeline and exercises.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Reduce complex fractures.** Some breaks require specific reduction technique.

**Maintain perfect alignment during healing.** Even small misalignment causes permanent disability.

**Recognize when surgery (amputation) is necessary.** Some limbs cannot be saved. The specialist
knows when to stop trying and recommend amputation.

**Manage post-healing rehabilitation.** Proper rehab is critical to regaining function.

**Recognize and manage complications.** Infection, vascular injury, or nerve damage require
different treatment.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Bones that heal crooked.** Misalignment results in permanent disability and pain.

**Infections from open fractures.** Compound fractures often become infected despite treatment.

**Nerve damage from improper reduction.** Aggressive reduction can damage nerves.

**Vascular injury limiting blood flow.** Poor set can compromise blood supply to the limb.

**Permanent stiffness from over-immobilization.** Joints that are immobilized too long
become permanently stiff.

**Permanent deformity from inadequate reduction.** Bones that are not properly set before
healing cause permanent deformity.
