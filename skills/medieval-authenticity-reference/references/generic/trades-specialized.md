# Trades — Specialized Skills (Trade-Learned)

**Category:** Generic Medieval
**Part of:** `medieval-authenticity-reference` skill

The skills that take years to learn, months of practice to maintain, and
a lifetime to master. These are not things a man picks up by watching.
These are the trades passed from master to apprentice, from father to son,
from the old woman in the village to the girl she chose to teach.

A carpenter does not just cut wood. He reads wood — sees the grain
direction that determines strength, knows by weight whether a beam is
green or seasoned, feels with his thumb whether a joint is tight enough
or will work loose in six months. This knowledge lives in the hands,
not in descriptions. These entries describe what the specialist knows
and does — so that prose can show the gap between the trained hand and
the desperate amateur.

## Carpentry

**The carpenter is the most essential tradesman in any northern
settlement.** Every building, every ship, every bridge, every cart,
every bed-frame, every loom, every door — wood. In a culture that
builds in timber, the carpenter is what the architect and the civil
engineer and the furniture maker and the structural surveyor are,
combined, in the modern world.

### Tools of the Trade

**The axe is the primary tool.** Not the felling axe and not the
weapon — the carpenter's side axe, with a flat face on one side
and a bevelled cutting edge on the other. This tool hews a log to
a flat surface, shaves joints to fit, and trims timber with a
control that the untrained hand cannot imagine. A skilled carpenter
with a side axe produces a surface smoother than most men can
achieve with modern sandpaper.

**The adze** — an axe blade mounted perpendicular to the handle,
swung between the legs into the surface of a timber laid flat.
Used for hollowing (troughs, boats, coffins) and for smoothing
large surfaces. Terrifying to watch: the blade passes inches from
the carpenter's shins with every stroke. The apprentice learns by
watching the scar on the master's leg from the one time he
misjudged.

**Auger and bore.** Drilling tools for creating peg holes and
mortises. The spoon bit turns slowly, clearing chips with each
rotation. Holes must be dead-straight and correctly sized for the
peg — too loose and the joint rattles, too tight and the timber
splits when the peg is driven.

**The drawknife.** A blade with two handles, pulled toward the
body. Used on a shaving horse (a foot-operated clamp) to round
and smooth poles, chair legs, tool handles, and spokes. The
carpenter controls the cutting depth by adjusting the blade angle
— thousandths of an inch, by feel.

**The marking tools.** A chalk line for straight marks on timber.
A scribe (a sharp steel point) for marking joints. A plumb bob
(a weight on a string) for vertical lines. A level (a trough
of water) for horizontal. These are the tools that separate
the tradesman from the bodger — because a building that is not
plumb and level falls down.

### Core Knowledge

**Reading grain.** The carpenter knows that a board with grain
running diagonally will split under load. That a knot in a
beam's tension face weakens it more than a knot in its
compression face. That a warped board was sawn incorrectly
and cannot be flattened. That reaction wood (from a tree that
grew on a slope, fighting gravity) twists as it dries. He
reads these things in the standing tree, before the first cut.

**Joinery.** The carpenter's art is the joint. Mortise-and-tenon:
a projecting tongue on one piece fits into a rectangular hole
in another, pegged through. Dovetails: interlocking fans that
resist pulling apart. Scarf joints: two timbers joined end-to-end
with overlapping cuts so the joint is as strong as the timber
itself. Lap joints, bridle joints, tongue-and-groove. Each has
a purpose. Each has failure modes the carpenter knows by
experience.

**Seasoning.** Green timber is easy to work but shrinks as it
dries — sometimes dramatically. Joints cut in green wood work
loose in six months. The carpenter knows which timber to use
green (for bending, for cooperage, for rapid field construction)
and which to season first (for permanent buildings, for
furniture, for doors and shutters that must not warp). Oak
takes two to five years to season fully. Ash, one year. Pine,
one to two years.

**Timber selection.** Oak for strength and permanence — it resists
rot and takes enormous loads. Ash for flexibility and shock
resistance — tool handles, cart frames, oars. Pine and spruce
for long straight timbers — building beams, masts, planking.
Elm for anything in constant contact with water — it resists
rot when permanently wet (bridge piles, well linings, boat
keels). Willow and hazel for flexible rods (wattle, baskets,
hurdle fencing). Birch for everything else.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Cut a mortise-and-tenon that holds under load.** The tenon
must fit the mortise with zero visible gap on all four sides.
The peg hole through both pieces must align perfectly. The
shoulders must seat flat. One degree off and the joint
either binds (will not assemble) or is loose (will fail under
load). An apprentice practices this joint for months before
the master trusts him on a structural beam.

**Build a roof that does not spread.** A roof pushes outward
on the walls. The carpenter's tying beams, collar beams, and
king posts resist this thrust. Get the geometry wrong and the
walls bow outward under the roof's weight — slowly, invisibly,
until the whole structure shifts and the door no longer opens.

**Hang a door that stays hung.** A door is a precision
instrument disguised as a plank. It must swing freely without
binding, seat against its frame without gaps, and stay in plane
as the wood moves with the seasons. The amateur's door binds
in summer and rattles in winter.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Checking.** Deep cracks that develop as timber dries too
quickly. An exposed beam in full sun can check so deeply that
its structural integrity is compromised. The carpenter controls
drying by sealing the end grain, keeping timber in shade, and
allowing airflow around stacked material.

**Rot.** The carpenter's perpetual enemy. Anywhere wood contacts
soil or sits in persistent moisture, rot begins. The carpenter
raises timber off the ground on stone pads, angles exposed
surfaces to shed water, and uses rot-resistant species (oak,
elm) where moisture is unavoidable.

**Insect damage.** Beetle larvae boring through structural
timber can weaken a building invisibly. The carpenter recognizes
bore-dust, exit holes, and the hollow sound of infested wood.
He chars the surface of buried timbers (the carbonized layer
resists insects) and avoids sapwood (the outer, softer ring of
the tree that insects prefer).

## Wheelwright

**The wheelwright's trade is geometry and violence combined.**
A wheel must be perfectly round, its spokes perfectly evenly
spaced, its tyre (the iron band around the rim) shrunk on
with fire so tight that it compresses the entire structure
into a single unified ring of force. It must survive ruts,
rocks, side-loads, and the accumulated shock of ten thousand
impacts while carrying a load that would break a bridge.

### The Wheel's Anatomy

**The hub (nave).** A thick cylinder of elm, bored through
the center for the axle. The spoke mortises are cut into the
hub at precisely calculated angles — because the spokes do
not sit straight. They are dished: angled outward so the
wheel's face forms a shallow cone. This dish gives the wheel
lateral stability. The angles must be identical for every
spoke. One degree of error and the wheel wobbles at speed.

**The spokes.** Traditionally twelve or fourteen, split (never
sawn) from straight-grained oak or ash. Splitting follows
the grain exactly, which means the spoke's full length has
continuous fibres — far stronger than a sawn spoke, where
the saw cuts across the grain. Each spoke is shaved to shape
with a drawknife on a shaving horse, tapered from hub to rim,
with a round tenon at the hub end and a shaped tongue at the
rim end.

**The felloes.** Curved sections of ash or elm that form the
rim. Each felloe spans two or three spokes. They are cut from
naturally curved timber or steam-bent to shape. The spoke
tongues are driven into mortises in the felloes, and the
felloes are dowelled together end-to-end to form a continuous
ring.

**The tyre.** A flat iron band, made slightly smaller in
circumference than the wheel. The wheelwright heats the tyre
in a fire until it expands. It is dropped over the wheel and
quenched with water. As the iron contracts with cooling, it
compresses the felloes together, draws the spokes tight into
the hub, and locks the entire structure into compression. The
wheel cannot be disassembled without cutting the tyre off. This
is the moment: if the tyre is too small, it crushes the felloes.
If too large, it never tightens and the wheel falls apart on
the first pothole.

### Core Knowledge

**Dish and camber.** The wheel is not flat. It is dished — the
spokes angle outward from the hub — so that when a loaded cart
sits on the ground, the weight presses the spokes downward and
the lowest spoke sits approximately vertical. This means the
load transfers down a straight spoke to the ground. If the dish
is wrong, the bottom spoke is angled, the load transfers at an
angle, and the hub bearing grinds unevenly.

**Wood selection.** Elm for the hub (it does not split easily
when bored and mortised). Oak or ash for the spokes (strength
and resilience). Ash for the felloes (it bends without
breaking). The wheelwright touches timber to assess dryness,
taps it to hear if it is sound, and refuses material that a
carpenter would accept.

**Calculating spoke angles.** The wheelwright divides the hub
circumference into perfectly equal sections by eye and
measurement. The mortises must all be cut at the same compound
angle — both dished and slightly forward-raked. This is done
by hand, with a brace and bit and a chisel, working from a
template that the wheelwright made years ago and trusts more
than any measurement.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Dish a wheel correctly.** The mathematics of dish, camber,
and spoke angle are learned by making wheels under supervision.
There is no formula that substitutes for the wheelwright's eye
that sees the dish is two degrees off and knows what that two
degrees will do to the bearing after a hundred miles.

**Shrink a tyre.** The temperature window is narrow. Too cool
and the tyre does not expand enough to fit over the wheel. Too
hot and the wood under it chars and weakens. The wheelwright
judges by color and by how fast the tyre moves when tapped.
The amateur who tries this sets the wheel on fire, or the
tyre drops off the next day.

**Repair a broken spoke in the road.** The spokes are under
compression from the tyre. Removing one requires cutting the
tyre, which destroys the tyre's tension and the wheel
disintegrates. The wheelwright has a traveller's method —
a partial tyre cut and clamp system — that the farmer with
a broken cart has never seen.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Hub failure.** The hub cracks along its mortises after years
of use. Every impact on the road transmits through the spokes
into the hub mortise. Eventually the wood between two
mortises splits. The wheel is finished.

**Spoke fatigue.** A spoke that has been overloaded (rut,
pothole, collision) develops a compression fracture on its
inner face. Invisible. It fails suddenly under the next load,
often dropping a loaded cart onto the road.

**Tyre loosening.** Over years, the wood shrinks and the iron
tyre works loose. The wheelwright re-fires and re-shrinks it.
The farmer soaks it in water. The farmer's fix lasts weeks.
The wheelwright's fix lasts years.

## Cooperage

**The cooper builds vessels from shaped wood that hold liquid
without a single nail, screw, or drop of glue.** The barrel,
the bucket, the tub, the churn — all rely on the same
principle: staves shaped to precise curves, held in compression
by hoops, sealed by the wood's own swelling when wet. It is
pre-industrial precision engineering.

### The Barrel's Construction

**Stave preparation.** Each stave is split from straight-grained
oak, then shaped with a drawknife and plane. The stave must be
wider in the middle than at the ends (giving the barrel its
bulge, called the bilge). Both edges must be bevelled at a
precise angle so that when all the staves are assembled in a
circle, every edge meets its neighbour flush. This angle depends
on the number of staves. For a typical barrel of twenty staves,
each edge is bevelled at precisely nine degrees. A degree off —
one degree — and the barrel leaks.

**Raising the barrel.** The staves are stood upside-down inside
a temporary truss hoop, arranged in a circle. Then comes the
fire: the cooper heats the inside of the partially assembled
staves over a small fire or with boiling water, making the wood
pliable enough to bend inward at the open end. As the staves
bend, progressively smaller hoops are driven on from the open
end. The staves compress together, the edges seat, and the
barrel takes shape.

**The croze.** A groove cut inside each end of the barrel,
near the rim, to receive the barrel head (the flat discs that
close each end). The croze must be perfectly circular and at a
consistent depth all the way around. The barrel head — made of
tongue-and-groove planks — is fitted into this groove and sealed
with rush pith.

**Hooping.** Each hoop is heated and driven on with a hammer
and drift. The hoops compress the staves together. The friction
between hoops and staves holds everything in place. A well-
made barrel needs no fasteners — the compression holds the
structure permanently.

### Core Knowledge

**Why barrels leak.** Temperature and humidity changes cause the
wood to swell and shrink. A barrel shipped from a wet climate to
a dry one shrinks, the stave edges gap, the liquid inside
escapes. The cooper specifies the wood's seasoning, the stave
thickness, and the hoop tension to account for the conditions
the barrel will face.

**Tight versus slack cooperage.** Tight cooperage holds liquid
(ale, wine, water, oil). The staves are thick, the joints are
perfect, the wood is oak. Slack cooperage holds dry goods
(flour, salt, nails, dried fish). Cheaper wood, looser joints,
less precision — but still a skilled trade.

**Charring.** The inside of a barrel used for ale or wine is
charred over a fire. The char layer seals the wood and gives
the drink its flavour. The degree of char is deliberate — light
toast, medium char, heavy alligator char — and each level
changes the taste.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Cut staves to the correct bevel.** The bevel angle must be
uniform on both edges of every stave, and must account for the
barrel's diameter, the number of staves, and the bulge
geometry. A cooper calculates this once, makes a template, and
uses it for life. The amateur does not know the angle exists.

**Bend staves without breaking them.** The fire-bending stage
has a narrow window. Too little heat and the wood does not
bend — the stave breaks. Too much heat and the wood chars and
weakens. The cooper knows by how the steam rises, by the feel
of the wood under his hands, by the smell.

**Make a barrel that holds pressure.** Ale ferments. Fermentation
produces gas. Gas produces pressure. A barrel that holds still
water may blow its head out when the ale starts working. The
cooper builds the head thicker for fermenting vessels and seats
it deeper in the croze.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Leaking croze.** The most common barrel failure. The head
shrinks, pulls out of the groove, and the barrel weeps from the
ends. The cooper re-swells the head in water and re-seats it.
The farmer fills the barrel and hopes.

**Hoop failure.** Iron hoops rust. Willow hoops dry and snap.
When a hoop fails, the staves under it bulge outward and the
barrel's structural integrity is compromised. The barrel does
not explode — it sags, weeps, and eventually collapses when
moved.

**Sour wood.** A barrel that held bad wine or spoiled ale
absorbs the taint into the wood. No amount of cleaning removes
it. The next batch stored in that barrel goes sour. The cooper
knows to scorch the interior or condemn the barrel. The amateur
refills it and wonders why the ale tastes wrong.

## Thatching

**A thatched roof will last twenty to forty years under a
master thatcher's hand.** It sheds rain, insulates against
cold, and breathes moisture out of the building below. The
same roof, patched by an amateur, lasts two winters before
the water finds its way through.

### Materials and Selection

**Long straw.** Wheat straw, harvested by hand-reaping so the
stalks remain unbroken and full-length (three to four feet).
Machine-threshed straw (a later development) is crushed and
worthless for thatching. The thatcher knows this by picking up
a handful and bending it: good straw flexes without breaking.
Bad straw crumbles.

**Water reed.** The premium material. Cut from reed beds in
winter when the reed is dry and dormant. Longer than straw
(five to seven feet), harder, smoother, and more durable.
A reed roof lasts twice as long as a straw roof — but reed
beds are not everywhere, and transport is expensive.

**Heather.** Used in highland areas where straw and reed are
scarce. Bundled tightly and laid thick. Shorter lifespan than
straw, rougher appearance, but effective where nothing else
is available.

**Hazel spars.** The fasteners. Lengths of hazel, split in
half and twisted to form a hairpin shape (a spar or broach).
These are driven through the thatch into the roof timbers
to hold the bundles in place. The thatcher twists several
hundred per roof. The technique — splitting, twisting
without breaking, sharpening the points — is one of the
first things an apprentice learns.

### Core Knowledge

**Pitch and overlap.** A thatched roof works because every
drop of rain that lands on it runs downward across multiple
overlapping layers before it reaches the interior. The
standard overlap is two-thirds: each new course of thatch
covers two-thirds of the course below. At a roof pitch of
fifty degrees or steeper, this overlap ensures water never
reaches the inner layer.

**Working from eave to ridge.** The thatcher begins at the
bottom of the roof (the eave) and works upward. Each course
overlaps the one below. The top course (the ridge) is the
most critical — it must shed water to both sides and resist
wind. Ridge work is the mark of the master.

**Compression, not fastening.** Thatch is not nailed. Each
new course is positioned, then compressed with a leggett
(a flat wooden paddle used to beat the butts of the straw
into alignment). The spars hold the course in place while
the next course's weight compresses it further. The entire
roof is a stack of compressed layers, each one holding the
one below. The system is self-reinforcing.

**Dressing the surface.** After each course is laid and
sparred, the thatcher uses shears or a knife to trim the
protruding straw ends to a uniform surface. A properly
dressed thatch roof has a smooth, even surface like the
pelt of an animal. An amateur's roof looks like a haystack.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Achieve watertight overlap.** The two-thirds rule sounds
simple. In practice, maintaining consistent overlap on a
roof that changes pitch at the hips, curves around a
chimney, and transitions from wall to eave requires the
thatcher's eye and hand. The amateur's overlap varies — thick
in some spots, thin in others. The thin spots leak.

**Work a valley or hip.** Where two roof planes meet (a
valley or a hip), the thatch must be woven together so water
runs along the joint and off the roof, not into it. This is
the thatcher's most difficult work. The valley that leaks
rots the roof timbers underneath — invisibly, for years, until
the roof sags and the damage is catastrophic.

**Read a roof's wear.** The master thatcher looks at a roof
and sees where it is thin, where the pitch has slumped, where
moss growth indicates moisture retention that will rot the
layer below. He re-coats selectively. The amateur sees a roof.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Ridge failure.** Rain and wind attack the ridge first. A
degraded ridge lets water into the roof's core, where rot
spreads outward and downward through layers that look intact
from the outside. The thatcher re-ridges every eight to ten
years. The homeowner notices the leak when the ceiling drips.

**Biological attack.** Birds pull straw for nesting material.
Rats tunnel for warmth. Moss retains moisture against the
straw's surface. Algae grows in perpetual damp. Each
weakens the thatch. The thatcher nets the ridge against birds,
poisons against rats, and maintains the roof's pitch so water
runs off before moss can establish.

**Fire.** Thatch burns — fast, hot, and completely. A spark
from the hearth, a lightning strike, deliberate arson. The
thatcher is not to blame, but the risk is inherent. Chimneys
in thatched buildings are built tall, and the thatch around
them is kept clear or replaced with tile.

## Wattle-and-Daub Construction

**Wattle-and-daub is how most northern Europeans live.** Timber
framing provides the structure. Wattle-and-daub fills the walls
between timbers. The combination is cheap, effective, and within
the reach of any community — but the specialist does it faster,
stronger, and vastly more durable than the amateur.

### The Specialist's Approach

**Frame preparation.** Before any wattle is woven, the builder
assesses the timber frame. The staves (upright poles set into
holes in the frame's top and bottom beams) must be evenly
spaced, straight, and firmly seated. The spacing determines the
weave's tightness and the wall's strength. The specialist sets
staves at four-to-six-inch intervals for a standard wall —
close enough that the weave is dense, wide enough that the
weaving is not impossibly tight.

**Wattle selection and preparation.** Hazel is ideal — long,
straight, flexible, and abundant where it is coppiced (cut on
an eight-to-ten-year rotation specifically for rod production).
Willow works but rots faster. Green rods are preferred — they
bend without breaking and shrink slightly as they dry, tightening
the weave. The specialist sorts rods by thickness: thin rods for
the weave, thick rods for the staves. The amateur grabs whatever
is at hand and starts weaving.

**Weaving technique.** The rod passes alternately in front of
and behind each stave: over, under, over, under. At the end
of a rod, the next rod overlaps by two staves, maintaining
continuous structure. The specialist weaves tightly enough that
the wattle panel is rigid before any daub is applied — a
structural element in its own right. He works from both sides,
alternating to keep the panel centred between the frame timbers.

**Daub application — the specialist's method.** Three coats,
not one. The first coat (the render coat) is pushed through
the wattle from both sides simultaneously — one worker outside,
one inside. This ensures the daub completely envelops the wattle
rods and creates continuous coverage with no voids. The render
coat is left rough. The second coat (the float coat) builds
thickness and creates a more even surface. The third coat (the
finish coat) is a finer mix with less straw, smoothed with a
float (a flat board) to a surface that accepts limewash.

**Daub mix control.** The specialist adjusts the mix for each
coat. The render coat is coarse — more straw, more dung, thick
enough to hold against the wattle without flowing through. The
float coat is medium. The finish coat is fine — more clay, less
straw, sometimes with fine sand for a harder surface. The amateur
uses one mix for everything.

### Core Knowledge

**Shrinkage management.** The specialist's three-coat system
exists to manage cracking. Each thin coat dries with minimal
cracking. Each subsequent coat fills the cracks of the coat
below and covers them. A single thick coat dries with deep
cracks that go straight through the wall.

**Moisture-proofing.** Limewash (slaked lime and water) applied
to the finished surface creates a hard, alkaline surface that
repels water, discourages insects, and can be renewed annually.
The specialist applies multiple coats, each thin, building a
shell. The amateur slaps one coat on and it flakes within a
season.

**Foundation management.** The bottom of a wattle-and-daub wall
must be raised above ground level on a stone or timber plinth.
Ground contact means moisture wicking upward into the daub,
which dissolves the clay and rots the wattle. The specialist
builds on stone or brick footings at least six inches above
grade. The amateur builds to the ground and wonders why the
bottom of his wall falls off after the first winter.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Build a wall that lasts more than two seasons.** The amateur's
wall looks fine when finished. One coat, roughly applied,
whitewashed over. By the end of the first winter, the daub has
cracked, water has entered the cracks, freeze-thaw cycles have
widened them, and chunks of daub fall from the wall. By the
second winter, the wattle is exposed and rotting.

**Integrate with a timber frame.** The specialist keys the daub
into the frame timbers — pressing it around the wood so that
the daub and timber form a continuous surface. This prevents
water from running down the timber face and into the wall
cavity. The amateur leaves gaps between daub and timber. Every
gap is a water channel.

**Build around openings.** Windows and doors require the wattle
and frame to transition cleanly. The specialist reinforces the
staves around openings, thickens the daub at stress points,
and angles the sill (the bottom of the window opening)
outward so water drains away from the wall. The amateur frames
an opening and daubs up to it. The first rain runs in through
the edges.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Rising damp.** Water wicking up from the ground dissolves
the clay binder in the lowest courses. The daub turns soft,
the wall bulges, and the lower section slumps away from the
frame. Prevention (stone foundations) is the only cure.

**Rodent damage.** Mice and rats burrow through daub with
ease. A rat colony in a wattle-and-daub wall destroys the
structural integrity from the inside. The specialist mixes
lime into the base coat (rats dislike lime) and installs
stone footings that discourage burrowing. The amateur
discovers the rats when the wall collapses.

**Thermal cracking.** In buildings with internal fires (most
of them), the wall adjacent to the hearth heats and dries
unevenly. This causes one-sided shrinkage and cracking. The
specialist uses a richer clay mix with more fibre near the
hearth. The amateur learns about thermal cracking when smoke
starts coming through the wall.

## Masonry

**The mason builds structures that last centuries.** Every cathedral, every
fortified tower, every arch bridge, every precise corner — the mason's work.
Stone does not forgive poor layout. A wall that is out of plumb by even a
small amount is visibly wrong and progressively worse as it rises.

### Tools of the Trade

**The mason's hammer (pick-hammer).** A head with one pointed end for
detailed work and one flat end for finishing. Controls the stone with
precision that an amateur cannot achieve.

**The chisel (cold chisel).** Struck with the hammer to split stone along
grain lines or to shape a complex cut. The skilled mason knows the grain
direction and can split a stone along anything approaching a straight line.

**The level (water level or weighted plumb).** Absolute horizontal and
vertical are not optional. A wall out of plumb will eventually rotate and
fall. The mason checks constantly.

**The folding rule and chalk line.** Precision layout before any stone is
cut or placed.

### Core Knowledge

**Reading stone.** The mason looks at a block and sees the grain direction,
the weak points, the planes of weakness. He knows whether this stone will
split clean or shard. He knows what saw or chisel direction will follow the
grain and which will fight it.

**Coursing.** The horizontal layers of the wall must be level. All the
stones in one course should be the same height (or carefully graded if not).
The joints between courses must line up or the wall looks drunk and performs
badly. Coursing is the rhythm of the wall — the visual evidence of
competence.

**Ashlar.** Squared stone blocks laid in even courses. This requires every
stone to be shaped to fit its neighbors with minimal joints. The skill is
cutting stone to fit an adjacent stone exactly — not close, exactly. The
joint should be nearly invisible.

**Arch construction.** An arch distributes weight outward and downward
rather than straight down. The apprentice mason learns to cut wedge-shaped
voussoirs (the individual stones that make the arch), position them
carefully, and temporarily support them until the keystone (the top center
stone) wedges them all into compression. An arch that is slightly off does
not work — it fails under load.

**Pointing and jointing.** The mortar between stones must be dense and well-
packed. Poor mortar joints leak water and admit frost damage. The specialist
rakes out old mortar carefully (without disturbing the stones) and packs new
mortar with pressure, removing excess and finishing the joint smooth or
slightly concave (sloped to shed water).

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Cut a complex stone shape.** The mason receives a specification or takes
his eye and cuts a stone that will fit the hole. This requires
understanding the geometry of the space, the angles involved, and the grain
direction. An apprentice's first complex cuts are usually failures — split
wrong or shaped wrong.

**Build an arch.** The geometry of an arch is not obvious. Cut all the
stones wrong and the arch will not close. Position them slightly off and
the keystone will not wedge. The mason learns arch geometry through mistakes
that cost weeks of work.

**Point a wall that does not leak.** The mortar joint must be completely
filled and fully packed. Air gaps let water in. The joint must also shed
water — slightly sloped or V-shaped so rain does not sit in the joint. The
amateur's pointing looks fine until the first winter rain finds the air gaps.

**Build a wall that stays straight.** Without constant reference to the
plumb, the wall drifts off vertical. A course that is slightly off level
puts stress on the courses above. A small error that is unnoticed becomes a
big problem fifty courses up.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Poor foundation.** The wall is only as stable as what it stands on. If the
foundation was not properly placed on bedrock or rammed undisturbed soil,
the wall will settle unevenly and crack.

**Water entry.** The deadliest enemy. Water entering the joints during
winter freezes and expands, cracking the mortar and displacing the stones.
Repeated freeze-thaw cycles eventually destabilize the wall. Proper joints,
proper pitch, and proper maintenance (repointing regularly) extend the wall's
life.

**Structural incompleteness.** A stone wall depends on its weight and height
for stability. Make it too thin for its height and it will flex and crack
under wind load and the weight of the upper courses.

## Brickmaking

**The brickmaker's trade is chemistry disguised as labor.** Every step —
clay selection, mixing proportions, drying tempo, kiln temperature — affects
the final product. Good bricks are uniform, hard, and durable. Bad bricks
are soft, warped, and fail under load or weather.

### Clay Selection and Preparation

**Understanding clay type.** The specialist tastes soil (not swallows, tastes
— clay has a characteristic texture on the tongue and teeth). He knows the
clay content by how it works in his hands. High-clay soil makes bricks that
are strong but crack as they dry. Sandy soil makes weak, friable bricks. The
sweet spot has enough clay for strength, enough sand for stability.

**Aging clay.** Fresh clay has not finished weathering. The specialist mixes
clay and lets it sit exposed to frost and rain for a season. Frost breaks
down the clay particles, making them finer and more pliable. Fresh clay bricks
are worse than aged clay bricks.

**Proportioning and mixing.** The specialist knows his clay intimately and
adjusts fibre content (straw, chaff, dung), water content, and sand
additions by touch and eye. The goal: a clay that is plastic enough to mould
but stiff enough to hold shape, with enough fibre and sand to resist cracking
as it dries.

### Forming and Handling

**Moulding technique.** The specialist forms bricks quickly (one brick per
minute from experienced hands) with minimal air pockets. Air pockets create
weak spots that fail in the kiln or in the wall.

**Drying management.** Speed and evenness matter. Bricks that dry too unevenly
warp. Bricks exposed to wind and direct sun dry on the outside while the inside
stays wet, creating internal stress that causes cracking in the kiln. The
specialist dries bricks slowly and evenly, often under cover, and stacks them
to allow air flow without wind exposure.

**Sorting for firing.** Before the kiln, the specialist removes misshapen,
cracked, or deformed bricks. These will fail or waste kiln space. The kiln
loads only the best bricks.

### Kiln Management

**Building the kiln (clamp or permanent structure).** The brick specialist
either builds a temporary clamp for one firing or maintains a permanent brick
kiln. The kiln's internal structure, the fuel arrangement, the draft control
all affect temperature uniformity.

**Temperature control.** The specialist judges kiln temperature by color,
flame behavior, and draw samples (small bricks pulled from the kiln at
intervals to assess progress). The goal is uniform firing throughout the load.
The kiln that is hotter on one side produces two different products in one
firing.

**Cooling.** The cooled bricks must cool slowly to avoid stress-induced cracks.
Rapid cooling causes thermal shock. The specialist manages the kiln's cooling
time.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Predict firing behavior.** The specialist knows that a clamp loaded too
tightly will have cold spots in the center that will not fully fire. That fuel
deposited unevenly will cause hot and cold zones. That ambient humidity affects
water content and drying speed. The amateur fills a clamp with dried bricks,
burns it, and hopes.

**Identify quality in raw or green bricks.** The specialist feels a dried brick
and knows if it will survive firing. An internal crack that is invisible will
open in the kiln. A brick that is too soft will crush under kiln load. The eye
and hand know these things after years of handling thousands of bricks.

**Adjust the mix.** Every clay source is different. This year's clay may be
different from last year's. The specialist adjusts the recipe in real time. The
amateur follows a recipe and wonders why this batch is different.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Underfire.** The kiln temperature never reaches full temperature. The bricks
are soft and friable. A wall built with underfire bricks fails as the bricks
crumble under load or dissolve in wet weather.

**Dunting (kiln cracking).** Bricks cooled too quickly after firing develop thermal
stress and crack in the kiln or on the road to the site. Prevents the entire
firing from having usable product.

**Bloom (salt efflorescence).** Minerals in the clay migrate to the surface as the
brick dries, leaving a white crystalline coating. This is usually cosmetic but
indicates water-soluble minerals in the clay — which will eventually cause the
brick to fail as salt dissolves in rain and refreezes with the seasons.

## Lime-Burning

**The lime-kiln is a chemistry laboratory that reaches 900°C.** The specialist
knows the limestone type, the fuel type, the burn duration, the quenching
method, and the storage conditions. Each affects the product's strength and
working properties.

### Kiln Operation

**Limestone selection.** High-purity limestone produces stronger quicklime than
limestone with impurities. The specialist knows the local stone and tests
batches. Chalk (natural limestone) burns differently than extracted limestone.

**Kiln design.** A permanent lime-kiln is usually a tall, narrow tower that
promotes updraft and high temperature. A temporary clamp is less efficient. The
specialist controls the fuel-to-limestone ratio and the temperature with air
flow management (dampers, vents, fuel placement).

**Fuel selection.** Wood, coal, or charcoal. Different fuels burn at different
temperatures and rates. Coal burns hot and fast. Wood is more controllable.
Charcoal is expensive but very reliable.

**Temperature judgment.** The color of the lime changes as it burns: gray limestone
→ white and yellow → dark orange → orange-red → gray again (this last stage is
overburn). The specialist knows by flame color, smoke behavior, and the smell
when the burn is complete.

### Quenching and Storage

**Slaking.** Hot quicklime is plunged into water. The reaction is vigorous and
hot — the quicklime becomes slaked lime (calcium hydroxide), a more stable powder
or putty. The specialist controls this — too much water and it's a runaway burn,
too little and clumps of unslaked quicklime remain.

**Putty storage.** Slaked lime can be stored as a putty (wet paste) under water or
left to age. Aged lime putty is stronger and easier to work with. The specialist
stores lime putty for months or years before use.

**Lime mortar production.** The specialist mixes lime putty with sand and fiber,
carefully proportioning to the sand type and texture needed for the mortar's
strength and workability.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Control the burn temperature precisely.** Under-burning leaves unreacted limestone
in the product, weakening it. Over-burning produces a brittle quicklime that hydrates
poorly and does not gain strength. The specialist judges the moment of completion by
observation. The amateur runs the kiln and hopes.

**Quench safely.** Adding water to hot quicklime is violent. The specialist does it
in controlled amounts with practiced hand and arm position. The amateur can be
blinded or severely burned.

**Predict lime quality from the stone.** Different limestone sources produce limes of
different strength and working properties. The specialist tests and adjusts. The
amateur buys lime and uses it the same way every time.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Unslaked quicklime in the mortar.** If quicklime specks remain in the mortar after
mixing, they will later encounter water (in the wall, in the weather), slake, and
expand. This expansion cracks the mortar joint and displaces stones.

**Water-soluble impurities.** Some limestone contains salts that dissolve in water and
migrate through the mortar, crystallizing on the wall's surface (salt bloom). This is
ugly and indicates that the mortar is degrading.

**Weak lime from poor burning or storage.** Lime that was under-burned or exposed to
CO₂ (which recarbonates the lime, reversing the process) is weak and does not set properly.
Walls built with weak lime never fully harden.

## Quarrying

**The quarryman reads geology.** He knows where to dig for good stone, how to extract
it with minimal waste, and how to break it to size. A good quarry produces stone that
comes out in large blocks. A wasted quarry produces small pieces and waste.

### Geology and Placement

**Reading stone in situ.** The specialist walks a hillside and sees the stone type
and quality by how it breaks and weathers. He knows the joints and bedding planes in
the stone — the lines along which it will naturally split. He sinks the quarry at an
angle to these bedding planes, not across them.

**Placement for efficiency.** The quarry should be placed close to the site (transport
is the expensive part) but not so deep that groundwater is a problem. The first few
feet of stone are usually weathered and worthless. The quarry begins below this.

**Removing overburden.** The soil and rock above the useful stone must be removed and
piled aside. This is wasteful labor but unavoidable.

### Extraction

**Splitting along natural planes.** Drive a chisel or wedge into a natural joint and
the stone splits cleanly along that line. Cuts perpendicular to the joint require more
force and leave a rougher surface.

**Secondary splitting.** Large blocks broken from the bedrock are split to smaller
units. This is done by drilling holes (with hand augers, in later periods) and driving
wedges, or by careful hammer and chisel work.

**Shaping complex stones.** For architectural pieces (moldings, voussoirs for arches),
the rough quarried stone is shaped with specialized tools and techniques. This work
typically happens on-site with the mason.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Predict stone quality.** Surface appearance is misleading. Stone that looks good can
have internal flaws that make it useless. The specialist strikes the stone with a
hammer and listens to the sound — a solid ring means good stone, a dull thud means
internal cracks.

**Avoid wasting stone through bad extraction.** Driving a wedge in the wrong direction
shatters the stone and wastes pieces. The experienced quarryman sees where the stone
wants to break and works with it, not against it.

**Calculate overburden and profit.** The quarryman needs to know the volume of
unusable stone, the volume of usable stone, the breakage rate, and the labor required,
to know if a site is worth quarrying. Bad calculation and a quarry fails.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**Hitting a deep flaw.** The stone looks sound to depth, then hits a flaw and
fractures unpredictably. Quarrying time and effort are wasted.

**The pit fills with water.** A quarry near the water table or in wet ground fills
with water, preventing further work and requiring constant bailing. The quarry must be
abandoned or drained — expensive.

**Getting trapped by a block.** A stone that is split partially but not completely
free can roll or shift, pinning the worker. Quarrying deaths are often this way.

## Well-Digging

**The well-digger combines geology, engineering, and luck.** He must find water,
reach it without drowning or collapsing the shaft, and protect it from contamination.
Every element is learned through failures that cost lives.

### Hydrology and Placement

**Reading the landscape.** Groundwater gathers in low spots, follows certain soil
types, and emerges as springs in the right places. The specialist looks for plants
that indicate water (willows, alders), for damp ground, for places where water
naturally flows. He places the well where it is most likely to find water at a
reasonable depth.

**Depth prediction.** From soil type and geology, the specialist guesses the depth
to water. Digging ten meters is easier than digging forty. The wrong guess wastes
weeks of labor.

### Construction

**Shaft lining.** As the shaft goes deeper, it must be lined to prevent collapse.
Stone lining (corbelled courses, each row stepping inward slightly for stability)
or dry-laid stone with clay chinking. The lining is built as the digging proceeds,
not after.

**Water testing.** Once the water is reached, the specialist tastes it (for salt),
observes its clarity, lets it settle and checks for sediment. Some wells are
brackish or clearly contaminated. The well is abandoned and another is dug.

**Filtration.** The bottom of the well can be lined with layers of sand and gravel
to filter water before it enters the lower well chamber. This removes some sediment
and pathogens.

**Wellhead construction.** The top 6-12 inches above ground level (for drainage), a
cover to prevent contamination from falling objects or animals, a simple windlass to
raise water.

### What the Amateur Cannot Do

**Predict water quality below ground.** Groundwater that passes through certain rock
types or soils carries minerals or pathogens. A well that tastes fine today may kill
people slowly over months — parasites, arsenic, fluoride. The specialist tests for
the things he knows to test for. Many dangers are invisible.

**Shore a deep shaft.** As depth increases, the pressure of the ground pushing inward
increases. A crude lining that is good enough at five meters fails at fifteen meters.
The specialist designs the lining heavier with each meter deeper.

**Navigate geology.** A shaft that hits solid bedrock stops. A shaft that hits a
layer of impermeable clay finds no water. A shaft that hits sand and gravel may be
good or may be hit a pocket of stagnant water full of pathogens. The specialist
knows these things from local geology and experience. The amateur digs and finds out
later.

### Failure Modes of the Trade

**The shaft collapses.** A wall failure traps the digger. Usually fatal.

**Striking contaminated water.** A well that taps water near a latrine, a burial
ground, or surface water contamination introduces disease to the settlement. The
contamination may not be obvious for months.

**The well runs dry seasonally.** A shallow well that is fine in spring may run dry
in autumn when the water table drops. The settlement must haul water from elsewhere
in the dry season.

**Saltwater intrusion.** Coastal or low-lying wells may tap into salt water, making
the well useless. Once saltwater breaks through, it is difficult to reverse.
