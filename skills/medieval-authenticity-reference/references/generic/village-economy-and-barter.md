# Village Economy and Barter

**Category:** Generic (pre-industrial northern European)
**Part of:** `medieval-authenticity-reference` skill

How money, goods, and services move at the village level. Most
transactions are barter. Coin is rare outside market towns and
military hands. Writers need this to price scenes correctly: what
a night costs, how a smith gets paid, what it means to receive
wages in grain instead of silver.

## Currency Baseline

**One silver buys a week's food for one person.** That is the
anchor. Everything else prices against it.

| Denomination | Value          | What it means at the village level           |
| ------------ | -------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| 1 copper     | Smallest coin  | Half a day's begging; a cup of thin ale      |
| 2 copper     | Day's labor    | Ditch-digging, hauling, threshing — unskilled work |
| 5 copper     | Day's skilled  | A mercenary's mission pay; casual journeyman labor  |
| 1 silver     | 10 copper      | A week's food; two days' journeyman pay      |
| 3-5 silver   | Serious money  | A good axe-head; a veteran's weekly pay      |
| 20-30 silver | Major purchase | A sound riding horse; a laborer's annual income |

A copper coin is small — roughly the size of a thumbnail — and
stamped, clipped, or unmarked depending on which jarl or king
minted it. Most copper pieces circulating in villages are worn
smooth, their markings unreadable. Nobody cares. The metal is
the value, not the stamp.

Silver is different. Silver is weighed. A "silver coin" is a
convenient shorthand for a standardized weight of silver, but
in practice people cut rings, hack arm-bands, and shave ingots
to get the exact weight. A man who handles silver carries a
folding balance and a set of reference weights in a leather
pouch.

## Coin Scarcity

**A peasant family might handle three silver in a full year.**
That is not poverty by their reckoning — they eat, they are
sheltered, they have clothes. But almost none of their daily
life involves coin.

The grain goes to the lord as rent. The lord's grain goes to
the market town. Silver flows back in small amounts: the lord
pays the smith; the smith pays the tanner for bellows-leather;
the tanner pays in shoes to the farmwife who sells eggs at the
market. Most of these exchanges never touch metal at all.

**Where coin concentrates:**

- **Market towns.** Silver changes hands at the weekly market.
  Merchants carry scales and know weights. Farmers bring goods,
  leave with the few coppers they cannot barter for directly.
- **Tolls and taxes.** The jarl takes his share in coin when
  he can, in grain and labor when he cannot. Road tolls, bridge
  tolls, ferry fees — these are copper transactions that drain
  coin from travelers back toward the lord.
- **Military hands.** Mercenaries are paid in silver. A band
  passing through a village may carry more coin than the entire
  settlement sees in a year. This makes them simultaneously
  valuable customers and dangerous guests.
- **Churches and temples.** The tithe, where it exists, pulls
  coin upward. Offerings at shrines — a copper for a prayer,
  a silver for a healing — concentrate metal in religious hands.

**Where coin is absent:**

- Between neighbors. Olaf does not pay Kari for helping thatch
  his roof. Kari's son will help Olaf's family at slaughter-time.
  The ledger is kept in memory and enforced by reputation.
- In the household. A wife does not buy food from her husband's
  stores. The family economy is collective. Coin enters the
  household as a unit and leaves as a unit.
- In barter-heavy trades. The charcoal-burner sells to the
  smith in exchange for tool repair. The shepherd pays the
  weaver in wool. The brewer pays the farmer in ale. No metal
  touches these transactions.

**Failure mode:** A village that suddenly has more coin than
usual — because a mercenary band paid well, because a merchant
overpaid, because someone found a buried hoard — attracts
attention. The tax collector notices. The lord's reeve notices.
Neighbors notice. Sudden visible wealth at the village level
is dangerous.

## Barter as Default

**The default transaction in a village is not "I buy this" but
"what do you need that I have?"** Barter is not primitive
haggling between strangers. It is an ongoing relationship
between people who see each other every week.

### How Barter Works in Practice

The farmer needs his plowshare repaired. He goes to the smith.
The smith needs grain. They agree: one repair for a sack of
barley. This is not negotiated from scratch each time — the
rough equivalence is established by custom and adjusted at the
margins. If the repair is unusually difficult, the smith asks
for a sack and a quarter. If the barley is poor quality this
year, the farmer brings a sack and a half. Neither man needs
to state the logic. Both know.

**Categories of barter goods:**

| Category        | Examples                                              | Notes                                      |
| --------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Food staples    | Grain, flour, dried fish, smoked meat, cheese, butter | Seasonal value — cheap at harvest, dear later |
| Preservatives   | Salt, honey, vinegar                                  | Always valuable; salt especially prized     |
| Raw materials   | Timber, firewood, charcoal, wool, flax, hides         | Bulky; transport cost matters               |
| Finished goods  | Cloth, rope, shoes, tools, pottery, nails              | Higher value per weight than raw materials  |
| Animals         | Chickens, goats, sheep, pigs, cattle, horses           | Living barter; value depends on condition   |
| Labor           | Days of work: plowing, threshing, building, hauling    | The most common barter unit                 |
| Services        | Healing, midwifery, prayers, teaching, guiding         | Hard to price; reputation sets the rate     |
| Access rights   | Grazing rights, fishing rights, water access, wood-lot | Seasonal and jealously guarded              |
| Luxury goods    | Amber, furs (marten, fox), walrus ivory, rare spices   | High value; used mostly in trade-town deals |

### The Informal Ledger

**Nobody writes it down, but everyone remembers.** The village
runs on an invisible balance sheet of favors, obligations, and
debts. When Thorvald helped raise Erik's barn, Erik owes labor
in return. When the widow Gudrun gave cheese to the smith's
family during the hard winter, the smith shoes her horse for
free the next spring. These debts are not precisely equal —
they are roughly proportional, and the community enforces
proportionality through gossip, shame, and social pressure.

A man who takes more than he gives is known. A family that
hoards when neighbors are hungry is marked. This is not charity.
It is insurance. The winter you starve is the winter your
neighbors decide whether you ate alone during the last lean
year.

**Failure mode:** The newcomer — the traveler, the mercenary,
the outsider — has no position in this ledger. He cannot barter
on credit. He cannot call in favors. He pays in coin or goods,
at the stranger's premium. The community knows this and prices
accordingly. An outsider always pays more.

## Tavern and Inn Costs

**Three tiers of accommodation exist, and they share nothing
but a roof.**

### Village Alehouse

Not a building — a room in someone's home where the goodwife
brews and sells. A bench by the fire, a straw pallet on the
floor, food if there is any to spare. No sign outside — you
ask where to drink when you arrive, and someone points you to
the right door.

| Item                | Copper | Barter equivalent                 |
| ------------------- | -----: | --------------------------------- |
| Cup of ale (weak)   |      1 | A small chore; carry water, chop wood |
| Cup of ale (strong) |      2 | —                                 |
| Bowl of pottage     |      1 | Bring your own bowl               |
| Bread and cheese    |      1 | A favor owed                      |
| Floor pallet        |      1 | Help with morning chores          |
| Stabling (no feed)  |      1 | Not always available              |

The ale is unfiltered, slightly sour, and served in a shared
cup or your own. The pottage is whatever went into the pot that
day — barley, turnip, onion, maybe a bone for flavor. The
pallet is straw on packed earth, and you share the room with
the family, their animals, and the other drinkers. It is warm.
It smells like smoke, sweat, and livestock. It is better than
sleeping in the rain.

### Road Inn

A purpose-built waypoint on a trade road or at a crossroads.
Run by a professional innkeeper. Separate sleeping room (still
shared, but not with livestock). A proper hearth. Stabling
with feed. Food cooked intentionally, not scooped from the
family pot.

| Item                | Copper | Silver | Notes                          |
| ------------------- | -----: | -----: | ------------------------------ |
| Ale (standard)      |      2 |      — | Better quality than alehouse   |
| Meal (basic)        |      2 |      — | Bread, stew, perhaps sausage   |
| Meal (good)         |      4 |      — | Meat, bread, cheese, ale included |
| Bed in common room  |      3 |      — | Straw mattress, shared room    |
| Private corner      |      — |      1 | Curtained, if available        |
| Stabling with feed  |      3 |      — | Hay and water; grain extra     |
| Hot bath            |      5 |      — | If the inn has a tub           |

The road inn is where merchants sleep, where travelers hear
news, and where mercenaries drink away their mission pay. The
innkeeper knows everyone who passes through and sells
information as readily as ale — not for coin, but for the
social currency of knowing things first.

### Town Tavern

A full establishment in a market town. Multiple rooms. A
kitchen. A cellar. Possibly a yard for horses. The innkeeper
is a businessman who may own property, employ servants, and
carry weight in town politics.

| Item                 | Copper | Silver | Notes                          |
| -------------------- | -----: | -----: | ------------------------------ |
| Ale (good)           |      2 |      — | Properly brewed, consistent    |
| Mead                 |      — |      1 | Expensive everywhere           |
| Wine (imported)      |      — |    2-3 | Rare; a luxury                 |
| Meal (standard)      |      3 |      — | Two courses, bread, ale        |
| Meal (fine)          |      — |      1 | Roast meat, cheese, good bread |
| Common room bed      |      4 |      — | Proper mattress, wool blanket  |
| Private room         |      — |      2 | Rare; reserved for merchants   |
| Stabling (full)      |      5 |      — | Feed, water, rubdown           |
| Laundry              |      3 |      — | Returned next morning          |
| Message service      |      2 |      — | Innkeeper passes word to a named person |

Mead is expensive because honey is expensive. Wine is
astronomically dear because it traveled by ship and cart from
far away. A man who orders wine is making a statement about
his wealth. A man who orders mead is celebrating or mourning.
A man who orders ale is thirsty.

**Failure mode:** The mercenary who throws silver around at
the village alehouse gets noticed — by the goodwife who will
raise her prices tomorrow, by the farmer who wonders where the
money came from, by the thief who watches from the corner, and
by the reeve who will ask questions at the next Thing. Spending
visibly above the local baseline is a social signal, sometimes
intended, often not.

## Service Pricing

**Every craftsman and service provider prices differently, and
all of them charge more for strangers.**

### The Smith

The most important craftsman in any settlement. He works iron,
repairs tools, shoes horses, and makes weapons. His prices
depend on the work, not on who asks — but he takes regular
customers first and charges strangers a premium.

| Service               | Copper | Silver | Barter equivalent               |
| --------------------- | -----: | -----: | -------------------------------- |
| Sharpen a blade       |      1 |      — | A meal                           |
| Repair a tool         |    2-3 |      — | A day's labor or equivalent food |
| Shoe a horse (4)      |      — |      1 | Grain or hay supply              |
| New axe-head          |      — |    3-5 | A week's food + charcoal         |
| New spear-head        |      — |    2-3 | Iron or charcoal supply          |
| Sword repair          |      — |    2-5 | Depends on damage                |
| New sword (common)    |      — |  15-25 | Months of negotiation            |
| New sword (fine)      |      — |  40-80 | Patron relationship required     |

A new sword is not bought off a rack. It is commissioned, the
iron sourced, the work done over weeks. A fine sword is a
collaboration between smith and patron — the patron supplies
materials, the smith supplies craft, and the result is named
and remembered. Sword-making is not a transaction. It is a
relationship.

### The Healer

Healers are rarely paid in coin. They are paid in food, labor,
and obligation. A family whose child is saved owes the healer —
not a fixed price, but an ongoing relationship of support.

| Service             | Typical payment                                 |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Set a broken bone   | A chicken, or a day's labor                     |
| Tend a fever        | Food for the healer's family that week          |
| Stitch a wound      | A meal and a night's shelter                    |
| Treat infection     | Rare herbs if available; labor or food if not   |
| Attend a birth      | The family feeds the midwife for a week         |
| Chronic treatment   | Ongoing food supply and household help          |
| Mercenary wound     | 1-3 silver (cash; stranger rate)                |

The healer who asks for coin from a neighbor is not trusted.
The healer who charges a passing mercenary three silver for
stitching a cut is conducting fair business. The mercenary has
coin and no relationship. Coin is the substitute for community.

### Other Services

| Service                    | Copper | Silver | Barter equivalent               |
| -------------------------- | -----: | -----: | -------------------------------- |
| Carpenter: small repair    |    2-3 |      — | Return labor or materials        |
| Carpenter: furniture       |      — |    2-5 | Timber + labor or food           |
| Guide (per day)            |      2 |      — | A meal and lodging each day      |
| Ferryman (person)          |      1 |      — | Sometimes free for locals        |
| Ferryman (horse + rider)   |      3 |      — | —                                |
| Ferryman (cart + team)     |      — |      1 | —                                |
| Carter (per day, loaded)   |      5 |      — | Return load or food              |
| Midwife (delivery)         |      — |    1-2 | Food for a week; ongoing favors  |
| Scribe (single document)   |      — |    1-3 | Rare service; priced by demand   |
| Grave-digging              |      3 |      — | Community obligation; often unpaid |
| Tanner: leather piece      |    3-5 |      — | Raw hide + labor                 |
| Weaver: bolt of cloth      |      — |    2-4 | Wool supply + labor              |

**The stranger premium:** Outsiders pay 50-100% more than
established community members. This is not greed — it is risk
pricing. The smith who repairs a neighbor's axe knows the
neighbor will be back. The smith who repairs a stranger's axe
may never see the stranger again, cannot call in future favors,
and has no one to complain to if the payment is short.

## Being Paid in Goods

**Mercenaries and travelers are sometimes paid in goods instead
of silver.** This is not inferior payment — goods can be worth
more than coin if you know what you are carrying and where to
sell it.

### Common Goods-as-Payment

| Good            | Weight (approx.)  | Equivalent    | Notes                           |
| --------------- | ----------------- | ------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Sack of grain   | 25-30 lbs         | 2-3 silver    | Seasonal: cheap at harvest      |
| Barrel of ale   | 60-70 lbs         | 2-3 silver    | Heavy; spoils in warm weather   |
| Salt (1 lb)     | 1 lb              | 2-3 silver    | Always tradeable; light         |
| Smoked meat     | Varies            | 1 silver/5 lbs| Keeps well; universal demand    |
| Pelts (common)  | 2-5 lbs each      | 1-3 silver ea.| Marten and fox best; rabbit low |
| Pelts (fine)    | 2-5 lbs each      | 5-15 silver   | Bear, wolf, ermine              |
| Bolt of cloth   | 10-15 lbs         | 3-5 silver    | Good trade value at market      |
| Iron bar        | 5-10 lbs          | 3-5 silver    | Heavy but universally valued    |
| Timber rights   | N/A               | 5-20 silver   | Seasonal access; non-portable   |
| Horse (sound)   | N/A               | 20-30 silver  | A major asset; maintenance cost |
| Cattle (one)    | N/A               | 15-25 silver  | Walking meat; breeding value    |
| A winter's board| N/A               | 8-12 silver   | Room and food, harvest to thaw  |

### Evaluating Goods Payment

A mercenary leader considers four things when offered goods
instead of coin:

1. **Weight.** Can you carry it? A barrel of ale sounds good
   until you realize it weighs seventy pounds and you are
   marching twelve miles tomorrow. Grain is heavy. Salt is
   light. Pelts are light for their value.

2. **Spoilage.** Will it last? Grain keeps if dry. Meat must
   be smoked or salted. Ale turns. Cheese lasts if hard, not
   if soft. Fresh goods must be consumed or traded quickly.

3. **Liquidity.** Can you sell it? Salt sells anywhere. Pelts
   sell at market towns. Timber rights are worthless to a man
   traveling north. A horse sells, but finding a buyer takes
   time. Grain sells only if you reach a settlement before it
   rots.

4. **Title.** Is it clean? Goods stolen, borrowed, or
   encumbered with debt are worthless — worse than worthless,
   because accepting them makes you a party to the dispute. A
   third-party guarantor (the village elder, a respected
   merchant, a priest) witnesses the transfer and attests that
   the goods are free of claim.

**Failure mode:** The band that accepts a cartload of grain in
winter pay and then gets caught in an early thaw has two
hundred pounds of sprouting barley and no silver. The band
that accepts timber rights in a forest three days' march from
any road has a contract worth nothing. The band that accepts
pelts without checking for moth damage finds itself holding
ruined fur at the next market.

## Market Day

**Market day is the pulse of the rural economy.** It is the
one day when the village opens to the wider world, when goods
flow in and out, when news arrives, when debts are settled,
and when disputes are aired.

### Frequency

| Settlement type    | Market frequency                              |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| Small village      | None; trade at the nearest market town        |
| Large village      | Monthly, or during seasonal fairs             |
| Market town        | Weekly, on a fixed day                        |
| Seasonal fair      | 2-4 times/year, lasting 3-7 days              |

A weekly market draws people from a day's walk in every
direction — roughly a ten-mile radius. They arrive the evening
before, sleep rough or at the inn, trade in the morning, and
walk home by nightfall. The market begins at dawn and is done
by midday. Afternoon is for drinking, settling accounts, and
walking home.

### What Market Day Looks Like

The market square is an open area — sometimes paved, usually
packed earth — near the center of town. Traders set up on
blankets, trestle tables, or the backs of carts. Livestock is
penned or tethered to one side (downwind if the town has any
sense). The smell is animal dung, wood smoke, baking bread,
and unwashed people.

Goods are displayed openly. Grain in sacks, tied so buyers
can check the quality by running a hand through the top layer.
Cloth on bolts, unrolled for inspection. Metalwork laid on
leather. Pottery stacked. Livestock walked in circles so buyers
can check the gait.

The noise is constant. Sellers call out. Buyers argue. Animals
complain. Children run between the stalls. The ale-seller does
brisk business from a cart with a barrel on it.

### Seasonal Price Variation

| Good      | Harvest       | Midwinter     | Spring (scarcity) |
| --------- | ------------- | ------------- | ------------------ |
| Grain     | Cheap (×0.5)  | Standard (×1) | Expensive (×2-3)   |
| Salt      | Standard      | Standard      | Slightly up (×1.2) |
| Firewood  | Cheap (×0.7)  | Expensive (×2)| Standard (×1)      |
| Pelts     | Standard      | Expensive (×1.5)| Cheap (×0.7)     |
| Dried fish| Standard      | Expensive (×1.5)| Expensive (×1.5) |
| Fresh meat| Standard      | Scarce (×2)   | Scarce (×2.5)      |
| Ale       | Standard      | Standard      | Slightly up (×1.3) |
| Wool/cloth| Standard      | Standard      | Slightly cheap     |

The grain price ladder governs the entire economy. When grain
is cheap, everything is cheap — people eat well, animals are
fat, and sellers compete. When grain is dear, everything tightens.
The farmer who held grain back from the autumn market to sell
in spring is either wise or reviled, depending on how hungry
his neighbors are.

**Failure mode:** The merchant who arrives at market after a
good harvest finds his prices undercut by locals dumping
surplus. The traveler who needs grain in spring finds prices
triple what they were in autumn and sellers who remember every
face that bought cheap last year.

## Weights and Measures

**All measurement is local, and the discrepancies are
deliberate.**

### Weight

The reference unit is the pound, but whose pound? The market
town's pound may differ from the village's pound by five to
fifteen percent. The silver-weight pound is different from the
grain-weight pound. A man who trades between towns carries two
sets of weights — the local standard and his own reference —
and knows exactly where the discrepancies fall.

**The balance.** For silver and precious goods, a folding
balance with standardized weights. For bulk goods, a beam
balance with hanging pans. The weights themselves are marked
with the town seal if official, unmarked if personal. A
trader's weights are inspected at market and verified against
the market standard.

### Volume

| Unit          | Approximate capacity      | Used for                    |
| ------------- | ------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Cup / ladle   | ~0.5 pint                 | Ale, honey, small measures  |
| Pitcher       | ~2 pints                  | Ale service                 |
| Bushel        | ~35 liters / ~60 lbs grain| Grain, flour, legumes       |
| Barrel        | ~120 liters               | Ale, wine, salted fish      |
| Sack          | ~25-30 lbs                | Grain, flour, salt          |
| Bolt (cloth)  | ~30-40 yards              | Woven fabric                |
| Bundle (wood) | What one man carries      | Firewood, kindling          |

### How Discrepancies Are Exploited

The grain merchant buys by the large bushel in the producing
village and sells by the small bushel in the consuming town.
The difference is his profit margin — not the markup, which
everyone can see, but the hidden gain on volume.

The smith whose weights run two percent heavy charges fairly
by the official scale but receives two percent more metal than
he gives in return.

The innkeeper whose ale measures are a finger short of the
standard serves twenty cups from a barrel that should yield
eighteen.

These are not considered theft unless they are discovered. A
man who is caught using false weights faces public punishment
— fines, whipping, or banishment. A man who is merely suspected
faces gossip, which is sometimes worse.

## Standing and Trust

**In a village economy, trust is the currency that underlies
all others.**

### Witness and Agreement

A verbal agreement witnessed by two or more people is binding.
Written contracts exist for major transactions (land, large
livestock, long-term service) but are rare at the village level
because few can read. What matters is who heard the terms.

The village elder, the innkeeper, or the priest serves as
default witness and neutral guarantor. A transaction conducted
before a respected witness is enforceable at the Thing. A
transaction conducted in private is one man's word against
another's.

### False Weights

Accusing a man of using false weights is accusing him of theft.
It is a public claim that must be supported. If the accuser
is right, the cheater pays reparation — double the loss, plus
a fine to the community. If the accuser is wrong, the accuser
pays for the slander.

In practice, most false-weight disputes are settled by the
market reeve weighing both sets of weights against the town
standard. The man whose weights are further from standard
loses. The margin of acceptable error is small — a few grains
of silver weight — and everyone knows exactly where that margin
falls.

### The Innkeeper as Guarantor

At a road inn, the innkeeper serves as neutral ground. A deal
struck in his hall carries his implicit witness. He does not
guarantee the deal's fairness — he guarantees that neither
party will be robbed under his roof and that both parties
acknowledged the terms. For this service, he expects the
custom of both parties: their ale money, their bed money, and
their presence at his table.

An innkeeper who allows violence in his hall loses his
reputation as safe ground. An innkeeper who takes sides in a
dispute loses both customers. The innkeeper's neutrality is
his livelihood.

## What a Village Has vs. What It Shows

**A village never shows everything it has.** The elder who
says the grain store is running low may be telling the truth —
or may be protecting reserves against a worse season, a lord's
tax collector, or a mercenary band that looks hungry.

### What a Village Actually Holds

A functioning village in reasonable times has:

| Resource        | Typical reserve                                |
| --------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Grain           | 2-4 months beyond current need (more post-harvest) |
| Salt            | Enough for current preservation needs          |
| Dried/smoked meat | 1-2 months in cold storage or smoke-house    |
| Firewood        | Stockpiled before winter; low by spring        |
| Animals         | Breeding stock not for sale; surplus tradeable |
| Tools           | Personal property; not communal                |
| Silver/copper   | Hidden; 5-20 silver across all households      |
| Ale/mead        | The alewife has stock; volume varies            |

### What It Shows to Outsiders

The village shows what it must and hides what it can. To a
merchant, it shows surplus goods for sale. To a tax collector,
it shows just enough to meet the assessed levy and not a grain
more. To a mercenary band, it shows poverty — empty granaries,
thin livestock, and the elder wringing his hands about the
harsh winter.

**The buried coinbox.** Half the households in the village have
a small cache — a clay pot or leather bag — buried under
the hearthstone, under a fence post, or at the base of a
tree. This is the family's emergency reserve: three to eight
coppers, maybe a silver piece. It is known to the family head
and no one else. It is used when the roof collapses, when the
cow dies, or when the lord demands more than the harvest can
bear.

**The smith's stranger premium.** The smith knows exactly how
much an outsider will pay because the outsider has no choice.
The next smith is a day's walk away. The price is fair but
firm, and haggling earns politeness, not a discount.

**Why outsiders always pay more.** Partly risk (the stranger
may not come back), partly information (the stranger does not
know the local rate), and partly insurance (the community
subsidizes itself through the surplus extracted from travelers).
This is not exploitation. It is how a small economy survives
contact with a larger one.

**Failure mode:** The mercenary captain who threatens a village
into showing its hidden stores gets what he wants — once. The
next village on the route has already heard about him. Doors
are barred. Livestock is driven into the woods. The grain is
hidden in the forest in sealed barrels. And the lord who
protects these villages has heard too, and sends riders.
