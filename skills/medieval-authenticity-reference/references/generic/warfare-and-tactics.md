## Shield Wall

### Forming the Wall

A shield wall is not a decision. It is the default.
When men stand shoulder to shoulder with shields
overlapping, they are harder to kill than when they
stand alone. Every man in the band knows this from
the first day he holds a shield.

**The overlap.** Each man holds his shield on his left
arm. The right edge of his shield overlaps the left
edge of the man to his right. This means the man on
the far left of the line is the most exposed — his
left side has no neighbor's shield covering it. This
is where the best fighter stands, or the man with the
biggest shield.

**Stance.** Left foot forward, right foot back, weight
low. The shield is not held out — it is braced against
the left shoulder, the rim resting on the ground or
just above it. The boss takes impacts. The arm takes
the weight. After twenty minutes the arm shakes. After
an hour it is agony.

**Depth.** A single rank dies. Two ranks hold. Three
ranks push. The second rank braces the first — shield
pressed into the back of the man ahead, feet dug in,
pushing forward. The third rank replaces casualties and
adds mass. More than three ranks is wasted weight
unless the ground is sloped or the intent is to
steamroll through by mass alone.

### Holding the Wall

**The push.** When two walls meet, the fight becomes a
shoving match. Weight wins. The heavier, deeper, better-
braced line drives the lighter one back. Men in the
front rank stab over their shield rims — short jabbing
motions, targeting the face, throat, armpits, groin.
There is no room to swing. A man who raises his arm for
a full swing opens his side and dies.

**Rotation.** The front rank tires. Every two to five
minutes (it feels like an hour), fresh men push forward
from the second rank while exhausted men drop back. This
is done without orders — the man behind taps the man
ahead on the shoulder, steps into his place, and the
tired man slides sideways and back. A band that cannot
rotate smoothly loses to one that can, regardless of
individual skill.

**The man who falls.** When a man in the front rank goes
down — stabbed, tripped, shield split — the wall has a
gap. The man behind steps forward immediately. If he
hesitates, the enemy pours through the gap and the wall
is done. The fallen man is stepped over. If he is lucky,
hands from behind drag him back. If he is not lucky, he
is trampled by both sides.

### When the Wall Breaks

A shield wall does not break everywhere at once. It
breaks at a point — one man flinches, one section gives
ground, one gap opens and is not filled. The break
spreads fast. Once the enemy is through the wall and
behind it, the formation is useless and men fight as
individuals or die in a crush.

**The rout.** When a wall breaks, the losing side either
rallies behind a second line (if they have one) or runs.
Men who run with shields on their backs survive. Men who
drop shields and sprint sometimes survive. Men who stand
alone in a broken line die. The decision to run is not
cowardice — it is arithmetic. A man alone against three
is dead. A man who runs may fight tomorrow.

## Formation Fighting

### The Wedge (Svinfylking)

The swine-array. A triangular formation with the
strongest fighters at the point. Purpose: to punch
through a shield wall at a single point, splitting it
open so the men behind can exploit the gap.

**Shape.** One man at the point, two behind him, three
behind them, widening to the base. The point man is
armored, expendable, or both — he absorbs the first
impacts. The flanks of the wedge crash into the
separated halves of the enemy wall and roll them back.

**When it works.** Against a thin line or an exhausted
wall. The momentum of the charge and the concentrated
mass at the point splits the formation before it can
brace. The men inside the wedge are partially shielded
by the men at the edges.

**When it fails.** Against a deep wall that absorbs the
impact and wraps around the wedge's flanks. The wedge
becomes a surrounded pocket. Against archers who can
see the clear triangular target and concentrate fire
on the point.

### The Line

A single extended line. No tricks. The purpose is to
present the maximum number of weapons along the greatest
frontage, preventing the enemy from flanking. Used when
defending a position with anchored flanks (river on one
side, cliff on the other) or when numbers are roughly
equal and the fight will be decided by staying power.

### The Column

Men in file, narrow front. For moving through tight
terrain — forest paths, mountain passes, village
streets. Not a fighting formation. The danger is being
caught in column by an enemy in line — only the front
few can fight while the rest stand useless. Transition
from column to line is the critical drill.

### Refusing a Flank

When one end of the line is threatened — enemy appearing
on the side, or the line is shorter than the enemy's —
the end of the line bends back at an angle, forming an
L shape. The men at the bend fight facing two directions.
This is ugly, costly, and better than being rolled up
from the side.

## Ambush

### Setting One

**Site selection.** The ambush site needs three things:
concealment for the ambushers, a kill zone the enemy
must pass through, and an escape route for the
ambushers if things go wrong. A narrow trail between
dense forest. A ford where men are mid-stream. A
road that curves around a blind hillside.

**The kill zone.** The section of trail or road where
the ambushers' weapons can reach. Ideally less than
thirty paces long — concentrated fire, concentrated
chaos. The enemy is caught in a space where they
cannot spread out, cannot form up, cannot see where
the attack is coming from.

**The blocking force.** A small group positioned past
the kill zone to prevent the enemy from running
through the ambush and escaping forward. They do not
need to fight the whole enemy column — just hold for
thirty seconds while the main force does its work.

**The trigger.** The ambush is sprung when the maximum
number of enemy are inside the kill zone. Too early
and only the vanguard is caught. Too late and the
rear guard is not engaged. The trigger is usually the
leader's signal — a horn blast, a thrown spear, a
shout. Once triggered, everyone attacks simultaneously.
Hesitation destroys an ambush.

**Patience.** The hardest part of an ambush is waiting.
Men lie in concealment for hours, cold, stiff, needing
to piss. When the enemy appears, every instinct says
attack now. The leader who springs early catches three
men instead of thirty. An ambush is an exercise in
collective discipline, not individual courage.

### Surviving One

**Immediate action.** When an ambush is sprung on you,
the wrong response is to freeze. The wrong response is
to try to form a shield wall in the kill zone. The
correct response depends on position:

- **If in the kill zone:** Charge the ambush line.
  Directly into the attackers. This is counterintuitive
  and the only option that works. Staying in the kill
  zone means death. Retreating means death (the blocking
  force). Closing with the ambushers at least gives you
  a fight. The ambushers expect you to run — not to come
  crashing through the brush toward them.
- **If behind the kill zone:** Do not march forward into
  it. Pull back, find cover, form up. Support the men in
  the kill zone with missile weapons if possible. Flank
  the ambush position if the terrain allows.
- **If ahead of the kill zone:** You are past the
  blocking force or not yet engaged. Keep moving. Rally
  at a defensible point ahead. Send runners back only
  when you have a position of strength.

**Breaking contact.** If the ambush is too strong or too
well-sited, the surviving elements disengage — smoke,
noise, a sudden rush in an unexpected direction — and
scatter to a pre-designated rally point. A band that
has discussed rally points before the march survives
ambushes. A band that has not discussed rally points
becomes fugitives.

## Raiding

### The Approach

A raid is not a battle. A raid is theft with the option
of violence. The approach is quiet — by night, by fog,
by a route that avoids roads and settlements. The raiding
party is small. Ten men can raid. Fifty can sack. Five
hundred is an army, and armies are slow.

**Timing.** Before dawn is ideal. The defenders are asleep,
fires have burned low, dogs are their only alarm. Sentries
at the end of their watch are the most tired, the most
inattentive. The attackers have been awake and moving for
hours, blood up, eyes adjusted.

### The Breach

**Gates.** A gate is barred from inside. If there is no
gate, there is a gap in the fence or wall. The first men
through the gap secure it for those behind. If the gate
must be broken, an axe and two minutes of noise — which
means surprise is lost and speed must compensate.

**Fire.** Throwing fire onto thatch roofs drives defenders
out of buildings and into the open where they are
disorganized. Fire is the raider's best friend and worst
enemy — it spreads to the stores you came to take.
Controlled fire (one building, the one blocking your
route) is useful. Uncontrolled fire means you leave
with nothing and the smoke signals your position for
miles.

### Extraction

Take what you came for and leave. The mistake is staying
too long — looting one more building, chasing one more
fleeing defender, breaking into the locked strongroom.
Every minute in the target increases the chance of
organized resistance, of reinforcements arriving, of
your men scattering into individual looters who cannot
be rallied.

**Priority of looting.** Weapons and armor first (high
value, immediately useful). Silver and coin second
(compact, universally traded). Animals third (can carry
other loot, can be eaten). Grain and supplies fourth
(heavy, slow, but keeps the band alive). Luxuries last
(cloth, spices, goods that require a market to sell).

## Field Fortifications

### The Ditch and Bank

The most basic fortification. A trench dug in a circle
or arc, earth thrown up on the inner side to form a bank.
A man standing on the bank is three feet higher than a
man standing in the ditch — equivalent to fighting from
horseback. The ditch is three feet deep, four wide. The
bank is two feet high. Total height difference: five feet.
This is built in two hours by twenty men with entrenching
tools. In an emergency, axes and shields used as scoops.

### Staked Ground

Sharpened stakes driven into the ground at an angle,
points facing the enemy, about knee to waist height.
Purpose: to slow a charge and break a shield wall's
momentum before it reaches your position. A running man
impales himself. A cautious man stops to pick his way
through — and while he is picking, he is being shot.
Stakes work once. A determined enemy cuts them down, pulls
them up, or pushes through at cost. But "at cost" is the
point. Every casualty from stakes is one fewer in the
assault.

### The Wagon Laager

Circle the supply wagons into a ring. Fill the gaps
between wagons with whatever is available — barrels,
packs, timber, dead horses. Men fight from behind and
between the wagons. This is an emergency measure — it
works against a rush but not against a siege. The
advantage is speed: a wagon laager can be formed in
minutes. The disadvantage is immobility: you are
committed to that spot until the enemy leaves or you
break out.

### Hasty Palisade

Sharpened logs driven vertically into the ground, lashed
together. Chest height is sufficient — head height is
better. A full palisade around a camp requires hundreds
of logs and half a day's labor. A partial palisade
protecting one exposed side is more realistic for a
small band. Combined with the ditch-and-bank on other
sides, it provides genuine protection against anything
short of a determined assault with ladders.

### Abatis

Felled trees dragged into position with branches facing
the enemy. The tangle of branches creates an obstacle
that is extremely difficult to cross quickly. An abatis
line fifty yards in front of a position forces attackers
to climb, cut, or detour — all while under fire. Combined
with stakes in the gaps, it is as effective as a low wall.
The material cost is trees and labor, both available in
forested terrain.

## Siege Craft

### Attacking a Fortified Position

**Ladders.** The simplest and cheapest siege tool. Two
long poles with crosspieces lashed at intervals. Carried
by two men, set against the wall, climbed by one man at
a time. The man at the top of the ladder is the most
exposed human being on the battlefield. Defenders push
ladders sideways (they topple), pour boiling water or
heated sand, or simply stab the man as his head appears
above the parapet. Ladders work only through volume —
enough ladders on enough walls that the defender cannot
cover them all.

**Rams.** A heavy log carried by six to twenty men,
swung against a gate or a weak section of wall.
Protected by a roof of shields or a wheeled shed (the
"turtle"). The rhythm matters — the log must swing
together. An uncoordinated ram bounces off. A coordinated
ram will break any wooden gate in minutes. Stone walls
require sustained effort at a weak point — a corner, a
joint, a section where the mortar has crumbled.

**Fire.** Against wooden walls and gates, fire is faster
than rams. Bundles of brush and straw piled against the
base and lit. Oil or fat poured over the gate first.
The defenders pour water. The question is whose supply
runs out first. Against stone walls, fire is useless
except against the gate.

**Undermining.** Digging a tunnel under the wall,
supporting it with wooden props, then setting the props
on fire. When the props burn through, the tunnel
collapses and the wall above it falls. This takes days
to weeks, requires men who know digging (miners, not
soldiers), and can be countered by the defenders
digging counter-mines or listening for the sound of
digging underground (a bowl of water on the ground
ripples from the vibration).

**Starving out.** The slowest and most certain method.
Surround the fortification, cut supply lines, wait.
The question is: who is more patient, and who has
supplies? A well-stocked fort can hold for months. An
under-supplied siege camp starves faster than the
defenders. Most sieges are decided by politics, disease,
or treachery before starvation.

### Defending a Fortified Position

**Economy of force.** A defended wall multiplies a man's
effectiveness by five or ten. One man behind a parapet
can hold off five men on a ladder. This is why
fortifications exist — they are a machine for converting
a small number of defenders into an adequate number.

**Reserves.** The wall is the first line. The reserve is
the second — a group of men in the center of the
enclosure, ready to rush to whichever section is
threatened. If the reserve is committed too early to one
section, the enemy attacks another. If the reserve is
committed too late, the wall is already breached.

**Sorties.** A sudden attack out of the gate against the
besiegers. Purpose: to destroy siege equipment, to
disrupt preparations, to keep the enemy off-balance.
Timing is critical — the sortie force must be strong
enough to achieve its objective and fast enough to
withdraw before the full besieging force can respond.

### Terms and Parley

A siege that has not yet begun is an opportunity for
negotiation. Common terms: the garrison surrenders and
is allowed to leave with personal weapons. Or: the
garrison holds until a relief force arrives by a
specified date, and if no relief comes, surrenders.
Terms offered before an assault are generous. Terms
offered after a wall is breached are not offered.

## Pursuit and Retreat

### Pursuing a Beaten Enemy

**The decision to pursue.** When the enemy breaks, the
instinct is to chase. The risk is that the chase
scatters your formation, strings your men out along a
road, and leads them into a counterattack or an ambush
by the enemy's rearguard. Disciplined pursuit means:
one designated group pursues while the rest hold
position, reorganize, and secure the ground.

**Speed.** A retreating enemy drops equipment — shields,
packs, weapons. They move faster than the pursuers
until exhaustion catches them. Mounted pursuit catches
foot soldiers. Foot pursuit catches only the wounded
and the slowest. The return on pursuit is prisoners,
loot, and preventing the enemy from reforming.

**When to stop.** Pursuit into unknown terrain, into
forest, into the dark — all invitations to ambush. The
leader who calls off pursuit at the right moment saves
more men than the one who chases to the last fugitive.

### Retreating in Good Order

**The rearguard.** When the band must withdraw — the
fight is lost, the position is untenable, the orders
say fall back — the rearguard holds while the main body
moves. The rearguard is composed of the steadiest men.
Their job is to die slowly — to trade casualties for
time. When the main body has reached the next position,
the rearguard breaks contact and sprints to join them.
Then a new rearguard is designated and the process
repeats.

**Delay.** A retreating force makes the pursuit expensive.
Choke points are held briefly. Bridges are dropped.
Trails are blocked with felled trees. Caltrops (iron
stars that always land point-up, designed to lame
horses and cripple bare feet — rare but devastating)
are scattered on the road. Each obstacle buys ten
minutes. Ten minutes multiplied by ten obstacles is
the difference between annihilation and survival.

**Rally points.** Predetermined locations where scattered
men reassemble after a broken retreat. A distinctive
terrain feature known to everyone — the stone bridge,
the hilltop church, the ford with the dead oak. Without
rally points, a rout becomes permanent. With them, a
shattered band can reform in hours.

## After the Fight

### The Battlefield

**The wounded.** Yours first. A man down and screaming is
retrieved, carried to where the healer can work. A man down
and silent is checked — if breathing, carried. If dead,
left for now. The enemy wounded are not a priority. If
they are in the way, they are moved. If they are
suffering and beyond help, mercy killing is an option
that no one discusses aloud and everyone understands.

**The dead.** Stripped of weapons, armor, and valuables.
This is not disrespect — it is logistics. A dead man's
sword keeps a living man alive. Identification of your
own dead: any personal item that proves who they were.
If there is time, burial or burning. If there is not
time, they are left. A man whose body was left on a
battlefield is mourned differently but not less.

**Looting protocol.** Who gets what is decided before the
fight, not after. Common systems: everything goes into a
common pool, divided by the leader. Or: each man keeps
what he takes, but weapons and armor go to whoever needs
them most. The system does not matter as long as it is
understood. Most fights within a mercenary band happen
over loot.

### Prisoners

**Ransom.** A prisoner worth money is kept alive, fed
enough to survive, and guarded. His value depends on his
family, his lord, and their willingness to pay. A soldier
is worth little. A lord is worth much. A relative of a
king is worth a fortune. Negotiations can take months.
The prisoner is currency that must be maintained.

**Interrogation.** A prisoner who knows the enemy's
numbers, position, and intentions is more valuable than
his ransom. Information is extracted by conversation
first — a man relieved to be alive talks freely. By
threat second. By force third. The reliability of
information obtained by force is low — a man in pain
says whatever stops the pain.

**Release.** A prisoner with no ransom value and no
information is a mouth to feed and a guard to assign.
He is released (unarmed, often stripped of boots to
slow pursuit), sold, or killed. The decision is
practical, not moral.

## Camp Defense Under Threat

### Standing To

When attack is possible — near enemy territory, after
contact with scouts, during retreat — the camp goes to
a heightened state. Every man knows his position. Weapons
are not stowed — they are beside the sleeping man. Armor
is not removed. The fire is banked low (enough for the
watch to see, dim enough not to silhouette the camp).
Half the men sleep while half watch. The sleeping half
can be on their feet with weapons in thirty seconds.

**The alarm.** A horn blast, a shout, a specific word.
The camp's response is drilled: each man goes to his
assigned position on the perimeter. This is not
improvised. The positions are assigned when the camp is
set. The man by the northern tree goes north. The man
by the supply pile goes to the gap in the earthwork.
Confusion in the dark kills more men than enemies do.

### Fire Discipline

In a night defense, fire is the enemy. A lit camp is
visible for miles. Fire silhouettes defenders and blinds
them to the dark beyond. The choice: fire for warmth and
cooking (peacetime camp), or darkness for survival
(operational camp). When under threat, fires are
extinguished or reduced to barely glowing coals covered
with earth. Men eat cold food. They curse the cold. They
survive.

### Fighting Withdrawal

When the camp is being overrun — the perimeter is
breached, the enemy is inside — the withdrawal follows a
simple plan: consolidate around the supply pile, grab what
you can carry, and move to the pre-designated withdrawal
route. The rearguard fights while the non-combatants and
wounded move first. The last men out set fire to anything
they cannot carry. Leave nothing the enemy can use.
