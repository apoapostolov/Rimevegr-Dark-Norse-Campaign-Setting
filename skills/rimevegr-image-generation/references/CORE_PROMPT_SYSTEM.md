# Rimevegr nanobanana core prompt system

This document is a template engine for an AI that will write the final prompts. It should be broad, modular, and reusable across the whole project. The goal is not to make every image look the same. The goal is to make every image feel like it comes from the same live-action world.

Use these templates for outdoor scenes, interiors, panoramas, portraits, character illustrations, landmarks, faux screenshots, montage layouts, covers, and wordmarks.

## Template library first

Start from the template type that matches the asset. Then fill only the slots needed for that scene.

### Universal base wrapper

Use this as the common foundation under most templates:

> [main subject], set in the Rimevegr, grounded live-action historical-fantasy film still, authentic early-medieval Norse frontier in a world where the gods went silent and the sky still bears old scars, harsh twilight world, realistic wool leather wood iron and stone, weathered practical costume design, restrained desaturated palette, human-scaled survival mood, low magic and deniable supernatural dread, cinematic natural light, tactile materials, atmospheric depth, serious and austere, not heroic fantasy, not glossy, not ornate

## Opt-in supernatural translation law

When the user does not explicitly ask for Veil presence, keep it out of the image.

When the user does ask for it, do not rely on the lore term by itself in the
final prompt. Translate it into camera-visible symptoms such as:

- grey-white rime-mist rolling low
- swallowed distance and softened landmarks
- muted or dead sound implied through posture and stillness
- sudden hard cold, wrong shadows, or lost direction
- faint corpse-lights or pressure-filled silence near barrows and old stones

Handle the Cracking the same way. Do not rely on the raw term in the final
prompt. If the user wants it visible, describe it as faint scar-like broken
veins high in the cloud deck or a pale wounded sun with no warmth.

## Outdoor templates

### 1. Outdoor wide establishing template

Use for settlements, roads, coastlines, moors, forest edges, and travel scenes.

> [place or route], wide exterior establishing shot, [terrain type], [weather and light], [small signs of human survival], [optional low rime-mist wrongness only if the user wants Veil presence], [optional faint wounded-sky evidence only if the user wants the Cracking and broad sky is visible], grounded live-action realism, the land feels larger than the people inside it

Best focus options:

- human smallness against hostile land
- the cost of travel
- a settlement barely holding on
- weather pressure
- omen-haunted silence

### 2. Outdoor mid-range action template

Use for movement, scouting, work, or confrontation in the open.

> [character or group] [action] in [location], mid-distance cinematic frame, practical Norse frontier gear, [1 terrain cue], [1 weather cue], [2 to 4 supporting elements], grounded urgency, realistic body weight and motion, no theatrical fantasy posing

Best action uses:

- marching the road
- hauling supplies
- climbing a pass
- watching from a ridge
- crossing bog or ford
- approaching a barrow
- holding a perimeter

### 3. Outdoor opt-in Veil template

Use only when the user explicitly wants Veil presence in the shot.

> [subject] in a cold part of the Rimevegr where grey-white rime-mist rolls low, distance is swallowed early, sound feels unnaturally muted, [terrain type], [cold light condition], one or two subtle reality-strain cues, grounded live-action realism, uncanny but deniable

Best use cases:

- barrow zones
- exposed moor crossings
- northern ice
- old standing-stone sites
- forest hush scenes

### 4. Outdoor travel template

Use for mercenary movement, pack animals, sleds, survival journeys, and road hardship.

> a band of [traveler type] moving through [terrain], [weather], burdened with [gear and supplies], boots and cloaks heavy with moisture, realistic marching posture, long-road fatigue, survival-first tone, cinematic but unsentimental

### 5. Outdoor weather-led template

Use when weather is the real subject.

> [subject or place] under [specific weather event], the atmosphere dominates the scene, the environment swallowing contrast, practical survival details visible in clothing, posture, and shelter, grounded live-action film realism

Strong weather drivers:

- sleet and sea-haar
- wet snow and rime
- thaw mud and floodwater
- cutting wind over moor
- hard cold under pale overcast

### 6. Outdoor settlement-edge template

Use for arriving at, leaving, or observing a settlement.

> [settlement or hall exterior] seen from [distance or angle], palisade, smoke, paths, animal pens, stacked wood, signs of scarcity and labor, [weather], the place feels inhabited, poor, defensive, and necessary

## Indoor templates

### 7. Indoor longhouse template

Use for halls, communal rooms, meals, tense meetings, or domestic survival.

> interior of [hall or house type], low firelight and smoke-dark beams, rough wood, wool, bone, and iron details, [people present], [social activity], practical clutter, survival economy visible in every object, grounded cinematic realism

Best focus options:

- hierarchy by seating
- warmth versus cold outside
- cramped survival
- suspicion across the room
- work rather than comfort

### 8. Indoor workroom template

Use for forge scenes, carving benches, archive rooms, fish sheds, herbal tables, and repair corners.

> interior working scene, [craft or labor type], hands-on material detail, tools, soot, damp wool, wood shavings, iron fragments, lamplight or hearthglow, authentic frontier labor atmosphere, practical realism, no fantasy workshop glamour

Good settings:

- forge bay
- smithy corner
- rune-carving bench
- loom room
- smokehouse
- mine office
- map table

### 9. Indoor ritual or sacred template

Use for shrines, offerings, grief rites, seidr sessions, and hushed spiritual scenes.

> interior sacred or ritual space, small-scale Norse devotional atmosphere, muted light, carved wood or standing stones, offerings, ash, cloth, bone, and breath in cold air, subtle post-sacred dread, restrained supernatural pressure, no spectacle magic

### 10. Indoor poor-shelter template

Use for barns, lean-tos, half-collapsed houses, stable corners, or emergency shelter.

> rough shelter interior, bad light, damp wool drying by weak fire, cracked boards, mud, straw, gear piled near sleeping bodies, realism of exhaustion and cold, survival before beauty

Important rule:

Do not use the Cracking indoors. Only let optional rime-mist intrusion appear indoors if the user asked for Veil presence and it is visibly entering through a doorway, window, cave mouth, or ruined structure.

## Panorama templates

### 11. Grand world panorama template

Use for project-defining vistas and broad setting mood.

> ultra-wide panoramic view of the Rimevegr, [2 to 4 regions visible in one sweep], tiny settlements and roads against immense cold land, weak twilight, severe beauty, lived-in but under-populated world, grounded cinematic matte-painting realism

### 12. Regional panorama template

Use when one region dominates.

> sweeping panoramic view of [specific region], emphasizing [terrain identity], [weather and light], [1 to 3 man-made survival details], atmosphere of hardship, age, and isolation, realistic scale and texture

### 13. Sky-heavy panorama template

Use for broad exterior compositions where the heavens matter.

> wide open-sky panorama over [terrain], the land low and severe beneath a huge clouded sky, [optional subtle Cracking], bleak daylight, solemn silence, the sky feels wounded but distant

Important rule:

Use the Cracking only in panoramas or wide exteriors with real sky visibility and only if the user wants the sky-wound visible. It should read as faint evidence in the upper cloud layers, never the main showpiece.

## Illustration templates

### 14. Single-character illustration template

Use for full-body or three-quarter hero images that still feel grounded.

> [character role], full-body or three-quarter illustration, practical early-medieval Norse frontier clothing, [2 to 4 physical traits], [1 status cue], [1 personal prop or weapon], background lightly anchored in [region or setting], live-action realism rather than fantasy poster energy

### 15. Duo illustration template

Use for tense pairings, allies, rivals, kin, or negotiation scenes.

> [character A] and [character B], contrasted posture and status, grounded costume realism, subtle relationship tension through body language, [location cue], [survival or political context], serious cinematic composition

### 16. Small-group illustration template

Use for mercenary bands, work crews, family clusters, or patrols.

> small group of [3 to 7 figures], each with slightly different gear, age, posture, and status markers, unified by the same harsh environment, practical motion and believable spacing, no identical faces or cloned costumes

### 17. Action illustration template

Use for compact story beats without turning them into epic fantasy battle art.

> [character or group] in the middle of [specific action], grounded impact and motion, messy footing, wet ground or poor visibility, practical weapons and shields, human-scale tension, realism over choreography

### 18. Aftermath illustration template

Use for silence after violence, travel cost, ruined camps, or barrow consequences.

> aftermath of [event], scattered gear, tired survivors, broken wood, blood-dark cloth, smoke, mud, rime, and silence, emotional focus on cost rather than spectacle

## Portrait templates

### 19. Tight portrait template

Use for faces, emotion, status, and personality.

> close portrait of [character role], weathered face, practical hair, tired eyes, skin marked by wind cold and labor, realistic Norse frontier styling, shallow background carrying only a hint of place, live-action seriousness

### 20. Half-body portrait template

Use when hands, gear, or posture matter.

> half-body portrait of [character], [hand position or held object], layered wool and leather, one strong status or history marker, restrained natural light, background softly suggesting [environment type]

### 21. Social-rank portrait template

Use for jarls, widows, captains, rune-readers, elders, ferrymen, and similar figures.

> character study portrait emphasizing social role through clothing quality, brooches, rings, scars, grooming, and posture, not through ornate fantasy costume, the hardship of the setting still visible beneath rank

Important portrait rule:

Do not overload portraits with every world symbol. Usually one background cue is enough.

## Dynamic montage templates

These are for photo-comic energy without speech bubbles, captions, or stylized comic ink. Think editorial film-still collage or sequential cinematic panels.

### 25. Two-scene contrast montage template

Use for place versus person, before versus after, or outside versus inside.

> two-scene cinematic montage, scene one showing [wide setting or approach], scene two showing [character reaction or interior consequence], consistent Rimevegr palette and film realism, visual storytelling without text, no speech bubbles

### 26. Three-scene escalation montage template

Use for mini-narratives with momentum.

> three-panel photo-comic style montage, panel one [approach or warning], panel two [action or encounter], panel three [aftermath or silent reaction], all grounded in the same harsh Norse world, cinematic realism, no text or graphic-novel stylization

### 27. Character-environment montage template

Use for showcasing a person in relation to the land.

> dynamic 2 to 3 scene montage built from one close portrait, one mid-shot action frame, and one wide environment frame, all tied by the same location and palette, the rhythm feels like a prestige-film sequence board, not a comic page with words

### 28. Contract-life montage template

Use for the mercenary-survival core of the setting.

> montage of 2 to 3 grounded scenes from life in the Rimevegr, such as hiring, marching, campfire accounting, wound care, or bleak payment, cinematic continuity, severe realism, no text bubbles or comic lettering

## Setting reference rules

These sections exist so the AI prompt writer understands the world terms correctly.

### Core principle

Every image should feel like it belongs to the same live-action setting:

- early-medieval Norse material culture
- three centuries after the gods fell silent and the sky was wounded
- a cold world under a pale, damaged sky
- low magic, high consequence
- survival pressure more important than heroism

The image should feel poor, weathered, practical, and filmed rather than painted from pure fantasy spectacle.

### World facts

Usually carry only two to four of these into a final prompt:

- the gods are silent
- the world sits in long twilight, weak sun, and brutal cold
- old roads, barrows, standing stones, and abandoned halls still shape daily life
- settlements are small, hard, and one bad winter from collapse
- survival, contract work, debt, food, and firewood matter more than glory

### The Veil

The Veil is not normal fog. It is the setting's grey-white rime-mist: a cold, living-looking haze that dulls contrast, swallows distance, softens edges, and seems to muffle sound. It should feel unnatural but still deniable.

Only use it when the user has explicitly turned Veil presence on for the shot.

Barrows, far-north ice, exposed moors, and old standing-stone sites make Veil presence plausible, but they do not switch it on automatically.

Do not force the Veil into every image. Some scenes should have only cold air, low cloud, sea-haar, dusk, or clear hard weather without obvious Veil presence.

### The Cracking

The Cracking should not read as lightning, glowing magic, or open sky-rifts. It is the old wound in the heavens: vast, faint, grey fracture-lines and branching stress-patterns high above the world, like a dead daytime aurora or cracks in a weathered celestial dome. The shapes should feel ancient, cold, and enormous in scale, usually half-obscured by thick overcast.

Visually, think hairline breaks, broken-vein geometry, and dim sheet-like scars in the upper cloud layers rather than bright bolts or sharp neon lines. It should be subtle enough that a viewer notices it after the mood, land, and weather, not before.

Use the Cracking only when the user explicitly wants the sky-wound visible and the composition has a lot of real sky, such as wide landscapes, coastlines, mountain passes, moors, or large exterior establishing shots. In most images it should function as quiet background evidence that the world was damaged long ago and never healed.

Do not add the Cracking to indoor scenes, tight portraits, logos, dense canopy scenes, or compositions where the sky is barely visible.

### Color language

Keep the palette restrained and mostly desaturated.

| Base colors                                       | Supporting accents                                 | Rare accents                                       |
| ------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| iron black, ash grey, weathered bone, frost white | muted pine green, cold blue, peat brown, wet stone | dim amber firelight, dried-blood rust, dull silver |

Keep a little warmth from fire, skin, leather, or lamp glow so the cold feels real by contrast.

### Human look baseline

Characters should read as authentic Norse-adjacent frontier people from a collapsed early medieval world, not fantasy costume models.

#### Clothing

- rough wool tunics
- heavy cloaks and mantles
- leather jerkins
- patched layers
- practical boots
- fur only as trim or survival gear, not luxury overload
- mail is rare and valuable
- helmets are plain iron, practical, and scarce
- round shields, axes, spears, seaxes, utility belts

#### Faces and bodies

- wind-burned skin
- tired eyes
- broken noses, scars, cracked hands
- practical hair, braids, tied-back hair, work-worn beards
- lean, sturdy, underfed, cold-tested bodies
- no glam makeup, no perfect hair, no clean heroic shine

#### Status cues

- poor: undyed wool, mismatched patches, simple iron brooches
- established: better dye, cleaner cloak shape, good boots, silver ring or brooch
- warrior wealth: mail shirt, better helm, named sword, but still weathered and practical

## Anti-sameness rules

This matters more than extra detail.

### Never stack all the signature elements at once

Avoid making every image contain all of the following together:

- the Cracking in the sky
- the Veil in full effect
- barrow mound
- rune stone
- fjord
- black pine forest
- mercenary band
- ravens
- firelight

Pick a few, not all.

### Rotate the terrain identity

Let different prompts lean on different parts of the world:

- black-water fjords and salt cliffs
- peat moors and low stone circles
- deep black pine and charcoal smoke
- mine roads and iron-stained earth
- mountain passes and wind-scoured granite
- northern ice and wrong-shadow aurora

### Rotate human variety too

Do not make every important figure into the same scarred dark-cloaked captain. Vary:

- age
- class
- grooming
- body type
- work role
- degree of wealth
- weapon ownership
- emotional state

### Keep the supernatural deniable

The setting works best when the uncanny is a pressure, not a fireworks display.

Use hints like these:

- the light is wrong
- the Veil mutes distance and sound
- a rune surface seems faintly warm
- a barrow breathes cold air
- birds have gone silent
- shadows lean the wrong way

Avoid obvious spell blasts, glowing wizard hands, or high-fantasy energy effects.

Also avoid using the Veil and the Cracking as automatic default scenery. They are special setting signals, not wallpaper.

## Recommended build order for the AI

1. Choose the asset type.
2. Select the matching template from the library above.
3. Fill the subject slot.
4. Add one terrain cue.
5. Add one light or weather cue.
6. Add one focus cue.
7. Add 2 to 4 survival or material details.
8. Add Veil presence only if the user selected it for the scene.
9. Add the Cracking only if the user selected it and the shot has broad sky visibility.
10. Add one subtle uncanny hint if needed.
11. End with a short negative block.

## Negative guidance

Avoid these unless a scene explicitly requires them:

- horned helmets
- ornate fantasy armor
- glowing magic overload
- bright saturated colors
- heroic power poses
- clean modern grooming
- oversized weapons
- anime stylization
- glossy game-key-art polish
- crowded epic-battle imagery
- generic Viking clichés without hardship or decay
- the Veil in scenes where the user has not turned it on
- the Cracking in close shots, scenes with little visible sky, or scenes where the user did not request it

## Short master suffix

Use this when a compact reusable ending is needed:

> grounded live-action Rimevegr feel, early-medieval Norse frontier after the gods fell silent and the sky was wounded, weak twilight, practical wool and iron, weathered materials, restrained desaturated palette, survival pressure, subtle post-sacred dread, realistic cinematic lighting, no heroic-fantasy excess
