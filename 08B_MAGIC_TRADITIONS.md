# The Three Traditions and Society

## The Three Traditions: Phenomenology

The mechanics of magic — dice rolls, rank tables, failure cascades — live
in [20_SIMULATION_RULES.md](20_SIMULATION_RULES.md). This section covers
what the mechanics cannot: what magic feels like. To the person doing it.
To the person watching. To the land itself, after the practitioner has
gone.

These descriptions govern how magic appears in prose and how the GM
narrates its effects. The dice tell you whether galdr succeeds. This
section tells you what success smells like.

### Galdr — The Carving

**What the practitioner feels.** Galdr-workers describe it the same way
across settlements: listening. The rune already exists inside the stone,
the bone, the wood. The carver's job is to find the grain of it and cut
along the line. When the stroke is wrong, it feels like a tooth being
pulled — a wrongness deep in the hand, radiating to the elbow. When the
stroke is right, it feels like nothing at all. The absence of resistance.
Ash described it once to Voss, who did not ask again: "You stop carving.
The rune finishes itself."

This is not metaphor. Galdr-workers' hands move in patterns they did not
choose. At rank 1, the sensation is faint — a nudge, easy to dismiss as
muscle memory. At rank 3 and above, the carver's hands are not entirely
their own. The rune demands a shape. The carver can refuse, but refusal
is what the failure cascade measures (see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, Galdr
failure table). Fizzle means the rune gave up. Backlash means the carver
fought the shape and the shape won.

**What witnesses feel.** Minor galdr — trail-marks, one-night wards, Gest
scratching a rune on a tent-pole — is unremarkable. The witnesses notice
nothing because there is nothing to notice. This is rank 1, and rank 1 is
barely distinguishable from superstition.

Major galdr is different. The air pressure drops. Hair rises on arms and
necks. There is a smell — hot iron, but no forge — that clings to the
inside of the nose. Nobody describes it the same way twice. Some say
copper. Some say lightning-struck sand. Binding runes and curse-work at
rank 4 make observers nauseous, and for hours afterward there is a thin
ringing in the ears, like a bell struck in an adjacent room. The men in
the band do not discuss this. They notice, and they do not discuss it,
and those are separate decisions.

**What the land remembers.** Frost crystallizes along rune-lines even in
summer — not ice, but a mineral bloom that follows the carving's path.
Runes glow in peripheral vision: a faint blue-white flicker that vanishes
when you look directly at it. You learn not to look directly. Old galdr
sites — stones carved decades ago, ward-posts long rotted — have warmer
soil. Dig where a galdr-worker's rune-post stood and the earth is soft
when the ground around it is frozen. Farmers know these spots. They do
not build on them.

**What it costs over years.** The ranked costs in
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` — Willpower, hit points, materials — are the
immediate price. The long price is written on the body. Galdr-workers
develop scored marks on their hands: thin white lines that follow no
blade's pattern, emerging over months like frost-cracks in leather. Ash's
palms are cross-hatched with them. Thorne's fingertips are frostbitten
from carving in cold — but Thorne is rank 1, a man who reads rune-stones
and lays simple wards. He calls it "reading" and keeps the practice small
enough to avoid the ergi question. His hands show use, not
transformation. Ash's hands show something else. The difference between a
man who uses galdr and a man who is used by it.

### Seiðr — The Drowning

**What the practitioner feels.** Trance entry feels like sinking into
cold water. Not metaphorical cold — the body temperature drops,
measurably, and the practitioner's extremities go bloodless and grey. The
world above recedes. Sound inverts: whispers become shouts, shouts become
whispers, and somewhere below the surface there are voices that were
always speaking and are now, finally, audible.

Contact with spirits is not communication in any useful sense. It is a
hand closing over your throat. Intimate. Terrifying. Impossible to
mistake for imagination, which is the one comfort — the seiðr-worker may
be many things, but she is never uncertain whether the dead are real.
They are real the way a fist is real. The question is not belief. The
question is survival.

At rank 3 and above, the trance takes an hour to enter and leaves the
practitioner physically helpless — which is why seiðr-workers need
someone guarding their body, and why that guard is the most feared duty
in any settlement. You sit beside a woman whose eyes have gone to glass
and whose breath comes in intervals too long for the living, and you
wait, and sometimes she speaks in a voice that belonged to your dead
father, and you do not leave your post.

**What witnesses feel.** The seiðr-worker stops blinking. Their eyes go
wet and reflective, catching light at angles a living eye should not. The
temperature around them drops — not figuratively. Breath clouds. Fingers
go numb. Sometimes they speak: a different pitch, a different accent,
occasionally a language nobody present recognizes. The air tastes of
turned earth.

Small objects drift. A cup on a bench shifts a finger's width toward the
practitioner. A knife rotates. A pouch slides. Not dramatically — you
could convince yourself you imagined it, if you wanted to badly enough.
Most people want to. The effect persists for hours after the trance ends.
Dalla's cook-fire burns unevenly for a full day after she has been
listening to the standing stones, and the men eat cold food without
asking why.

**What the land remembers.** Animals flee. Not just during the trance —
they avoid the spot for days. Dogs whimper and press their bellies to the
ground. Fire burns blue or refuses to catch. Settlements where powerful
seiðr was performed develop cold spots that persist across seasons: a
corner of a hall where frost forms on the inside wall, a patch of ground
where grass grows thin and pale.

**What it costs over years.** The seiðr-wife begins to see the dead
everywhere. Not visions. Not hallucinations. An inability to stop
perceiving what was always present. Ragnhild Isarnsdottir at Icebreak —
WYR 5, Spirit-lore 4, the most powerful practitioner in the region —
cannot enter a room without counting the dead who died there. A longhouse
where three people passed in their sleep: she sees three. A settlement
built on a battle-site: she sees hundreds. She is gaunt and fever-eyed
and she lives alone in an ice cave shrine hung with whale-bone charms,
and nobody thinks this is strange, because everyone understands that the
alternative is worse. A seiðr-wife who lives among people begins to
confuse the living voices with the dead ones. This is why they live
alone. Not choice. Necessity.

Male practitioners carry the additional cost of the ergi stigma — not
effeminacy, as commonly assumed, but uncontrollability. A man who will
commune with the dead has already demonstrated willingness to cross
boundaries that social order depends on. What else will he cross? The
stigma is political, not sexual. It is still ruinous.

### Wyrd-Reading — The Vertigo

**What the practitioner feels.** Casting lots or reading entrails feels
like leaning over a cliff edge. The world tilts. Not the physical world —
time itself becomes weight, becomes texture. You feel what has happened
pressing against what might happen, the way you feel a river's current
when you stand knee-deep. True readings arrive not as vision but as
certainty: you know, the way you know where the ground is when you close
your eyes. You cannot explain the mechanism. You cannot reproduce it on
demand. The lots fall, and sometimes you know.

At rank 1, this is barely distinguishable from good instinct. Petra
shakes her bone lots before a march and says "bad road" and the band
adjusts, and nobody is sure whether the lots told her or the weather did.
Thorne touches his rune-amulet when danger is near — not prayer, not
galdr. A check. Something between habit and sense. His WYR is 1. He is
not a reliable practitioner. He is a man with a knack that might be
something more, or might be nothing at all. This ambiguity is the point.

At rank 3 and above, the certainty is absolute and the practitioner
wishes it were not. The future shown is always true but never complete.
You see the shipwreck, not the rescue. You see the betrayal, not the
reason. Knowledge without context is indistinguishable from curse.

**What witnesses feel.** Nothing. This is the critical fact. The readers
shakes stones, stares, goes still for a moment, speaks. No pressure drop.
No temperature change. No smell of iron. Occasionally the reader's hands
tremble or they hold very still for a breath too long. That is all.

This is why wyrd-reading is the most socially acceptable tradition. A
jarl consults a wyrd-reader before a sea crossing and nobody thinks less
of him. A warrior carries rune-lots and the band shrugs. There is nothing
to see, therefore nothing to fear, therefore nothing to stigmatize.

**What the land remembers.** Nothing that anyone can measure. No cold
spots. No warm soil. No frost patterns. This is also why some people —
smart people, practical people — insist that wyrd-reading is fraud. The
readers themselves cannot argue. They have no evidence except the
readings that came true, and memory is selective, and coincidence is
generous.

**What it costs over years.** Wyrd-readers develop a fatalism that is
indistinguishable from depression. The mechanical costs escalate — mental
fatigue, then dread, then permanent stat changes at rank 5 (see
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`) — but the phenomenological cost is subtler.
They stop making plans. They stop arguing outcomes. They accept what
arrives before it arrives, because they have felt the weight of
what-will-be pressing against what-is, and they know which one wins.

Thorne moves his bedroll to the danger side of camp. Every time. Nobody
remarks on it. Everybody notices. This is not prescience. His
Wyrd-reading is rank 1 — barely functional, coin-flip reliability. What
it looks like is resignation. A man who has glimpsed the current, even
once, even poorly, and decided that swimming against it is not worth the
effort. Whether this makes him wise or broken is a question the text does
not answer, because the text does not know, because Thorne does not know.
He just moves the bedroll.

_The gap between these phenomenological descriptions and the mechanical
tables is deliberate. The dice measure success and failure. The prose
measures what success and failure feel like. A character who rolls 43 on
a galdr check and succeeds has carved a ward that works. What the section
above tells you is that his hands moved on their own for the last three
strokes, and he smelled iron, and the men nearby went quiet in a way that
had nothing to do with respect._

## Folk Magic: The Household Layer

Magic that ordinary people practice. Not galdr, seiðr, or wyrd-reading —
those require training, talent, and a willingness to pay in blood or
sanity. Folk magic is older and cheaper. It costs nothing but habit.

No one teaches it formally. Mothers show daughters how to press a rune
into bread dough. Fathers show sons where to bury the threshold knife.
The knowledge arrives the way language arrives — through repetition,
through watching, through being corrected once and remembering. Whether
any of it works is a question that no one in the Rimevegr asks aloud,
because the asking would require an answer, and the answer might be _no_,
and then what? You stop salting the doorway? You stop sewing the dead
man's mouth? You stop keeping the midwinter fire lit?

You do not stop. No one stops.

### Birth Rites

When a child is born, the father or eldest woman scratches a rune into
the door-frame. The cut is shallow — fingernail-deep in soft pine — and
traced with the mother's blood. Not the child's. The mother's. The logic,
if it is logic, is that the mother's body already kept the child alive
for nine months. Her blood knows the work.

Which rune depends on the family. Thurisaz for protection — the most
common, carved by families who want the world to leave their child alone.
Berkana for growth — favored by farming families who think in seasons.
Isa for ice, for stasis, for _endure_.

Families who carve Isa are considered unlucky. The rune means cold,
stillness, a thing frozen in place. Neighbors pity them. But their
children survive the Long Dark at higher rates than anyone else's, and
nobody discusses why. The women who carve Isa do not explain themselves.
They have buried fewer children than their neighbors. That is explanation
enough.

The rune stays in the door-frame until the wood rots or the house burns.
Some farmsteads in the older settlements have door-frames so scored with
birth-runes that the wood looks diseased.

### Death Rites

The body is laid out facing north. A copper coin placed on each eye —
Hael's toll, payment for passage to whatever the Half-Rotted keeps. The
mouth is sewn shut with leather thread. Not cord, not sinew — leather
specifically, punched through the lips with an awl. The stitching is
ugly. It is not meant to be beautiful. It is meant to hold.

The mouth-sewing keeps the dead from speaking. A corpse that can speak
can argue its way back across the Veil. A corpse that can speak can call
its name and remind the living who it was. A corpse that can speak can
say _let me in_, and someone — a wife, a child, a friend who misses the
voice — might open the door.

Everyone has a story about a family that skipped the mouth-sewing. The
details vary by settlement. The ending does not. Families that skip it
are shunned — not with violence, but with distance. A quiet stepping-away
that leaves the offending household alone in a community that cannot
afford to be alone.

### Threshold Magic

Every farmstead in the Rimevegr has three things at its doorway: a line
of salt on the threshold stone, iron nails driven into the door-frame,
and a knife buried under the doorstep with the blade pointing up.

No galdr-worker taught this. No seiðr-wife prescribed it. The practice
predates the Cracking by generations — old enough that no one remembers a
time before it. The salt is replaced at every new moon. The nails are
checked when the door sticks. The knife is never dug up. It rusts in the
ground and is replaced by feel — a father pushes a new blade into the
dirt beside the old one, not wanting to see what the earth has done to
the iron already there.

A home without threshold iron feels wrong. Not dangerous — _wrong_. The
way a meal without salt feels wrong, or a fire without smoke. Visitors
notice. They do not always say anything. But they notice.

Galdr-workers who set proper runic wards (see `data/culture/rituals.yaml`
— `RIT_RNC_001`) regard folk threshold practices with a mix of
condescension and unease. The condescension is professional — their wards
are carved, blooded, activated with words of power. The salt line is a
kitchen habit. The unease is harder to explain. The salt lines are older
than the runes.

### Seasonal Rites

Four points in the year carry folk obligations that exist alongside — and
sometimes beneath — the formal blóts performed at altar-stones.

**The Midwinter Fire.** Every household keeps a fire burning through the
entire Long Dark. Not a hearth-fire for cooking, though it serves that
purpose — a specific fire, lit at the first true dark of winter and fed
continuously until thaw. If the fire goes out, the household is cursed
for a year. What _cursed_ means varies: bad luck, sick children, a
harvest that fails. The practical truth is that a household whose fire
goes out in the Long Dark is a household that has stopped paying
attention, and inattention kills in winter regardless of curses. The fire
is tended in shifts. Children learn fire-watching before they learn
letters. The sound of a household in deep winter is the sound of someone
feeding a fire at three in the morning.

**Spring Blood.** The first animal slaughtered after thaw — usually a
goat too old to breed — has its blood poured directly on the nearest
field. Not the altar-stone. The field. The blood soaks into half-frozen
ground and disappears. This is not a blót. No god's name is spoken. The
blood is for the land, and the land is not a god. It is older than gods.

**Midsummer Silence.** One hour at solar noon on the longest day, the
settlement goes quiet. No speech, no hammering, no music, no crying
children — mothers hold hands over mouths if they must. The silence is
for the Hush. The Hush lives in the deep woods where all sound dies for
minutes at a time, where something listens. On midsummer, when the Veil
thins and the light is longest, the settlements offer an hour of silence
so that whatever listens has nothing to hear. Whether this appeases the
Hush or simply avoids drawing its attention is a theological question
that no one wants answered.

**Harvest Share.** A portion of every harvest — grain, root vegetables,
dried fish, whatever the land gave — is left at the field's edge. Not for
beggars, though beggars take it. For the land. The share is placed at
dusk and not checked until morning. What remains is ignored. What is gone
is accounted for by saying _the land took its share_. Families that skip
the harvest share are not shunned the way mouth-sewing families are. They
are watched. If their next harvest fails, the watching turns to
certainty.

### Food and Craft Magic

Folk magic is thickest where the hands are busiest.

Bread is baked with a rune pressed into the dough before it goes in the
oven. The rune varies — Fehu for prosperity, Jera for a good year,
sometimes just a cross-mark that might be a rune or might be a habit so
old the original meaning is lost. The bread does not taste different. The
household eats it anyway, and the eating is the point. A family that eats
rune-bread together is a family that still believes it will be fed
tomorrow.

Iron quenched in urine makes harder steel. This is true. The nitrogen in
urine affects the carbon content during quenching. Every smith in the
Rimevegr knows this. Every smith in the Rimevegr also knows that the
urine must come from a man who has not lain with a woman in three days,
and that the quenching must happen before dawn, and that the smith must
not speak during the plunge. The metallurgy is real. The ritual around
the metallurgy is not, probably, but the smith does it all the same,
because the one time he skipped the fasting rule the blade cracked, and
once is enough to make a rule permanent.

Wool dyed during the waning moon holds its color longer. The dyers swear
by this. Whether the lunar phase affects the fixative chemistry or
whether the dyers simply take more care during what they consider an
important window is a question for a philosopher. The Rimevegr has no
philosophers. It has dyers with consistent results.

Salt spread on the woodpile before the Long Dark preserves the wood —
keeps rot and insects at bay through the wet months. Salt preserves
everything. This is not magic. But the people who do it call it magic,
because the alternative is admitting that they understand chemistry, and
understanding chemistry is uncomfortably close to understanding how the
world works, and understanding how the world works is the galdr-worker's
business, and ordinary people do not want that business.

### Navigation Superstition

The road between settlements is not safe. The people who travel it carry
rules the way they carry knives — close, always, without thinking.

Never camp within sight of a barrow. A fire near a barrow is a lantern
near a window. Something on the other side might look out.

If you hear singing where no one should be singing, walk the other
direction. Do not investigate. Do not try to identify the voice. Walk. If
the singing follows, run.

If a raven follows your party for three days, someone in the group owes a
death. Odynn's birds collect debts. The raven will not say whose debt it
is. It does not need to. The man who owes it knows.

If fog rolls in from the north, stop moving. Northern fog is not weather.
Wait until it lifts or shifts. Movement in northern fog leads to places
that are not on any map, and some of those places do not let you leave.

When the fog lifts, count your companions. If the number is wrong — if
there is one more person than there should be — do not ask who the extra
is. Do not look at faces. Walk until the number is right again. It always
becomes right again. The question is what happens to whoever was extra,
and that is not a question you want answered.

### Mechanical Integration

Folk magic has no dice. No ranks, no checks, no WIL expenditure. It is
background — the texture of a world where the supernatural is ambient,
not exceptional.

But it touches the simulation at two points: morale and reputation.

**Settlement morale** (tracked in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, Section 6) is
affected by whether seasonal rites are observed. A settlement that misses
its midwinter fire lighting, or that has no one to perform the harvest
blót, takes a morale penalty. The folk rites are the social glue beneath
the formal religion. When they stop, people feel it — not as theological
crisis but as wrongness, the same wrongness as a home without threshold
iron.

**Band reputation** (tracked in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`, Section 13) is
affected by how the band interacts with folk practice. Bands that
interrupt seasonal rites — making noise during the Midsummer Silence,
butchering animals near someone's harvest share, camping on a
threshold-stone — take a reputation penalty in that settlement. Bands
that participate — adding wood to the midwinter fire, standing silent at
midsummer, respecting the harvest share — gain trust.

Two specific rules:

- A band that **makes noise during the Midsummer Silence**
  (combat, argument, shouting, metal-on-metal) within earshot of a
  settlement costs that settlement 1 morale and the band loses 1 reputation
  there.
- A band that **participates in a settlement's midwinter fire
  ceremony** — contributes fuel, stands vigil, adds their captain's voice
  to the night — gains +1 reputation in that settlement.

These are small numbers. Folk magic is small magic. But small numbers,
accumulated across fifteen settlements and three hundred days of winter,
are the difference between a band that finds open gates and one that
finds closed ones.

_Folk magic exists on a continuum. Some households practice all of it —
the birth-rune, the threshold iron, the fire vigil, the bread rune, the
harvest share. Some practice the death rites and nothing else. Some
settlements enforce participation; others tolerate skeptics. But even the
skeptics sew the mouths of their dead. Even the skeptics salt their
doorways. The sky broke three hundred years ago and the dead walk and
something listens in the deep woods. A line of salt costs nothing. Not
having one costs sleep._

## Magic, Power, and Authority

Nobody does magic for wisdom. Wisdom does not stop the Long Dark
from killing your livestock or the dead from walking through your
grain-stores. Power stops those things. Rune-wards stop them.
Salt-lines and binding-marks and a galdr-worker who knows which
strokes hold and which ones have been failing for the last decade.

Magic in the Rimevegr is a commodity. It is bought, sold, taxed,
withheld, and killed over — the same as iron, the same as grain,
the same as fighting men. The difference is scarcity. Iron can be
mined. Grain can be grown. A man who can carve a ward that holds
through the Long Dark cannot be manufactured. He can only be found,
hired, controlled, and — when he becomes inconvenient — replaced.

This section covers how the three magical traditions function as
political instruments within the union structure of Year 312. For
what magic feels like and what it costs, see Section 2. For its
mechanical rules, see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`.

### The Galdr-Worker as Political Asset

A jarl needs wards the way a jarl needs walls. The longhouse, the
grain-store, the armory, the harbor pilings, the border-stones that
mark where his authority ends and the moor begins — all of it needs
rune-work. Galdr wards keep the dead from walking through doors that
should be closed. They keep the Veil-mist from pooling in root
cellars. They keep the binding-marks on the old barrows from
failing a decade early.

A jarl without a galdr-worker has unprotected walls. In the Long
Summer, this is a problem. In the Long Dark, it is a death sentence
— not for the jarl, who sleeps behind the thickest doors, but for
the outer farmsteads, the granaries at the settlement edge, the
families whose homes are closest to the treeline. The dead do not
care about politics. They walk where the wards are weakest.

This creates dependency. Deepholm's galdr workshop — the only
permanent facility of its kind in the Rimevegr, maintained by Jarl
Sigrun's silver — trains apprentices and stores rune-lore on
archive scrolls. Skaldhaven's rune archive supplements this with
older knowledge, pre-Cracking inscriptions that nobody alive can
fully read. Between them, the Fjord Compact controls the region's
largest concentration of galdr knowledge. This is not an accident.
Sigrun funds the workshop the way she funds the garrison: because
infrastructure is power.

A galdr-worker who understands his value can negotiate. Separate
quarters — not in the longhouse, where the jarl's men drink and
fight. Exemption from labor levies. Silver payment, not barter.
The best galdr-workers live better than the jarl's huscarls. Some
live better than minor headmen.

But a galdr-worker who overreaches dies. This is the fundamental
equation. Galdr is the most common tradition because it is the most
tolerated — physical, visible, comprehensible. A man carves a rune
and the rune glows. The process has inputs and outputs. A jarl can
watch it happen and understand, roughly, what he is paying for.
This transparency makes galdr workers controllable. And
controllable means replaceable.

Grim — the Pale Widow's curse-carver at Ashen Reach, WYR 4,
hands shaking, eyes gone to milk — is what happens when a
galdr-worker stops being controllable. He carves nithing-poles and
ill-runes. He thins the Veil at locations the Widow designates.
He does work that no legitimate galdr-worker would accept, and the
cost is written on his body. His hands shake because the runes are
fighting him. His eyes are pale because something is looking back
through the carvings. A jarl who employs a man like Grim has
crossed a line, and the line is not moral. It is practical. Grim
will die of his work within a year or two. When he does, the Widow
will need another. There is always another. That is the galdr
economy. Renewable, expendable, and just skilled enough to be
necessary.

### The Seiðr-Wife as Power Broker

The dead talk. This is not theology. This is intelligence.

A seiðr-wife who communes with a dead jarl knows where the silver
is buried. She knows which oaths were broken. She knows who the
jarl bedded, who he cheated, who he promised land to and never
delivered. The dead do not keep secrets — not because they are
honest, but because the trance strips away the defenses the living
use to hide. A living man lies. A dead man cannot remember how.

This means a seiðr-wife who speaks with the previous jarl holds
power over the current one. Every jarl in the Rimevegr rules by
some combination of force, wealth, and legitimacy. Legitimacy is
the weakest leg. It depends on the perception that the jarl earned
his seat — through blood, through merit, through the old jarl's
dying wish. A seiðr-wife who can prove the old jarl wanted someone
else, or that the current jarl poisoned his way to the chair, or
that the silver in the strongroom was stolen from a man who was
promised peace — that seiðr-wife has leverage that no amount of
armed men can answer.

This is why seiðr-wives live outside settlements. Not by choice.
By mandate. The jarl does not want her in the longhouse, hearing
things, learning names. She lives a half-day's walk out, in a
cave or a hut or a driftwood shelter that she maintains herself.
You come to her. On your terms, at your pace, with your question
prepared. The arrangement gives the jarl the illusion of control:
he decides when to consult the dead, what to ask, and how much to
pay. The seiðr-wife allows this illusion because the alternative
is the pyre.

Ragnhild Isarnsdottir at Icebreak — WYR 5, the most powerful
practitioner in the region — lives in an ice cave shrine at the
edge of habitable land. She is gaunt, fever-eyed, surrounded by
whale-bone charms. She offers divination for a blood price. Nobody
controls Ragnhild because nobody can reach her without risking
Veil territory. This makes her the only truly independent
practitioner in the Rimevegr, and it makes her useless for
day-to-day political manipulation. She is consulted for the large
questions — war, succession, catastrophe. The small questions go
unanswered because the small questions require a seiðr-wife you
can reach, and a seiðr-wife you can reach is a seiðr-wife you
can threaten.

The Cave-Woman at Ashen Reach is the opposite model. WYR 5,
WIL 7, physically wrecked — has not eaten solid food in two
months. She dream-sends and death-reads at the Pale Widow's
direction. She is a weapon, not an advisor. The Widow controls
her through dependency: the Cave-Woman is too deteriorated to
survive alone, too deep into her practice to stop, too valuable
to discard until she breaks entirely. When she dies — soon — the
Widow loses her most powerful asset and gains nothing but the
need to find another.

Dalla, the band's seiðr-wife — WYR 3, heal 3, wyrd-reading 2
— sits between these extremes. She is the only band member at
the magic threshold. She is useful but not powerful enough to
threaten anyone. She heals. She reads signs. She does not commune
with dead jarls because she is not strong enough, and because
Voss is smart enough not to ask. A band seiðr-wife who starts
answering political questions stops being a healer and starts
being a target.

Male seiðr practitioners are doubly dangerous in political
terms. The ergi stigma is not about sexuality — it is about
the impossibility of control. A man who will commune with the
dead has already shown he will cross the boundary that holds
the social order together. He carries death-knowledge and broken
gender taboos simultaneously. He cannot be married into a
family for alliance. He cannot be integrated into a huscarl
retinue. He cannot be made safe. A jarl can hire him, but
every other jarl will wonder what the man whispers at night,
and whose dead he speaks with when no one is watching.

### The Wyrd-Reader as Reluctant Advisor

Every band has someone who shakes lots before a march. Every
jarl consults someone before a battle, a journey, a marriage
pact. Wyrd-reading is the most common tradition, the least
powerful, and the most politically manageable — which is why
it survives everywhere while the other traditions cluster around
the practitioners desperate or skilled enough to endure them.

Petra shakes bone lots. WYR 2, wyrd-reading 1. She says "bad
road" and the band adjusts route. She says "wait a day" and
Voss considers it. Nobody calls this magic. Nobody calls it
anything. It is what it is: a woman with a knack and a bag of
bones, useful often enough to keep around.

Voss himself carries a rune-amulet and has WYR 2, wyrd-reading

1. He touches the amulet before decisions the way other men
   check the wind. Whether the amulet tells him anything or
   whether he simply thinks better with his hand on something
   familiar is a question Voss has never asked himself, and
   would not answer honestly if he did.

The wyrd-reader's limitation is also his protection: he cannot
change what he sees. The reading is informational, not
interventional. A jarl who hears "you will lose this battle"
can choose to fight anyway. The wyrd-reader delivered the
message. What happens next is the jarl's problem. This makes
wyrd-readers safe to keep around. They do not reshape events.
They report on the current the way a man with a wet finger
reports on the wind.

The exception destroys the rule. A wyrd-reader who sees a
jarl's death holds a succession crisis in his mouth. If he
speaks, every heir begins positioning. If he stays silent,
the jarl dies surprised and the succession is violent. If he
speaks to the jarl privately, the jarl may kill him to prevent
the reading from spreading — because a death foretold is a
death invited, in the minds of men who live by reputation.

Wise wyrd-readers learn to lie by omission. They see a
shipwreck and say "the sea is restless." They see a betrayal
and say "watch the man on your left." They see a death and
say nothing at all, because nothing is the only safe reading
when the answer is final.

### Magic in Union Politics

The three unions of Year 312 handle magic differently, and the
differences are where the friction lives.

**Ward-rights.** The Allthing meets days 90-100, post-harvest,
early in the Long Dark. The ground where it meets must be warded.
Someone must carve the binding-marks on the Allthing stones —
the border-posts that define the assembly space as neutral ground.
Whoever controls that appointment controls who can speak safely
within the wards and, more importantly, who cannot.

Currently, the Fjord Compact provides the Allthing's galdr-worker
through Skaldhaven. This is Sigrun's quiet leverage. The Iron Grip
has not challenged it because Ordovast considers ward-work beneath
his attention. The Whispering Circle has not challenged it because
the Pale Widow prefers that her enemies feel safe at the Allthing.
Safe men speak freely. Free speech reveals plans.

**Seiðr testimony.** Can a seiðr-wife testify at the ting about
what the dead said? The answer depends on which union is asking.
The Fjord Compact permits it — Sigrun believes information is
information regardless of source, and a dead man's testimony
about a broken oath is no less valid than a living witness. The
Iron Grip forbids it — Ordovast will not have his authority
undermined by women who talk to corpses, and his huscarls enforce
this position with the casual violence that makes the Iron Grip
function. The Whispering Circle has no formal position, because
the Pale Widow does not bring her intelligence to the ting. She
uses it beforehand.

This split is a source of inter-union tension that has not yet
produced violence. It will. A disputed succession where the only
witness is dead and only a seiðr-wife can reach him — that case
is coming. When it arrives, the Allthing will either accept seiðr
testimony as binding or reject it permanently. Either outcome
reshapes power. Acceptance gives every seiðr-wife in the region
the authority of a judge. Rejection makes dead men's secrets the
exclusive currency of whoever employs seiðr-wives privately.

**Curse arbitration.** A nithing-pole raised against a jarl is an
act of magical aggression. The question is who adjudicates. The
Iron Grip treats curse-work as assault: find the carver, kill the
carver. The Fjord Compact treats it as a legal claim requiring
investigation — sometimes the curse has a legitimate grievance
behind it. The Whispering Circle's position is invisible, because
the Whispering Circle is the one raising the poles.

**Dark arts prohibition.** The Allthing officially prohibits
"blood-galdr and death-seiðr." The words are in the law-speaker's
recitation, repeated every year at the assembly opening. The
definitions are political, not magical. A ward that requires
the carver's own blood — standard practice, ranked in
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` under galdr material costs. A ward that
requires someone else's blood — forbidden. A seiðr-wife who
speaks with the dead — tolerated. A seiðr-wife who sends the
dead to act — forbidden. The line between "communes with" and
"commands" is a legal fiction that shifts with whoever has the
most armed men in the room.

The Whispering Circle operates at dark arts level 2: active
curse-carving and nithing-poles. This is known to the other
unions. It is not proven, which is the distinction that matters.
The consequences are already measurable — Vargheim's Jarl Ulf
distrusts the Pale Widow and suspects seiðr. Two trade partners
have refused dealings with Ashen Reach on rumor alone. The
Circle's cohesion sits at 2, fragile, one exposure from
collapse. One confirmed report of Grim's work reaching the
Allthing and Frostfjord Hollow defects immediately. Hrothgar
is hedging, not loyal.

The prohibition's real function is not prevention. It is
leverage. Every union violates the spirit of the law — Ordovast's
men have beaten confessions out of suspected curse-workers
without ting sanction, which is itself a prohibited act. Sigrun
funds a galdr workshop that experiments with rune combinations
the law-speaker's formula would cover if anyone looked closely
enough. The Pale Widow's violations are simply more visible
because her union is weakest, and weak unions cannot afford the
ambiguity that strong ones take for granted.

The dark arts scale runs to 5. The Whispering Circle is at 2.
Level 3 is Veil-thinning — deliberate weakening of the barrier
between the living and the dead. Level 4 is death-reading and
targeted assassination through magical means. Level 5 is
invocation: calling something through the Veil and giving it
purchase in the world.

Grim can thin the Veil. The Cave-Woman can death-read. Between
them, the Whispering Circle is one decision away from level 3,
and one desperate night away from level 4. The distance between
2 and 5 is not measured in skill. It is measured in what the
Pale Widow is willing to pay, and what she believes she has
left to lose.

_Power in the Rimevegr is local, personal, and temporary. A
jarl's authority extends one day's march from his seat. Magic
does not change this. Magic is the tool a jarl uses to hold
what force alone cannot — the grain-store through the Long
Dark, the border-stones through the Veil-mist, the silence
of the dead who know too much about how he came to sit where
he sits. The man with the runes controls the ward-line. The
ward-line is the only thing between the settlement and three
hundred days of dark. Everything else is negotiation._

## Contradictions and Disputes

The three traditions do not agree. This is not a failing of
scholarship or a gap in the record. It is the fundamental
condition of the supernatural in the Rimevegr: nobody knows how
any of it works, and the people who understand it best disagree
the loudest.

These disputes are not academic. They determine whether you burn
a draugr or try to speak with it. Whether you reinforce a
ward-stone or abandon the barrow. Whether you trust the
seiðr-wife's screaming trance or the galdr-worker's quiet runes.
Getting the answer wrong means someone dies in a way that proves
nothing, because the dead do not explain themselves and the
survivors argue about what happened for the next thirty years.

Five disputes define the fracture lines. Each has been argued
around fires, in mead-halls, at barrow-mouths with the bodies
still warm. None has been settled.

### What Is the Hush?

The Hush is real. Every band that has marched through deep forest
has experienced it: sound dies. Not muffles — dies. Birdsong
stops mid-note. Wind noise vanishes. Your own breathing becomes
the loudest thing in the world, and then even that seems to thin,
as though the air itself is holding still. It lasts minutes.
Sometimes longer. Then it lifts, and the forest resumes as though
nothing happened.

(See `06_DICTIONARY_OF_NORSE_TERMS.md` — _The Hush_: "Something
is listening.")

The galdr-workers say the Hush is rune-death. The world's magical
structure is inscribed in patterns older than human carving — the
runes that hold reality together, the deep grammar beneath weather
and wood and stone. Where those patterns decay, the fabric frays.
Sound is structure. Where structure fails, sound fails. The Hush
marks a place where the world's own rune-work has gone blank.

This predicts that the Hush should be location-fixed — tied to
specific sites where the old patterns have degraded. It sometimes
is. There are stands of pine in the Ironwood where the Hush falls
reliably, and galdr-workers who have examined the area report that
rune-work carved there fades faster, as though the medium itself
is spent. Ash has felt it. He does not carve in Hush-zones if he
can avoid it. The strokes feel wrong — not resistant, but hollow.
Cutting into wood that has forgotten what shape means.

The seiðr-wives say the Hush is attention. Something behind the
Veil is listening, and it is large enough — present enough — that
its attention alone displaces sound the way a hand displaces
water. The Hush is not absence. It is pressure. The silence of a
room when someone powerful walks in and everyone stops talking at
once.

This predicts that the Hush should move. It does. It follows no
wind pattern, no terrain logic, no seasonal rhythm. It drifts.
The seiðr-wives say it drifts the way a gaze drifts — scanning,
pausing, moving on. Dalla has been caught in two Hush events on
the march, a week apart, in locations thirty miles from each
other. She sat very still both times and said nothing afterward.
She did not need to. Her face said it.

The wyrd-readers say the Hush is the absence of fate. A moment
where nothing is woven. The loom stops, the thread hangs slack,
and the world holds its breath because for that interval nothing
is determined — not the bird's next note, not the wind's next
gust, not whether the man standing in the silence will live to
hear sound again. Petra's bone lots, cast during a Hush, came up
blank. Not a bad reading. No reading. The bones fell and meant
nothing, which had never happened to her before and which she
refuses to discuss.

All three predictions are occasionally correct. The Hush sometimes
stays in one place. The Hush sometimes moves. The Hush sometimes
coincides with wyrd-dead zones where readings fail. Nobody has
assembled the full pattern because nobody who studies one tradition
trusts the observations of the other two, and the Hush does not
last long enough for collaborative research even if collaboration
were possible, which it is not, because galdr-workers and
seiðr-wives can barely stand to be in the same room.

### Do the Gods Still Exist?

The temples stand. No one answers. This is established fact and
has been for three hundred years (see Section 1 — _The Silence of
the Gods_).

The interesting question is not the silence. The interesting
question is what still works and why.

Seiðr-wives who contact very old dead — pre-Cracking dead,
spirits that remember the world before — receive fragmentary
impressions of divine presence. Not words. Not messages. An
aftertaste, the way a room smells of smoke long after the fire
is out. Ragnhild at Icebreak (WYR 5, Spirit-lore 4) reached a
dead man who had served as a temple-keeper before the Cracking.
What came back was not language. It was weight. A sense of
something vast that had been there and was no longer there, and
the shape of its absence was specific enough that Ragnhild could
tell the difference between the absence of Odin and the absence
of Freyja. Different voids. Different silences. She described it
as walking through a hall where the furniture has been removed
but the floor is still worn in the paths where people walked.

Post-Cracking dead report nothing. No divine presence. No divine
absence. Nothing to compare against. Whatever the gods were, the
dead who never knew them cannot feel the hole.

But galdr still works.

This is the problem that no one wants to examine. Galdr was
originally Odin's gift — the runes given to those who could hear
them, the carving-knowledge that channels power through inscribed
form. If Odin is gone, who is powering the runes? The channels
should be dry. The tools should be inert. Ash should be scratching
meaningless lines into meaningless stone.

He is not. The runes light up. The wards hold. Something answers
when a galdr-worker carves a binding-mark. Something provides the
force that makes cold iron glow blue and dead men flinch away
from a threshold.

Galdr-workers avoid this question with a discipline that borders
on mania. Some theorize that the runes are self-sustaining — that
Odin's gift, once given, operates independently of the giver, the
way a mill keeps grinding after the builder dies. Others suggest
that the power source was never Odin at all, that the gods merely
discovered the runes and passed them along, and the real source
is something older and less personal. This theory is unpopular
because it raises the follow-up question — _what is older than
the gods?_ — and the seiðr-wives' theory about the Devouring
provides an answer that no one wants.

Three galdr-workers in living memory have pursued this question to
its end. Two went mad. The third walked into the Veil and did not
return. The community treats these outcomes as evidence that the
question is dangerous, not that it has no answer. The distinction
matters. A question with no answer is philosophy. A question that
destroys the people who pursue it is something else.

The rituals continue. Not because anyone believes they are heard.
Because stopping is an admission, and the admission is a door,
and no one knows what is on the other side of that door but the
seiðr-wives have guesses and their guesses keep them up at night.

### Can Draugr Be Healed?

The seiðr tradition says yes. A draugr is a restless dead — a
spirit that has not been properly laid, or that has been pushed
back across the Veil by whatever frightens the dead (see
Section 1 — _The Seiðr-Wives' Theory_). Communion can reach them.
The right rites — speaking their name, acknowledging their grief,
performing the funerary obligations that were skipped or botched
— can guide them to stillness. A seiðr-wife will sit with a
draugr the way she sits with a frightened child. She will speak
to it in trance. She will find out what it wants and try to
give it.

Dalla has done this. Once. The details are not shared widely, but
the draugr stopped walking and the barrow resealed itself and
Dalla did not speak for four days afterward. She considers it a
success. The band is not sure.

The galdr tradition says no. A draugr is a broken rune — a body
animated by decaying magic, the same way a ward-stone glows after
the galdr-worker who carved it is dead. The body moves because
the pattern inscribed on it by life has not fully degraded. There
is nothing to commune with. There is no spirit to lay. You would
not negotiate with a cracked millstone still turning after the
miller has gone home. You would smash it before it grinds
someone's hand off.

Common wisdom agrees with the galdr-workers but for simpler
reasons: burn it. Draugr are dangerous. They kill people. The
theological question of whether they have an inner life is a
luxury for practitioners who do not have to stand in a barrow
mouth at midnight with a torch and a hand-axe.

The real question — the one nobody asks because the implications
are unbearable — is whether the draugr was ever actually dead.
The named draugr of the Rimevegr complicate every theory. Eydis
Hearth-Ash (WYR 3) remembers her household. She has preferences.
She avoids firelight not because it harms her but because it
reminds her of something. Skorri Iron-Lock (WYR 4) guards his
barrow not from mindless territorial instinct but because he
knows what is buried there and does not want it found. These are
not broken runes. These are not empty patterns grinding on
without purpose. These are beings with agendas, knowledge, and
secrets.

(See `06_DICTIONARY_OF_NORSE_TERMS.md` — _Draugr_: "Not
mindless — they remember old grudges.")

If a draugr remembers, can it forget? If it has an agenda, can
the agenda be fulfilled? If it can be fulfilled, is destruction
murder?

The galdr-workers do not engage with these questions. The
seiðr-wives engage with nothing else. The bands hired to clear
barrows develop their own heuristics: if it talks, try talking
back. If it does not talk, use fire. If it talked last week and
stopped, use fire faster. These are not theological positions.
They are survival rules, and they contradict each other as often
as the theories do.

### Is Wyrd-Reading Magic?

Many practitioners insist it is not. Casting lots is pattern
recognition. Reading entrails is observation — the state of a
liver tells you about the animal's health, the quality of its
grazing, the season's water. Birds fly predictably if you know
wind, terrain, and migration timing. A good tracker reads sign
on the ground. A good wyrd-reader reads sign in the air. No
spirits involved. No divine gift. No Veil.

This is a comfortable position and it has the weight of common
sense behind it. Thorne holds it. He calls his rune-amulet work
"reading" — the way you read a room, read a footprint, read
the sky before a storm. Not magic. Attentiveness. A life skill
honed by thirty years of not dying. The captain consults Petra's
bone lots before marches the way he consults the weather: input,
not prophecy.

The philosophical problem is the practitioners themselves.

If wyrd-reading is pattern recognition, why do wyrd-readers
develop the same fatalism? The same thousand-yard stare, the
same inability to plan more than three days ahead, the same
quiet certainty that nothing they do will alter what arrives?
Petra sleeps poorly. Thorne moves his bedroll to the danger
side of camp every single night. Neither of them can articulate
why. Pattern-recognition experts do not develop existential
dread from reading weather signs.

The mechanical evidence is harder to dismiss. The wyrd_chance
formula in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` includes the Wyrd stat:
`(Wyrd × 5) + (Wyrd-reading × 10) + 15`. Wyrd is the
supernatural attribute. It rises with exposure to the uncanny.
If wyrd-reading is merely clever observation, why does the
supernatural stat determine whether the lots fall true? Why
does a man's capacity for touching the Veil affect his ability
to read bird-flight?

The wyrd-readers have no answer. They have a silence, which
they maintain with the same dogged commitment that the
galdr-workers bring to not asking who powers the runes. The
alternative — admitting that their craft is supernatural —
brings consequences. Galdr-workers are respected but watched.
Seiðr-workers are feared and stigmatized, especially the men.
Wyrd-readers pass unnoticed because their craft looks like
common sense. The moment it stops looking like common sense,
they become practitioners, and practitioners pay costs that
readers do not. Nobody volunteers for that reclassification.
So the lots fall, and the readings come true more often than
chance allows, and everyone agrees it is not magic, and nobody
sleeps well.

### Who Owns Barrow Contents?

When a barrow is cleared — the draugr put down, the wards
assessed, the contents catalogued — four separate claims land
on the loot before the dust has settled.

**Galdr-workers** claim rune-inscribed artifacts as sacred
heritage. A binding-ring carved before the Cracking is not
treasure. It is a surviving specimen of a dying art, as
valuable as the Deepholm archive scrolls, irreplaceable and not
for sale. Galdr-workers who examine barrow finds handle them
the way a healer handles a wound — gently, seriously, with an
anger directed at anyone who would melt the ring down for its
silver.

**Seiðr-wives** say the contents should stay in the barrow.
The dead placed them there. The dead remember them. Removing
grave goods offends the spirits and thins the Veil at the
barrow site. Every barrow opened and looted is a hole in the
world's defenses that nobody can patch. The seiðr-wives have
evidence: cleared barrows that were looted show higher
Veil-thinning activity in subsequent years than cleared barrows
where the contents were left in place. The galdr-workers dispute
the methodology. The seiðr-wives dispute the galdr-workers'
right to dispute anything involving the dead.

**Jarls** say barrow loot belongs to whoever paid for the
clearing. A jarl who hires a band to deal with a barrow that
threatens a settlement has funded the operation, assumed the
political risk, and will answer to the settlement if the
clearing goes wrong. The loot is payment, or at least
collateral. This is a property argument, not a theological one,
and it has the force of law behind it — or what passes for law
in the Rimevegr, which is the jarl's word backed by the jarl's
armed men.

**Bands** say it belongs to whoever went in and survived. The
jarl did not fight the draugr. The galdr-worker did not dodge
the axe. The seiðr-wife was not standing in the burial chamber
when Skorri Iron-Lock came off his slab with murder in his
dead eyes and a blade that had not rusted in two hundred years.
The people who bled for the contents own the contents. This is
not a legal argument. It is a moral one, and it is shouted
louder than any of the others because the people shouting it
are the ones with fresh wounds.

The Grave Wardens — a rival band that specializes in barrow
work — have built their entire reputation on navigating these
claims. They negotiate splits before the first torch is lit.
Jarl gets the silver. Band keeps weapons and armor. Galdr-workers
get first examination rights on any inscribed artifacts, with a
time limit. Seiðr-wives get to perform rites before removal.
The system works until it doesn't, and it doesn't whenever a
barrow produces something genuinely powerful — a blade that
hums, a ring that warms when draugr are near, an amulet carved
with runes that no living galdr-worker can read.

Then the four claims collide and someone draws a weapon and the
barrow-clearing becomes a different kind of problem entirely.

Every band contract in the Rimevegr carries this dispute in its
margins. The written terms say one thing. The unwritten
understanding says another. The galdr-workers and seiðr-wives
watch each other across the barrow mouth, each waiting for the
other to reach for something they shouldn't. The jarl's man
counts coins. The band leader counts exits.

_The five disputes above share a single root: nobody understands
the supernatural, and the people who understand it best disagree
the most. This is not ignorance. It is the condition of a world
where magic is real, measurable, and opaque — a machine that
works but has no manual, operated by people who have each read a
different page of the maintenance log and are convinced their
page is the only one that matters._

## Magic, Power, and Authority

Nobody does magic for wisdom. Wisdom does not stop the Long Dark
from killing your livestock or the dead from walking through your
grain-stores. Power stops those things. Rune-wards stop them.
Salt-lines and binding-marks and a galdr-worker who knows which
strokes hold and which ones have been failing for the last decade.

Magic in the Rimevegr is a commodity. It is bought, sold, taxed,
withheld, and killed over — the same as iron, the same as grain,
the same as fighting men. The difference is scarcity. Iron can be
mined. Grain can be grown. A man who can carve a ward that holds
through the Long Dark cannot be manufactured. He can only be found,
hired, controlled, and — when he becomes inconvenient — replaced.

This section covers how the three magical traditions function as
political instruments within the union structure of Year 312. For
what magic feels like and what it costs, see Section 2. For its
mechanical rules, see `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md`.

### The Galdr-Worker as Political Asset

A jarl needs wards the way a jarl needs walls. The longhouse, the
grain-store, the armory, the harbor pilings, the border-stones that
mark where his authority ends and the moor begins — all of it needs
rune-work. Galdr wards keep the dead from walking through doors that
should be closed. They keep the Veil-mist from pooling in root
cellars. They keep the binding-marks on the old barrows from
failing a decade early.

A jarl without a galdr-worker has unprotected walls. In the Long
Summer, this is a problem. In the Long Dark, it is a death sentence
— not for the jarl, who sleeps behind the thickest doors, but for
the outer farmsteads, the granaries at the settlement edge, the
families whose homes are closest to the treeline. The dead do not
care about politics. They walk where the wards are weakest.

This creates dependency. Deepholm's galdr workshop — the only
permanent facility of its kind in the Rimevegr, maintained by Jarl
Sigrun's silver — trains apprentices and stores rune-lore on
archive scrolls. Skaldhaven's rune archive supplements this with
older knowledge, pre-Cracking inscriptions that nobody alive can
fully read. Between them, the Fjord Compact controls the region's
largest concentration of galdr knowledge. This is not an accident.
Sigrun funds the workshop the way she funds the garrison: because
infrastructure is power.

A galdr-worker who understands his value can negotiate. Separate
quarters — not in the longhouse, where the jarl's men drink and
fight. Exemption from labor levies. Silver payment, not barter.
The best galdr-workers live better than the jarl's huscarls. Some
live better than minor headmen.

But a galdr-worker who overreaches dies. This is the fundamental
equation. Galdr is the most common tradition because it is the most
tolerated — physical, visible, comprehensible. A man carves a rune
and the rune glows. The process has inputs and outputs. A jarl can
watch it happen and understand, roughly, what he is paying for.
This transparency makes galdr workers controllable. And
controllable means replaceable.

Grim — the Pale Widow's curse-carver at Ashen Reach, WYR 4,
hands shaking, eyes gone to milk — is what happens when a
galdr-worker stops being controllable. He carves nithing-poles and
ill-runes. He thins the Veil at locations the Widow designates.
He does work that no legitimate galdr-worker would accept, and the
cost is written on his body. His hands shake because the runes are
fighting him. His eyes are pale because something is looking back
through the carvings. A jarl who employs a man like Grim has
crossed a line, and the line is not moral. It is practical. Grim
will die of his work within a year or two. When he does, the Widow
will need another. There is always another. That is the galdr
economy. Renewable, expendable, and just skilled enough to be
necessary.

### The Seiðr-Wife as Power Broker

The dead talk. This is not theology. This is intelligence.

A seiðr-wife who communes with a dead jarl knows where the silver
is buried. She knows which oaths were broken. She knows who the
jarl bedded, who he cheated, who he promised land to and never
delivered. The dead do not keep secrets — not because they are
honest, but because the trance strips away the defenses the living
use to hide. A living man lies. A dead man cannot remember how.

This means a seiðr-wife who speaks with the previous jarl holds
power over the current one. Every jarl in the Rimevegr rules by
some combination of force, wealth, and legitimacy. Legitimacy is
the weakest leg. It depends on the perception that the jarl earned
his seat — through blood, through merit, through the old jarl's
dying wish. A seiðr-wife who can prove the old jarl wanted someone
else, or that the current jarl poisoned his way to the chair, or
that the silver in the strongroom was stolen from a man who was
promised peace — that seiðr-wife has leverage that no amount of
armed men can answer.

This is why seiðr-wives live outside settlements. Not by choice.
By mandate. The jarl does not want her in the longhouse, hearing
things, learning names. She lives a half-day's walk out, in a
cave or a hut or a driftwood shelter that she maintains herself.
You come to her. On your terms, at your pace, with your question
prepared. The arrangement gives the jarl the illusion of control:
he decides when to consult the dead, what to ask, and how much to
pay. The seiðr-wife allows this illusion because the alternative
is the pyre.

Ragnhild Isarnsdottir at Icebreak — WYR 5, the most powerful
practitioner in the region — lives in an ice cave shrine at the
edge of habitable land. She is gaunt, fever-eyed, surrounded by
whale-bone charms. She offers divination for a blood price. Nobody
controls Ragnhild because nobody can reach her without risking
Veil territory. This makes her the only truly independent
practitioner in the Rimevegr, and it makes her useless for
day-to-day political manipulation. She is consulted for the large
questions — war, succession, catastrophe. The small questions go
unanswered because the small questions require a seiðr-wife you
can reach, and a seiðr-wife you can reach is a seiðr-wife you
can threaten.

The Cave-Woman at Ashen Reach is the opposite model. WYR 5,
WIL 7, physically wrecked — has not eaten solid food in two
months. She dream-sends and death-reads at the Pale Widow's
direction. She is a weapon, not an advisor. The Widow controls
her through dependency: the Cave-Woman is too deteriorated to
survive alone, too deep into her practice to stop, too valuable
to discard until she breaks entirely. When she dies — soon — the
Widow loses her most powerful asset and gains nothing but the
need to find another.

Dalla, the band's seiðr-wife — WYR 3, heal 3, wyrd-reading 2
— sits between these extremes. She is the only band member at
the magic threshold. She is useful but not powerful enough to
threaten anyone. She heals. She reads signs. She does not commune
with dead jarls because she is not strong enough, and because
Voss is smart enough not to ask. A band seiðr-wife who starts
answering political questions stops being a healer and starts
being a target.

Male seiðr practitioners are doubly dangerous in political
terms. The ergi stigma is not about sexuality — it is about
the impossibility of control. A man who will commune with the
dead has already shown he will cross the boundary that holds
the social order together. He carries death-knowledge and broken
gender taboos simultaneously. He cannot be married into a
family for alliance. He cannot be integrated into a huscarl
retinue. He cannot be made safe. A jarl can hire him, but
every other jarl will wonder what the man whispers at night,
and whose dead he speaks with when no one is watching.

### The Wyrd-Reader as Reluctant Advisor

Every band has someone who shakes lots before a march. Every
jarl consults someone before a battle, a journey, a marriage
pact. Wyrd-reading is the most common tradition, the least
powerful, and the most politically manageable — which is why
it survives everywhere while the other traditions cluster around
the practitioners desperate or skilled enough to endure them.

Petra shakes bone lots. WYR 2, wyrd-reading 1. She says "bad
road" and the band adjusts route. She says "wait a day" and
Voss considers it. Nobody calls this magic. Nobody calls it
anything. It is what it is: a woman with a knack and a bag of
bones, useful often enough to keep around.

Voss himself carries a rune-amulet and has WYR 2, wyrd-reading

1. He touches the amulet before decisions the way other men
   check the wind. Whether the amulet tells him anything or
   whether he simply thinks better with his hand on something
   familiar is a question Voss has never asked himself, and
   would not answer honestly if he did.

The wyrd-reader's limitation is also his protection: he cannot
change what he sees. The reading is informational, not
interventional. A jarl who hears "you will lose this battle"
can choose to fight anyway. The wyrd-reader delivered the
message. What happens next is the jarl's problem. This makes
wyrd-readers safe to keep around. They do not reshape events.
They report on the current the way a man with a wet finger
reports on the wind.

The exception destroys the rule. A wyrd-reader who sees a
jarl's death holds a succession crisis in his mouth. If he
speaks, every heir begins positioning. If he stays silent,
the jarl dies surprised and the succession is violent. If he
speaks to the jarl privately, the jarl may kill him to prevent
the reading from spreading — because a death foretold is a
death invited, in the minds of men who live by reputation.

Wise wyrd-readers learn to lie by omission. They see a
shipwreck and say "the sea is restless." They see a betrayal
and say "watch the man on your left." They see a death and
say nothing at all, because nothing is the only safe reading
when the answer is final.

### Magic in Union Politics

The three unions of Year 312 handle magic differently, and the
differences are where the friction lives.

**Ward-rights.** The Allthing meets days 90-100, post-harvest,
early in the Long Dark. The ground where it meets must be warded.
Someone must carve the binding-marks on the Allthing stones —
the border-posts that define the assembly space as neutral ground.
Whoever controls that appointment controls who can speak safely
within the wards and, more importantly, who cannot.

Currently, the Fjord Compact provides the Allthing's galdr-worker
through Skaldhaven. This is Sigrun's quiet leverage. The Iron Grip
has not challenged it because Ordovast considers ward-work beneath
his attention. The Whispering Circle has not challenged it because
the Pale Widow prefers that her enemies feel safe at the Allthing.
Safe men speak freely. Free speech reveals plans.

**Seiðr testimony.** Can a seiðr-wife testify at the ting about
what the dead said? The answer depends on which union is asking.
The Fjord Compact permits it — Sigrun believes information is
information regardless of source, and a dead man's testimony
about a broken oath is no less valid than a living witness. The
Iron Grip forbids it — Ordovast will not have his authority
undermined by women who talk to corpses, and his huscarls enforce
this position with the casual violence that makes the Iron Grip
function. The Whispering Circle has no formal position, because
the Pale Widow does not bring her intelligence to the ting. She
uses it beforehand.

This split is a source of inter-union tension that has not yet
produced violence. It will. A disputed succession where the only
witness is dead and only a seiðr-wife can reach him — that case
is coming. When it arrives, the Allthing will either accept seiðr
testimony as binding or reject it permanently. Either outcome
reshapes power. Acceptance gives every seiðr-wife in the region
the authority of a judge. Rejection makes dead men's secrets the
exclusive currency of whoever employs seiðr-wives privately.

**Curse arbitration.** A nithing-pole raised against a jarl is an
act of magical aggression. The question is who adjudicates. The
Iron Grip treats curse-work as assault: find the carver, kill the
carver. The Fjord Compact treats it as a legal claim requiring
investigation — sometimes the curse has a legitimate grievance
behind it. The Whispering Circle's position is invisible, because
the Whispering Circle is the one raising the poles.

**Dark arts prohibition.** The Allthing officially prohibits
"blood-galdr and death-seiðr." The words are in the law-speaker's
recitation, repeated every year at the assembly opening. The
definitions are political, not magical. A ward that requires
the carver's own blood — standard practice, ranked in
`20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` under galdr material costs. A ward that
requires someone else's blood — forbidden. A seiðr-wife who
speaks with the dead — tolerated. A seiðr-wife who sends the
dead to act — forbidden. The line between "communes with" and
"commands" is a legal fiction that shifts with whoever has the
most armed men in the room.

The Whispering Circle operates at dark arts level 2: active
curse-carving and nithing-poles. This is known to the other
unions. It is not proven, which is the distinction that matters.
The consequences are already measurable — Vargheim's Jarl Ulf
distrusts the Pale Widow and suspects seiðr. Two trade partners
have refused dealings with Ashen Reach on rumor alone. The
Circle's cohesion sits at 2, fragile, one exposure from
collapse. One confirmed report of Grim's work reaching the
Allthing and Frostfjord Hollow defects immediately. Hrothgar
is hedging, not loyal.

The prohibition's real function is not prevention. It is
leverage. Every union violates the spirit of the law — Ordovast's
men have beaten confessions out of suspected curse-workers
without ting sanction, which is itself a prohibited act. Sigrun
funds a galdr workshop that experiments with rune combinations
the law-speaker's formula would cover if anyone looked closely
enough. The Pale Widow's violations are simply more visible
because her union is weakest, and weak unions cannot afford the
ambiguity that strong ones take for granted.

The dark arts scale runs to 5. The Whispering Circle is at 2.
Level 3 is Veil-thinning — deliberate weakening of the barrier
between the living and the dead. Level 4 is death-reading and
targeted assassination through magical means. Level 5 is
invocation: calling something through the Veil and giving it
purchase in the world.

Grim can thin the Veil. The Cave-Woman can death-read. Between
them, the Whispering Circle is one decision away from level 3,
and one desperate night away from level 4. The distance between
2 and 5 is not measured in skill. It is measured in what the
Pale Widow is willing to pay, and what she believes she has
left to lose.

_Power in the Rimevegr is local, personal, and temporary. A
jarl's authority extends one day's march from his seat. Magic
does not change this. Magic is the tool a jarl uses to hold
what force alone cannot — the grain-store through the Long
Dark, the border-stones through the Veil-mist, the silence
of the dead who know too much about how he came to sit where
he sits. The man with the runes controls the ward-line. The
ward-line is the only thing between the settlement and three
hundred days of dark. Everything else is negotiation._

## Contradictions and Disputes

The three traditions do not agree. This is not a failing of
scholarship or a gap in the record. It is the fundamental
condition of the supernatural in the Rimevegr: nobody knows how
any of it works, and the people who understand it best disagree
the loudest.

These disputes are not academic. They determine whether you burn
a draugr or try to speak with it. Whether you reinforce a
ward-stone or abandon the barrow. Whether you trust the
seiðr-wife's screaming trance or the galdr-worker's quiet runes.
Getting the answer wrong means someone dies in a way that proves
nothing, because the dead do not explain themselves and the
survivors argue about what happened for the next thirty years.

Five disputes define the fracture lines. Each has been argued
around fires, in mead-halls, at barrow-mouths with the bodies
still warm. None has been settled.

### What Is the Hush?

The Hush is real. Every band that has marched through deep forest
has experienced it: sound dies. Not muffles — dies. Birdsong
stops mid-note. Wind noise vanishes. Your own breathing becomes
the loudest thing in the world, and then even that seems to thin,
as though the air itself is holding still. It lasts minutes.
Sometimes longer. Then it lifts, and the forest resumes as though
nothing happened.

(See `06_DICTIONARY_OF_NORSE_TERMS.md` — _The Hush_: "Something
is listening.")

The galdr-workers say the Hush is rune-death. The world's magical
structure is inscribed in patterns older than human carving — the
runes that hold reality together, the deep grammar beneath weather
and wood and stone. Where those patterns decay, the fabric frays.
Sound is structure. Where structure fails, sound fails. The Hush
marks a place where the world's own rune-work has gone blank.

This predicts that the Hush should be location-fixed — tied to
specific sites where the old patterns have degraded. It sometimes
is. There are stands of pine in the Ironwood where the Hush falls
reliably, and galdr-workers who have examined the area report that
rune-work carved there fades faster, as though the medium itself
is spent. Ash has felt it. He does not carve in Hush-zones if he
can avoid it. The strokes feel wrong — not resistant, but hollow.
Cutting into wood that has forgotten what shape means.

The seiðr-wives say the Hush is attention. Something behind the
Veil is listening, and it is large enough — present enough — that
its attention alone displaces sound the way a hand displaces
water. The Hush is not absence. It is pressure. The silence of a
room when someone powerful walks in and everyone stops talking at
once.

This predicts that the Hush should move. It does. It follows no
wind pattern, no terrain logic, no seasonal rhythm. It drifts.
The seiðr-wives say it drifts the way a gaze drifts — scanning,
pausing, moving on. Dalla has been caught in two Hush events on
the march, a week apart, in locations thirty miles from each
other. She sat very still both times and said nothing afterward.
She did not need to. Her face said it.

The wyrd-readers say the Hush is the absence of fate. A moment
where nothing is woven. The loom stops, the thread hangs slack,
and the world holds its breath because for that interval nothing
is determined — not the bird's next note, not the wind's next
gust, not whether the man standing in the silence will live to
hear sound again. Petra's bone lots, cast during a Hush, came up
blank. Not a bad reading. No reading. The bones fell and meant
nothing, which had never happened to her before and which she
refuses to discuss.

All three predictions are occasionally correct. The Hush sometimes
stays in one place. The Hush sometimes moves. The Hush sometimes
coincides with wyrd-dead zones where readings fail. Nobody has
assembled the full pattern because nobody who studies one tradition
trusts the observations of the other two, and the Hush does not
last long enough for collaborative research even if collaboration
were possible, which it is not, because galdr-workers and
seiðr-wives can barely stand to be in the same room.

### Do the Gods Still Exist?

The temples stand. No one answers. This is established fact and
has been for three hundred years (see Section 1 — _The Silence of
the Gods_).

The interesting question is not the silence. The interesting
question is what still works and why.

Seiðr-wives who contact very old dead — pre-Cracking dead,
spirits that remember the world before — receive fragmentary
impressions of divine presence. Not words. Not messages. An
aftertaste, the way a room smells of smoke long after the fire
is out. Ragnhild at Icebreak (WYR 5, Spirit-lore 4) reached a
dead man who had served as a temple-keeper before the Cracking.
What came back was not language. It was weight. A sense of
something vast that had been there and was no longer there, and
the shape of its absence was specific enough that Ragnhild could
tell the difference between the absence of Odin and the absence
of Freyja. Different voids. Different silences. She described it
as walking through a hall where the furniture has been removed
but the floor is still worn in the paths where people walked.

Post-Cracking dead report nothing. No divine presence. No divine
absence. Nothing to compare against. Whatever the gods were, the
dead who never knew them cannot feel the hole.

But galdr still works.

This is the problem that no one wants to examine. Galdr was
originally Odin's gift — the runes given to those who could hear
them, the carving-knowledge that channels power through inscribed
form. If Odin is gone, who is powering the runes? The channels
should be dry. The tools should be inert. Ash should be scratching
meaningless lines into meaningless stone.

He is not. The runes light up. The wards hold. Something answers
when a galdr-worker carves a binding-mark. Something provides the
force that makes cold iron glow blue and dead men flinch away
from a threshold.

Galdr-workers avoid this question with a discipline that borders
on mania. Some theorize that the runes are self-sustaining — that
Odin's gift, once given, operates independently of the giver, the
way a mill keeps grinding after the builder dies. Others suggest
that the power source was never Odin at all, that the gods merely
discovered the runes and passed them along, and the real source
is something older and less personal. This theory is unpopular
because it raises the follow-up question — _what is older than
the gods?_ — and the seiðr-wives' theory about the Devouring
provides an answer that no one wants.

Three galdr-workers in living memory have pursued this question to
its end. Two went mad. The third walked into the Veil and did not
return. The community treats these outcomes as evidence that the
question is dangerous, not that it has no answer. The distinction
matters. A question with no answer is philosophy. A question that
destroys the people who pursue it is something else.

The rituals continue. Not because anyone believes they are heard.
Because stopping is an admission, and the admission is a door,
and no one knows what is on the other side of that door but the
seiðr-wives have guesses and their guesses keep them up at night.

### Can Draugr Be Healed?

The seiðr tradition says yes. A draugr is a restless dead — a
spirit that has not been properly laid, or that has been pushed
back across the Veil by whatever frightens the dead (see
Section 1 — _The Seiðr-Wives' Theory_). Communion can reach them.
The right rites — speaking their name, acknowledging their grief,
performing the funerary obligations that were skipped or botched
— can guide them to stillness. A seiðr-wife will sit with a
draugr the way she sits with a frightened child. She will speak
to it in trance. She will find out what it wants and try to
give it.

Dalla has done this. Once. The details are not shared widely, but
the draugr stopped walking and the barrow resealed itself and
Dalla did not speak for four days afterward. She considers it a
success. The band is not sure.

The galdr tradition says no. A draugr is a broken rune — a body
animated by decaying magic, the same way a ward-stone glows after
the galdr-worker who carved it is dead. The body moves because
the pattern inscribed on it by life has not fully degraded. There
is nothing to commune with. There is no spirit to lay. You would
not negotiate with a cracked millstone still turning after the
miller has gone home. You would smash it before it grinds
someone's hand off.

Common wisdom agrees with the galdr-workers but for simpler
reasons: burn it. Draugr are dangerous. They kill people. The
theological question of whether they have an inner life is a
luxury for practitioners who do not have to stand in a barrow
mouth at midnight with a torch and a hand-axe.

The real question — the one nobody asks because the implications
are unbearable — is whether the draugr was ever actually dead.
The named draugr of the Rimevegr complicate every theory. Eydis
Hearth-Ash (WYR 3) remembers her household. She has preferences.
She avoids firelight not because it harms her but because it
reminds her of something. Skorri Iron-Lock (WYR 4) guards his
barrow not from mindless territorial instinct but because he
knows what is buried there and does not want it found. These are
not broken runes. These are not empty patterns grinding on
without purpose. These are beings with agendas, knowledge, and
secrets.

(See `06_DICTIONARY_OF_NORSE_TERMS.md` — _Draugr_: "Not
mindless — they remember old grudges.")

If a draugr remembers, can it forget? If it has an agenda, can
the agenda be fulfilled? If it can be fulfilled, is destruction
murder?

The galdr-workers do not engage with these questions. The
seiðr-wives engage with nothing else. The bands hired to clear
barrows develop their own heuristics: if it talks, try talking
back. If it does not talk, use fire. If it talked last week and
stopped, use fire faster. These are not theological positions.
They are survival rules, and they contradict each other as often
as the theories do.

### Is Wyrd-Reading Magic?

Many practitioners insist it is not. Casting lots is pattern
recognition. Reading entrails is observation — the state of a
liver tells you about the animal's health, the quality of its
grazing, the season's water. Birds fly predictably if you know
wind, terrain, and migration timing. A good tracker reads sign
on the ground. A good wyrd-reader reads sign in the air. No
spirits involved. No divine gift. No Veil.

This is a comfortable position and it has the weight of common
sense behind it. Thorne holds it. He calls his rune-amulet work
"reading" — the way you read a room, read a footprint, read
the sky before a storm. Not magic. Attentiveness. A life skill
honed by thirty years of not dying. The captain consults Petra's
bone lots before marches the way he consults the weather: input,
not prophecy.

The philosophical problem is the practitioners themselves.

If wyrd-reading is pattern recognition, why do wyrd-readers
develop the same fatalism? The same thousand-yard stare, the
same inability to plan more than three days ahead, the same
quiet certainty that nothing they do will alter what arrives?
Petra sleeps poorly. Thorne moves his bedroll to the danger
side of camp every single night. Neither of them can articulate
why. Pattern-recognition experts do not develop existential
dread from reading weather signs.

The mechanical evidence is harder to dismiss. The wyrd_chance
formula in `20_SIMULATION_RULES.md` includes the Wyrd stat:
`(Wyrd × 5) + (Wyrd-reading × 10) + 15`. Wyrd is the
supernatural attribute. It rises with exposure to the uncanny.
If wyrd-reading is merely clever observation, why does the
supernatural stat determine whether the lots fall true? Why
does a man's capacity for touching the Veil affect his ability
to read bird-flight?

The wyrd-readers have no answer. They have a silence, which
they maintain with the same dogged commitment that the
galdr-workers bring to not asking who powers the runes. The
alternative — admitting that their craft is supernatural —
brings consequences. Galdr-workers are respected but watched.
Seiðr-workers are feared and stigmatized, especially the men.
Wyrd-readers pass unnoticed because their craft looks like
common sense. The moment it stops looking like common sense,
they become practitioners, and practitioners pay costs that
readers do not. Nobody volunteers for that reclassification.
So the lots fall, and the readings come true more often than
chance allows, and everyone agrees it is not magic, and nobody
sleeps well.

### Who Owns Barrow Contents?

When a barrow is cleared — the draugr put down, the wards
assessed, the contents catalogued — four separate claims land
on the loot before the dust has settled.

**Galdr-workers** claim rune-inscribed artifacts as sacred
heritage. A binding-ring carved before the Cracking is not
treasure. It is a surviving specimen of a dying art, as
valuable as the Deepholm archive scrolls, irreplaceable and not
for sale. Galdr-workers who examine barrow finds handle them
the way a healer handles a wound — gently, seriously, with an
anger directed at anyone who would melt the ring down for its
silver.

**Seiðr-wives** say the contents should stay in the barrow.
The dead placed them there. The dead remember them. Removing
grave goods offends the spirits and thins the Veil at the
barrow site. Every barrow opened and looted is a hole in the
world's defenses that nobody can patch. The seiðr-wives have
evidence: cleared barrows that were looted show higher
Veil-thinning activity in subsequent years than cleared barrows
where the contents were left in place. The galdr-workers dispute
the methodology. The seiðr-wives dispute the galdr-workers'
right to dispute anything involving the dead.

**Jarls** say barrow loot belongs to whoever paid for the
clearing. A jarl who hires a band to deal with a barrow that
threatens a settlement has funded the operation, assumed the
political risk, and will answer to the settlement if the
clearing goes wrong. The loot is payment, or at least
collateral. This is a property argument, not a theological one,
and it has the force of law behind it — or what passes for law
in the Rimevegr, which is the jarl's word backed by the jarl's
armed men.

**Bands** say it belongs to whoever went in and survived. The
jarl did not fight the draugr. The galdr-worker did not dodge
the axe. The seiðr-wife was not standing in the burial chamber
when Skorri Iron-Lock came off his slab with murder in his
dead eyes and a blade that had not rusted in two hundred years.
The people who bled for the contents own the contents. This is
not a legal argument. It is a moral one, and it is shouted
louder than any of the others because the people shouting it
are the ones with fresh wounds.

The Grave Wardens — a rival band that specializes in barrow
work — have built their entire reputation on navigating these
claims. They negotiate splits before the first torch is lit.
Jarl gets the silver. Band keeps weapons and armor. Galdr-workers
get first examination rights on any inscribed artifacts, with a
time limit. Seiðr-wives get to perform rites before removal.
The system works until it doesn't, and it doesn't whenever a
barrow produces something genuinely powerful — a blade that
hums, a ring that warms when draugr are near, an amulet carved
with runes that no living galdr-worker can read.

Then the four claims collide and someone draws a weapon and the
barrow-clearing becomes a different kind of problem entirely.

Every band contract in the Rimevegr carries this dispute in its
margins. The written terms say one thing. The unwritten
understanding says another. The galdr-workers and seiðr-wives
watch each other across the barrow mouth, each waiting for the
other to reach for something they shouldn't. The jarl's man
counts coins. The band leader counts exits.

_The five disputes above share a single root: nobody understands
the supernatural, and the people who understand it best disagree
the most. This is not ignorance. It is the condition of a world
where magic is real, measurable, and opaque — a machine that
works but has no manual, operated by people who have each read a
different page of the maintenance log and are convinced their
page is the only one that matters._
